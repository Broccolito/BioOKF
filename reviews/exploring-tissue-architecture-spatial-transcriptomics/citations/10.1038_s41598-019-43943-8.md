Published: xx xx xxxx **Multiplexed error-robust fuorescence in situ hybridization (MERFISH) allows simultaneous imaging** **of numerous RNA species in their native cellular environment and hence spatially resolved single-cell** **transcriptomic measurements. However, the relatively modest brightness of signals from single RNA** **molecules can become limiting in a number of applications, such as increasing the imaging throughput,** **imaging shorter RNAs, and imaging samples with high degrees of background, such as some tissue** **samples. Here, we report a branched DNA (bDNA) amplifcation approach for MERFISH measurements.** **T his approach produces a drastic signal increase in RNA FIS H samples without increasing the** **fuorescent spot size for individual RNAs or increasing the variation in brightness from spot to spot,** **properties that are important for MERFISH imaging. Using this amplifcation approach in combination** **with MERFISH, we demonstrated RNA imaging and profling with a near 100% detection efciency.** **We further demonstrated that signal amplifcation improves MERFISH performance when fewer FISH** **probes are used for each RNA species, which should allow shorter RNAs to be imaged. We anticipate** **that the combination of bDNA amplifcation with MERFISH should facilitate many other applications** **and extend the range of biological questions that can be addressed by this technique in both cell culture** **and tissues.**

Single-molecule fuorescence in situ hybridization (smFISH) provides both quantitative measurements of RNA expression and information about RNA spatial localization by directly imaging individual RNA molecules in single cells 1,2 . Te ability of smFISH to visualize gene expression at single-cell resolution has generated many critical insights for diferent biological processes, such as cell fate determination during cell division, local trans- lation, cell migration, the establishment of cell polarity, and body patterning in development3. In recent years, multiplexed smFISH 4–9 and in situ sequencing 10–12 have been developed to increase the number of RNA species that can be simultaneously imaged within cells or tissues, with several technologies enabling the profling of hundreds to thousands of RNAs simultaneously at single cell resolution 9,11–13 . Tese approaches have been used to reveal the internal organization of the transcriptome within cells, discover novel cell types and identify cells based on their expression profle, and map out the organization of diferent cell types within tissues 9,12–14 . Among these approaches, multiplexed error robust fuorescence in situ hybridization (MERFISH) massively multiplexes smFISH by assigning error-robust barcodes to individual RNA species, labeling RNAs with oligonucleotides that represent each barcode, and sequential smFISH imaging to read out these barcodes9, which has allowed single-cell transcriptomic profling in both cultured cells or tissue slices 9,14,15 . Te fuorescent signal produced from a single RNA molecule in MERFISH is generated from the binding of multiple fuorescently labeled probes to each RNA. While the signal produced from these probes is sufciently bright to allow individual RNA molecules to be identifed and detected in cell culture 9,16 and cleared slices of the mouse brain 14,15 , the limited brightness of these signals makes some biological questions still challenging to address. For example, limited signal brightness requires relatively long camera exposures with high-power laser illumination sources, which in turn limits the number of cells and the volume of tissue that can be imaged in a given time. Moreover, the use of multiple unique probes per RNA requires targeted RNAs to have sufcient length to bind these probes, making the measurement of short RNAs challenging. Finally, although a customized clearing approach has been reported for MERFISH15, residual autofuorescence that is difcult to remove (e.g. from lipofuscin) 17 can produce signals that challenge the imaging of single RNA molecules in some samples. Each of these limitations could be addressed by using amplifcation to increase the brightness of the signal produced via individual RNA molecules; thus, it is desirable to combine MERFISH with methods for signal amplifcation. In addition to the degree of signal increase, there are other important properties that should be considered for the ideal amplifcation method. First, to accurately detect and distinguish closely spaced RNAs, it is desirable to have an amplifcation method that does not substantially increase the fuorescent spot size afer amplifcation. Next, to more easily diferentiate background fuorescence spots from spots produced by real RNAs, it is desirable not to substantially increase the spot-to-spot brightness variation of RNA signals afer ampli- fcation. While greater degrees of amplifcation would provide greater benefts for the issues discussed above, more amplifcation is likely to result in larger spot size and more variability in spot brightness; thus, the ideal amplifcation approach would also allow the degree of amplifcation to be precisely controlled thereby setting the best balance between amplifcation, spot size, and brightness variability. Last, MERFISH utilizes multiple distinct readout sequences, one associated with each bit in the barcodes assigned to RNAs; thus, the amplifcation method needs to be easily extended to the amplifcation of a large number of orthogonal sequences. Several methodologies have already been introduced to amplify FISH signals. In hybridization chain reac- tion (HCR), two fuorescently labeled metastable hairpin oligonucleotides self-assemble into long fuorescent polymers starting from an initiator sequence present on each probe 18–20 . In rolling-circle amplifcation (RCA), each probe is circularized to create a template which is then replicated into long concatenated copies containing binding sites for a second fuorescently labeled oligonucleotide probe 21–23 . Finally, in branched DNA (bDNA) amplifcation, primary and secondary amplifer oligonucleotides, each containing multiple replicate binding sites, are assembled on each smFISH probe to form a branched structure which binds multiple copies of a fuorescently labeled probe 24–27 . Recently, a bDNA-like approach was introduced, clampFISH, in which the amplifer molecules in each round of amplifcation are covalently circularized via click chemistry, topologically entangling individual amplifer molecules to increase binding specifcity28. Each technique has advantages and potential disadvantages. HCR and RCA have the advantage that the degree of amplifcation can be tuned by changing the hybridization or polymerization time and a very large degree of amplifcation can be obtained. However, both techniques have been reported to increase the size of fuores- cent spots, with the post-amplifcation spot sizes ranging from being difraction limited up to ~1 micron 20,29 . In addition, HCR has been reported to introduce a variable degree of amplifcation for diferent copies of the same target molecule (with a variation of ~10-fold) 13 . By contrast, the degree of amplifcation in bDNA amplifcation is fxed by design, i.e. the assembled bDNA structures cannot grow indefnitely, even in the presence of abundant reagents. We thus anticipate that it will be more straightforward to control the spot size or limit the increase in variability in brightness from molecule to molecule with this approach. However, only a small number of primary and secondary amplifer pairs have been reported, and bDNA has not yet been combined with highly multiplexed RNA imaging. Here, we developed an approach that combines MERFISH with bDNA amplifcation to dramatically increase the MERFISH signal brightness. We demonstrated that bDNA amplifers can be rapidly assembled, that they can increase the spot brightness by more than an order of magnitude without increasing spot size, and that they do not increase the variability in spot brightness. To combine this approach with MERFISH, we developed 16 pairs of orthogonal bDNA amplifers and demonstrated substantial increase in single-molecule signals in MERFISH measurements using these amplifers. Although the MERFISH performance was similar with and without ampli- fcation when a large number of hybridization probes were used per gene, when fewer hybridization probes were used, bDNA amplifcation substantially improved the detection efciency of MERFISH. Tis improvement should allow MERFISH to be extended to the study of short RNAs. bDNA amplifcation thus promotes high per- formance MERFISH measurements by providing a fast, simple, and efcient way to simultaneously amplify the signal from a large number of RNAs in single cells.

### Results

**The design of a bDNA amplifcation scheme for MERFISH measurements.** To perform MERFISH measurements, we assign each RNA of interest a unique barcode drawn from a barcoding scheme that enables error detection and, when needed, error correction. Te sample is then stained with a complex library of oli- gonucleotide probes, termed encoding probes, which efectively imprint the desired barcode onto each RNA species 9,16 . Each encoding probe contains a 30-mer ‘target region’, whose sequence is complimentary to a region of the target RNA, and multiple 20-mer ‘readout’ sequences (Fig. 1a). For binary barcodes, we assign one unique readout sequence to each bit in the barcode, and the encoding probe set corresponding to a given RNA contains the readout sequences for the bits at which the barcode assigned to that RNA reads ‘1’. Te value (‘1’ or ‘0’) of a given bit is then determined by hybridizing a fuorescently labeled ‘readout probe’ complementary to the corre- sponding readout sequence. Our strategy to incorporate bDNA amplifcation with MERFISH is summarized in Fig. 1. First, we bind to the sample MERFISH encoding probes as described above. We then bind a set of primary amplifer oligonu- cleotides, in which a unique primary amplifer is targeted to each readout sequence (Fig. 1b). Each primary amplifer contains N repeats of a unique 20-mer binding site. A set of secondary amplifer oligonucleotides are then added to the sample (Fig. 1c). Each unique secondary amplifer is targeted to the binding site on one corre- sponding primary amplifer and contains N repeats of another unique 20-mer binding site (Fig. 1c). In this fashion, each original readout sequence would be amplifed into N2 copies of another binding sequence that allows the binding of N2 fuorescent probes. Tus, the efective readout signal would be theoretically amplifed N2 fold. We term this process N × N amplifcation. In addition, we made one modifcation in our design of the bDNA amplifers as compared to those pre- viously reported 24,30 to potentially improve the performance of these amplifers. Specifcally, we designed the amplifers sequences using only three of the four nucleotides with the primary amplifers comprising only A, T, and C and the secondary amplifers comprising only A, T, and G. It has been shown previously that FISH probe sequences comprising only three of the four nucleotides are substantially less likely to form secondary structures than sequences comprising all four nucleotides31. In addition, we have previously shown that MERFISH readout probes designed using this reduced nucleotide alphabet strategy have higher hybridization rates and binding specifcity, likely because of the reduced probability in forming secondary structures16. Tus, we anticipate that amplifers comprising only three of the four nucleotides will also have a lower probability to form undesired sec- ondary structures, which should in turn lead to higher hybridization rates and higher probabilities of assembling correctly.

**Properties of bDNA amplifcation.** To test this strategy and quantify the performance of bDNA ampli- fication on readout sequences, we first designed one pair of three-letter bDNA amplifiers and performed

amplifcation on smFISH probes targeting a single RNA. Te smFISH probes were designed in a similar way to MERFISH encoding probes: each smFISH probe contains a 30-mer target sequence complimentary to the target RNA template and four diferent 20-mer readout sequences used in MERFISH measurements. We only used one of these readout sequences for this smFISH measurement. We designed 48 encoding probes targeting diferent regions of the flamin A (FLNA) mRNA. We then stained a culture of human osteosarcoma cells (U-2 OS) with these probes. To reduce background, we utilized a matrix imprinting and clearing approach in which these sam- ples were also stained with an acrydite-modifed poly(dT) locked nucleic acid (LNA) oligonucleotide ‘anchor probe’ that targets the poly(A) tail of mRNAs15. We then embedded these samples in a thin flm of polyacrylamide to which the anchor probes were covalently incorporated, followed by detergent and proteinase K treatment to clear the sample of lipids and proteins. Following the clearing, we stained these samples with the primary amplifer for 15 minutes. We then washed the sample for 15 minutes in the same conditions to remove excess primary amplifer and repeated this process with the secondary amplifer. Each amplifer contained 5 binding sites in this 5 × 5 amplifcation scheme. We then measured the average FLNA smFISH spot brightness of samples either directly labeled with readout probes on the encoding probes (unamplifed) or labeled with readout probes targeting the amplifed bDNA (amplifed) (Fig. 2a,b). We observed a 10.5-fold signal increase averaged across ~10,000 molecules in the amplifed scheme versus the unamplifed detection scheme (Fig. 2c). Tis increase represents 42% of the theoretical maximum amplifcation value. To test whether the lower than theoretical maximum amplifcation factor was due to an insufcient staining time, we conducted a time series of the same 5 × 5 amplifcation, with increasing amplifer staining time. We found that the amplifed signal was already saturated when we hybridized amplifers with 15 minutes each round (Fig. 2c). Next, we asked whether the number of repeating sequences on each amplifer could change the amplifcation performance. We designed a pair of 4 × 4 amplifers and 9 × 9 amplifers using the same binding sequences in the 5 × 5 amplifers described above and repeated the amplifcation with FLNA smFISH probes (Fig. 2d). We observed a 5.5-fold and a 30.6-fold signal increase with 4 × 4 amplifcation and 9 × 9 amplifcation, respectively. Tus, the degree of amplifcation can be tuned with the number of binding sites, and for all amplifcation schemes tested we observed ~40% of the theoretical amplifcation values. One potential, undesired consequence of amplifcation is the increase of FISH spot sizes, which could limit the density of RNAs that can be imaged and identifed due to an increased chance of signal overlap between neighboring molecules. As a rough estimate of the potential spot size increase due to bDNA amplifcation, we considered the length of a fully extended 9 × 9 amplifer scafold which is expected to be 132 nm. Tis length is within the difraction limit; thus, we did not anticipate a measurable increase in the spot size with even the largest amplifcation considered. Indeed, the measured spot sizes of unamplifed, 4 × 4 amplifed, 5 × 5 amplifed, and 9 × 9 amplifed samples were identical (Fig. 2e). Another concern for amplifcation approaches is the potential to increase the variability in brightness from one molecule to another based on diferential amplifcation. In principle, the fnite amplifcation provided by the defned assembly of the bDNA structures should limit this variability, as the assembly reaction can be run to completion or saturation. To determine any potential increase in the variation in spot brightness due to amplif- cation, we measured the coefcient of variation in spot brightness for unamplifed, 4 × 4 amplifed, 5 × 5 ampli- fed, and 9 × 9 amplifed samples. Notably, we found that the coefcient of variation is similar for all degrees of bDNA amplifcation, indicating that this approach does not increase the variation in spot brightness beyond that observed for unamplifed samples (Fig. 2f).

**Amplifer screening for MERFISH imaging.** To extend bDNA amplifcation to MERFISH measurements, it is necessary to have a unique primary and secondary amplifer pair for each readout sequence used in the meas- urement. For example, with a previously published 16-bit, modifed Hamming distance-4 (MHD4) encoding scheme9, 16 pairs of primary and secondary amplifers are needed. However, previous applications of bDNA have only reported a few amplifer pairs 24,26,30 and the reported pairs did not utilize the three letter alphabet; thus, it was necessary for us to design a large set of new amplifer pairs. To this end, we anticipated that the lower probability of secondary structure formation provided by the use of three-letter sequences would be benefcial. We designed random 20-mer, three-letter repeating sequences with a per-base probability of 25% for A, 25% for T, and 50% for C (for primary amplifers) or 25% for A, 25% for T, and 50% for G (for secondary amplifers). We selected from these sequences a set of orthogonal sequences with limited cross homology using a previously described algorithm32 and then blasted these sequences to the human transcriptome to avoid homology regions longer than 11 nucleotides, as described previously16. We designed a set of orthogonal 5 × 5 amplifer pairs with 20-mer, three-letter sequences and screened each of these pairs for its ability to amplify smFISH signals. We found that 80% of the amplifer pairs designed worked as predicted, producing uniform, bright signals. Te remaining 20% of the amplifers revealed two types of defects (Fig. 3). First, we observed that a small number of secondary amplifers bound to other cellular components than RNA targets (i.e. the binding of these amplifers did not require encoding probes or primary amplifers) (Fig. 3a,b). Tese signals were RNase-dependent indicating that these amplifers were binding non-specifcally to cellular RNA (Fig. 3c). Given the cellular distribution of this binding, we suspected these guanine-rich secondary amplifer sequences might form G-quadruplex structures with mitochondrial RNAs33. In parallel, we observed a small number of amplifer pairs that assembled with much lower efciency, producing only small degrees of amplifcation (Fig. 3e), perhaps due to low melting temperature or the formation of G-quadruplex structures that inhibit proper amplifer assembly. When we replaced these amplifers with new pairs containing diferent sequences, both the high background problem (Fig. 3d) and the low amplifcation efciency problem were solved (Fig. 3f). To determine the performance of MERFISH in amplifed samples, we considered several performance met- rics. First, we examined the average count per cell of the 10 barcodes not assigned to any RNAs, i.e. ‘blank’ bar- codes. We found that 121 of the 130 RNA species in the 5 × 5 amplifed MERFISH measurement had a higher copy number per cell than the maximum copy number per cell observed with the blank barcodes (Fig. 4d). A similar rate of ‘blank’ barcode detection was observed previously in unamplifed samples16, indicating that ampli- fcation does not increase RNA misidentifcation rates. Next, we investigated the average 1-to-0 error and 0-to-1 error rate for each bit (Fig. 4e). We observed an average 1-to-0 error rate of ∼1.7% and a 0-to-1 error rate of ∼0.6%, comparable to the values observed previously with unamplifed data15. In addition, we found that the copy number per cell results were highly reproducible between replicates of MERFISH experiments with amplifcation (Fig. 4f). Next, to determine if amplifcation resulted in a decreased ability to detect RNAs, we compared the average copy number per cell from 5 × 5 amplifed data with that from previously unamplifed MERFISH data15, with the measured values per gene averaged across three replicates of amplifed and unamplifed samples. We found that these values correlated strongly with a Pearson correla- tion coefcient of 0.98 (Fig. 4g; ρ10 = 0.98 for the 123 RNA species whose measured copy numbers were larger than that observed for the largest blank barcode count) and that the average ratio was 1.04 ± 0.03 (SEM, n = 123 RNAs). Thus, we conclude that amplification maintained the high detection efficiency (>90%) previously reported for MERFISH15. In addition, we compared the average copy number per cell detected for these RNAs by 5 × 5 amplifed MERFISH measurements with the RNA abundance measured by RNA-seq (Fig. 4h). We observed a Pearson correlation coefcient of 0.91, comparable to our previously published data from unamplifed samples16. Tus, based on each of these performance metrics, we conclude that bDNA amplifcation substantially increases the brightness of individual molecules measured with MERFISH without introducing additional noise.

#### Amplifcation improves the performance of MERFISH for measurements with fewer encod-

To test the ability of bDNA amplifcation to improve the performance of MERFISH in circumstances where fewer encoding probes are used per gene, we designed an encoding probe library targeting the same 130 genes utilized above but in which we included only 16 encoding probes for each gene as opposed to the 92 utilized above. We have previously shown that the detection efciency of MERFISH experiments is nearly 100% when 92 encoding probes are used per gene. Tis experimental design thus allowed us to compare the results with the 92-probe experiments to quantify detection efciency. We frst performed MERFISH measurements with 16 encoding probes per gene in U-2 OS cells (Fig. 5a,b) without bDNA amplifcation. As expected, we observed that the signals from individual RNA molecules in each round of staining and imaging were indeed substantially dimmer than the signals observed when 92 encoding probes were used per gene. Nonetheless, we found that the average copy number per cell for individual RNAs correlated well with that measured using 92 encoding probes per gene (Fig. 5c), with a Pearson correlation coefcient of 0.8. However, we detected fewer RNA molecules per cell in these measurements: the ratio of the total RNA copy number per cell measured with 16 encoding probes

relative to 92 encoding probes was 0.32. Across three independent replicates, this ratio was 0.33 ± 0.02 (SEM) (Fig. 5g), confrming that the dimmer signals from individual RNA molecules led to fewer RNAs being detected. To then determine the improvement provided with bDNA amplifcation, we repeat the MERFISH measure- ments with 16 encoding probes per gene in combination with the 5 × 5 bDNA amplifcation scheme (Fig. 5d,e). As expected, amplifcation increased the brightness of these signals relative to the unamplifed measurements. Tis increased brightness produced an increase in the correlation between the average copy number per cell determined with amplification to that determined with 92 encoding probes per gene but no amplification (Fig. 5f). In addition, the total RNA copy number measured with 16 encoding probes increased substantially afer amplifcation – the ratio of the total RNA copy number measured with the amplifed 16-encoding-probe measurement relative to the unamplifed 92-encoding-probe measurement was measured to be 0.61 ± 0.03 (SEM, 3 replicates) (Fig. 5g), indicating that amplifcation improved the detection efciency of the 16-encoding-probe measurements by about 2-fold. Because a minimum RNA length is required to accommodate a given number of encoding probes per gene, the ability to use fewer encoding probes per gene will allow MERFISH to target shorter genes, and bDNA amplif- cation is likely to be highly benefcial for these measurements. Moreover, we anticipate that with improved encod- ing probe design, hybridization conditions, and other advances, it should be possible to increase the efciency of encoding probe binding and further increase the detection efciency of MERFISH for shorter genes.

### Discussion

We have presented here a combination of MERFISH and bDNA amplifcation. We showed that the bDNA ampli- fers composed of three of the four nucleotides bind to targets rapidly, reaching saturated binding within 15 min- utes. We demonstrated that this approach amplifed RNA smFISH signals by 5.5-fold, 10.5-fold, and 30.6-fold using 4 × 4 amplifcation, 5 × 5 amplifcation and 9 × 9 amplifcation, respectively, without increasing the size of fuorescent spots or increasing the variation in brightness from molecule to molecule, properties that are impor- tant for MERFISH performance. We also demonstrated that with bDNA amplifcation MERFISH maintained the ability to accurately identify and count RNAs with a near 100% detection efciency with 92 encoding probes per gene. Finally, we showed that bDNA amplifcation substantially improved the detection efciency of MERFISH (from ~30% to ~60%) when only 16 encoding probes were used per gene. Notably, we observed a maximum amplifcation around 40% of the theoretical maximum for all amplifer designs (4 × 4, 5 × 5, and 9 × 9) tested. Tus, the binding efciencies of the amplifers were not 100%. If we assume that the binding efciencies were equal for each round of amplifer staining, the average binding ef- ciency per round would have been ~65%. In addition, we found that, of the screened amplifer sequences, 20% of the amplifers had either a high RNA-dependent background or a low amplifcation efciency. In particular, the RNA-dependent background was unexpected because the sequences were blasted to avoid homology with the human transcriptome. We suspect that these specifc amplifers, containing a relatively large number of gua- nine nucleotides in the 20-mer repeating sequences, might form hybrid G-quadruplex structures, which have been reported to bind to mitochondrial RNA33. Te specifc spatial distribution observed with the staining of these amplifers (Fig. 3a,b) was consistent with the distribution of mitochondria, supporting this hypothesis. In parallel, the low amplifcation efciency of a few amplifers could have arisen for a few reasons. One reason could be that the melting temperatures of amplifer sequences were unexpectedly low, inhibiting the assembly of these amplifers or promoting their disassembly in the hybridization and wash conditions used. Alternatively, it is possible that these amplifers also formed stable G-quadruplex structures, which inhibited the binding of those secondary amplifers to primary amplifers. Despite these few failures, the vast majority of the designed ampli- fers worked as anticipated, indicating that this bDNA approach could be readily extended to the amplifcation of more readout sequences and hence to MERFISH measurement with longer barcodes, detecting more RNAs. We also anticipate that it is possible to design amplifers to allow a third or fourth round of amplifcation and, thus, produce signal intensities ~100–1000-fold larger. When the signal amplifcation requirement is not high, it should also be possible to perform experiments with just a single round of amplifcation. While our manuscript was in preparation, a preprint was posted on bioRxiv reporting a method for the enzymatic production of long bDNA amplifers and simultaneous imaging of ~10 RNA species using these amplifers34. Unlike the solid-phase synthesized bDNA amplifers used here, which have a well-defned length and can be purchased ready-to-use, this enzymatic production method generates amplifer molecules with a distribution (albeit likely narrow) of lengths and requires some hands-on work to produce amplifers, but can produce longer amplifer molecules at a potentially lower cost. Te substantial increase in signal brightness generated by bDNA amplifcation should facilitate several areas of applications of MERFISH. First, with amplifcation, we have performed MERFISH measurements using 16 encoding probes per RNA, which gave substantially higher detection efciency than the unamplifed case. Tus, this amplifcation approach should allow shorter RNAs to be probed. Since our encoding probes target 30-nt-long regions of each RNA, RNAs as short as 480 nt could be targeted with the 16 encoding probe design. Furthermore, because only a fraction of the designed encoding probes actually bind to each RNA, encoding probes can be designed such that they target overlapping regions of the same RNA in order to increase the number of probes that could be designed for a fxed length of RNA, thereby increasing the signal 14,35 . Indeed, we have successfully used MERFISH encoding probes that overlap by as much as 20-nt to reduce the RNA length requirement by 3 fold, as compared to the non-overlapping probe design, with no obvious reduction in signal brightness 14,35 . Tus, with this overlapping probe design, we anticipate that RNAs as short as ~160 nt could potentially be detected. With additional improvements in encoding probe design and hybridization conditions, it may be possible to use even few probes and detect even shorter genes. Moreover, the ability to detect RNA molecules with relatively few probes would also improve RNA isoforms discrimination. Te need to use fewer encoding probes per gene will also reduce the cost of encoding probes. Because encoding probes difer for diferent gene sets, but the same

set of amplifers can be used for diferent gene sets, and because the per-sample costs for amplifers are low (see Materials and Methods), this amplifcation scheme could thus potentially reduce the cost of MERFISH measure- ments when multiple diferent gene sets are probed. Second, with signal amplifcation, it should be possible to substantially reduce the imaging duration by using shorter exposure times. As the imaging time is typically a size- able portion of the total time required to perform a MERFISH measurement, it should be possible to substantially improve MERFISH imaging throughput with amplifcation. Likewise, signal amplifcation will also allow lower power illumination sources to be used, which will reduce the cost of the MERFISH instrument setup and allow a wider set of commercial microscopies to be used for MERFISH measurements. Tird, bDNA amplifcation could increase MERFISH performance for high background samples, for example, samples in which background fuorescence is difcult to remove even with tissue clearing approaches (such as samples containing lipofuscin) 17 . Tus, bDNA amplifcation should greatly facilitate imaging of tissues with high autofuorescence background. Tus, we anticipate that the improved MERFISH performance and versatility provided by this amplifcation method should facilitate the application of spatially resolved single-cell transcriptomics to a wide array of biolog- ical questions.

### Materials and Methods

**Design of the encoding probes.** MERFISH measurements in human osteosarcoma cells (U-2 OS) (ATCC) were performed with a previously described MERFISH-encoding probe set16. Briefy, a 16-bit MHD4 code was used to encode the RNAs. In this specifc encoding scheme, each of the 140 possible barcodes has a constant Hamming weight (i.e., the number of “1” bits in each barcode) of 4 to avoid potential bias in the measurement of diferent barcodes due to a diferential rate of “1” to “0” and “0” to “1” errors. In addition, all barcodes have a Hamming distance of at least 4 to enable error detection and error correction. We used 130 of the 140 possible barcodes to encode cellular RNAs and the remaining 10 barcodes were not assigned to an RNA and served as blank controls. In the frst encoding probe library, each RNA species had 92 encoding probes, with each encoding probe containing three of the four readout sequences assigned to each RNA. Our second encoding probe library was designed identically to the frst with the distinction that each RNA species had 16 encoding probes. Te encoding probe set targeting FLNA (Biosearch) was described previously16. Encoding probe construction, coverslip silanization, cell culture and fxation, encoding probe staining, and gel embedding and clearing were performed as previously described15.

**Amplifer staining.** Amplifers were purchased as PAGE-purifed oligonucleotides from Integrated DNA Technologies (IDT). With this degree of purifcation, each 5 × 5 amplifer oligonucleotide cost ~$85 while the 9 × 9 amplifers each cost $200. However, this synthesis method produced sufcient amplifer oligo for staining ~40,000 samples; thus, the per-sample cost for the 16 amplifer pairs required to amplify 16 diferent readout sequences was low (~$0.07/sample for the 5 × 5 amplifcation scheme and would have been $0.16/sample for the 9 × 9 amplifcation scheme). In addition, we note that by placing the target-binding region of each amplifer at the 3′ end of the oligo, our amplifers were designed such that truncated oligos cannot bind to the encoding probes. Tus, it may be possible to utilize our approach without the expensive PAGE purifcation step and further lower the cost amplifers. Alternatively, it may also be possible to lower the cost of the amplifers by using the same oligopool-based synthesis and amplifcation method we utilize for generating MERFISH encoding probes15. Te recently developed Primer Exchange Reaction approach, which allows long oligos with repeating sequence to be made enzymatically36, could also potentially reduce the cost for generating branched DNA amplifers and could make longer amplifers than possible by solid-phase synthesis, although this approach produces amplifers with a distribution of the number of repeating sequences (i.e. binding sites). To label the gel embedded and cleared samples with primary and secondary amplifers, samples were frst incubated for 5 min in a 10% formamide wash bufer, containing 2 × SSC (TermoFisher) and 10% (vol/vol) formamide (TermoFisher) in nuclease-free water. Next, 50 μL of 5 nM each of the primary amplifers (IDT) in amplifer hybridization bufer, containing 2 × SSC, 10% (vol/vol) formamide, 0.1% (wt/vol) yeast tRNA (Life Technologies), 1% (vol/vol) murine RNase inhibitor (New England Biolabs), and 10% (wt/vol) dextran sulfate (Sigma), was added to a Paraflm-coated surface to form droplets. Samples were inverted onto these 50 μL drop- lets afer removing extra 10% formamide wash bufer with Kimwipes and incubated in a humidity-controlled 37 °C incubator for 30 minutes (for all MERFISH measurements) or for 15 minutes, 30 minutes, 60 minutes and 180 minutes for the binding rate measurements. Ten the samples were washed three times in 10% formamide wash bufer at room temperature in Petri dishes for 5 minutes each. To bind the secondary amplifers, 50 μL of 5 nM each of the secondary amplifers (IDT) in the same amplifer hybridization bufer described above was placed on a fresh paraflm-coated surface, the samples were inverted onto these droplets, and the hybridization was performed as described for the primary amplifers. Ten the samples were washed twice in 10% formamide wash bufer at room temperature for 5 minutes each followed by a third wash in 10% formamide wash bufer in 37 °C incubator for 15 minutes. Te samples were either imaged immediately or stored in 2 × SSC supplemented with 0.1% (vol/vol) murine RNase inhibitor at 4 °C for no longer than 48 h. Readout probes were hybridized to these samples as described previously15.

**ME RFIS H imaging platforms.** Te samples were imaged on a home-built high-throughput imaging plat- form at the Center for Advanced Imaging, Harvard University. Briefy, this microscope was constructed around a Nikon Ti Eclipse microscope body and a Nikon, CFI Plan Apo Lambda 60x oil objective. Illuminations in 750, 647, 560, 488 and 405 nm were provided using solid-state lasers (MBP Communications, 2RU-VFL-P- 500-750-B1R; MBP Communications, 2RU-VFL-P-2000-647-B1R; MBP Communications, 2RU-VFL-P-2000- 560-B1R; MBP Communications, 2RU-VFL-P-500-488-B1R; Coherent, Cube 405). These laser lines were

used to excite readout probes labeled with Alexa750 and Cy5, orange fducial beads, a Poly dT readout probe (Alexa 488) and DAPI, respectively. Te illumination profle was fattened with a πShaper (Pishaper). Te fu- orescence emission from the sample was separated from the laser illumination using a penta-band dichroic (Chroma, zy405/488/561/647/752RP-UF1) and imaged with a scientifc CMOS camera (sCMOS; Hamamatsu, C11440-22CU) afer passing through two duplicate custom penta-notch flters (Chroma, ZET405/488/561/647– 656/752 m) to remove stray excitation light. Te pixel size for the sCMOS camera corresponded to 109 nm in the sample plane. Te exposure time was 500 ms for each imaging frame. Sample X/Y position was controlled via a motorized microscope stage (Ludl). Sample focus was maintained by feedback on the refection of an IR laser (Torlabs, LP980-SF15) of the sample coverslip interface. Te refected IR signal was detected by a CMOS cam- era (Torlabs, DCC1545M) and the sample to objective distance was controlled by an objective nanopositioner (Mad City Labs, Nano-F100S). Te sample coverslip was held inside a fow chamber (Bioptechs, FCS2), and bufer exchange within this chamber was directed using a custom-built automated fuidics system described previously9, controlling three eight-way valves (Hamilton, MVP and HVXM 8–5) and a peristaltic pump (Gilison, Minipuls 3).

**S ample imaging.** Sequential MERFISH imaging was carried out on the high-throughput imaging platform descripted in the MERFISH imaging platforms section. Briefy, each MERFISH round consisted of readout probes staining (10 min), wash bufer washing (5 min), imaging bufer exchange (3 min), imaging of 100–400 difer- ent felds of view (FOV), readout fuorophore cleavage (15 min), and 2xSSC washing (5 min). 8 rounds of two color MERFISH imaging were performed on each sample. Specifcally, the readout probes staining was done by fowing 3 nM readout probes in hybridization bufer comprised of 2 × SSC, 10% (vol/vol) ethylene carbonate (Sigma-Aldrich), 0.1% (vol/vol) murine RNase inhibitor (NEB) in nuclease-free water. Wash bufer contained 2 × SSC and 10% (vol/vol) ethylene carbonate in nuclease-free water. Imaging bufer was made of 2 × SSC, 50 mM Tris ⋅HCl pH 8, 10% (wt/vol) glucose, 2 mM Trolox (Sigma-Aldrich), 0.5 mg/mL glucose oxidase (Sigma-Aldrich), 40 μg/mL catalase (Sigma-Aldrich), and 0.1% (vol/vol) murine RNase inhibitor in nuclease-free water. Cleavage bufer comprising 2 × SSC and 50 mM of Tris (2-carboxyethyl) phosphine (TCEP; Sigma) was used to cleave the disulfde bond conjugating dyes to the probes. smFISH imaging was performed using these protocols but without the cleavage or 2 × SSC washing steps. Each MERFISH measurement contained ~1000 cells.

**Image processing and decoding.** For FLNA smFISH data, the brightness and PSF sizes of unamplifed

and amplifed FISH spots were calculated via a Gaussian ftting routine previously described37. For the MERFISH data, registration of images of the same FOV across diferent imaging rounds as well as decoding of the RNA barcodes was conducted using a previous analysis pipeline16. Briefy, the drif between images in each imaging round was corrected using the localizations of the fducial beads in each round of imag- ing. Next, background in the images were removed via a high-pass flter, and RNA spots were tightened by decon- volution. We have previously found that the signal from the same RNA can vary slightly in position (<100 nm) from round to round. To address this small variation, we then low-pass fltered each image to ensure the sig- nal from the same RNA overlapped in diferent imaging rounds. To remove the natural variation in brightness between color channels and diferent imaging rounds, we normalized the intensity measured for each color chan- nel in each imaging round via the 95% quantile of the corresponding brightness histogram, efectively equalizing the brightness histograms observed for diferent bits. We then compared the normalized intensity for a given pixel in each of the 16 images to the expected intensity produced by each barcode for each of the 16 bits, and we selected the barcode that best matched the observed intensity pattern for that pixel. However, if the Euclidean dis- tance between this pixel intensity pattern and the expected intensity pattern from the closest barcode was larger than a maximum threshold value, the pixel was not assigned to a barcode. Tis threshold distance was set by the maximum Euclidean distance between a correct barcode and each of the incorrect barcodes generated by fipping a single bit in that correct barcode. Finally, adjacent pixels assigned to the same barcode were then combined to form a putative RNA. Tis pipeline was run on a desktop server that contained two 10-core Intel Xeon E5-2680 2.8-GHz CPUs and 256 GB of RAM.
