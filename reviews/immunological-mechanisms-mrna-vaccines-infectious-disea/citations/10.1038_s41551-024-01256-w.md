### Abstract

Lipid nanoparticles (LNPs) are the most clinically advanced delivery vehicle for RNA therapeutics, partly because of established lipid structure–activity relationships focused on formulation potency. Yet such knowledge has not extended to LNP immunogenicity. Here we show that the innate and adaptive immune responses elicited by LNPs are linked to their ionizable lipid chemistry. Specifically, we show that the amine headgroups in ionizable lipids drive LNP immunogenicity by binding to Toll-like receptor 4 and CD1d and by promoting lipid-raft formation. Immunogenic LNPs favour a type-1 T-helper-cell-biased immune response marked by increases in the immunoglobulins IgG2c and IgG1 and in the pro-inflammatory cytokines tumour necrosis factor, interferon γ and the interleukins IL-6 and IL-2. Notably, the inflammatory signals originating from these receptors inhibit the production of anti-poly(ethylene glycol) IgM antibodies, preventing the often-observed loss of efficacy in the LNP-mediated delivery of siRNA and mRNA. Moreover, we identified computational methods for the prediction of the structure- dependent innate and adaptive responses of LNPs. Our findings may help accelerate the discovery of well-tolerated ionizable lipids suitable for repeated dosing.

Some of these efficacious LNPs played a critical role in the SARS-CoV-2 mRNA vaccines that were developed, approved, and globally deployed with unprecedented speed2, stifling the spread of COVID-19. In addition to ionizable lipids, other components of RNA drugs elicit adverse reactions linked to unwanted immune responses. For example, unmodified siRNA suppresses angiogenesis through the Toll-like receptor (TLR) 3 pathway regardless of sequence22. This finding eventually led to the termination of clinical trials of the siRNA macular degeneration drug, bevasiranib23. Since then, several chemical modifications have been introduced in siRNA to minimize off-target toxicities and suppress siRNA-driven immunostimulation23. Further, in some individuals, the PEGylated lipid in the FDA-approved mRNA-1273 and BNT162b2 vaccines invoked a B cell-mediated antibody response with increased systemic reactogenicity in vaccinated individuals17. Separately, in preclinical studies, anti-PEG antibodies have been shown to alter LNP pharmacokinetics and abolish siRNA and mRNA delivery efficacy upon repeat dosing24,25.

These safety concerns are not observed for all LNP structures, which suggests that adverse immune responses can be averted by optimizing LNP chemistry. However, selecting the ideal structure during early developmental stages remains challenging because structure-immune response relationships are unestablished and the fundamental mechanisms governing lipid-immune receptor interactions are poorly understood. Consider the FDA- approved ionizable lipids SM-102 and DLin-MC3-DMA, which both form exceptionally effective RNA delivery vehicles but have strikingly divergent immune profiles12. SM-102, from the Moderna COVID-19 vaccine mRNA-1273, is inflammatory, whereas DLin-MC3- DMA, from Alnylam’s transthyretin amyloidosis siRNA therapy patisiran, is relatively inert12. These lipids were selected for their respective prophylactic and therapeutic applications, because of their differential immune profiles. Unfortunately, the chemical motifs in these and other ionizable lipids that govern their immunogenicity are largely unknown. This fundamental knowledge gap makes it difficult to design next-generation lipids with predictable potency and immune reactiveness that are well tolerated.

### Author Manuscript Results and Discussion

The goal of this study was to establish structure-immunogenicity relationships for the ionizable components in LNPs with a long-term goal of using them in conjunction with structure-potency relationships for enhanced RNA delivery materials development. To this Author Manuscriptend, we synthesized a library of 15 ionizable lipids from three alkyl-amines and five alkyl- acrylate tails (Fig. 1A). We chose 10 to 13 carbon-long tails (O10-O13). Additionally, a tail with a terminal iso-group was used (Oi10) to study the effect of branched tails. Headgroups that formed efficacious lipids with the selected tails were chosen for the study (313, 306,

304). We formulated LNPs using these ionizable lipids along with cholesterol, a helper lipid, a polyethylene glycol (PEG)-lipid and siRNA using a lipid molar ratio of 50:38.5:10:1.5, which is optimal for many siRNA-LNP formulations26,27. All LNP formulations included in this study passed quality controls, i.e., diameters less than 120 nm and dispersity less than 0.2 (Fig. S1A), RNA entrapment values greater than 75% (Fig. S1B), comparable zeta potential (Fig. S1C), and endotoxin-free (Fig. S1D). Further, LNPs were non-toxic to RAW Blue cells in vitro (Fig. S2). Additionally, fluorescently labelled siRNA-LNPs showed similar organ-level distribution with liver and heart highest and lowest uptake, respectively (Fig. S3).
#### Ionizable lipids provoke an innate immune response in vivo

To determine structure-immune response relationships, we first incubated LNPs with peripheral blood mononuclear cells (PBMCs) isolated from healthy human volunteers for 24 hours and measured levels of the inflammatory cytokines TNFα, IL-6, and IL-1β. LNPs were loaded with an siRNA against a non-native target, green fluorescent protein (siGFP), to avoid immune responses that could occur with the knockdown of an innate protein. Because the human cells produced detectable cytokines for only 7 of the 15 LNPs, statistically significant conclusions regarding structure-dependent responses were not possible (Fig. S4). The general absence of cytokines implies the lack of an acute inflammatory response in human PBMCs. These data prompted in vivo studies, given that we required improved signal to noise ratios for inflammatory assays and better recapitulation of immune system complexity.

#### Ionizable lipids provoke an innate immune response via Toll-like receptor 4

Having shown that LNPs activate the innate immune system, we next asked how LNPs stimulate pattern recognition receptors, with a specific interest in the effect of LNP chemistry on this process. We chose to investigate the toll-like receptors 2 (TLR2) and 4 (TLR4), both of which have been previously shown to bind a subset of cationic and ionizable lipids and provoke pro-inflammatory responses30–32. TLR2 is a membrane- bound pattern recognition receptor that detects lipids present in bacteria and yeast, such as lipoteichoic acid33, whereas TLR4 is a transmembrane receptor that recognizes lipopolysaccharide (LPS), a molecular signature of bacterial infection34. Because these lipid-binding receptors are expressed on the cell surface and in endosomal membranes, they are likely the first immune receptors to detect LNPs.

To determine whether TLR2 and/or TLR4 recognize the ionizable lipids in this study, we used Raw Blue™ reporter cells. These RAW 264.7-derived macrophages stably express the secreted embryonic alkaline phosphatase (SEAP) gene, which is activated by the TLR transcription factor NF-κB. Thus, incubation of Raw Blue™ cells with a TLR agonist will trigger the secretion of the surrogate marker SEAP, which is quantified using an absorbance assay. We used the agonists LPS, FSL-1, and Pam3CSK4 as positive controls to stimulate TLR4, TLR2/6, and TLR1/2, respectively35–37. Stimulation of TLR2 occurs only when it forms a heterodimer with TLR1 or TLR638; thus, positive controls are available only in heterodimerized conformation. We also used the antagonists TLR2-mAb and TAK-242 to specifically inhibit TLR2 and TLR4 signaling, respectively39.

The positive controls behaved as expected, with the TLR4 agonist, LPS, stimulating NF-κB activation and an immune response (Fig. 2A). This activation was partially inhibited by co-incubation with the TLR4 inhibitor, TAK-242. Similarly, both FSL-1 and Pam3CSK4 activated NF-κB through TLR2/6 and TLR1/2. Blocking TLR2 using the TLR2-mAb

antibody partially inhibited TLR2/6 and completely inhibited TLR1/2. Of the ionizable

lipids tested, two (313Oi10 and 313O13) activated NF-κB and induced expression of SEAP. Because that activity was mitigated only by the TLR4 inhibitor and not the TLR2 inhibitor (Fig. 2A), we can conclude that our ionizable lipid library stimulates the innate immune system through the TLR4 pathway. Other lipids in the library, such as 306O10, also activate TLR4, but at 4-fold higher dose (Fig. S6).

#### Simulated TLR4 binding predicts the innate immune response to ionizable lipids

To determine the structural basis of LNP recognition by TLR4, we used in silico molecular docking to study the interaction between ionizable lipids with the TLR4/MD-2 complex. MD-2 forms a heterodimer with TLR4 and facilitates the interaction of ligands with

### Author Manuscript

TLR4’s amino acid residues40. This direct interaction of the lipid ligands with TLR4 is critical because it induces necessary conformational changes that activate intracellular signaling cascades41. For example, TLR4 agonists, such as LPS and lipid A, bury their acyl chains in the binding cavity of MD-2 while their phosphorylated glucosamine headgroups protrude out of the cavity and directly interact with TLR4 amino acids through hydrophilic

### Author Manuscript

and hydrophobic interactions40. In contrast, the TLR4 antagonists lipid IVa and eritoran block TLR4 activity by burying inside in the MD-2 cavity without interacting with TLR4 residues34,42.

We observed that ionizable lipids bind weakly TLR4 (>−40 kcal/mol, Table S1) compared to LPS (−166 kcal/mol)43. However, they elicit a similar binding mode as TLR4 ligands under simulated conditions. All lipids bound to the hydrophobic binding pocket of MD-2 (white). However, only a subset interacted with TLR4 amino acid residues (black and red) through hydrophobic interactions and hydrogen bonds (blue), which was required for TLR4 stimulation (Fig. 2B, S7). Therefore, we analyzed these differences in the binding modes of ionizable lipids to gain insight into their structure-dependent immunostimulatory nature.

We found that MC3 and 304O13 are completely buried in the binding pocket of MD-2 (Fig. 2C, 2D) and that they have low binding affinities to the TLR4/MD-2 complex (−19.3 and −26 kcal/mol, respectively). More negative affinity values correlate with increased binding. Consistent with the binding modes of lipid IVa and eritoran discussed above34, MC3 does not interact with any amino acids of TLR4 outside of the binding pocket (Fig. 2C). Further, 304O13 interacts only with one residue at the dimerization interface (black,

Fig. 2D). Finally, neither MC3 nor 304O13 form hydrogen bonds with any residues (Fig.

S7A, S7B), suggesting the absence of TLR4/MD-2-specific binding44.

Conversely, the headgroups of 306O13 and 313O13 partially protrude from the binding cavity (Fig. 2E, 2F), while 200O13 juts out completely (Fig. 2G). The protruding headgroups interact with TLR4 amino acids, with all three lipids interacting with the dimerization interface residues (black) and 200O13 additionally interacting with the primary interface residues (red) (Fig. 2G). All three lipids form at least one hydrogen bond, indicating binding specificity (Fig. S7C–E) and exhibit stronger binding affinity to the TLR4/MD-2 complex: −32.7, −34.6, and −39.9 kcal/mol for 306O13, 313O13, and 200O13, respectively. #### Ionizable lipid structure dictates lipid raft formation

TLR4-mediated signaling requires the formation of a large transmembrane protein complex that includes two TLR4 homodimers and two heterodimers of TLR4 and MD245. To keep

### Author Manuscriptthe protein complex intact during ligand binding and signal transduction, TLR4 is recruited

in rigid cholesterol-rich lipid raft domains of the plasma membrane46. Therefore, TLR4 signal transduction requires not only the binding specificity discussed above, but also recruitment of the TLR4 complex into lipid rafts.

Pertinent to this study, fatty acids are known to alter the lipid composition of lipid rafts and influence TLR4 recruitment in raft regions47. Further, these effects occur in a fatty acid structure-dependent manner. For example, saturated fatty acids (e.g., lauric acid) promote TLR4 activity in a ligand-independent manner by promoting lipid raft formation48–50, while unsaturated fatty acids (e.g., oleic acid) impede it48,51. We have already shown that ionizable lipids differentially stimulate TLR4 according to structure (Fig. 2A) and that this is, at least in part, is due to differences in binding affinities for the TLR4 binding pocket. We next asked whether ionizable lipids could also be promoting or interfering with lipid raft assembly in a structure-dependent manner.

To test this hypothesis, we incubated Raw 264.7 macrophages with LNPs and monitored lipid raft formation using the Vybrant™ Alexa Fluor™ 555 lipid raft labelling kit via flow cytometry. In this assay, fluorescently labelled Cholera Toxin-B binds to the pentasaccharide region of the ganglioside GM1a, which selectively partitions into lipid raft regions. Therefore, higher mean fluorescence intensity of Cholera Toxin-B indicates higher lipid raft formation in cells and, therefore, enhanced TLR4 signaling.

As expected, neither naked siRNA nor cholesterol boosted lipid raft formation relative to PBS-treated cells, while the TLR4-agonist LPS (positive control) induced the most aggregation (Fig. 3A). Additionally, saturated lauric acid promoted raft formation while unsaturated oleic acid showed significantly lower effect (Fig. S10). Among LNPs, 313O13 and 304O10 generated the most and least lipid raft formation in the cell membrane, respectively. Further, for both the O13 and O10 tail groups, LNPs containing the 313 headgroup induced the highest degree of lipid raft formation, followed by 306 and 304. To confirm that this trend is governed by the ionizable lipid, we incubated Raw 264.7 cells with

pure ionizable lipids rather than LNPs. We observed a similar trend in which ionizable lipids

### Author Manuscript

containing the 313 and 306 headgroup stimulated significantly higher lipid raft formation than lipids containing 304 (Fig. 3B).

Having shown that ionizable lipids differentially affect lipid raft activity, we next asked if they also affect TLR4 activity in the raft fractions in a similar manner. For this, we treated Author ManuscriptRaw 264.7 macrophages with blank LNPs that showed highest and lowest raft levels in Fig. 3A (i.e., 313O13 and 304O10). Three hours later, we isolated raft fractions from the cells and measured TLR4 levels. LPS was used as a positive control. Because TLR4 is endocytosed after activation52–55, we observed a significant decrease in TLR4 for LPS and 313O13 (Fig. S11A). Further, 313O13 and LPS increased levels of the raft marker Flotillin-1, confirming

### Author Manuscriptthat they promote raft formation (Fig. S11B). Together, these results indicate that LNPs

differentially activate TLR4 in rafts.

Next, we asked why lipid raft formation depends on the ionizable lipid headgroup. To this end, we studied ionizable lipid interactions with lipid rafts using molecular dynamics simulations. Coarse-grained MARTINI forcefields simulated phase separation of a model

### Author Manuscript

lipid bilayer containing DPPC, DIPC, and cholesterol into a cholesterol-rich raft phase and a cholesterol-depleted disordered phase in the presence of ionizable lipids. MARTINI condenses three or four heavy atoms into a coarse-grain bead and assigns thermodynamic properties based on the polarity and charge of the constituent atoms56,57. The mapping scheme for the ionizable lipids is shown in Figure S12.

Starting with an initial arrangement in which all lipids were randomly distributed throughout the bilayer, we observed that lipids containing 313 and 306 induced phase separation (Fig. 3C, 3D), which is necessary for lipid raft formation and subsequent TLR4 signaling. Both ionizable lipids partitioned into the liquid-disordered phase along with DIPC (green), while DPPC (grey) formed a rigid liquid-ordered phase along with cholesterol (orange). However, 304 interfered with phase separation throughout the course of the simulation. Although we saw small DIPC islands form in the system, phase separation was not observed (Fig. 3E).

In addition to phase separation, we observed differences in the organization of the ionizable lipids in the lipid bilayer. Looking at the relative position of the SN0, SC5, and N0 beads (Fig. S12) from the center of the bilayer for the 313, 306, and 304 lipids, respectively, we observed that 313 and 306 are densest at 0 nm. This suggests that the headgroups are located primarily at the center of the bilayer (Fig. 3F). However, the 304 group is densest at 2 nm and −2 nm relative to the bilayer center, suggesting that the headgroup faces the surrounding aqueous milieu (Fig. 3F).

This contrasting headgroup organization offers insight about the phase separation abilities of different lipid structures. Previously, aromatic compounds have been shown to promote phase separation of lipid rafts by translocating to the bilayer center, competing with cholesterol for its position in the fluid phase and driving cholesterol towards the raft phase58. In contrast, aliphatic compounds move towards the phase boundary and thermodynamically favor lipid mixing that destabilizes phase boundaries58. A similar phenomenon was observed in our system in which the hydrophobic SC1 and SC5 groups of the 313 and 306 lipids

promoted headgroup clustering towards the center of the bilayer, inducing phase separation.

Conversely, the polar 304 headgroup was miscible with the other lipids and prevented phase separation.

To answer this question, we analyzed the rate of phase separation by calculating the average distance of the ionizable lipids from cholesterol. Starting with an initial average distance of approximately 7 nm, the distance decreased as the mobility of cholesterol and the lipids reduced after phase separation. We observed that the time for phase separation was the least for 313 (< 1000 ns), followed by 306 (2000–3000

Specifically, lipids with hydrophobic moieties in their amine headgroups move towards the center of the bilayer and induce phase separation. This phase separation then promotes lipid raft formation, TLR4 recruitment, and an innate immune

#### LNPs elicit structure-dependent adaptive immune responses in vivo

Although innate immune signaling is critical for activating initial immune cascades, several documented LNP-induced adverse events are triggered by B and T cells24,25. Therefore, we sought to establish a structure-based understanding of LNP interactions with the adaptive immune system as well. To examine whether LNPs elicit a structure-dependent adaptive response, we injected mice with two doses of 1 mg/kg LNPs, one month apart. Two and seven days after the second dose, we analyzed changes in B and T cell populations in the spleen (Fig. S13, 4A, 5A–C) and quantified immunoglobulins (Fig. 5D–F) and inflammatory cytokines in the bloodstream (Fig. 4B) and splenocytes (Fig. S14).

First, to assess cellular immune responses, we measured splenic T cell numbers (Fig. 4A). All lipid chemistries caused a decrease in T cells between day 2 and day 7 (Fig. 4A), indicating that LNPs do not induce T cell proliferation in the spleen.

In addition to inducing cellular changes, LNPs elicited structure-dependent changes in cytokines that are associated with T cell activity59. 313 and 306 group LNPs significantly elevated levels of the cytokines TNFα, IL-6, IFNγ, and IL-2 on days 2 and 7 in serum (Fig. 4B) and on day 7 in stimulated splenocytes (Fig. S14) compared to the 304 group. Additionally, IL-6, IFNγ and IL-2 levels changed between days 2 and 7 (Fig. 4B). IL-2 is a T cell proliferation marker60, and reduced IL-2 levels between day 2 and day 7 mimic the decreases we had observed in splenic T cells (Fig. 4A), corroborating the conclusion that LNPs, regardless of structure, do not induce T cell proliferation. These data support the presence of a low-level TH1 biased T cell response marked by secretion of pro-inflammatory cytokines.

Normally, an innate immune response lasts a few hours in mice61,62. However, here we

observed elevated cytokine levels even seven days after LNP dosing as well as increased IL-6 levels after the second dose (Fig. 1D). Therefore, we suspected that another receptor in addition to TLR4 was involved in recognizing lipids and activating adaptive immune cells. We hypothesized that the LNP immune response was also being determined by CD1 receptor binding (Fig. 4C). The CD1 receptor is part of the Major Histocompatibility

Complex (MHC) class I family and presents glycolipids to Natural Killer T cells, leading to Natural Killer T cell activation and pro-inflammatory cytokine secretion63. Humans express four isoforms of CD1 (a-d), while mice express only the CD1d isoform. Therefore, to assess ionizable lipid-CD1d interactions, we evaluated whether these lipids could bind CD1d strongly enough to inhibit its binding to a potent synthetic glycolipid, KRN7000.

For this experiment, we isolated mouse splenocytes, incubated them with pure ionizable lipids for 12 hrs, and then added KRN7000 to the splenocytes. We expected that ionizable lipid structures with CD1d affinity would inhibit KRN7000 binding. To assess KRN7000 binding, we labelled splenocytes with a fluorescent CD1d antibody and evaluated samples

Further, 313O13 and 306O13 elicited dose-dependent inhibition of CD1d binding (Fig. 4E). Because only two of the six lipids inhibited KRN7000 binding, these data suggest that ionizable lipids bind CD1d in a structure-dependent manner.

Next, to assess LNP-specific humoral responses, we evaluated specialized B cell subsets that are involved in the germinal center reaction and produce antigen-specific antibodies64. Specifically, we analyzed germinal center B cells, memory B cells, and antibody-secreting plasma cells. Germinal center and memory B cell compartments did not show any changes (Fig. 5A, 5B), suggesting that LNPs so not cause proliferation of germinal center cells. There was a minor structure-based response in plasma cell numbers, with the 313 group elevating numbers on day 7 compared to the 304 and 306 groups (Fig. 5C).

To assess humoral responses at the protein level, we also measured serum antibody levels 2 and 7 days following the second LNP dose. Because naïve B cells class switch from IgM to IgG under the presence of pro-inflammatory TH1 cytokines, such as IFNγ and IL-1265, we measured levels of IgM and four IgG subtypes – IgG1, IgG2b, IgG2c, and IgG3. IgG1 and IgG3 are versatile subtypes that bind to all Fc receptors in the body, propelling responses against several foreign substances, including viruses, proteins, allergens, and polysaccharides66. IgG2 responses, however, are limited to bacterial polysaccharides, and are promoted by TH1 cytokines, such as IFNγ66,67,68. Therefore, high levels of IgG2 and IgG2/IgG1 ratio indicate a TH1-biased immune response69.

Based on this, we hypothesized that pro-inflammatory IFNγ-producing LNPs would polarize the humoral response towards TH1 IgG subtypes. Consistent with our hypothesis, 313 and 306 LNPs, which had produced more IFNγ compared to 304 LNPs (Fig. 4B), also elicited higher levels of IgG2c and IgG2c/IgG1 (Fig. 5D, 5E). Further, we observed

that only 304 LNPs increased IgM levels between days 2 and 7 (Fig. 5F). Together, these

data demonstrate that LNPs elicit a headgroup-dependent (313>306>304) pro-inflammatory TH1-biased adaptive immune response.

#### LNPs with stronger TLR4 and CD1d binding retain efficacy upon repeat dosing

We were intrigued by higher IgM levels for the 304 headgroup LNPs because the IgM

### Author Manuscript

response to the PEGylated lipids of some LNPs has previously been shown to alter pharmacokinetics and cause loss of efficacy upon repeat dosing24,70–73. For such LNP formulations, anti-PEG IgM antibodies produced by B cells instigate efficacy loss by accelerating LNP clearance, thus preventing LNP uptake by intended organs74.

### Author Manuscript

To interrogate potential IgM-driven efficacy loss for our formulations, we IV-injected mice with LNPs containing Factor VII siRNA at a dose of 0.5 mg/kg. We then measured serum levels of Factor VII, a blood clotting factor produced by liver75, two days post-injection to assess gene silencing. Interestingly, all LNPs from the 306 and 313 groups and none of those from the 304 group maintained efficacy upon repeat dosing (Fig. 6A). This

### Author Manuscriptcompelling structure-influenced result was independent of the baseline efficacy of the LNP.

For example, 306Oi10 and 304Oi10 potently silenced 95–100% of Factor VII activity, yet only 304Oi10 lost efficacy. Similarly, 313O10 and 304O10 initially displayed moderate knockdown, but only 304O10 failed upon repeat dosing.

To confirm that this phenomenon extended to other clinically relevant RNA formats, we injected 5 doses of the two most initially efficacious LNPs: 306Oi10 and 304Oi10 encapsulating reporter firefly luciferase mRNA. Consistent with our observations with siRNA, 306Oi10 was similarly efficacious for all 5 doses (Fig 6B, 6C). However, 304Oi10 potency dropped over 100-fold by dose 5 (Fig. 6B, 6C).

Next, we measured changes in anti-PEG antibody levels for 306Oi10 and 304Oi10. Neither of these LNPs altered anti-PEG IgG antibody concentrations over the course of three weeks following a single dose (Fig. 6D). Strikingly, however, 304Oi10 provoked anti-PEG IgM antibody production that peaked 7 days after the injection and declined back to baseline over the next two weeks (Fig. 6E). As expected, anti-PEG IgM antibodies were produced only if PEG was included in the LNP formulation. Next, we measured anti-PEG IgG and anti-PEG IgM levels for all fifteen LNPs after two doses. Compared to 306 and 313 LNPs, 304 LNPs had significantly lower anti-PEG IgG and higher anti-PEG IgM levels on day 7 (Fig. 6F, 6G). Further, the only antibodies that increased between days 2 and 7 were anti-PEG IgM in response to the 304 headgroup (Fig. 6F, 6G).

These results offer insight about efficacy loss for 304 LNPs. Previously, anti-PEG IgM antibodies have been shown to lyse LNPs by triggering serum complement factors76. Complement binding lyses LNPs and expose the encapsulated cargo which compromises delivery efficacy76. Further, this study showed that among anti-PEG IgM and IgG, only anti-PEG IgM levels correlated with efficacy loss76. This is likely because of IgM’s higher complement triggering affinity compared to IgG. A single IgM molecule is sufficient to activate the complement system77, while IgG requires oligomerization of six molecules78. We suspect that the PEG-IgM response of 304 LNPs contributes to their poor efficacy

after the second dose. In contrast, 313 and 306 LNPs don’t cause substantial production of

### Author Manuscript

PEG-IgM (Fig. 6D), which likely contributes to their sustained delivery efficacy.

Since pro-inflammatory cytokines can inhibit IgM production79,80, we next examined whether inflammatory signals from lipid-TLR4 and -CD1d binding regulate anti-PEG antibody levels. First, we isolated splenocytes from healthy C57BL/6 mice and incubated

### Author Manuscriptthem with LNPs along with TAK-242 (TLR4 inhibitor) or a CD1d neutralizing monoclonal

antibody (clone: 20H2). Because PEG triggers initial IgM release by directly crosslinking B-cell receptors in the spleen81, IgM can be measured ex vivo even in the absence of intact splenic architecture82. We observed that blocking inflammatory signaling with either TAK-242 or 20H2 increased anti-PEG IgM production increased for splenocytes treated Author Manuscriptwith 304 LNPs in the presence of 20H2 (Fig. 6H). Next, to confirm that a similar phenomenon occurs in vivo, we injected mice with 304Oi10 and 306Oi10 LNPs along with CD1d and TLR4 inhibitors and corresponding isotype and solvent controls. Indeed, CD1d and TLR4 blockade caused a significant increase in PEG-IgM (Fig. 6I, S15A) and PEG-IgG antibodies (Fig. 6J, S15B). The effect was more pronounced after the second dose. These Author Manuscript results indicate that the inflammatory cytokines produced by the binding of LNP to receptors inhibit anti-PEG IgM.

Given that LNP immunogenicity influences loss in delivery efficacy, we asked if in silico calculated lipid-receptor binding energies could identify lipids with low anti-PEG IgM levels that can repetitively deliver RNA. To this extent, we used molecular docking to assess if TLR4 and CD1d binding correlated with anti-PEG antibody response. These simulations demonstrated that lipids from the 304 family that lost efficacy (pink circles) had lower binding energy levels for both TLR4 and CD1d, whereas 313 and 306 lipids elicited stronger binding (Fig. 6K, 6L). Therefore, we concluded that calculated binding energies can quickly and inexpensively identify lipids that bind strongly to TLR4 and CD1d and are not susceptible to efficacy loss.

To determine whether our results extend to clinically-approved lipids, we compared FVII efficacy, cytokine levels, B cell levels, and anti-PEG responses for SM-102 with 306Oi10 and 304Oi10. We observed that SM-102 retained efficacy after a second dose (Fig. S16A). Additionally, SM-102 resulted in a similar acute cytokine profile (Fig. S16B, S16C) compared to 306Oi10. Seven days after the second dose, the lipids resulted in similar B cell levels (Fig. S16D–S16F) and elevated TNFα and IL-6 levels (Fig. S16G, S16H). Further, both 306Oi10 and SM-102 elicited lower anti-PEG IgM levels than 304Oi10 (Fig. S16I) and comparable IgG levels (Fig. S16J).

Together, our data show that immunogenic LNPs stimulate the production of inflammatory cytokines and immunoglobulin subtypes by binding to TLR4 and CD1d and promoting lipid raft formation in immune cells (Fig. 6M). TLR4 and CD1d are expressed on the cell and endosomal membranes of immune cells, and it is likely that immune stimulation occurs at both sites. Lipid-receptor binding prompts the release of pro-inflammatory cytokines from innate and adaptive immune cells that reduce the concentration of anti-PEG IgM antibodies available to bind LNPs, thus preserving LNP efficacy upon repeat dosing.

### Author Manuscript Conclusion

To our knowledge, this study is the first to link the structure of lipid nanoparticles to their immune response. We demonstrate that the immune response is predominantly determined by the amine headgroup of the ionizable lipid. LNPs with immunogenic ionizable lipids stimulated both the innate and adaptive arms of the immune system, as was evidenced Author Manuscriptby changes in B and T cell populations in the spleen and structure-dependent levels of cytokines and immunoglobulins. We determined that the mechanism involves immunogenic LNPs promoting lipid raft formation and eliciting stronger binding to TLR4 and CD1d. Interestingly, immunogenic lipids retained siRNA and mRNA delivery efficacy upon repeat dosing because they suppressed anti-PEG IgM levels by prompting cytokine release. Finally,

### Author Manuscriptwe showed that computationally calculated lipid-receptor binding energies predict both

innate and adaptive immune responses. Together, these fundamental mechanisms, structure- immune response relationships, and rapid computational tools will accelerate the design of next-generation LNPs with enhanced safety profile and facilitate the widespread adoption of these life-saving therapeutics in the clinic.

#### Ionizable lipid synthesis and purification.

Ionizable lipids were synthesized as described previously83. Briefly, alkyl-amines and alkyl-acrylates were added to a glass scintillation vial at a stoichiometric ratio of 1:4 and stirred at 90 °C for three days in the absence of solvent. Crude lipids were dissolved in

### Author Manuscript

#### Nanoparticle formulation and characterization.

### Author Manuscript

Nanoparticles were formulated as described previously84. Briefly, the ionizable lipid, DPPC, cholesterol, and C14-PEG2000 were dissolved in ethanol (90% v/v) and 10 mM sodium citrate (10% v/v) at a molar ratio of 50:10:38.5:1.5. The siRNA was dissolved in 10 mM sodium citrate. The solutions were prepared such that the final ionizable lipid:siRNA ratio (w/w) was 5:1. Nanoparticles were formed by rapidly pipetting an equal volume of the lipid solution into the RNA solution and vortexing for 30 sec. All nanoparticles administered in vivo were dialyzed in PBS for 90 min using 3500 MWCO dialysis cassettes (Thermo Fisher, Waltham, MA). Nanoparticle size, dispersity, and zeta potential were measured using a Malvern Zetasizer Nano ZSP (Malvern, UK). siRNA entrapment was determined using the Quant-iT RiboGreen assay (Thermo Fisher) and LNP endotoxin levels were assessed using ToxinSensor™ Chromogenic LAL Endotoxin Assay.

### Author Manuscript

#### Molecular Dynamics.

All simulations were conducted using Gromacs 2020.291 and the MARTINI 2.1P force field. Ionizable lipids were parametrized into MARTINI beads using a 3:1 or 4:1 mapping scheme. Ionizable lipid, cholesterol, DIPC, and DPPC were placed in a 20 × 20 × 10 nm3 box in the molar ratio 0.64:2:3:5 using the insane.py script92. The rest of the box was filled with 150 mM NaCl and water molecules. Lipids, along with the water and ions, were coupled separately at 310 K using the v-rescale temperature coupling with a time constant of 1 ps. Pressure was coupled semi-isotropically using the Parinello-rahman barostat with 12 ps time constant for the production run and the Berendsen barostat with a 5 ps time constant for equilibration. The compressibility was set to 3 × 10−4 bar-1. For nonbonded interactions, the Lennard–Jones potential was shifted to zero between 0.9 and 1.2 nm, and the Coulomb potential was shifted to zero between 0 and 1.2 nm. The relative dielectric constant was 15.

The time step was 30 fs with the neighbor list updates every 10 steps. Simulation results

### Author Manuscript

were analyzed using the gmx density and gmx distance commands in GROMACS and visualized in VMD (v1.9.4, Champaign, IL)93.
