## Abstract

Optimizing vaccine design to induce CD8 T cell responses has been challenging, but lipid nanoparticle (LNP)–encapsulated mRNA vaccines effectively generate CD8 T cell memory. Interleukin-12 (IL-12) supports CD8 T cell expansion and acquisition of effector function, but the role of IL-12 in the generation of CD8 T responses to mRNA vaccination is unclear. Here, we determine that endogenous IL-12 is not required for CD8 T cell responses to mRNA-LNP vaccination. We assessed the adjuvant activity of an mRNA-LNP encapsulating a codon-optimized mRNA that encodes both subunits of IL-12 (LNP–IL-12). Coadministration of LNP–IL-12 with ovalbumin (OVA) mRNA-LNPs enhanced CD8 T cell expansion and effector function and expanded circulating, effector, and tissue-resident memory CD8 T cells. LNP–IL-12 increased CD8 T cell responses against SARS-CoV-2 and influenza virus antigens and improved protection against *Listeria monocytogenes*–OVA and B16F0-OVA melanoma. Thus, modification of mRNA-LNP formulations to include a cytokine mRNA provides a strategy to enhance CD8 T cell–mediated protection.

## Editor’s summary

Lipid nanoparticle (LNP) mRNA vaccines induce robust immune responses and provide protection against infectious diseases. However, this immunity can be short-lived, resulting in the need for frequent booster vaccinations. Aunins *et al*. investigate how adjuvanting LNP-mRNA vaccines with mRNA encoding the cytokine interleukin-12 (IL-12) influences CD8 T cell responses in mice. *Il12* mRNA-LNPs (LNP–IL-12) enhanced antigen-specific CD8 T cell responses and augmented CD8 T cell memory. Furthermore, LNP–IL-12 provided greater protection after challenge with *Listeria monocytogenes* and in a B16F0 melanoma model. This work demonstrates that cytokine-adjuvanted mRNA-LNPs represent a strategy to tailor the magnitude of the immune response to vaccination. —Hannah Isles

## INTRODUCTION

Lipid nanoparticles (LNPs) that encapsulate mRNA encoding target antigens (mRNA-LNPs) provide a modular platform, which formed the basis for the development of several vaccines against severe acute respiratory syndrome coronavirus 2 (SARS-CoV-2). Although mRNA-LNPs generate robust humoral immunity and antiviral CD8 T cell responses ([1](#R1)–[5](#R5)), vaccine-induced humoral immunity against SARS-CoV-2 wanes, and selective pressure by antibody leads to outgrowth of viral variants associated with widespread breakthrough infection in the vaccinated population ([5](#R5)–[7](#R7)). The observation that vaccinated individuals remain protected from severe disease despite reduced antibody titers indicates that other layers of immunity, such as CD8 T cells, contribute to viral control ([8](#R8)–[10](#R10)). Although the ability to generate CD8 T cell responses to vaccination has previously been a challenge ([11](#R11)–[13](#R13)), mRNA vaccine–induced CD8 T cells are associated with protection against severe COVID-19 infection, and these memory CD8 T cells are poised to provide long-lasting and cross-variant immunity ([2](#R2), [4](#R4), [14](#R14)–[16](#R16)). Nevertheless, although current mRNA-LNP vaccines result in expansion of spike-specific CD4 and CD8 T cells that persist at detectable levels for at least 6 months, they also eventually wane ([17](#R17)). In mice, the ability of mRNA-LNPs to induce the production of type I interferons (IFNs) is important for the expansion of SARS-CoV-2–specific CD8 T cells ([14](#R14)). However, little else is known about how the adjuvanticity of mRNA-LNP vaccines contributes to CD8 T cell responses or how this approach can be engineered to generate a larger and more protective memory CD8 T cell pool.

The acute response to infection typically generates diverse CD8 T cell subsets that include highly differentiated effectors and memory precursor populations that are associated with development of long-lived memory ([18](#R18)–[21](#R21)). After resolution of infection, a memory T cell population remains ([18](#R18)–[20](#R20)). By contrast, in a murine model of mRNA vaccination, the expansion of vaccine-specific CD8 T cells is dominated by cells that exhibit a memory precursor phenotype. These cells predominantly develop into central and effector memory populations but do not establish tissue-resident memory ([2](#R2), [8](#R8)). In several models of vaccination or infection, the size of the initial CD8 T cell response positively correlates with the magnitude of the memory pool after contraction ([22](#R22), [23](#R23)). Furthermore, the ability to protect against challenge is a function of the size of the memory CD8 T cell pool and the phenotype and location of these CD8 T cells ([24](#R24)–[30](#R30)). It seems likely that the ability to modify mRNA vaccination approaches could prove useful to generate a larger, more heterogeneous effector response that in turn may give rise to a more efficacious, polyfunctional memory CD8 T cell pool.

Interleukin-12 (IL-12) is a heterodimeric cytokine composed of IL-12p40 and IL-12p35 subunits, which is produced by monocytes, macrophages, and dendritic cells (DCs) in response to pattern recognition receptor signaling ([31](#R31)). IL-12 signals through the IL-12 receptor, which is expressed by natural killer (NK) cells and T cells. During certain infections, the production of IL-12 induces these cells to proliferate and produce IFN-γ ([32](#R32)–[35](#R35)). Given these properties, IL-12 has been used to promote cell-mediated immunity against a variety of parasitic, bacterial, and viral infections as well as cancer ([36](#R36)–[40](#R40)). More recently, repeated intratumoral injection of *Il12* mRNA-LNPs combined with stimulator of interferon genes (STING) agonists or programmed death ligand 1 (PD-L1) blockade have been shown to suppress tumor growth ([41](#R41)–[45](#R45)). However, others have shown that IL-12 production has no effect on CD8 T cell expansion after subunit vaccination ([46](#R46)), and there are reports that the ability of IL-12 to drive CD8 T cells toward terminal effector differentiation is detrimental to the development of memory CD8 T cells ([19](#R19), [47](#R47), [48](#R48)). Whether IL-12 is produced in response to mRNA-LNP vaccination or influences the subsequent immune response is unclear. Here, we investigate the role of endogenous IL-12 in the generation of vaccine-specific CD8 T cells after mRNA vaccination and determine whether the inclusion of mRNA that encodes for IL-12 can be used to modulate the magnitude and effector functions of these CD8 T cell responses.

## RESULTS

### Endogenous IL-12 is not required for the CD8 T cell response to mRNA vaccination

Macrophages are a potent source of IL-12, and in response to mRNA vaccination, these cells efficiently take up mRNA-LNPs and become activated in draining lymph nodes ([14](#R14), [49](#R49)). To assess whether mRNA-LNPs induce IL-12, bone marrow–derived macrophages (BMDMs) were incubated with empty LNPs (eLNPs) or mRNA-LNPs that contained either unmodified mRNA (a potent activator of intracellular sensors) or N1-methyl-pseudouridylated (Ψ) mRNA overnight and assessed for production of IL-12p40 and IFN-α. As anticipated, unmodified mRNA-LNPs induced high levels of IFN-α, whereas Ψ-mRNA-LNPs led to modest induction of IFN-α. Neither of these stimuli resulted in robust IL-12 production however ([Fig. 1A](#F1)). Similarly, when mice were injected intramuscularly with 1 μg of mRNA-LNP, the analysis of these cytokines in the draining popliteal and inguinal lymph nodes (dLNs) highlighted that Ψ-mRNA-LNP induced IFN-α but IL-12p40 was unchanged ([Fig. 1B](#F1)).

To determine whether endogenous IL-12 contributes to the CD8 T cell response to mRNA vaccination, OT-I CD8 (OT-I) T cells that recognize SIINFEKL—the immunodominant CD8 T cell epitope of ovalbumin (OVA) presented on the H2-Kb allele of major histocompatibility complex class I (MHC-I)—were transferred into wild-type (WT) or IL-12p40–deficient (IL-12p40−/−) hosts before immunization with 1 μg of OVA-encoding Ψ-mRNA-LNPs (LNP-OVA). At days 8 and 30 postimmunization, the expansion, phenotype, and function of the OT-I T cells in the dLNs, spleen, and lung were analyzed. In these tissues, there was comparable expansion of OT-I T cells between WT and IL-12p40−/− mice ([Fig. 1, C](#F1) and [D](#F1), and [fig. S1A](#SD1)). Next, a panel of surface receptors (KLRG1, CD127, CXCR3, and CX3CR1) was used to assess the differentiation and activation status of the OT-I T cells at early and late time points. IL-12 promotes the expression of killer cell lectin type receptor G1 (KLRG1), a marker of highly differentiated effector CD8 T cells ([18](#R18), [19](#R19), [35](#R35)). When KLRG1 is used in combination with CX-3CR1, functionally distinct populations of effector CD8 T cells can be identified ([19](#R19), [50](#R50)). After vaccination of WT animals, a KLRG1−CX3CR1−CXCR3+CD127int phenotype was dominant, indicating that these cells were not highly differentiated effectors but rather had a memory precursor phenotype ([Fig. 1E](#F1)). In WT or IL-12p40−/− hosts, there was no difference in the expression of these markers by OT-I T cells at day 8 or 30 postimmunization ([Fig. 1](#F1), [E](#F1) and [F](#F1)). Likewise, at day 8 postimmunization, the absence of IL-12 did not affect the ability of OT-I T cells to produce IFN-γ and tumor necrosis factor–α (TNF-α) and degranulate (as measured by surface expression of CD107a) ([Fig. 1](#F1), [G](#F1) and [H](#F1)). In similar experiments, use of an H2-Kb tetramer loaded with SIINFEKL allowed identification of endogenous OVA-specific CD8 T cells, and the absence of IL-12 did not affect these responses ([fig. S1B](#SD1)). In other experimental systems, the ability of CD4 T cells to license type I conventional DCs (cDC1s) to produce IL-12 contributes to CD8 T cell activation ([51](#R51), [52](#R52)). However, after LNP-OVA vaccination, the absence of CD4 T cells or cDC1 did not affect the ability of CD8 T cells to expand ([fig. S1](#SD1), [C](#SD1) to [E](#SD1)). Thus, multiple pathways associated with the production of IL-12 do not appear to be major contributors to the ability of mRNA-LNP vaccination to induce initial expansion, differentiation, or acquisition of effector function by CD8 T cells.

### *Il12* mRNA-LNPs alter the early immune response to mRNA vaccination

Although endogenous IL-12 is not required for the CD8 T cell response to LNP-OVA, we next assessed whether the inclusion of an LNP containing Ψ-mRNA that encodes for IL-12 (LNP–IL-12) would alter these T cell responses. Here, we synthesized a codon-optimized Ψ-mRNA encoding for the two subunits of IL-12, p40 and p35, encapsulated in LNPs ([Fig. 2A](#F2)). These coding sequences were linked by a flexible glycine-serine (GS) linker, such that the two subunits are cotranslated and efficiently heterodimerize into functional IL-12 ([53](#R53)). To test the efficiency of this formulation, WT and IL-12p40−/− BMDMs were incubated with LNP–IL-12 particles alone or in combination with an irrelevant mRNA-LNP (LNP-irr). Intracellular IL-12p40 staining revealed induction of IL-12 that was mirrored by dose-dependent secretion of IL-12 ([Fig. 2](#F2), [B](#F2) and [C](#F2)). When WT mice were immunized with LNP–IL-12, a dose-dependent translation of IL-12 in the dLNs was observed ([Fig. 2D](#F2)). The dLN and serum levels of IL-12 peaked at approximately 3.5 to 4 ng/ml at 6 hours posttreatment and returned to basal levels by 72 hours ([Fig. 2E](#F2)). To determine which cell types produced IL-12, WT mice were immunized with 5 μg LNP-OVA or LNP-OVA mixed with LNP–IL-12 (LNP-OVA + IL-12). Eighteen hours later, cells from the dLNs were incubated with protein transport inhibitors for 4 hours before staining for surface markers and intracellular cytokine. In naive mice and mice vaccinated with LNP-OVA alone, cDCs and CD64+ cells produced low constitutive amounts of IL-12, whereas B cells and lymphocytes did not ([Fig. 2F](#F2)). In response to LNP-OVA + IL-12, B cells (CD19+) and lymphocytes (CD90.2+) still did not express IL-12, but levels of IL-12 expression \[based on geometric mean fluorescence intensity (gMFI)\] in CD64+ macrophages (Ly6c−) and cDCs were increased ([Fig. 2F](#F2)). Although cDC2s represented the major source of IL-12+ cells, CD64+ cells were also a significant source of IL-12 by proportion ([Fig. 2G](#F2)). The use of an antibody that recognizes SIINFEKL complexed with the H2-Kb allele of MHC class I ([54](#R54)) revealed that at 18 hours postvaccination, a similar frequency of cells in the dLN presented antigen ([Fig. 2H](#F2)). The majority of H2-Kb:SIINFEKL+ cells were CD64+Ly6c− macrophages and cDC2s ([Fig. 2I](#F2)). The analysis of IL-12 production by H2-Kb:SIINFEKL+ cells revealed that in mice treated with LNP-OVA, only 5% of these cells also expressed IL-12p40 ([Fig. 2J](#F2)). However, when LNP–IL-12 was included in vaccination, on average, 60% of the H2-Kb:SIINFEKL+ cells stained positive for IL-12 ([Fig. 2J](#F2)). Thus, a significant proportion of myeloid cells cotranslated OVA and *Il12* mRNA after vaccination with these different particles.

Because vaccination with mRNA-LNP induces the activation and recruitment of immune cells to the dLN ([14](#R14), [55](#R55)), and IL-12 treatment is associated with production of IFN-γ and toxicity ([56](#R56), [57](#R57)), we next assessed the acute impact of LNP–IL-12. Mice immunized with LNP-OVA alone produced minimal levels of IFN-γ ([Fig. 2K](#F2)). However, when these particles were used in combination with LNP–IL-12, by 18 hours postimmunization, about 40% of NK cells, 0.1% of CD8 T cells, and 0.03% of CD4 T cells stained positive for IFN-γ ([Fig. 2K](#F2)). We did not detect any difference in serum levels of aspartate aminotransferase (AST) and alanine aminotransferase (ALT), indicators of liver damage ([fig. S2A](#SD1)). At 72 hours postimmunization, LNP-OVA alone and LNP-OVA + IL-12 were associated with an accumulation of CD64+ macrophages and monocytes in dLNs. At this time point, CD64+Ly6c− cells were still the dominant H2-Kb:SIINFEKL+ cell population ([fig. S2B](#SD1)). Incorporation of LNP–IL-12 did not alter the size of the cDC population, but in the LNP–IL-12 group, there was a marked increase in myeloid cell expression of PD-L1 and CD64 (FCγRI), two canonical targets of IFN-γ signaling ([fig. S2](#SD1), [C](#SD1) and [D](#SD1)). This treatment was also associated with enhanced expression of the costimulatory proteins CD80, CD86, and CD40 by antigen-presenting cell (APC) populations, most notably cDC2s ([fig. S2E](#SD1)). Thus, the delivery of LNP–IL-12 leads to transient production of IL-12 in dLNs and cotranslation of antigen and *Il12* mRNA by CD64+ cells and cDCs. Furthermore, the delivery of LNP–IL-12 is associated with increased production of IFN-γ and activation of local APCs.

### *Il12* mRNA-LNPs enhance effector CD8 T cell expansion and augment CD8 T cell memory

To determine whether incorporation of LNP–IL-12 into mRNA vaccination alters the CD8 T cell response, WT OT-I T cells were transferred into congenically distinct C57BL/6 mice 1 day before vaccination with 1 μg of LNP-OVA or LNP-OVA + IL-12. The response of OT-I T cells was then tracked in the blood. To ensure equal lipid and antigen dose were given in each group, an eLNP was mixed with LNP-OVA at the same ratio (1:1 by mass) as LNP-OVA + IL-12. Administration of LNP-OVA led to expansion of OT-I T cells, but in comparison, mice that received LNP-OVA + IL-12 had a greater than fourfold increase in the frequency of circulating OT-I T cells ([Fig. 3](#F3), [A](#F3) and [B](#F3)). Likewise, the inclusion of LNP–IL-12 led to an increase in the frequency and total number of OT-I T cells in the lungs, dLNs, and spleens at day 8 ([Fig. 3C](#F3)). Similar results were obtained when examining the endogenous response to OVA ([fig. S3](#SD1), [A](#SD1) and [B](#SD1)). To assess the impact of LNP–IL-12 in a secondary vaccination, mice were immunized with LNP-OVA and 21 days later boosted with LNP-OVA or LNP-OVA + IL-12. In response to boost with LNP-OVA, SIINFEKL-specific CD8 T cells expanded in the lung and spleen ([fig. S3C](#SD1)). However, when LNP–IL-12 was included in the boost, this expansion was exaggerated and resulted in an enlarged SIINFEKL-specific CD8 T cell pool ([fig. S3C](#SD1)).

### *Il12* mRNA-LNPs affect CD8 T cell functionality

To determine the functional impact of the inclusion of LNP–IL-12 in mRNA vaccination, we assessed CD8 T cell cytokine production in response to restimulation after immunization against OVA, SARS-CoV-2 spike (S2P), or influenza virus hemagglutinin (H5). To assess OT-I functionality, OT-I T cells were transferred into recipient mice, followed by vaccination with LNP-OVA or LNP-OVA + IL-12. At acute (8 dpi) and memory (>28 dpi) time points, splenocytes from each group were restimulated with SIINFEKL peptide, and cytokine secretion and degranulation were assessed. At 8 dpi, OT-I T cells from each group produced IFN-γ and degranulated in response to restimulation at a similar frequency, but LNP–IL-12 resulted in an increase in the levels of IFN-γ synthesized on a per cell basis ([Fig. 5A](#F5)). Similarly, at 4 weeks postimmunization, LNP–IL-12 resulted in an increased frequency of OT-I T cells able to degranulate and produce IFN-γ in response to SIINFEKL ([Fig. 5A](#F5)). In response to immunization with LNP-S2P or LNP-H5 alone, mice developed a CD8 T cell response, as assessed by IFN-γ and TNF-α production in response to peptide pool restimulation ([Fig. 5](#F5), [B](#F5) and [C](#F5)). The addition of LNP–IL-12 to either vaccine antigen enhanced the frequency of CD8 T cells that produced IFN-γ and coproduced IFN-γ and TNF-α, as well as cells that stained positive for granzyme B and IFN-γ ([Fig 5, B](#F5) and [C](#F5), and [fig. S5](#SD1), [A](#SD1) and [B](#SD1)). Inclusion of LNP–IL-12 in a booster with LNP-S2P also enhanced IFN-γ production by S2P-specific CD8 T cells ([fig. S5C](#SD1)). Thus, the effects of LNP–IL-12 are conserved across multiple vaccine formulations.

Next, a series of in vivo assays were used to determine whether inclusion of LNP–IL-12 enhances vaccine-mediated secondary responses and protection. To assess the cytotoxic capacity of SIINFEKL-specific CD8 T cells generated by vaccination with LNP-OVA or LNP-OVA + IL-12, an in vivo cytotoxic T lymphocyte (CTL) assay in which vaccinated mice were challenged with SIINFEKL-pulsed target cells was performed ([24](#R24)). Those that received LNP-OVA killed about 30% of the target cells, but the inclusion of *Il12* mRNA increased the size of the SIINFEKL-specific CD8 T cell pool and doubled the level of target cell killing to about 66% ([Fig. 5D](#F5)). Additionally, vaccinated mice were challenged >4 weeks after primary vaccination with an OVA-expressing strain of *Listeria monocytogenes* (Lm-OVA) ([60](#R60)–[62](#R62)). Before challenge, mice that received LNP-OVA + IL-12 had a greater frequency of circulating OVA-specific CD8 T cells, and the highest levels of protection were observed in those mice that received LNP-OVA + IL-12 ([Fig. 5](#F5), [E](#F5) and [F](#F5)). The prechallenge SIINFEKL-specific CD8 T cell frequency was correlated with bacterial control ([fig. S5D](#SD1)). To test whether a reduced dose of LNP-OVA + IL-12 would still result in enhanced protection against *L. monocytogenes*, mice were vaccinated with 1 μg of LNP-OVA, 1 μg of LNP-OVA + IL-12, or 0.25 μg of LNP-OVA + IL-12 and challenged 33 days later with Lm-OVA. Despite more modest differences in circulating OVA-specific CD8 T cells at the time of challenge ([Fig. 5G](#F5)), both doses of LNP-OVA + IL-12 were significantly more protective than 1 μg of LNP-OVA alone ([Fig. 5H](#F5)). The enhanced CD8 T cell pool was sustained at later (>2 months postimmunization) time points ([fig. S5E](#SD1)). When challenge was performed 60 dpi, mice that received LNP-OVA only were no longer protected, but mice that received LNP-OVA + IL-12 had a larger SIINFEKL-specific CD8 T cell pool and showed superior protection against Lm-OVA challenge ([fig. S5](#SD1), [F](#SD1) and [G](#SD1)).

Previous studies have shown that mRNA vaccination can be used in a therapeutic setting in a model of B16F0 melanoma in which CD8 T cells mediate protection ([63](#R63), [64](#R64)). Therefore, to assess the utility of LNP–IL-12 in this system, B16F0 melanoma cells that expressed OVA (B16F0-OVA) were implanted subcutaneously into the flanks of mice. On the basis of a previous protocol ([65](#R65)), at day 4 postimplantation, mice were vaccinated intramuscularly in the same side as the tumor with vehicle control \[phosphate-buffered saline (PBS)\], LNP-OVA, LNP-OVA + IL-12, or LNP–IL-12 alone, and a second dose was given 12 days postimplantation. In mice that received vehicle, palpable tumors appeared as early as 8 days postimplantation, and by day 22, all mice in this treatment group had reached end-point tumor volume ([Fig. 5I](#F5)). In mice that received LNP-OVA, there was a delay in the onset of tumor growth until day 11, and most animals reached the end-point tumor volume by day 25 postimplantation ([Fig. 5I](#F5)). By contrast, mice that received LNP–IL-12 alone had a marked delay in tumor outgrowth (day 24). Once established however, these tumors grew to an end-point size of 4000 mm3 within 11 days of onset ([Fig. 5I](#F5)). Only mice that received the combination of LNP-OVA + IL-12 had sustained tumor suppression past 40 days postimplantation ([Fig. 5I](#F5)). Thus, incorporation of LNP–IL-12 in therapeutic vaccination against a tumor antigen markedly delays the rate of tumor growth compared with LNP-OVA or LNP–IL-12 alone.

## DISCUSSION

Here, we demonstrate that application of *Il12* mRNA-LNPs to augment CD8 T cell responses represents a strategy in which cytokine mRNA-LNPs can enhance the magnitude and phenotype of the immune response to vaccination. The use of Ψ modified mRNA is a critical feature that minimizes the toxicity of mRNA-LNP formulations, but there are adjuvant effects associated with the ionizable lipids used in the LNP formulations to allow cytosolic delivery of mRNA. This combination can engage retinoic acid inducible gene (RIG-I) and Toll-like receptor (TLR) signaling to mediate the innate production of type I IFN and IL-6 that contribute to humoral and cellular responses ([1](#R1), [14](#R14), [66](#R66)). The utility of altering the innate response to mRNA-LNPs was highlighted by recent findings that mRNA-LNPs that encode self-reactive cyclic GMP-AMP synthase (cGAS) enhanced vaccine immunogenicity and the resulting cellular immune response ([67](#R67)). However, the data presented here indicate that the formulations commonly used for approved mRNA vaccines do not engage innate sensors that lead to the production of IL-12. This result is in agreement with the lack of reports that these mRNA vaccine formulations induce IL-12 ([68](#R68), [69](#R69)) and is consistent with our observations that IL-12 appears to be dispensable for the ability of mRNA vaccination to generate CD8 T cell responses.

The potent adjuvanticity of cytokines like IL-12 has motivated their use to enhance therapeutic immune responses ([56](#R56), [70](#R70)–[74](#R74)), but their systemic administration in the clinic is associated with adverse effects ([57](#R57)). A number of strategies have been developed to limit cytokine toxicity by delivering lower doses of cytokines to specific tissues ([75](#R75)), and a recent report that engineered *Il12* mRNA transcripts to be restricted to the intramuscular injection site showed that this strategy can amplify the humoral response to the BNT162b2 SARS-CoV-2 mRNA vaccine independent of age ([69](#R69)). In the present study, the formulation of *Il12* mRNA into mRNA-LNPs allowed for transient production of IL-12 by professional APCs in the dLN. Administration of *Il12* mRNA-LNPs at the time of immunization enhanced costimulatory protein expression and activation of APCs at the site and time of naive T cell priming, which resulted in significantly expanded effector and memory CD8 T cell pools. These CD8 T cell populations mimicked responses observed with live infection and were characterized by a more diverse, protective T cell population. One major challenge with the generation of protective CD8 T cell responses to mRNA vaccination is the generation of CD8 TRM cells ([76](#R76), [77](#R77)). The ability of infection to cause local inflammation in affected tissues can instill a program in pathogen-specific CD8 T cells to traffic to the relevant site and seed memory populations that reside in these tissues. Parenteral immunization does not typically induce inflammation at the relevant infection site, which prompted the development of strategies such as “prime and pull” to overcome this limitation ([78](#R78)–[80](#R80)). However, the data presented here indicate that OVA-specific CD8 T cells generated in the presence of LNP–IL-12 contribute to greater seeding of tissue-resident memory in crucial barrier sites such as the lung, skin, and gut. The ability of LNP–IL-12 to induce these broadly distributed CD8 TRM cells is likely relevant to the development of vaccine strategies against a variety of infections.

Although *Il12* mRNA induced effector populations were characterized by high KLRG1 expression, considered a marker of short-lived effector cells that form poor memory ([18](#R18), [19](#R19)), recent studies have highlighted that memory CD8 T cells that express KLRG1 retain plasticity and can contribute to long-term protection ([26](#R26)–[28](#R28), [81](#R81)). The datasets presented here demonstrate sustained enhancement of the memory CD8 T cell pool and durable protection against challenge after a single immunization. The utility of the inclusion of *Il12* mRNA-LNPs was further emphasized by the observation that a reduced dose (0.25 μg) of LNP-OVA + IL-12 was more protective than 1 μg of LNP-OVA in a *L. monocytogenes* challenge. This finding suggests that this strategy can reduce the amount of mRNA-LNP needed per vaccine dose and thus reduce adverse events associated with the LNP vaccination ([68](#R68)). In addition, the data presented here confirm the findings of recent reports that demonstrate the efficacy of *Il12* mRNA constructs for cancer treatment ([41](#R41)–[45](#R45)). However, the ability to use *Il12* mRNA-LNP as an adjuvant given alongside an mRNA-LNP that encodes a surrogate tumor antigen highlights the enhanced efficacy of approaches that stimulate robust adaptive immune responses.

There are some limitations to this study. The use of the *Il12* mRNA-LNP was able to potentiate CD8 T cell responses and was associated with enhanced tumor control and protection against *L. monocytogenes*. Although we focused on the impact on CD8 T cell activity, we cannot rule out a contribution for other IL-12–responsive cells such as CD4 T cells, NK cells, and innate lymphoid cells. In addition, although LNP–IL-12 promoted CD8 TRM cells in the lung, gut, and skin, additional studies will be required to assess how these TRM cells respond to site-specific challenges.

The ability of the mRNA-LNP platform to induce robust cellular and humoral immunity is an important advance in the vaccine field, but there remain challenges that may be overcome by approaches that incorporate other cytokine mRNA-LNPs into these formulations. For example, the use of cytokines that can influence antibody class switching or CD4 T cell effector phenotype (e.g., IL-4, IL-21, etc.) may lead to stronger protection in systems where different forms of immunity are important. In recent years, the field of engineered cytokines has highlighted how their structural modification can be used to bias downstream signaling and biological function (*[82](#R82)–[86](#R86)*). The modularity of the mRNA-LNP platform means that this is a tractable system that would allow expression of these altered ligands and may allow precise tuning of the levels of polarization and survival signals that influence T cell fate. Thus, the efficacy of *Il12* mRNA-LNPs provide an important proof of concept of the opportunity to capitalize on decades of cytokine biology research to design adjuvants from a mechanistically informed perspective for vaccines and therapeutics that provide potent, long-lasting protection.

## MATERIALS AND METHODS

### Study design

This study was conducted to understand the role of IL-12 in contributing to CD8 T cell responses generated by mRNA vaccination. To do so, we primarily compared the CD8 T cell response of mice immunized with LNPs containing mRNA encoding for OVA with that of mice immunized with LNPs containing mRNA encoding for OVA combined with LNPs containing mRNA encoding for IL-12. Analysis of CD8 T cell responses was performed at acute and memory time points, and end-point analyses consisted of flow cytometry, *L. monocytogenes* challenge, and B16F0-OVA tumor growth curves. All experiments used randomly assigned mice without investigator blinding, except for B16F0-OVA tumor challenges, in which investigators were blinded to treatment groups until 4 weeks post-tumor implantation. All data points and *n* values reflect biological replicates. Normality and log-normality tests were performed before statistical testing to determine the appropriate test, which is described in the “[Statistics](#S25)” section in [Materials and Methods](#S8). Outlier testing was performed for all datasets, and outliers were excluded as described in the “[Statistics](#S25)” section in [Materials and Methods](#S8).

### Mice

For all strains of mice used in this study, both male and female mice were used. For each experiment, mice were age-matched. In addition, mice were sex-matched such that all animals in the experiment were male or female or, if a mixture was used, an equal number of males and females were used in each experimental group. For adoptive transfer experiments, male OT-I T cells were transferred into male mice, and female OT-I T cells were transferred into male or female mice. OT-I mice were not used past 12 weeks of age. Wild-type (WT) C57BL/6 mice (stock no. 000664, RRID:IMSR\_JAX:000664) (6 to 7 weeks old) were purchased from the Jackson Laboratory (Bar Harbor, ME, USA) and housed in the University of Pennsylvania’s Department of Pathobiology vivarium for 1 to 4 weeks before use. Male and female *IL12p40*−/− (stock no. 002693, RRID:IMSR\_JAX:002693) mice, *Irf8*+*32*−/− (stock no. 032744, RRID:IMSR\_JAX:032744), and OT-I (stock no. 003831, RRID: IMSR\_JAX:003831) mice were previously purchased from the Jackson Laboratory and bred in the University of Pennsylvania’s Department of Pathobiology vivarium in accordance with institutional guidelines and were used between 7 and 12 weeks of age. All protocols for animal care were approved by the Institutional Animal Care and Use Committee of the University of Pennsylvania (protocol #805432).

### mRNA and mRNA-LNP vaccine production and characterization

The OVA and the murine IL-12 cytokine (IL-12) amino acid sequences were codon-optimized and GC-enriched using our proprietary algorithm to improve expression and reduce immunogenicity. Optimized gene blocks were synthetized by GenScript and cloned into our proprietary in vitro transcription template containing an optimized T7 promoter, 3′/5′ untranslated regions, and a 100-adenine tail. Nucleoside-modified mRNA sequences were prepared using the MegaScript Transcription Kit (Thermo Fisher Scientific), capped using the 3′ OMe CleanCap (TriLink Biotechnologies), purified via cellulose base chromatography ([34](#R34)), quantified using the NanoDrop One system, and verified for length and integrity using the Agilent BioAnalyzer 2100 system. Endotoxin levels were determined to be below the limit of detection (0.1 EU/ml) using the GenScript Toxisensor chromogenic assay. mRNA in nuclease-free water was then frozen at −20°C until formulation. mRNAs were formulated into LNPs as previously described ([87](#R87)) using the ionizable lipid (ALC0307, proprietary to Acuitas Therapeutics) composition described in the patent application no. WO 2017/004143. The physiochemical characteristics of mRNA-LNPs and mRNA encapsulation were measured as described ([65](#R65), [88](#R88)). Endotoxin levels were determined to be below <0.5 EU/ml using the GenScript Toxisensor chromogenic assay. The mRNA sequences used in mRNA-LNP vaccination are provided in [table S1](#SD1).

### Immunization

All mice were immunized intramuscularly in their hind legs with the indicated dose of mRNA-LNP in 50 μl of Dulbecco’s PBS (DPBS) or 50 μl of DPBS alone as a vehicle control. When vaccinating mice with multiple LNPs, particles were mixed such that an equivalent dose of LNP and antigen was given between groups. Where indicated, an LNP-irr was included to ensure that an equal dose of LNP was given across conditions. For example, 0.5 μg of LNP-OVA was mixed with 0.5 μg of eLNP or LNP-irr, and 0.5 μg of LNP-OVA was mixed with 0.5 μg of LNP–IL-12 when comparing the adjuvant effect of LNP–IL-12. Unless otherwise indicated, particles were mixed at a 1:1 ratio by particle mass. When immunizing mice that received OT-I T cell transfer, vaccination was performed the day after transfer. For depletion of CD4 T cells, 400 μg of anti-CD4–depleting antibody (clone GK1.5, Bio X Cell) or isotype control \[rat immunoglobulin G2b (IgG2b) isotype control, anti-keyhole limpet hemocyanin, Bio X Cell\] was administered 1 day before immunization.

### In vivo CTL assay

An equal mix of congenically distinct target cells that were either pulsed with SIINFEKL peptide (1 μg/ml; Anaspec, AS-60193–1) for 1 hour at 37°C or left unpulsed were transferred intravenously via lateral tail vein injection into mice that had been vaccinated with LNP-OVA or LNP-OVA + IL-12 30 days prior. The unpulsed cells were stained with a higher concentration of CellTrace Violet (1:2000 dilution; Invitrogen, [C34557](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/nuccore/C34557)), whereas unpulsed cells were stained at a low concentration (1:40,000 dilution) of CellTrace Violet to allow visual differentiation of the two populations. Sixteen hours posttransfer, mice were euthanized, and target cell killing was assessed as the ratio of pulsed cells to unpulsed cells.

### T cell transfers

For T cell transfers, OT-I transgenic mice were bred in in the University of Pennsylvania’s Department of Pathobiology vivarium in accordance with institutional guidelines. To isolate OT-I T cells, spleens were harvested, and leukocytes were obtained by processing spleens over a 70-μm filter (Thermo Fisher Scientific, 22–363-548) and washing them in RPMI (Corning, 10–040-CM) with 5% fetal bovine serum (FBS) (RP-5). Red blood cells were then lysed by incubating for 3 min at room temperature in 5 ml of lysis buffer (0.864% ammonium chloride diluted in sterile deionized H2O), followed by washing with RP-5. Before transfer, cells were checked for expression of the T cell receptor chains Vβ5 and Vα2. For transfer of OT-I T cells into WT hosts, splenocytes were rinsed with PBS, and total splenocytes containing 1 × 103 OT-I T cells in 100 μl of DPBS were transferred retro-orbitally. For the transfer of OT-I T cells into non-WT mice, 1 × 103 OT-I T cells in 100 μl of DPBS were transferred by retro-orbital injection into recipient mice after enrichment by magnetic activated cell sorting using the CD8α+ T cell isolation kit (Miltenyi Biotec, 130–104-075). A purity of >90% CD8α+ cells was achieved before transfer.

### Tissue preparation for flow cytometry

Leukocytes from the spleens and dLNs were obtained by homogenizing tissues between frosted edges of two microscope slides, passing them over a 70-μm filter, washing them in complete medium, and lysing red blood cells as described above. Cells were pelleted and resuspended in RP-5 as described above. Leukocytes from the lung were obtained by digesting lungs in RP-5 containing type IV collagenase (400 U/ml; Worthington, [LS004189](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/nuccore/LS004189)) and deoxyribonuclease I (DNase I) (0.33 mg/ml; Roche Diagnostics) for 30 to 40 min at 37°C. Lungs were then processed over a 70-μm filter and washed with RP-5. Red blood cells were lysed, and the cells were pelleted then resuspended in RP-5. Leukocytes from the intraepithelial lymphocyte compartment of the ileum were prepared from ileal sections by shaking diced tissue at 37°C for 20 min in Hank’s balanced salt solution (HBSS) with 5 mM EDTA and 1 mM dithiothreitol. Cell pellets were then passed through 70- and 40-μm filters. Leukocytes from the skin were harvested from the ears of mice (both ears were pooled). Leukocytes from the skin were harvested from the ears of mice (both ears were pooled). The ventral and dorsal halves of the ear were separated and digested in RPMI 1640 1X (Corning) containing Liberase TL (0.25 mg/ml; research grade, Roche) and DNase I (0.01 mg/ml) for incubation at 37°C for 70 to 90 min. Cells were then processed over a 40-μm filter and washed through with purification buffer \[1X PBS, 0.5% bovine serum albumin (BSA), and 2 mM EDTA\]. Cells were centrifuged and then resuspended in PBS. Centrifugation was performed at 300 to 450*g* for 5 min.

### Flow cytometry

After processing, cells were stained for cell death using Ghost Dye violet 510 viability dye (Tonbo Biosciences, 13–0870-T100) or Ghost Dye red 780 viability dye (Tonbo Biosciences, 13–0865-T100) for 20 min at 4°C. Cells were washed with fluorescence-activated cell sorting (FACS) buffer \[1X PBS, 0.2% BSA (Gemini, 700–100P), and 1 mM EDTA (Gibco, 15575–038)\] and incubated in Fc block \[99.5% FACS buffer, rat IgG isotype (3 μg/ml; Thermo Fisher Scientific, 10700), 2.4G2 (1 μg/ml; Bio X Cell, BE0307), and mouse IgG isotype (3 μg/ml; Thermo Fisher Scientific, 10400C)\] at 4°C for 10 min before staining. Tetramer-specific CD8 T cells were captured by staining in 50 μl of FACS buffer containing MHC-I tetramer for SIINFEKL (NIH Tetramer Core) for 35 to 45 min at 4°C. Cells were surface-stained in 50 μl at 4°C for 20 to 25 min and washed in FACS buffer before acquisition. All antibodies used are listed in [table S2](#SD1). If no intracellular stain was used, then cells were fixed in a 1% paraformaldehyde (PFA) solution for 20 to 30 min at 4°C and washed in FACS buffer before acquisition. For transcription factor staining, cells were rinsed with FACS buffer and surface-stained as described above, fixed using the eBioscience Foxp3 transcription factor fixation/permeabilization concentrate and diluent (Thermo Fisher Scientific, 00–8222) for 30 to 45 min at 4°C, and then washed with eBioscience permeabilization buffer (Thermo Fisher Scientific, 00–8333-56). Cells were then stained for transcription factors in 50 μl of 1X eBioscience permeabilization buffer at 4°C overnight. Cells were then washed in eBioscience Foxp3 transcription factor permeabilization buffer and resuspended in FACS buffer before acquisition. For intracellular cytokine staining, cells were rinsed with FACS buffer and surface-stained as described above and fixed using the BD Cytofix/Cytoperm (BD Biosciences, 554714) permeabilization/fixation solution for 20 min at 4°C. Cells were then washed in BD Perm/Wash buffer and stained for intracellular cytokines in Perm/Wash buffer for 45 to 60 min at 4°C. Cells were then washed in BD Cytofix/Cytoperm Perm/Wash buffer and resuspended in FACS buffer before acquisition. Data were collected on a BD Biosciences LSR Fortessa or FACS Symphony A3 and analyzed using FlowJo (V9 and V10). Gating strategies for all figures are included in [fig. S6](#SD1).

### Cell preparation for intracellular cytokine stain

For detection of IL-12p40 protein in draining lymph nodes after vaccination with LNP–IL-12, cells were replated in a 96-well plate in medium containing brefeldin A (5.0 mg/ml) (BioLegend, 420601) and monensin protein transport inhibitors (4 μl per 6 × 106 cells) (GolgiStop, BD Biosciences, 554724) and incubated for 4 hours at 37°C and 5% CO2 before staining. For intracellular cytokine staining of SIINFEKL- and H5-restimulated splenocytes, 4 × 106 cells from the spleen were plated in a 96-well plate and incubated with SIINFEKL peptide (1 μg/ml; Anaspec) or H5 peptide pool (10 μg/ml; GenScript) before staining. For SIINFEKL restimulation, anti-CD107a conjugated to PE Cy7 was added to cells at the start of restimulation and incubated for 2 hours at 37°C and 5% CO2 before brefeldin A (5.0 μg/ml; BioLegend, 420601) and monensin protein transport inhibitors (4 μl per 6E6 cells; GolgiStop, BD Biosciences, 554724) were added to the medium. Cells were incubated for an additional 2 hours at 37°C and 5% CO2 before surface and cytokine staining as described above. For H5 restimulation, cells were incubated for 6 hours at 37°C and 5% CO2 before surface and cytokine staining as described above. For intracellular cytokine staining of S2P restimulated splenocytes, 3 × 106 splenocytes were incubated with SARS-CoV2 spike peptide pools, which covered the entire length of the protein (15-nucleotide oligomer, 4–amino acid gap, 11–amino acid overlap; 2.5 μg/ml, >90% purity by liquid chromatography–mass spectrometry) for 6 hours at 37°C and 5% CO2. Anti-CD28 and anti-CD49 were added for costimulation. Brefeldin A (5 μg/ml) and monensin (2 μM) were added after 1 hour of stimulation along with anti-CD107a conjugated to FITC. Dimethyl sulfoxide and a mixture of phorbol 12-myristate 13-acetate (50 μg/ml ) and ionomycin (1 μg/ml) were used as negative and positive controls, respectively. At the end of a 6-hour stimulation period, cells were washed with PBS containing 2% FBS. Cells were stained with near infrared stain to detect dead cells and blocked using anti-mouse CD16/32 antibody in FACS buffer for 15 min at 4°C. After washing with FACS buffer, cells were fixed with BD Cytofix/Cytoperm solution for 20 min at 4°C. Cells were washed again and stained with extracellular and intracellular antibodies ([table S2](#SD1)) for 30 min at 4°C in Perm/Wash buffer. Cells were washed and fixed with 1% paraformaldehyde. More detailed antibody information is provided in [table S2](#SD1).

### Cell culture and BMDM assays

Bone marrow was harvested from the femurs and tibias of WT or IL-12p40−/− mice and washed with complete Dulbecco’s Modified Eagle Medium (DMEM) (DMEM with 10% FBS, Hepes, sodium pyruvate, and penicillin-streptomycin). Red blood cells were lysed as described above, and cells were resuspended in complete DMEM containing 30% L929-supernatant (L-sup). Bone marrow cells were grown at 37°C, 5% CO2, and 7 to 9 days later, BMDMs were used for LNP experiments or frozen down for future use. BMDMs were frozen down overnight to −80°C using a Mr. Frosty freezing container (Thermo Fisher Scientific, 5100–0001) and then transferred to −120°C for long-term (>1 week) storage. BMDMs were frozen down at 1 × 107 cells/ml in 90% FBS and 10% DMSO. Cells were frozen down to −80°C and 2 to 3 days later moved to −120°C. For subsequent analysis, BMDMs were incubated at 37°C until they were just thawed and immediately washed with complete DMEM. Thawed cells were plated and allowed to recover in complete DMEM with 30% L-sup for 3 days at 37°C and 5% CO2 before LNP experiments were performed. For LNP experiments, 100 μl of BMDMs at a concentration of 1.5 × 106 cells/ml was plated in each well of a 96-well plate. Fifty milliliters of medium containing the indicated dose of LNP was added to BMDM cultures and incubated overnight. To detach cells for flow cytometry staining, medium was removed and wells were washed with 1× DPBS followed by addition of 0.25% trypsin-EDTA (Gibco, 25200056) and incubation at 37°C for 5 min. Cells were gently triturated to remove them from wells, and triplicate wells were pooled for flow cytometry staining as described above.

### Enzyme-linked immunosorbent assay

#### Draining lymph node lysates

Samples were kept on ice for the entirety of the following process. Popliteal and inguinal dLNs were harvested and put into 600 μl of EZLys tissue protein extraction reagent (BioVision, 8002–500) containing 1× Halt protease and phosphatase inhibitor cocktail (Thermo Fisher Scientific, 78440). Lymph nodes were mechanically lysed using a mini-beadbeater (BioSpec Products) set to “homogenize” for 90 s. After homogenization, samples were aliquoted and chilled to −80°C until they were assayed.

#### BMDM supernatants

After overnight incubation (16 to 20 hours) with mRNA-LNPs, BMDMs and their supernatants were directly chilled to −80°C until they were assayed. Draining lymph node and BMDM samples did not undergo more than one round of freeze thaw. IFN-α production was assayed using the R&D Biosystems mouse IFN-α all subtype Quantikine ELISA kit (MFNAS0) according to the manufacturer’s instructions. To assay IL-12 production, a 4HBX Immulon 96-well plate (Thermo Fisher Scientific, 3855) was coated with anti–IL-12/23p40 clone 15.6 (0.5 μg/ml; BioLegend, 505201) in 1× DPBS at 4°C overnight. Standards were prepared by diluting recombinant IL-12p70 (PeproTech, 210–12) in assay medium at a max concentration of 20,000 pg/ml with twofold serial dilutions. Samples were added at the appropriate dilution in assay medium and incubated for 2 hours at 37°C. One hundred microliters of biotinylated anti-IL-12/23p40 clone 17.8 (BioLegend, 505301) in 1× DPBS + 0.1% BSA was added at a concentration of 1 μg/ml and incubated for 1 hour at room temperature. Horseradish peroxidase (HRP) conjugated to avidin (HRP-avidin; BioLegend, 405103) diluted 1000-fold in 1× DPBS + 0.1% BSA was added at 100 μl per well. The plate was developed using 100 μl of 2,2′-azinobis \[3-ethylbenzothiazoline-6-sulfonic acid\]–diammonium salt (ABTS) peroxidase substrate (SeraCare, 5120–0041) per well, and development was stopped using an equal volume of 1% SDS solution. The enzyme-linked immunosorbent assay (ELISA) was read at 450 nm. Between each step, the plate was washed at least five times with 1× PBS containing 0.05% Tween.

### Tumor cell lines

The OVA-secreting B16F0-OVA cell line was provided by D.W. Tumor cells were grown in RPMI 1640 containing 10% FBS, 10 mM Hepes, 1 mM sodium pyruvate, 1× GlutaMAX, 1× MEM nonessential amino acids, 55 μM β-mercaptoethanol, 1× penicillin-streptomycin G, and G418 (400 μg/ml; InvivoGen) ([65](#R65)).

### Melanoma tumor model

The right flanks of study mice were shaved before tumor implantation to visualize tumor growth. B16F0-OVA cells (2 × 105) were injected subcutaneously into the flank in 200 μl of sterile HBSS (Gibco). For immunization, 10 μg of indicated mRNA-LNPs was administered intramascularly on days 4 and 12 after tumor inoculation. The growth of tumors was monitored every 2 to 3 days for at least 40 days or until a humane end point was reached. Tumor volumes were monitored by using a vernier caliper and calculated using the equation: V\=(4×3.14×A×B2)/3, where V is volume (mm3), A is the largest diameter (mm), and B is the smallest diameter (mm). Mice were euthanized when the tumor size reached 4000 mm3 or if the tumors ulcerated ([65](#R65)).

### UMAP analysis

The FlowJo (V9) UMAP plug-in version 4.0.3 was used to generate UMAP projections of flow cytometry data. Before UMAP analysis, keywords were used to label features of interest for each sample (e.g., tissue, time point, and treatment) to preserve them during concatenation, and populations of interest were concatenated using FlowJo’s concatenate function. UMAP analysis was then run using all parameters except those used for negative selection.

### Statistics

Statistics were performed using GraphPad Prism version 10.2.2 statistical analysis software. Normality and log-normality tests were carried out before testing. For normally distributed data, comparing two groups along a single variable, a standard Student’s *t* test was used. If the data were found to be abnormally distributed, then a Mann-Whitney rank-sum test was used. In the comparison of more than two groups along a single variable, a one-way analysis of variance (ANOVA) with follow-up Tukey’s test was used. In the comparison of two groups along more than one variable, a two-way ANOVA with a follow-up Šidák’s multiple comparison test was used. *P* values less than 0.05 were considered significant. Outliers were found using the ROUT method at *Q* = 2% and removed. The statistical analysis is indicated in each figure caption.
