## Abstract

Lipid nanoparticles (LNPs) represent the leading delivery platform for mRNA vaccines with advantageous biocompatibility, scalability, adjuvant activity, and often an acceptable safety profile. Here, we investigate the physicochemical characteristics and adjuvanticity of four-component LNPs. Previous vaccine studies have demonstrated that altering the ionizable lipid influences the adjuvanticity of an LNP; however, the impact of the PEG lipid and phospholipid has received less attention. Our mRNA-LNP vaccine formulations utilized different phospholipids and varying ratios of PEG lipid, while the ionizable lipid and cholesterol remained approximately constant. We demonstrate that such modifications impact the magnitude and quality of the vaccine-elicited immune responses. We also dissect the underlying mechanisms and show that the biodistribution and cellular uptake of LNPs correlate with the magnitude and quality of the immune responses. These findings support the rational design of novel LNPs to tailor immune responses (cellular- or humoral-focused) based on the vaccine application.

**Keywords:** lipid nanoparticle, mRNA vaccine, nucleoside-modification, ionizable lipid, phospholipid, PEG lipid, T cell, B cell, antibody

* * *

The nucleoside-modified mRNA-lipid nanoparticle (mRNA-LNP) technology is in the spotlight of vaccine development due to its success in the fight against the coronavirus disease 2019 (COVID-19)[1](#R1). Pfizer/BioNTech’s BNT162b2 and Moderna’s mRNA-1273 are highly effective in preventing COVID-19-associated hospital admissions and deaths by eliciting potent antigen-specific neutralizing antibody and T-cell responses[1](#R1)–[4](#R4). Both vaccines utilize LNP-encapsulated N1-methylpseudouridine-containing mRNA encoding the prefusion-stabilized spike glycoprotein of SARS-CoV-2[5](#R5),[6](#R6). The use of modified nucleosides facilitates decreased innate immune sensing of the mRNA and a high level of antigen production[7](#R7),[8](#R8). The ionizable lipid-containing LNPs function as a safe and efficient delivery modality[9](#R9) and also serve as potent adjuvants[10](#R10),[11](#R11). LNPs typically have 4 components: ionizable lipid, cholesterol, phospholipid, and PEGylated lipid incorporating polyethylene glycol (PEG). The ionizable lipid serves to encapsulate nucleic acids within the LNPs and facilitates endosomal escape of the mRNA after cellular uptake[12](#R12). Furthermore, it promotes robust IL-6 production, and the elicitation of neutralizing antibodies as a response to vaccination with mRNA-LNPs[10](#R10). Cholesterol provides rigidity and stabilizes the LNPs[13](#R13). Phospholipids contribute to longer circulation time, influence overall stability in solution[13](#R13), and can be optimized for better tissue-specific targeting of LNPs[14](#R14)–[16](#R16). PEG lipids can impact size and polydispersity, particle stability, nucleic acid encapsulation efficiency, circulation half-life, in vivo distribution, and transfection efficiency[13](#R13).

By refining the LNP formulation, it may be possible to influence not only the magnitude but also the quality of mRNA-LNP vaccine-elicited immune responses. In previous work, we showed that the ionizable lipid component of LNPs is important for its adjuvant effect[10](#R10). Hassett et al. demonstrated that ionizable lipid optimizations can improve the safety and immunogenicity profile of mRNA-LNP vaccines[17](#R17). In a more recent study, Han et al. developed an adjuvant lipidoid that enhances the adjuvanticity of mRNA-LNP vaccines[18](#R18). Other recent publications have reported how the phospholipid and PEG lipid modifications influence the physicochemical properties and biodistribution of LNPs[16](#R16),[19](#R19)–[24](#R24). However, the impact of phospholipid and PEG lipid on the adjuvanticity of LNPs has not been thoroughly investigated.

Here, we studied four LNPs with different phospholipids and varying amounts of PEG lipids to determine how these variations affect the adjuvant activity of the LNP in the context of vaccination. We generated mRNA vaccines by formulating viral antigen-encoding nucleoside-modified mRNA into the four different LNPs, determined physicochemical properties of the mRNA-LNPs, and evaluated vaccine-induced immune responses in mice. We demonstrated that the type of phospholipids and ratios of PEG lipids influence the adjuvant properties of LNPs and affect not only the magnitude but also the quality of the induced immune responses. More specifically, we show examples of how novel LNPs can be designed to preferentially elicit potent antibody responses or CD8+ T cell responses for mRNA vaccines targeting infectious diseases or cancer. We then provide mechanistic data suggesting that adjuvanticity is likely driven by the biodistribution of mRNA vaccines, LNP-driven efficiency of mRNA uptake and endosomal release, and the inflammatory properties of mRNA-LNPs. This study shows that optimization of LNPs can enhance mRNA vaccine efficacy and tailor the adjuvanticity for infectious diseases and potentially for cancer therapy.

## LNP Design and Characterization

All LNPs examined in this study are composed of an ionizable lipid[25](#R25), cholesterol, a PEG lipid, and a phospholipid, wherein the phospholipid is either distearoyl-sn-glycero-3-phosphocholine (DSPC), 1,2-dioleoyl-sn-glycero-3-phosphoglycerol (DOPG), or 1,2-dioctadecenoyl-sn-glycero-3-phosphoserine (DOPS). In contrast to the FDA-approved COVID-19 mRNA vaccines that contain DSPC, a zwitterionic phospholipid with saturated tails, both DOPG and DOPS are anionic phospholipids with oleyl chains containing a *cis*-double bond ([Fig. 1a](#F1)).

LNP Q, our benchmark composition, has demonstrated a safe and highly immunogenic profile in a Phase 2 COVID-19 mRNA vaccine trial[26](#R26). Setting this as the base composition (ionizable lipid, cholesterol, DSPC, PEG at a molar ratio of 50: 38.5: 10: 1.5, respectively), we generated and characterized a panel of LNPs (F-T) by titrating PEG lipid while keeping the amount of ionizable lipid, cholesterol, and DSPC nearly constant ([Table S1](#SD1)). Using influenza virus hemagglutinin (HA)-encoding nucleoside-modified mRNA, we found that LNPs containing lower PEG concentrations elicited stronger antigen-specific antibody responses ([Fig. S1](#SD1), [Table S2](#SD1)). Based on these results, we selected LNP H, which uses a PEG lipid ratio of 0.5%. We next sought to explore the influence of phospholipid charge on these formulations. Previous LNP delivery studies have demonstrated that when administered intravenously, negatively charged LNPs traffic primarily to the spleen[15](#R15),[16](#R16). We hypothesized that delivery to a secondary lymphoid organ would be advantageous for vaccine applications, therefore we selected two commonly used negatively charged phospholipids, DOPG and DOPS[16](#R16). In addition, our studies used intramuscular administration, which can alter LNP trafficking compared to intravenous delivery[9](#R9). After formulation of LNPs (V-Z) using these negatively charged phospholipids and varying the PEG ratios ([Table S1](#SD1)), we selected LNPs for further study based on lower PEG ratios and acceptable size profiles. When formulating DOPS with 0.5% PEG (LNP V), the Z-average was 146 nm – much higher than the previously reported optimal sizing of LNPs for robust adjuvanticity in mice, which is around 100 nm[27](#R27). Larger-sized LNP formulations (>120nm) may present additional chemistry, manufacturing, and controls (CMC) challenges for any potential clinical product due to difficulties in filtration and the potential for further particle size growth upon longer-term storage or freeze-thaw. Therefore, we returned to the PEG ratio of 1.5% for this phospholipid which had an acceptable Z-average of 75 nm, LNP W (DOPS, 1.5% PEG lipid). In summary, four LNP formulations were selected for adjuvanticity and mechanistic studies. These formulations include LNP Q (1.5% PEG, DSPC) as the control, LNP H (0.5% PEG, DSPC), LNP Y (0.5% PEG, DOPG), and LNP W (1.5% PEG, DOPS).

For most subsequent studies with the selected LNPs, we used the prefusion-stabilized version of the spike glycoprotein (S-2P) of the Wuhan strain of SARS-CoV-2[28](#R28),[29](#R29). Lipid components of the S-2P mRNA-LNPs are depicted in [Fig. 1b](#F1), and [Table S1](#SD1). All LNPs reported narrow size distribution and polydispersity ([Fig. 1c](#F1) and [S3a](#SD1), [b](#SD1)) and high encapsulation efficiency ([Fig. 1d](#F1) and [S3c](#SD1)). Despite differences in phospholipid structures, similar particle sizes were observed with LNPs made with the same lipid molar ratios. Smaller particles were obtained with 1.5% PEG (74–79 nm) compared to those with 0.5% PEG (102–110 nm) ([Fig. 1c](#F1), [S3a](#SD1), and [Table S1](#SD1)). All S-2P mRNA-LNPs were stable for at least 12 weeks at −80 °C ([Fig. S3d](#SD1)). The apparent pKa was determined to be ~6.3 for all LNPs, driven primarily by the use of the same ionizable lipid across all LNPs ([Fig. S3e](#SD1)). By design, LNPs containing anionic phospholipids (LNP Y, LNP W) exhibited a negative surface charge at pH 7.2 ([Fig. 1e](#F1) and [S3f](#SD1)). Notable differences were observed in the particle morphology of LNPs containing different phospholipids by cryo-transmission electron microscopy (cryo-TEM). DSPC-containing formulations appeared to have more “bleb” structures, likely due to this phospholipid’s saturated tails promoting cylindrical geometry and a bilayer phase in particles[30](#R30)–[32](#R32) ([Fig. 1f](#F1), [Table S3](#SD1)). On the other hand, DOPG and DOPS contain *cis* double bonds in their oleyl chains that promote a ‘cone shape’ geometry and the inverse hexagonal phase in particles[33](#R33).

## LNP Formulation Impacts Humoral and Cellular Immune Responses

To investigate the impact of different types of phospholipids and the PEG lipid ratio on the adjuvant properties, mice were injected with S-2P nucleoside-modified mRNA-LNPs intramuscularly (i.m.) ([Fig. 2a](#F2)). All formulations induced high levels of SARS-CoV-2 spike receptor binding domain (RBD)-specific IgG that demonstrated robust neutralizing activity ([Fig. 2b](#F2), [2c](#F2)). However, at 4 weeks post-immunization, LNP H demonstrated more than a two-fold increase in antibody levels and neutralizing activity compared to LNP Y, and LNP W. By 16 weeks post-immunization, LNP H induced more than three-fold higher levels of antigen-specific IgG compared to LNP Q, LNP Y, and LNP W, and four-fold higher neutralization compared with LNP W.

Because our LNP formulations elicited varied antibody responses, we examined not only the magnitude but also the quality of the humoral immune response by assessing the levels of IgA and various IgG subtypes ([Fig. S4a](#SD1)). Our analysis revealed that diverse LNP compositions can impact IgG1/IgG2a ratios ([Fig. S4b](#SD1)).

Most successful vaccine approaches rely on the generation of memory B cells (MBC) and long-lived plasma cells (LLPC)[34](#R34). While MBCs can mount rapid recall responses upon secondary exposure, LLPCs residing in the bone marrow contribute to protection from infection by a persistent production of antigen-specific antibodies. To examine the magnitude and quality of antigen-specific LLPC and MBC responses, at 16 weeks post-vaccination the RBD-specific splenic MBCs and bone marrow LLPCs were identified by flow cytometry. Similar to the humoral immune responses, LNP H induced the largest amount of antigen-specific MBCs and LLPCs out of the four LNPs ([Fig. 2d](#F2), [e](#F2), and [S5](#SD1)), as well as the highest number of IgG1, IgG2a, and IgG2b producing RBD-specific antibody secreting cells (ASCs) quantified by ELISpot ([Fig. 2f](#F2)).

We previously demonstrated that nucleoside-modified mRNA-LNP vaccines induce robust antigen-specific CD4+ and CD8+ T cell responses in mice[35](#R35). To gain further insights into the cellular immune responses, we investigated the T cell responses induced by the different mRNA-LNPs. Mice were injected i.m. and spike-specific CD4+ and CD8+ T cell responses were evaluated after 10 days using intracellular cytokine staining and flow cytometry ([Fig. 3a](#F3) and [S6](#SD1)). All mRNA-LNPs elicited antigen-specific CD8+ and CD4+ T cells expressing type 1 (Th1) immune response cytokines, including interferon (IFN)-γ, and tumor necrosis factor (TNF)-α ([Fig. 3b](#F3)–[d](#F3), and [S7](#SD1)). There was no difference in the magnitude of antigen-specific CD4+ T cell responses ([Fig. S7](#SD1)). Interestingly, LNP W induced higher CD8+ T cell responses compared to the other LNP formulations ([Fig. 3b](#F3)–[d](#F3)).

Besides being important players in anti-viral responses, CD8+ T cells are also critical components of the adaptive immune response against cancer, playing key roles in recognizing and eliminating tumor cells[36](#R36),[37](#R37). Building on this, we evaluated T cell responses in mice following vaccination with an mRNA-LNP cancer vaccine utilizing gp100, an extensively studied melanoma-associated antigen. We selected LNP H and LNP W for comparison in these experiments, due to LNP H eliciting the highest humoral immune response, and LNP W showing superior capacity to elicit a CD8+ T cell response. To model T cell response at the level of endogenous precursor frequencies, we adoptively transferred a physiologically relevant number of naive gp100-specific CD8+ T cells (5×103) from pmel-1 mice into non-lymphodepleted mice. The following day, mice were immunized i.m with a prime and a week later with a boost bolus dose of the gp100 mRNA-LNP vaccine. We assessed the antigen-specific CD8+ T cell responses five days after booster immunization ([Fig. 3e](#F3) and [S8](#SD1)). In line with the data obtained with the S-2P antigen, LNP W induced a significantly higher frequency of antigen-specific CD8+ T cells producing TNF-α, as well as polyfunctional CD8+ T cells that produced both IFN-γ and TNF-α compared to LNP H ([Fig. 3f](#F3)–[h](#F3)). In a subsequent experiment, we compared LNP W and LNP H using an extended priming immunization protocol wherein the total dose (5 μg) was divided into four equal daily doses ([Fig. 3i](#F3)). This strategy of prolonging the exposure of the immune system to antigen has been demonstrated to enhance CD8+ responses[38](#R38). LNP W elicited significantly higher polyfunctional TNF-α and IFN-γ producing antigen-specific CD8+ T cells compared to LNP H ([Fig. 3j](#F3)–[l](#F3)), and had an increased proportion of KLRG1+CD127− effector T cells ([Fig. 3m](#F3)). This confirmatory finding using a second antigen underscores the capacity of LNP W to deliver strong adjuvant activity that fosters CD8+ T cell responses.

In summary, LNP H containing DSPC phospholipid and 0.5% PEG lipid ratio outperformed the other formulations in both humoral immune responses and in number of LLPCs and MBCs, while LNP W containing DOPS phospholipid and 1.5% PEG lipid ratio demonstrated the most robust antigen-specific CD8+ T cell responses.

## Phospholipids and PEG Lipid Ratio Influence LNP Biodistribution

To understand the mechanism driving the different immune responses induced by various LNP compositions, we analyzed the in vivo duration and biodistribution of protein production from different mRNA-LNPs after i.m. administration using an in vivo imaging system (IVIS). While all LNPs produced a robust bioluminescent signal, we observed different distribution profiles ([Fig. 4a](#F4), [S9](#SD1)). The overall bioluminescence signal measured across the full body of each mouse was over three times higher with LNP Q or LNP H containing zwitterionic phospholipids, compared to LNP Y and LNP W containing anionic phospholipids ([Fig. 4b](#F4),[c](#F4)). However, at the injection site muscle, we could measure at least 2.5-fold higher protein production in mice injected with LNP H compared to the other 3 groups ([Fig. 4b](#F4),[c](#F4)). Because IVIS does not reveal detailed organ-specific data, we evaluated the Luc activity within various organs of i.m. injected mice. The liver, spleen, and draining lymph nodes (dLNs) were harvested, and Luc activity, mRNA levels, and lipid content were measured from the various organs at 4 hours and 3 days post-administration. After 4 hours, Luc production normalized to total protein was predominantly found in the dLNs ([Fig. 4d](#F4)). LNP H demonstrated 2-fold higher Luc expression in dLNs compared to all other formulations, but considerable amounts were also detected in the spleen and persisted at least 3 days post-injection ([Fig. 4e](#F4)). Meanwhile, expression in the liver was highest with LNP Q, followed by LNP H at 4 hours post-dose ([Fig. 4d](#F4)), whereas LNP Y and W (containing anionic phospholipids) showed low expression in the liver. To evaluate individual components of the mRNA-LNPs, we independently assessed the mRNA and ionizable lipid present in the liver and spleen following injection of Luc mRNA-LNPs. Corroborating the Luc protein liver expression profile, mRNA and ionizable lipid levels were more than 3-fold higher in the liver following administration of LNP Q and H, compared to LNP Y and W at 4 hours post-injection ([Fig. 4f](#F4),[h](#F4)). In the spleen, LNP H demonstrated at least 3-fold higher Luc mRNA and ionizable lipid levels compared to all other formulations at 4 hours and 3 days post-injection ([Fig. 4f](#F4)–[i](#F4)).

To understand the differences in liver targeting amongst our LNPs, we examined the protein corona to see if apolipoprotein E (ApoE) binding was playing a role[39](#R39)–[41](#R41). Contrary to our expectations, we found that LNPs containing negatively charged phospholipids (LNP Y, LNP W) demonstrated high levels of ApoE binding ([Fig. S12](#SD1)) while LNP H exhibited the lowest ApoE binding. We also examined the presence of other HDL proteins including ApoA-1, ApoA-4, and ApoB in protein corona. Interestingly, LNP Y and W also had higher ApoA-1 and ApoB binding, but not ApoA-4. Overall, there seem to be more HDL bound to LNP Y and W, suggesting its poor liver uptake may be mediated by other mechanisms.

## LNP Composition Alters In Vitro Uptake and In Vivo Inflammation

To understand the mechanisms responsible for the distinct immune responses triggered by our various LNP compositions, we analyzed their in vitro and in vivo cellular uptake. The LNPs containing DSPC, LNP Q, and LNP H, were taken up more readily by DC2.4 cells ([Fig. 5a](#F5),[b](#F5)) and human monocyte-derived dendritic cells (huMDDCs) ([Fig. 5c](#F5),[d](#F5)), while the LNPs containing negatively charged phospholipids, LNP Y and LNP W had low cellular uptake. Notably, LNP H had significantly higher uptake than all other formulations in DC2.4, and in huMDDCs it demonstrated significantly higher uptake compared to LNP Y and LNP W. In vivo, we examined the immune cell infiltration into the dLNs and their uptake of mRNA-LNPs[42](#R42). We evaluated different subtypes of DCs, including dermal DCs, conventional type 1 and 2 DCs (cDC1 and cDC2), as well as inflammatory monocyte, and macrophage numbers. At 24 hours, we observed a higher absolute number of cDC1s in the dLNs in mice injected with LNP H compared to the other LNP formulations ([Figure S13](#SD1) and [S14](#SD1)), and at 48 hours a significant difference was observed between LNP Q and LNP Y in dermal DCs. However, when we analyzed LNP uptake by these immune cells, there was no significant differences between LNP formulations ([Figure S14](#SD1) and [S15](#SD1)).

After LNP uptake, the mRNA must escape into the cytosol for translation into a protein[43](#R43). In our RNA FISH experiments ([Fig. 5e](#F5)–[h](#F5)), confocal imaging of DC 2.4 cells ([Fig. 5e](#F5),[f](#F5)) revealed that mRNA encapsulated by LNP H had a higher percentage of mRNA not colocalized with an endosomal marker, implying greater endosomal escape compared to LNP W.

In addition to cellular mechanisms that may drive adaptive immune responses to mRNA-LNP vaccines, we investigated inflammatory responses in vivo. Our previous work has shown that IL-6 induction is critical for the adjuvant activity of an LNP[10](#R10), therefore, we evaluated the immunostimulatory cytokine profile of these four LNPs in dLNs at 4 and 24 hours post-immunization ([Fig. S16](#SD1)). All LNPs promoted a robust cytokine/chemokine response, and significant differences were detected between the groups for IL-6 and IL-1β at the 4 hour timepoint, as well as for IL-5 at the 24 hours timepoint. At the 4 hours timepoint, LNP H induced the highest IL-6 cytokine level, while LNP W induced the highest IL-1β level. At the site of injection, we evaluated the innate immune cell infiltration to the muscle at 1, 3, and 5 days post-injection ([Fig. S17](#SD1) and [S18](#SD1)). We found that neutrophils peaked on day 1, while macrophages and monocytes were highest on day 5 for all formulations. Higher levels of DCs were observed for LNP H on day 1, but there were no statistically significant differences among the groups.

## Conclusions

The authorization of two nucleoside-modified mRNA-LNP-based vaccines marks a significant validation of this technology in vaccine innovation[1](#R1). Our previous research has demonstrated that LNPs are not only delivery vehicles for mRNA but also possess adjuvant activity[10](#R10) that is crucial for the induction of a strong immune response against the target antigen. However, the relationship between LNP particle composition and the quality and magnitude of elicited immune responses remains poorly understood, primarily because most LNP research has focused on the optimization of the ionizable lipid and delivery efficiency, typically after systemic administration[15](#R15),[16](#R16),[21](#R21),[23](#R23). In contrast, our study addressed the impact of PEG lipid ratio and the type of phospholipid on vaccine efficacy using infectious disease and cancer models after i.m. injection.

We found that LNP H, characterized by its zwitterionic DSPC phospholipid and a lower PEG lipid ratio, induced the strongest humoral immune response, resulting in higher binding antibody production, improved virus neutralization capabilities and more robust memory B cell and LLPC responses. These enhancements may be attributed to increased protein synthesis measured within key immune sites such as the spleen, dLNs, and the vaccination site, accompanied by heightened levels of IL-6 measured in the dLNs. The size of LNP H was the largest of our four LNPs, aligning with the findings of Hasset et al. which highlighted a correlation between the LNP particle size and the antibody response in mouse models[27](#R27). In vitro, we also observed more efficient uptake of LNP H by DC2.4 cells and HuMDDCs, and improved ability to release endocytosed mRNA into the cytoplasm from endosomes in DC2.4 cells. This may stem from the lower PEG lipid content[44](#R44),[45](#R45) of LNP H or the presence of structural features known as blebs[31](#R31), which were measured at much higher frequencies in our LNPs containing neutrally-charged phospholipid.

On the other hand, LNP W that contained the negatively-charged DOPS phospholipid led to increased frequencies of IFN-γ and TNF-α producing spike-specific CD8+ T cells using S-2P as the model antigen. Given the critical role of CD8+ T cells in the elimination of cancer cells[37](#R37),[46](#R46), we investigated their response to the tumor antigen gp-100 and found again that LNP W yielded the highest number of IFN-γ and TNF-α producing CD8+ T cells. Additionally, it significantly increased the frequency of antigen-specific effector T cells characterized by the KLRG1+CD127− T cell phenotype. Our findings agree with work from Zhu et al. and Bevers et al, which present that modifying LNP formulations, such as changing phospholipids or PEG lipids, can impact the efficacy of cancer vaccines[47](#R47),[48](#R48). However, these papers focused on the optimal composition and evaluating functional outcomes in tumor models, while our comparative study investigated the underlying mechanisms of how the specific composition and physicochemical properties of LNPs link to immune responses.

DOPS, a form of PS, facilitates the targeted delivery of LNPs to immune cells by mimicking apoptotic cell markers, thereby improving cellular uptake by antigen-presenting phagocytes[49](#R49),[50](#R50). This process may contribute to the enhanced CD8+ T cell responses we observed with LNP W containing DOPS. Moreover, the role of IL-1β in CD8+ T cell induction[51](#R51)–[53](#R53) adds another layer to our understanding of mRNA-LNP vaccine efficacy. LNP W significantly increased the production of IL-1β in the dLNs compared to other formulations 4 hours post-injection. The involvement of IL-1β, as demonstrated by Tahtinen et al.[11](#R11), in initiating the release of other pro-inflammatory cytokines further supports its critical role in mRNA-LNP vaccine immunogenicity.

Overall, this work demonstrates that modifying the phospholipid identity and the PEG lipid ratio in an LNP formulation can tune the immune response to elicit either stronger antibody-mediated immunity, or stronger cellular immunity, which has implications in both infectious disease and cancer vaccine applications. Of note, our study utilized a single ionizable lipid for detailed characterization. Another limitation of this study is the absence of LNP groups containing 0.5% PEG with DOPS and 1.5% PEG with DOPG due to reasons mentioned above. Future research will assess our findings across a broader range of formulations. Testing these LNP formulations in non-human primates will provide further insights.

This study was performed in collaboration with researchers from multiple institutions, ensuring equitable contribution and authorship. In this study, peripheral blood mononuclear cells (PBMCs) were isolated from the blood of healthy donors and used to generate human monocyte-derived dendritic cells (huMDDCs). All animals were housed and cared for according to local, state, and federal policies in an Association for Assessment and Accreditation of Laboratory Animal Care International (AAALAC)-accredited facility.

### Mice

BALB/c mice aged 8 weeks were purchased from Charles River Laboratories. Female C57BL/6J (Jackson Laboratory strain #000664) and Pmel-1 TCR/Thy1.1 transgenic mice (Jackson Laboratory strain #005023) on a C57BL/6 background (aged 6–8 weeks) were purchased from the Jackson Laboratory. Pmel-1 mice were bred and maintained at the University of California, Los Angeles (UCLA) breeding vivarium and utilized for experiments when reaching 8 to 12 weeks of age.

Mice were housed under a 12-hour light/12-hour dark cycle at 22 °C ± 2 °C with a humidity of 50% ± 20%. Food and water were available ad libitum.

### Cell

Murine DC2.4 cell line was obtained from Millipore Sigma (#SCC142M) and maintained in Dulbecco’s modified Eagle’s medium (DMEM, Corning, #10–013-CV) supplemented with 10% fetal bovine serum (Cytivia Hyclone, #SH30071.03), 100 U ml−1 Penicillin/Streptomycin (Gibco. #15140–122).

Human monocyte-derived dendritic cells (HuMDDCs) were generated from a healthy donor apheresis product, provided by the University of Pennsylvania Human Immunology Core. Dendritic cells were derived from monocytes using complete RPMI media (Gibco, #22400–089) supplemented with human granulocyte colony stimulating factor (R&D Systems, #215GM010CF) and human IL-4 (R&D Systems, #204IL010CF). Expi293F cells were obtained from Thermo Fisher Scientific (#A14527) and maintained in Expi293™ Expression Medium (Thermo Fisher Scientific, #A1435101). Sf9 cells were obtained from Thermo Fisher Scientific (#12659017) and maintained in Sf-900™ III SFM, a serum-free medium (Thermo Fisher Scientific, #12658019), supplemented with 1× Penicillin-Streptomycin (Capricorn, 100× stock solution). High Five cells were obtained from Thermo Fisher Scientific (#[B85502](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/nuccore/B85502)) and maintained in HyClone™ SFX-Insect liquid medium (Cytiva, #SH30278.LS). 293T cells were obtained from the American Type Culture Collection (ATCC, #CRL-3216) and maintained in DMEM (Life Technologies, #11995065) supplemented with 10% fetal bovine serum (Hyclone, #SH30071.03). VeroE6-TMPRSS2 cells were generated in-house, selected once with blasticidin (#[R21001](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/nuccore/R21001)), and maintained in DMEM (Life Technologies, #11995065).

### mRNA-LNP vaccine production

mRNA vaccines were designed based on the SARS-CoV-2 prefusion stabilized spike (S-2P) sequence (Wuhan-Hu-1, GenBank: [MN908947.3](https://ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/nucleotide/MN908947.3)) the hemagglutinin (HA) sequence from A/Puerto Rico/8/1934 (H1N1) or the melanoma-associated antigen, gp100.

The HA mRNA was produced at Genevant. Briefly, the codon-optimized HA sequence was synthesized (GenScript) and cloned into the mRNA production plasmid. HA mRNA was produced using T7 RNA polymerase (MEGAscript, Ambion) on linearized plasmids. The HA mRNA was then enzymatically polyadenylated (NEB). Pseudouridine-5′-triphosphate (TriLink) was used instead of UTP (uridine 5’-triphosphate) to generate HA with modified nucleoside-containing mRNA. Capping of the in vitro-transcribed mRNA was performed co-transcriptionally using the trinucleotide cap1 analog, CleanCap (TriLink). mRNA was purified by cellulose purification[54](#R56).

To produce the S-2P, Luc, eGFP, and gp100 mRNAs, the sequences were codon-optimized, synthesized, and cloned into an mRNA production plasmid[55](#R57),[56](#R58). N1-methylpseudouridine-5’-triphosphate (m1Ψ-5′-triphosphate) (TriLink #N-1081) instead of UTP was used to generate modified nucleoside-containing mRNAs, and the mRNAs were transcribed to have 101 nucleotide-long poly(A) tails. Co-transcriptional capping of the in vitro transcribed mRNAs was performed using the trinucleotide cap1 analog, CleanCap (TriLink #N-7413). Cellulose (Sigma-Aldrich #11363–250G) was used for mRNA purification[54](#R56),[55](#R57). All mRNAs were evaluated by agarose gel electrophoresis and were stored frozen at −20 °C.

Cellulose-purified S-2P, HA, Luc, eGFP, and gp100 mRNAs were encapsulated by a controlled mixing, self-assembly process. An ethanolic lipid mixture of ionizable lipid (6Z,16Z)-12-((Z)-dec-4-en-1-yl) docosa-6,16-dien-11-yl 5-(dimethylamino) pentanoate (3D-P-DMA), phospholipid, cholesterol, and polyethylene glycol-lipid[57](#R59) was mixed with an aqueous solution containing mRNA at acidic pH 5. Post-formation, the ethanol was removed from the LNP via dialysis with 10,000 MWCO ThermoFisher Slide-a-Lyzer cassettes (A52973), concentrated with a Vivaspin® (10,000 MWCO, Cytiva, #28932363), and further dialyzed into Tris-sucrose buffer, pH 8. Samples were sterile filtered, characterized, adjusted to approximately 0.5 mg/ml total mRNA, and frozen at −80 °C. The ionizable and PEG-conjugated lipids were synthesized at Genevant. Cholesterol was obtained from Sigma-Aldrich (Synthechol, #W004591), and the phospholipids from Avanti Polar Lipids via Millipore-Sigma (DSPC #730365, DOPS #840035P, DOPG #840475). LNP for in vitro and in vivo uptake assays were prepared as above with the addition of 1% 1,1′-dioctadecyl-3,3,3′,3′-tetramethylindocarbocyanine (DiI; Millipore Sigma #42364) fluorescent lipid in the lipid stock.

### Recombinant protein production

#### RBD:

The DNA sequences encoding the signal peptide (amino acids 1–14) and the receptor binding domain (RBD, amino acids 319–541) of SARS-CoV-2 spike surface glycoprotein (NCBI Reference Sequence: [YP\_009724390](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/protein/YP_009724390)) in fusion with a C-terminal hexahistidine affinity-tag (His6, GHHHHHH) and a stop codon (hereafter referred to as RBD) was optimized to mammalian codon preference, produced by gene synthesis (GenScript), and sub-cloned into the pCDNA3.1(-) mammalian expression plasmid (#V79520, Thermo Fisher Scientific, Waltham, MA, USA). Recombinant RBD was produced in Expi293F mammalian cells and affinity purified from cell culture supernatant following the protocol described in detail in our prior study[58](#R60). Purified RBD was buffer exchanged to phosphate buffered saline (PBS) and concentrated to 1 mg/ml using Amicon Ultra centrifugal filter unit (MWCO=10 kDa, #UFC9010, Merck Millipore, Burlington, MA, USA), sterile-filtered, and flash frozen in liquid nitrogen. The integrity and purity of proteins were tested by reducing and non-reducing SDS-PAGE and western blotting techniques[58](#R60).

#### Hemagglutinin:

Soluble and homotrimeric recombinant hemagglutinin (hereafter referred to as HA) was synthesized in insect cells using a modified protocol previously detailed by Margine et al.[42](#R55),[59](#R61) The DNA sequence encompassing the signal peptide (amino acids 1–17) and the ectodomain (amino acids 18–529) of the hemagglutinin of Influenza A virus (A/Puerto Rico/8/1934 (PR8), GenBank No: [ADX99484.1](https://ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/nucleotide/ADX99484.1)) in fusion with the thrombin cleavage site (RS*LVPRGSP*), followed by the foldon trimerization domain of the T4 bacteriophage fibritin (*GSGYIPEAPRDGQAYVRKDGEWVLLSTFL*, according to Stevens et. al.[60](#R62) and Karmmer et. al.[61](#R63) and a C-terminal hexahistidine affinity-tag and stop codon was produced by gene synthesis (GenScript), and sub-cloned into the pFastBac-HTA vector (#10584027, Thermo Fisher Scientific, Waltham, MA, USA). Bacmids were created in DH10Bac *E. coli* cells, while recombinant baculovirus stocks (p1, p2 and p3) were generated and produced in *Spodoptera frugiperda* Sf9 insect cells at 27 °C in Sf-900 III serum-free medium following the guidelines of the Bac-to-Bac Baculovirus Expression System manual (#10359016, Thermo Fisher Scientific, Waltham, MA, USA). Recombinant HA was expressed in *Trichoplusia ni* HighFive insect cells cultured in HyClone SFX-Insect medium (#SH30278.LS, Cytiva) as follows: HighFive cells from six confluent T175 flasks were harvested, collected by centrifugation (1200 × g, 24 °C, 10 minutes), and mixed with 15 ml of p3 baculovirus stock. The mixture was incubated at room temperature (RT) for 20 minutes and added to 210 ml of HyClone SFX-Insect medium in 1L PETG tissue culture Erlenmeyer flasks (#4115–1000, Nalgene). The cells were grown at 28 °C on a shaker at 75 r.p.m. for 72 hours. The cell culture supernatant, containing the secreted homotrimeric HA protein, was collected by centrifugation (2000 *x g*, 4 °C, 10 minutes), filtered, and subjected to affinity purification following the steps outlined in the above detailed RBD-purification protocol[58](#R60). The purified HA was buffer exchanged to PBS, concentrated to 1 mg/ml, sterile-filtered, and flash-frozen in liquid nitrogen. The integrity and purity of HA were assessed using reducing and non-reducing SDS-PAGE and western blotting techniques.

### Blood collection

Blood was collected from the orbital sinus under isoflurane anesthesia. Blood was centrifuged for 5 minutes at 13,000 × g, and the serum was stored at −20 °C and used for further analysis.

### Endpoint Enzyme-linked immunosorbent assay (ELISA)

RBD-specific ELISA: In Corning 96 Well EIA/RIA Clear Flat Bottom Polystyrene High Bind Microplates (Corning #3590), 1 μg/ml purified RBD in PBS (100 μl/well) was added overnight at 4 °C for protein coating. The following day, the coated plates were washed four times with wash buffer (0.05% Tween-20 in PBS). The plates were then blocked with 2% BSA in PBS for 2 hours at RT and washed three times with wash buffer. Mouse sera was diluted in blocking buffer and incubated for 2 hours at RT, followed by three washes. HRP-conjugated anti-mouse secondary antibody (total IgG: Jackson Immunoresearch #115-035-003; IgG1: polyclonal, abcam, #AB98693; IgG2a: polyclonal, abcam, #AB98698; IgG2b polyclonal, abcam, #AB98703; IgA: polyclonal, abcam, #AB97235) was diluted 1:10,000 (IgA 1:5,000) in blocking buffer and incubated for 1 hour, followed by three washes. KPL 2-component TMB Microwell Peroxidase Substrate (Seracare #5120–0047) was applied to the plate and the reaction was stopped with 2N sulfuric acid. Using a SpectraMax 190 microplate reader, the absorbance was measured at 450 nm. A cutoff value of the average of the OD values of blank wells plus 3 standard deviations was determined for each plate and used for calculating the area under the curve (AUC). The limit of detection of the assay was a titer of 1:100.

### SARS-CoV-2 Neutralization Assay

Production of VSV pseudotypes with SARS-CoV-2 Spike: 293T cells were plated at 3.5 X 106 cells per 10 cm dish, and 24 hours later they were transfected using calcium phosphate with 25 μg of pCG1 SARS-CoV-2 Spike D614G delta18 expression plasmid encoding a codon optimized SARS-CoV2 Spike gene with an 18-residue truncation in the cytoplasmic tail. Twelve hours after transfection, the cells were fed with fresh media containing 5mM sodium butyrate to increase the expression of the transfected DNA. Thirty hours post-transfection, the SARS-CoV-2 spike-expressing cells were infected for 2–4 hours with VSV-G pseudotyped VSVΔG-RFP at a multiplicity of infection (MOI) of ~3–5. Following infection, the cells were washed twice with media to remove the unbound virus. Media containing the VSVΔG-RFP SARS-CoV-2 pseudotypes was harvested 28–30 hours after infection and clarified by centrifugation twice at 1250 × g then aliquoted and stored at −80 °C until used for antibody neutralization analysis.

Antibody neutralization assay using VSVΔG-RFP SARS-CoV-2: All sera were heat-inactivated for 30 minutes at 55 °C prior to use in neutralization assay. In a 96 well collagen coated plate, Vero E6 cells stably expressing TMPRSS2 were seeded in 100 μl at 2.5×104 cells/well. The next day, 2-fold serially diluted serum samples were mixed with VSVΔG-RFP SARS-CoV-2 pseudotype virus (100–300 focus forming units/well) and incubated for 1 hour at 37 °C. To neutralize any potential VSV-G carryover virus, this mixture also included 1E9F9, a mouse anti-VSV Indiana G, at a concentration of 600 ng/ml (Absolute Antibody, #Ab01402–2.0). The media on VeroE6 TMPRSS2 cells was then replaced with the serum-virus mixture. 22 hours post-infection, the cells were washed and fixed with 4% paraformaldehyde before visualization on an S6 FluoroSpot Analyzer (CTL, Shaker Heights OH). Individual infected foci were quantified and the values compared to control wells without antibody. The focus reduction neutralization titer 50% (FRNT50) was measured as the greatest serum dilution at which focus count was reduced by at least 50% relative to control cells that were infected with pseudotype virus in the absence of mouse serum. FRNT50 titers for each sample were measured in at least two technical replicates and were reported for each sample as the geometric mean.

### Flow cytometry analysis of MBCs/LLPCs in mouse splenocytes and bone marrow

Splenocytes were harvested from spleens by mechanical disruption between frosted slides and filtered through 63-micron Nitex mesh. Bone marrow was flushed from femurs and tibia from each mouse using a 23g X ¾” needle and syringe into FACS buffer (PBS + 2% fetal bovine serum) and filtered through 63-micron Nitex mesh. Red blood cells were lysed with ACK Lysing buffer (Lonza #10–548E) for 5 minutes on ice and the reaction was stopped with ten times the volume of PBS. Five million cells were then stained with fixable live dead aqua (Biolegend Zombie Aqua, 423101 – 1:500 in PBS) for 15 minutes at RT. Cells were then washed with FACS buffer and stained with the respective dilutions of antibodies in [Table S4](#SD1) in BD Brilliant Staining Buffer (BD Biosciences, #563794) for 15 minutes at 4 °C. To create fluorescently labeled RBD tetramers, recombinant RBD was biotinylated using the EZ-Link Micro Sulfo-NHS-Biotinylation Kit (ThermoFisher). Streptavidin-conjugated Alexa PE and Alexa Flour 647 (all from Biolegend) were then added at a 6:1 molar ratio (biotinylated-protein to streptavidin-conjugate). Specifically, after the volume of fluorochrome needed to achieve a 6:1 molar ratio was determined, the total volume of fluorochrome was split into 10 subaliquots. These subaliquots were then added, on ice, to the biotinylated protein and mixed by pipetting every 10 minutes (for a total of 10 additions). 2.5 million events per sample were acquired on a BD Symphony A3 Lite and analyzed with FlowJo v10 software. Gating strategy can be found in [Figure S5](#SD1).

### ELISpot assay

Bone marrow was flushed from femurs and tibia from each mouse using a 23g X ¾” needle and syringe into FACS buffer and filtered through 63-micron Nitex mesh. Red blood cells were lysed with ACK Lysing buffer (Lonza #10–548E) for 5 minutes on ice and the reaction was stopped with ten times the volume of PBS. Resulting cells were counted using a Cellaca MX cell counter (Nexcelom Bioscience LLC, MA, USA). MultiScreenHTS IP Filter Plate, 0.45 μm (Millipore Sigma, #MSIPS4W10) were coated with recombinant RBD protein at 5 μg/mL in Sodium Carbonate/Sodium Bicarbonate buffer pH 9.6 (35 mM NaHCO3 and 15 mM Na2CO3) for one hour at 37 °C. Plates were then washed with 200 μL PBS/well three times and blocked at 37 °C in complete RPMI + 10% FBS for 30 minutes. Bone marrow cells were plated in six halving dilutions beginning with one million total BM cells per well and incubated overnight in complete RPMI + 10% FBS. Plates were then washed with wash buffer (1X PBS + 0.1% Tween 20) five times and incubated with various biotinylated anti-IgG detection antibodies ([Table S5](#SD1)) in PBS+2%BSA at RT for one hour. Plates were once again washed five times, and streptavidin-alkaline phosphatase (1:20,000 dilution in PBS+2%BSA) was added prior to incubation at RT for 30 minutes. Plates were then washed five times with wash buffer, and 50 μL/well BCIP/NBT single solution (Sigma, #B1911–100mL) was added for approximately five minutes or until spots developed, at which time the reaction was quenched with 100 μL 1M sodium phosphate monobasic solution. After plates were rinsed with dH2O and dried overnight, they were scanned and counted using CTL ImmunoSpot hardware and software (ImmunoSpot®).

### Spike-specific CD4+ and CD8+ T cell studies

BALB/c mice received a single i.m. immunization with 5 μg of S-2P mRNA encapsulated in different LNP formulations. Splenocytes were collected from animals 10 days after injection. Sample Processing: spleens were mashed in complete Roswell Park Memorial Institute (RPMI) 1640 Medium (ATCC modification, Gibco, #A1049101) on ice and filtered through a 40 μm cell strainer. Red blood cells were lysed with ACK Lysing buffer (Lonza #10–548E) for 5 minutes on ice and the reaction was stopped with ten times the volume of PBS. Then, 1 × 106 cells per sample were stimulated for 11 hours at 37 °C and 5% CO2, in the presence of SARS-CoV-2 Spike Glycoprotein peptide pool (JPT Peptide Technologies, #PM-WCPV-S-2) at 1.8 μg/mL per peptide. GolgiPlug (1:250; brefeldin A; BD Biosciences, #51–2301KZ) and GolgiStop (1:250; monensin; BD Biosciences, #51–2092KZ) were added to each sample 1 hour after the start of the stimulation. Unstimulated samples for each animal were included. A phorbol 12-myristate-13-acetate (25 ng/mL; Sigma, #P1585) and ionomycin (2.5 μg/mL; Sigma, #13909)-stimulated sample was included as a positive control. After stimulation, cells were washed with PBS and stained for 10 minutes in dark at 25 °C with the LIVE/DEAD fixable aqua dead cell stain kit (1:60, Life Technologies, #[L34957](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/nuccore/L34957)). Samples were incubated in the Fc blocker (1:25, Purified Rat Anti-Mouse CD16/CD32, BD Biosciences, clone 2.4G2, #553142) for 10 minutes in dark at 4 °C and then surface-stained with the monoclonal antibodies anti-CD4 PerCP/Cy5.5 (1:100, clone GK1.5, BioLegend, #100434) and anti-CD8 Pacific Blue (1:100, clone 53–6.7, BioLegend, #100725) for 30 minutes at 4 °C. After surface staining, cells were washed with FACS buffer, fixed and permeabilized using the Cytofix/Cytoperm kit (BD Biosciences, #51–2090KZ), and washed again using the permeabilization buffer (BD Biosciences, #51–2091KZ). Cells were intracellularly stained with anti-CD3 APC-Cy7 (1:50, clone, 145–2C11, BD Biosciences, #561042), anti-TNF-α PE-Cy7 (1:50, clone MP6-XT22, BD Biosciences, #557644), and anti-IFN-γ AF700 (1:50, clone XMG1.2, BD Biosciences, #557998) monoclonal antibodies for 30 minutes at 4 °C. Next, the cells were washed twice with the permeabilization buffer (BD Biosciences, #51–2091KZ) and once with FACS buffer, fixed with 4% paraformaldehyde in PBS, and stored at 4 °C until analysis. Splenocytes were analyzed on an LSR II flow cytometer (BD Biosciences). In all, 200,000 events were collected per specimen. Data was analyzed with the FlowJo 10 program. Data was expressed by subtracting the percentages of the unstimulated stained cells from the percentages of the peptide pool-stimulated stained samples. Any value less than zero is represented as zero. The gating strategy can be found in [Figure S6](#SD1).

### Gp100-specific CD8+ T cell studies

Thy1.1+ CD8+ T cells were purified from the spleens of female Pmel-1 mice by negative selection using the EasySep Mouse CD8+ T cell Isolation Kit (Stem Cell Technologies, #19853) according to the manufacturer’s protocol. Enriched pmel-1 CD8+ T cells were washed three times with sterile 1x PBS. A physiological precursor frequency number of cells (5 × 103 to 7 × 103) was transferred to each naive, lymphoreplete recipient female C57BL/6 mouse through tail vein injection (200 μl per injection). Mice were subsequently immunized with either a 1 μg prime and 1 μg boost (bolus immunization) or four equal 1.25 μg injections (extended prime immunization) of mRNA-LNP encoding murine full-length gp100 and the modified peptide sequence (EGPRNQDWL). Negative control mice were injected with the same doses of mRNA-LNP encoding firefly luciferase as an irrelevant antigen (Luc mRNA-LNP). Flow cytometric analysis of the circulating antigen-specific CD8+ T cells in the peripheral blood was performed 5 to 6 days after the last immunization. Peripheral blood was obtained from mice through retro-orbital eye bleeds under anesthesia and collected into 1.5 ml Eppendorf tubes containing 2 μl of 0.5 M EDTA to inhibit coagulation. Blood samples were subjected to three cycles of red blood cell lysis using 500 μL of ACK lysis buffer each cycle. Samples were resuspended in FACS buffer (1 × PBS containing 5% FBS and 2 mM EDTA), passed through a 70 μm filter to remove debris, counted, and finally allocated for downstream flow cytometric analyses or functional assays.

For Intracellular Cytokine Staining (ICS) and Flow Cytometry analyses, PBMCs were plated at 1 × 105 cells per well in a 96-well round bottom plate and re-stimulated for 5 hours with the murine gp10025–33 epitope (EGSRNQDWL) peptide at 1 μg/ml in complete RPMI 1640 medium (supplemented with 10% FBS, 1% penicillin/streptomycin and 55 μM β-mercaptoethanol) at 37 °C in the presence of brefeldin-A (15 μg/ml). After 5 hours, cells were collected and stained with Fixable Viability Dye eFluor 780 stainings (1:1000, Thermo Fisher Scientific #65–0865) or Zombie Violet Fixable Viability dye (1:200, BioLegend, #423114) for 15–30 minutes on ice in 1 × PBS. After washing out the viability dye, cells were stained for cell surface markers in FACS buffer. Intracellular staining of the cytokines was performed using the Cyto-Fast Fix/Perm Buffer Set (Biolegend, #426803) according to the manufacturer’s protocol. Cell surface antibodies include: anti-CD3 on PerCP/Cyanine5.5 (1:100, Biolegend, #100218, clone 17A2) or APC (1:100, Biolegend, #100312, clone 145–2C11), anti-CD8 on PE/Cyanine7 (1:200, Biolegend, #100722, clone 53–6.7), anti-CD90.1 (Thy1.1) on FITC (1:400, Biolegend, #202504, clone OX-7), anti-KLRG1 on PE (1:200, Biolegend, #138408, clone 2F1/KLRG1), and anti-CD127 (IL-7Rα) on APC/Cyanine7 (1:100, Biolegend, #135040, clone A7R34). Cytokine antibodies include: anti-IFN-γ on PE (1:100, Biolegend, #505808, clone XMG1.2) or Brilliant Violet 421 (1:100, Biolegend, #505830, clone XMG1.2), anti-TNF-α on Brilliant Violet 711 (1:100, Biolegend, #506349, clone MP6-XT22). Following completion of antibody staining, cells were treated with 1.6% methanol-free formaldehyde and resuspended in 1x PBS. Sample data were acquired on the ThermoFisher Scientific NxT Attune flow cytometer in the UCLA Johnson Comprehensive Cancer Center (JCCC) Flow Cytometry Core Laboratory. Samples were compensated using single stain controls generated with UltraComp eBeads Plus Compensation beads (Invitrogen, #01333342). Data were analyzed using FlowJo software (version 10.10.0). The gating strategy can be found in [Figure S8](#SD1).

### In vivo bioluminescence imaging studies (IVIS)

Three μg of Luc mRNA formulated in the four LNP compositions were diluted in PBS and injected into the gastrocnemius muscle (40 μL injection volume) with a 3/10cc 29½G insulin syringe (Covidien, #8881600145). Bioluminescence imaging was performed with an IVIS Spectrum imaging system (Caliper Life Sciences), and data were collected and analyzed using Living IMAGE Software 4.7.4 (Caliper). Mice were administered D-luciferin (Regis Technologies, #RG-1-360242-200) at a dose of 150 mg/kg intraperitoneally. Mice were anesthetized after receiving D-luciferin in a chamber with 3% isoflurane (Piramal Healthcare Limited) and placed on the imaging platform while being maintained on 2% isoflurane via a nose cone. Mice were imaged at 5 minutes post administration of D-luciferin using an exposure time of 5 seconds or longer to ensure that the signal acquired was within effective detection range (above noise levels and below CCD saturation limit). Bioluminescence values were quantified by measuring photon flux (photons/second) in the region of interest where bioluminescence signal emanated using the Living IMAGE Software provided by Caliper.

### Luciferase expression in different organs

5 μg of Luc mRNA formulated in the four LNP compositions was diluted in PBS and injected into the gastrocnemius muscle (40 μL injection volume) with a 3/10cc 29½G insulin syringe (Covidien, #8881600145). Spleen, draining popliteal and inguinal lymph nodes, and liver were harvested and analyzed. Tissues were homogenized in cell lysis buffer (Promega Corp, #E1531) using PowerLyzer 24 (Qiagen), received three freeze/thaw cycles, and centrifuged for 10 minutes at 16,000 g at 4 °C[62](#R64). Luciferase activity of supernatant was measured using the Luciferase Assay System (Promega, # E1501) on a Victor3 1420 Multilabel Plate Counter (Perkin Elmer, Wellesley, MA). Luciferase activity was normalized by supernatant total protein determined by Lowry assay (DC Protein Assay; BioRad, #500–0116) using BSA as a standard and expressed as kLU/mg of protein.

### mRNA QuantiGene (QG) analysis within different organs

5 μg of Luc mRNA formulated in the four LNP compositions was diluted in PBS and injected into the gastrocnemius muscle (40 μL injection volume) with a 3/10cc 29½G insulin syringe (Covidien, #8881600145). Spleen and liver were harvested and analyzed. QG assays were conducted according to the manufacturer’s instructions (ThermoFisher, #QS0016) using custom-designed probes against luciferase mRNA. Unformulated mRNA was used to prepare the standard curve for quantitation.

### 3D-P-DMA (ionizable lipid) LC-MS analysis

Five μg of Luc mRNA formulated in the four LNP compositions was diluted in PBS and injected into the gastrocnemius muscle (40 μL injection volume) with a 3/10cc 29½G insulin syringe (Covidien, #8881600145). Spleen and liver tissues were homogenized in PBS buffer using a FastPrep machine, 3 cycles of 5 m/s × 15 s. Homogenates from the PBS-treated control animals were pooled and used for standard curve preparation. Samples were extracted into a deep well plate with 160 μL of an internal suitability standard. Plates were stored at −20 °C for 15 minutes and then centrifuged for 15 minutes at 4 °C, and 500 × g. Analysis was performed using a Q-Exactive Orbitrap MS coupled to a Thermo Vanquish LC, using reverse-phase LC separation with an ammonium acetate/IPA gradient. Quantitation of the ionizable lipid was by parallel reaction monitoring against a calibration curve. Calibration used a similar class of synthetic lipid as internal standard.

### LNP in vitro cellular uptake study

DC 2.4 cells or HuMDDCs were seeded on 35 mm polymer-bottom dishes (Cellvis) overnight using RPMI complete media \[RPMI 1640 (Gibco, #22400–089), 10% FBS (Cytivia Hyclone, #SH30071.03), 1% Penicillin/Streptomycin (Gibco, #15140–122)\] and then treated with DiI-labeled Luciferase mRNA-LNPs at 500 ng/ml for 2 hours. Cells were subsequently washed with PBS, then stained with Hoescht 33342 at 1 ng/μl for 5 minutes. Cells were washed with PBS again and covered with RPMI complete media. Images were taken immediately using a confocal laser scanning microscope (LSM 710, Zeiss) at University of Pennsylvania’s Cell & Developmental Biology (CDB) Microscopy Core. Images were analyzed using ImageJ2 (Fiji), an open-source image processing software.

### Cell Transfections with TransIT-mRNA reagent

DC2.4 cells and HuMDDCs were transfected with mRNA using TransIT-mRNA (Mirus Bio, #MIR 2225) as per the manufacturer’s instructions: mRNA (0.3 μg) was combined with TransIT-mRNA Reagent (0.34 μL) and Boost Reagent (0.22 μL) in 17 μL of serum-free medium, and the complex was added to 6 × 104 cells in 183 μL complete medium.

### Endosomal escape study

DC 2.4 cells or HuMDDCs were seeded on 8 well chamber slides (ThermoFisher Nunc Lab Tek II System) overnight using RPMI complete media \[RPMI 1640 (Gibco, #22400–089), 10% FBS (Cytivia Hyclone, #SH30071.03), 1% Penicillin/Streptomycin (Gibco, #15140–122)\] and then treated with Luciferase mRNA-LNPs at 2 μg/ml concentration for 2 hours. Cells were subsequently processed using EEA1/LAMP1 protein staining and Luciferase mRNA was tagged with florescence in situ hybridization (RNA-FISH) using ViewRNA Cell Plus Assay kit (ThermoFisher, #88-19000-99) following the manufacturer’s protocol. In brief, cells were permeabilized/fixed, blocked, and incubated with EEA1 and LAMP1 primary antibodies (ThermoFisher, #PA1063A, #PA1654A) and a control well with rabbit isotype IgG control (ThermoFisher, #31235) for 1 hour at RT at 1:500 dilution in blocking buffer. For secondary antibody, cells were incubated with AlexaFluor 488 secondary antibody (ThermoFisher, #A11070) for 1 hour at RT with 1:1000 dilution. Subsequently, cells were fixed and processed with RNA FISH probes using a custom designed probe for the Luciferase mRNA sequence. GAPDH was also probed as a positive control, and dap-B as a negative control. Nuclei were stained with DAPI mountant (Thermofisher, #[P36931](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/protein/P36931)), and slides were imaged on a CrestOptics X-Light V3 Spinning Disk Confocal at University of Pennsylvania’s Cell & Developmental Biology (CDB) Microscopy Core. Images were analyzed using ImageJ2 (Fiji), an open-source image processing software.

### Quantification and statistical analysis

Data were collected and expressed as mean ± standard error of the mean (SEM). Data were organized and analyzed using Microsoft® Excel® for Microsoft 365 MSO (Version 2411 Build 16.0.18227.20082). Statistical analysis was conducted using GraphPad Prism® v10.0.0 (GraphPad Software) software package. The significance of the differences between the groups was assessed using the test specified in the legend accompanying the related figure. Differences were considered statistically significant at p < 0.05.
