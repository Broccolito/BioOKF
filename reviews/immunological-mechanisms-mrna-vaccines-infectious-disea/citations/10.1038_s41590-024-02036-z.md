### Abstract

We performed a systems vaccinology analysis to investigate immune responses in humans to an H5N1 influenza vaccine, with and without the AS03 adjuvant, to identify factors influencing antibody response magnitude and durability. Our findings revealed a platelet and adhesion-related blood transcriptional signature on day 7 that predicted the longevity of the antibody response,

### Author Manuscriptsuggesting a potential role for platelets in modulating antibody response durability. Since platelets

originate from megakaryocytes, we explored the effect of thrombopoietin (TPO)-mediated megakaryocyte activation on antibody response longevity. We found that TPO administration enhanced the durability of vaccine-induced antibody responses. TPO-activated megakaryocytes also promoted survival of human bone marrow plasma cells through integrin β1/β2-mediated cell-cell interactions, along with survival factors APRIL, and the MIF/CD74 axis. Using machine learning, we developed a classifier based on this platelet-associated signature, which predicted antibody response longevity across six vaccines from seven independent trials, highlighting a conserved mechanism for vaccine durability.

### Introduction Author ManuscriptA major challenge in vaccinology is learning how to induce durable protective immunity.

Protection against infection is generally thought to be mediated by antibodies that prevent infection of host cells1, 2. Live attenuated viral vaccines such as those against smallpox and yellow fever induce durable antibody responses that can last a lifetime3, 4. However waning antibody responses have been documented with other types of vaccines, including the inactivated subunit vaccine against seasonal influenza, Bordetella pertussis, Salmonella enterica serovar Typhi and Neisseria meningitidis, the RTS,S malaria vaccine and candidate

Nat Immunol. Author manuscript; available in PMC 2026 January 02.

vaccines against HIV5, 6. Why some vaccines provide lifelong protection and others protect

### Author Manuscript

for only a few months remains one of the great mysteries of immunology.

Adjuvants are vaccine components that enhance the magnitude and durability of the immune response7, 8. For more than 70 years, insoluble aluminum salts (alum) were the only licensed adjuvant, but in the past 3 decades there has been a growth in the number of adjuvants available in licensed vaccines. These include oil-in-water emulsion-based adjuvants (MF59, AS03), used in influenza vaccines, adjuvants containing the TLR4 agonist 3-O-desacyl-4’-monophosphoryl lipid A (MPL) (AS01, AS04), used in respiratory syncytial virus, herpes zoster, and human papilloma virus vaccines, CpG 1018, a TLR9 agonist CpG oligonucleotide used in the HepB-CpG hepatitis B vaccine, and more recently, a saponin based adjuvant called Matrix M which is included in the recombinant COVID vaccine and the R21 malaria vaccine7, 9, 10. Despite this large growth in adjuvant technology, the

### Author Manuscript

molecular mechanisms by which most of these adjuvants work is poorly understood.

Systems vaccinology enables a comprehensive analysis of the cellular and molecular networks driving immune response to vaccination and has defined molecular signatures that predict antibody response to vaccination with the yellow fever, seasonal influenza and other vaccines11, 12, 13, 14, 15, 16, and revealed novel mechanistic insights17, 18, 19. A recent meta-analysis of over 3000 samples from 820 adults across 28 studies of 13 vaccines and revealed that the plasmablast signature was a conserved signature capable of predicting antibody response to many vaccines20. However, all these studies focused on defining early correlates of antibody responses at the peak time points, and none of these studies defined a signature that could predict the durability of immune responses.

Here we performed an in-depth multi-omics analysis of cellular, transcriptional, and

### Author Manuscript

metabolic responses to a pre-pandemic H5N1 avian influenza vaccine with and without the AS03 adjuvant in a cohort of healthy adults. AS03 is an adjuvant system containing DL-α-tocopherol (Vitamin E) and squalene in an oil-in-water emulsion, and has been shown to increase the breadth and magnitude of CD4+ T cell and antibody responses against multiple strains of influenza21, 22, 23, 24. Recent work in mice demonstrated that AS03 induced alterations in expression of lipid metabolism-related genes in the draining lymph nodes, as well as increased endoplasmic reticulum (ER) stress in macrophages, which drove elevated cytokine production and improved antibody responses25. Additionally, the similar oil-in-water emulsion-based adjuvant MF59 has been shown to induce a local release of extracellular ATP26, and to depend functionally on MyD88 and in an inflammasome- independent fashion27, suggesting that these type of adjuvants produce some degree of cell injury or stress that result in release of damage-associated molecular patterns (DAMPs) and

### Author Manuscript

induction of innate immune responses. However, the molecular pathways through which AS03 promotes these responses remain poorly defined.

Vaccination with H5N1+AS03 induced robust innate immune responses that increased in magnitude following booster immunization and a significant increase in circulating activated Tfh cells, which was correlated with increases in antibody titers. We identified a transcriptional signature induced within 3 to 7 days of vaccination, and comprised of mRNAs contained in platelets and those involved in cell adhesion, that was capable of

predicting the durability of the antibody response in an independent blinded cohort of

### Author Manuscript

H5N1+AS03 vaccinees and across other vaccines, including the BNT162b2 mRNA vaccine against COVID-19, meningococcal, pneumococcal and the RTS,S malaria vaccine, in 7 independent human trials. Single cell profiling revealed platelets as major driver of this cell adhesion-related longevity signature, and experiments in mice demonstrated that activation of megakaryocytes, platelet precursors residing in the bone marrow, via administration of thrombopoietin (TPO), a cytokine that stimulates the differentiation of megakaryocytes into platelets28, boosts the durability of antibody response to vaccination with an AS03- adjuvanted subunit COVID-19 vaccine. Furthermore, in vitro experiments involving the co-culture of megakaryocytes and plasma cells isolated from human or mouse bone marrow revealed enhanced survival of and immunoglobulin production by plasma cells, in a contact- dependent manner. Together, these results define a molecular signature that can be used to predict the durability of antibody responses to vaccination and reveal important insights into

### Author Manuscript

the mechanisms by which vaccines induce durable immunity.

### Results

#### Early transcriptional signatures of AS03 adjuvanted H5N1 vaccination

We performed a clinical trial in which we randomized in a 2:1 ratio a total of 50 healthy participants aged 21–45 years old to receive two doses 21 days apart of a monovalent, split- virion, inactivated H5N1 clade 2.1 A/Indonesia/05/2005 influenza vaccine, administered with (n=34) or without (n=16) the AS03 adjuvant (Figure 1A and Extended Data Table

1) (ClinicalTrials.gov ID NCT01910519). In order to obtain a detailed characterization of innate and adaptive immune responses to adjuvanted versus unadjuvanted H5N1 vaccination, we performed gene expression analyses on peripheral blood mononuclear cells
### Author Manuscript

(PBMCs) and measured key immunological parameters of vaccine induced B- and T-cell responses on the first day of vaccination and at regular intervals thereafter, as illustrated in

Figure 1A.

We began by examining the impact of AS03 on gene expression in PBMCs. First, we identified differentially expressed genes (DEGs) pre- versus post-vaccination in both the adjuvanted and unadjuvanted groups. H5N1+AS03 induced a stronger transcriptional response than H5N1 alone based on the number of genes, with the large majority of DEGs observed at day 1 post-prime and post-boost (Figure 1B). We observed that the peak response in the unadjuvanted group at day 3 post-prime exhibited similarity with day 1 prime responses in the adjuvanted group (Extended Data Figure 1A), suggesting that the kinetics of the response is delayed in unadjuvanted participants.

### Author Manuscript

To identify the specific pathways activated in response to H5N1 vaccination, we performed gene set enrichment analysis (GSEA)29 on genes ranked by post-vaccination fold change. As a basis for this analysis, we used a set of blood transcriptional modules (BTMs) previously identified by our group through large-scale network integration of publicly available human blood transcriptomes30. Merging BTM enrichment scores according to high-level functional categories revealed that AS03 increased expression of modules associated with a broad range of innate and adaptive immune cells and pathways on day 1 and 7 post-prime and boost vaccination, respectively (Figures 1C and 1D). Particularly, we found strong

enrichment of BTMs related to monocyte and dendritic cell (DC) activation at early time-

### Author Manuscript

points after each immunization, while day 7 responses were mostly dominated by robust B cell and plasma cell transcriptional responses. On the other hand, unadjuvanted participants showed very little immune activation after the prime dose, and required a boost dose to achieve modest enrichment of innate responses such as antigen presentation and interferon signaling, as detailed in Figure 1E. Accordingly, B cell and plasma cell modules were only mildly increased in the unadjuvanted group 7 days after prime (Figure 1C), but more strongly increased after boost (Figure 1D), although to a lesser extent than the adjuvant group.

Notably, while analyzing the effect of AS03 on gene expression, we noticed significant differences in transcriptional activity after each dose of adjuvanted vaccine. Indeed, we found several BTMs related to interferon signaling and DC activation to be more strongly

### Author Manuscript

upregulated on day 1 after the booster dose (d22) compared to day 1 post prime (Figures 1F and 1G), despite a broad similarity overall in the type of immune responses elicited by AS03 after each dose. Gene expression analysis on day 3 and day 24 (3 days after each dose), however, revealed more qualitative differences between prime and boost immunizations, with multiple BTMs that were downregulated or unchanged 3 days post-prime but were robustly upregulated on day 3 post-boost (Figure 1H) Several of the modules exhibiting this behavior were related to DC activation, and others such as M89.0 contained genes encoding proteins with chemotactic functions for granulocytes, monocytes, and macrophages such as IL-8 and HBEGF (Figure 1I). Kinetic analysis of these modules revealed that they were all increased on day 1 after both doses (Extended Data Figure 1B), thus indicating that specific innate responses to H5N1+AS03 are more prolonged following the booster immunization. This enhanced memory-like innate response following secondary vaccination is reminiscent

#### AS03 enhances magnitude, affinity, and breadth of antibody responses to H5N1

**vaccination** AS03 has been previously reported to enhance antibody responses in humans in the context of H5N1 influenza vaccination34, 35. Accordingly, we observed a significant increase in H5N1 A/Indonesia-specific microneutralization (MN) titers at every measured time-point following AS03-adjuvanted vaccination (Figure 2A). Importantly, a booster vaccination resulted in 70% and 97% seroconversion rates as assessed by hemagglutination inhibition (HAI) and MN assays, respectively, by day 42 (3 weeks following the last vaccination), both defined as a ≥4-fold post-vaccination increase over baseline levels (Figure 2A-B

### Author Manuscript

and Extended Data Table 2). In contrast, participants in the unadjuvanted group showed significantly lower MN and HAI responses, with no vaccine recipient achieving a 4- fold increase in HAI titers over baseline levels. In addition to inducing neutralizing antibodies against the vaccine strain, AS03-adjuvanted vaccination promoted broad cross- clade neutralizing antibodies against three heterologous H5N1 strains belonging to clade 2, namely clade 2.2.1 A/Turkey, clade 2.2.1 A/Egypt, and clade 2.3.4 A/Anhui (Figure 2C), consistent with previous analysis36. This broad antibody-mediated cross-protection against

phylogenetically close, but not distant H5N1 viruses suggested that AS03 induces antibody

### Author Manuscript

responses primarily against the HA head, which is less conserved across clades.

To explore this further, we used surface plasmon resonance (SPR) real time kinetics assays to quantitate total antibody binding and polyclonal sera antibody affinity against recombinant HA1 (head) and HA2 (stem) domains derived from the H5N1 vaccine strain. SPR measurements showed that participants who received the AS03-adjuvanted H5N1 vaccine exhibited significantly higher levels of HA-binding antibodies to both H5 head and stem subunits after both prime and boost when compared with participants in the unadjuvanted group (Figure 2D), also consistent with prior findings36. Although comparable to the unadjuvanted group after prime, antibody affinity for the H5 head domain was significantly higher in the AS03 group post-boost (Figure 2E). Fold changes in both total binding and affinity to the H5 head correlated with increase in MN titers post-boost

(Figure 2F-G). Overall, these results are consistent with a previous report by Khurana and colleagues36, and have been independently reproduced in a separate analysis published recently 37.

#### Vaccination-induced activated T follicular helper cells correlate with antibody titers

The generation of neutralizing antibodies with high affinity to the H5 antigen promoted by AS03 prompted us to investigate the role of T follicular helper (Tfh) cells in the context of H5N1 vaccination. Tfh cells are crucial for affinity maturation of B cells in the germinal center (GC)38 and have been previously monitored following immunization with AS03 in animal models25. Although bona fide GC Tfh cells are not commonly detected in peripheral blood, we measured before and after H5N1 vaccination a population of circulating Tfh-like CXCR5+ CD4+ T cells previously described to be functionally similar to GC Tfh cells39,

### Author Manuscript40, 41, 42, 43

(Extended Data Figure 2A). We found that immunization with H5N1+AS03 resulted in increased frequencies of activated PD-1+ ICOS+ blood Tfh cells on day 7 day after each immunization (Extended Data Figure 2B), which was absent following vaccination with H5N1 alone. The increase in activated Tfh observed after boost was correlated with increases in MN titers (Extended Data Figure 2C).

To further characterize vaccine-induced blood Tfh cells, we FACS-sorted PD-1+ ICOS+ CXCR5+ CD4+ activated- and PD-1- ICOS- quiescent CXCR5+ CD4+ T cells on days 7 and 28 and profiled their gene expression using Clariom S technology. There were significant transcriptional differences between activated versus quiescent Tfh cells, which were consistent in both Tfh1 (CXCR3+) and Tfh2 (CXCR3-CXCR6-) populations (Extended Data Figure 2D). Activated Tfh upregulated expression of the FGL2 gene,

### Author Manuscript which encodes fibrinogen-like protein 2, an important immune regulator of both innate

and adaptive responses constitutively secreted by both CD4+ and CD8+ T cells44, as well as FGFBP2 (previously known as KSP37), whose product is selectively produced by lymphocytes with cytotoxic potential45. Surprisingly, we also noticed strong modulation of genes encoding for proteins with known antimicrobial properties typical of innate immune cells, such as lysozyme (LYZ) and IL1β, in addition to C-type lectin receptor CLEC7A, normally found on myeloid cells, and the S100 calcium binding proteins S100A8, S100A9, and S100A12, abundantly present in monocytes46, 47, 48. In order to investigate these

findings further, we used CIBERSORT, a computational method developed to deconvolve

### Author Manuscript

cell type proportions using cell type-specific gene expression references49. In agreement with our GSEA analysis, estimated frequencies of monocytes were significantly higher in the activated versus quiescent Tfh populations (Extended Data Figure 2E). Previous work has described the existence of CD4+ memory T cell-monocyte complexes in human blood50. Notably, these CD3+ CD14+ T cell-monocyte doublets populate the live singlet gate when observed using conventional flow cytometry and form during active immune responses50. The origin as well as the biological role of these cellular doublet associations in the peripheral circulation are currently unknown.

Given our initial interest in the transcriptional mechanisms associated with Tfh activation, we used CIBERSORTx51 to estimate CD4 T cell-specific expression in sorted Tfh cells (hence computationally excluding the monocyte component), and then ran GSEA to identify

### Author Manuscript

BTMs enriched in activated versus quiescent Tfh. Not surprisingly, we found strong enrichment in cell cycle- and energy metabolism-related transcriptional modules, indicating a higher metabolic state and increased proliferation for activated Tfh cells versus their quiescent counterpart (Extended Data Figures 2F-G). Our analysis also suggests that the activation process of Tfh cells might revolve around polo-like kinase 1 (PLK1), one of the major kinases controlling T cell survival, differentiation, and expansion52, 53. Finally, gene level analysis of sorted Tfh cell populations followed by unsupervised clustering successfully segregated Tfh subsets based on their activation state, highlighting strong upregulation of genes encoding for histone proteins and interferon-stimulated genes (such as ISG15, known for potentiating IFN-g production) in activated Tfh cells (Extended Data

Figure 2I). Activation-induced changes of these genes were common between Tfh types

(Tfh1 versus Tfh2), and Tfh at different time-points (day 7 and day 28). Importantly, while

### Author ManuscriptAS03+H5N1 induced significantly higher frequencies of circulating activated Tfh after

vaccination compared to H5N1 alone, we found no major differences at the transcriptional level between activated Tfh cells isolated from participants in the two vaccination groups. Taken together, these data suggest that AS03-adjuvanted H5N1 vaccination promotes higher frequencies of circulating, activated Tfh cells after both prime and boost vaccinations compared to unadjuvanted vaccination. Frequencies of activated Tfh cells positively correlated with neutralizing antibodies and antibody affinity to the H5 head domain in the adjuvant group, thus indicating AS03-driven T cell-dependent mechanisms of B cell affinity maturation and antibody production.

#### Platelet and cell adhesion-related transcriptional signature predicts antibody response

**durability**

### Author Manuscript

The recent COVID-19 crisis has highlighted the importance of developing pandemic vaccines that induce long-lasting protection, particularly in a scenario where multiple epidemic waves might occur and vaccine demand might exceed supply, as in the early phases of a pandemic onset. Here we observed a four-fold decrease in geometric mean MN and HAI titers between day 42 and day 100 post H5N1+AS03 vaccination using a prime- boost strategy (Figure 2A-B). A comparison between antibody responses to H5N1+AS03 and those to IIV13 showed that this decrease in antibody titers against avian influenza was similar to that seen several months after immunization using seasonal influenza vaccines

(Extended Data Figure 3A). As a measure of the relative persistence of antibody responses,

### Author Manuscript

we calculated residuals from a linear fit between the day 42 and day 100 HAI titers (Extended Data Figure 3B). This approach removes the dependence of the day 100 titers on the initial day 42 response, and the residual can be viewed as a ‘normalized’ day 100 titer, with positive values indicating participants with a more persistent response than average.

First, we determined whether the day 7 “plasmablast signature” observed by us and others in previous studies with seasonal influenza vaccination12, 20, 54 which was shown to be a correlate of HAI titers at day 28, was correlated with durability of antibody response. As seen previously in our study with seasonal flu vaccine13, we observed that the plasmablast signature correlated with peak antibody responses (Extended Data Figure 3C) but not with response durability (Extended Data Figure 3D). In order to identify transcriptional signatures associated with a persistent antibody response, we performed GSEA on genes

### Author Manuscript

ranked by their correlation with the day 100/day 42 residual (Figure 3A). The enriched signatures appeared to differ between the prime and boost phases, with expression of cell cycle-related modules on day 7 post-prime and cell adhesion/platelet activation-related modules on days 1–7 post-boost being associated with increased persistence. We compared these results with previously identified signatures of persistence with IIV13 and found that there was significant overlap (Figure 3A). In particular, genes within the platelet activation/ actin binding module M196 showed strong agreement in their correlations with antibody persistence in both studies (Figure 3B), indicating a common underlying mechanism in the development of long-lived antibody responses to influenza vaccination.

To confirm that there was an association between platelet transcriptional signatures and antibody persistence following immunization with AS03-adjuvanted vaccines, we performed

### Author ManuscriptRNA sequencing on a cohort of rhesus macaques vaccinated in a prime-boost regimen

with AS03-adjuvanted nanoparticle COVID-19 vaccines55 (Extended Data Figure 4A). The macaques exhibited significant waning of antibody responses post-boost vaccination (Extended Data Figure 4B) and platelet transcriptional signatures on day 7 post-boost were again associated with the day 180/day 90 antibody residual, validating the signature in the nonhuman primate model and in (Extended Data Figure 4C).

To investigate the robustness of these signatures of antibody persistence, we used a machine learning approach to train a classifier capable of predicting antibody persistence across multiple influenza vaccine datasets. In brief, we trained a linear regression model in a forward selection format with BTM-level features to predict the antibody titer residual, using the H5N1+AS03 data as a training set and the 2010/2011 IIV data as a validation set. In each iteration, the model was trained using the training set and the performance was evaluated on

### Author Manuscript

the validation set. The BTM which best decreased the model error on the validation data was added to the feature set, and the model building was stopped and final feature set selected when adding additional features no longer increased performance using the validation data (Figure 3C). Furthermore, in order to confirm the robustness of our identified signatures, we collaborated with the NIH Center for Human Immunology (CHI), who provided us with gene expression data from a parallel trial of H5N1+AS03 vaccination56. Finalized model predictions of day 100/42 HAI residuals were generated using the provided gene expression data, and the team at CHI then compared the agreement of the predicted and actual antibody

persistence, allowing us to remain blinded to the HAI responses in the test dataset. Based on

### Author Manuscript

day 28/21 fold change gene expression data, we were able to build a model whose predicted HAI residuals significantly correlated with the measured residuals in all 3 datasets, including the test set in which we were blinded to the HAI data (Figure 3D). Notably, analysis of the genes within the modules selected by the predictive model showed that many of those most highly associated with antibody persistence were involved in cellular adhesion/ migration (CTTN, CDHR5, MYLK) and/or highly expressed in platelets (SELP, PROS1, PF4, PPBP) (Figure 3E), indicating a potential role for cellular migration and platelets in the establishment of durable antibody responses to AS03-adjuvanted vaccination.

#### CITE-seq analysis reveals a platelet origin for transcriptional signature of antibody

**persistence**

### Author Manuscript

Prompted by our findings, we sought to examine the cellular origins of this newly identified transcriptional signature of antibody persistence. To accomplish this, we performed CITE- seq (cellular indexing of transcriptomes and epitopes by sequencing)57 and constructed the single-cell protein and transcriptome landscape of PBMCs on day 21 and day 28 from 3 ‘persistent’ and 3 ‘waning’ antibody responders to H5N1+AS03 vaccination (day 100/42 HAI residual >0 and <0, respectively). These timepoints were chosen to capture the day 7 post-boost changes, the timepoint at which we identified a predictive signature of antibody persistence in bulk expression data. After initial preprocessing, we obtained transcriptomes for 62,789 cells. Through dimensionality reduction via UMAP and graph-based clustering (see Methods for details), we were able to identify 26 distinct cell clusters which were well distributed over all samples and time points (Extended Data Figure 5A-B). Following cell-type annotation and per cell quality control processing to filter out low quality cells, we constructed the single immune cell landscape (Figure 4A) and found that the antibody

### Author Manuscript

longevity-related genes in the predictive signature were indeed highly expressed in the platelet cluster C16, and in a small cluster of cells (C22) that shared characteristics of both monocytes and platelets (Figure 4B).

To ensure that the CITE-seq analysis captured the same immune response dynamics as the microarray data, we compared day 28/21 FCs of DEGs measured by microarray with pseudobulk estimates from CITE-seq (Extended Data Figure 5C). In this comparison, 5 participants showed strong correlations between the two measurements, while 1 subject exhibited no agreement and was therefore removed from downstream analysis. Overall, in accordance with the bulk PBMC microarray results, persistent responders had significantly higher pseudobulk expression of the antibody durability signature compared to waning responders (Figure 4C, left panel). Importantly, by removing 1 cell cluster at a time from

### Author Manuscript

the pseudobulk calculation, we found that exclusion of the platelet cluster from the analysis resulted in an almost complete loss of the transcriptional differences observed between persistent and waning antibody responders (Figure 4C, right panel). This ‘leave-one-out approach’ demonstrated once again that the durability signature observed in the bulk PMBC analysis was primarily originating from platelets. However, analysis of expression of individual genes in the predictive signature that were most associated with antibody durability (Figure 4D) across cell clusters revealed that, while predominately expressed in

platelets, a few of these genes were also expressed in other cell types, including monocytes,

### Author Manuscript

B and T cells, NK cells, and dendritic cells (Figure 4D).

While platelets lack a nucleus and are not part of the PBMC population, their similar density, small size, and adhesive properties result in them being a consistent source of contamination in PBMCs isolated using density-based methods, even after washing steps58. They are commonly detected among PBMC samples using highly sensitive single-cell RNA- seq and CITE-seq approaches48, 59, 60, 61. This raised the possibility that the signature observed in bulk PBMC expression was a result of altered levels of adhesion between platelets and other mononuclear cells, causing differing levels of platelet contamination. While in our dataset we detected a cluster of monocytes and potentially adherent platelets (C22), these cells had no impact on the detected antibody longevity gene signature as demonstrated by or “leave-one-out” analysis (Figure 4C). In contrast, bona fide platelets

### Author Manuscript

in cluster C16, which had a profound impact on the longevity signature, did not express markers of any other immune cells, including monocytes (VCAN, S100A8,9,12, CD14, FCGR3A, MS4A7), DCs (HLA-DR, CD74), B cells (IGHD, IGHM, IGKC, CD97A), T cells (CD8A, CD8B, TCF7, IL7R, GZMB, PRF1) and NK cells (GZMH, GZMA, NKG7, GZMB, PRF1) (Extended Data Figure 5D-E) and, consistent with their lack of a nucleus, had consistently low levels of total RNA and unique features compared to other identified cell clusters (Extended Data Figure 5F).

Beyond identifying the cellular origins of the predictive antibody durability signature, we also determined whether we could identify additional cell-specific differences between persistent and waning responders that were not initially detected via bulk expression measurements. To this end, we performed an unbiased comparison and determined the

### Author ManuscriptDEGs between cells from persistent and waning responders at each time point and

within each cluster followed by an overrepresentation analysis for genes included in the BTMs. As the primary cell type responsible for antibody responses to vaccination, of particular interest were differences among plasmablasts in spliceosome and electron transport expression (Figure 4E). Comparing the genes within these modules, we observed that plasmablasts of persistent responders had elevated expression of genes encoding for small nuclear ribonucleoproteins as well as mitochondrial respiratory electron transport complexes compared to waning responders (Figure 4F). These results are consistent with previous findings which indicate oxidative phosphorylation and mitochondrial remodeling as an important metabolic process necessary for B cell activation and antibody production by plasmablasts62, 63.

#### Megakaryocyte activation via thrombopoietin receptor signaling increases plasma cell

#### survival and antibody response durability

Platelets are produced from precursor cells called megakaryocytes that reside in the bone marrow. As platelets lack a nucleus and cannot transcribe RNA, their RNA levels are determined by the amount provided from megakaryocytes during production as well as the platelet age, with RNA being gradually degraded over time64, 65. We thus hypothesized that the platelet-associated transcriptional signature of antibody response durability may be a reflection of megakaryocyte activity in the bone marrow. In support of this hypothesis, prior

work has shown that megakaryocytes can produce important plasma cell survival factors

### Author Manuscript

including APRIL and IL-6 and that activation of megakaryocytes with thrombopoietin (TPO) can increase survival of long-lived plasma cells in the bone marrow66, which are responsible for long-term production of antibodies following infection or vaccination. Waning antibody responses and loss of protective efficacy over time are limitations common to many vaccines70. As such, we sought to explore whether megakaryocyte-dependent mechanisms of antibody response durability were conserved in response to other vaccines. Although bone marrow samples are difficult to obtain and not commonly collected in vaccine trials, there is a growing amount of data of transcriptomic responses in peripheral blood to various vaccines. We therefore collected publicly available datasets containing both transcriptional and long term (from ~3 months to ~2 years) antibody titer measurements post-vaccination. In total, these data contained over 900 transcriptomics samples covering responses to 7 different vaccines administered to a total of 244 participants. In addition to H5N1+AS03 and seasonal influenza vaccines, this dataset also included the live attenuated yellow fever vaccine, conjugated and polysaccharide meningococcal vaccines, RTS,S malaria vaccine, and Pfizer BNT162b2 COVID-19 mRNA vaccine (Extended Table 1). All

### Author Manuscript

of these vaccines induced waning antibody responses (Figure 8A), with the exception of the live attenuated yellow fever vaccine YF-17D.

We then correlated day 7 gene expression data with these residuals and found that platelet (Figure 8B, Extended Data Figure 7A) and cell migration/adhesion (Figure 8C) related modules were associated with antibody response persistence across vaccines. The only vaccine which did not show such a relationship was YF-17D, which could be explained by the fact that YF-17D induces antibody responses that are consistently highly persistent in almost all individuals.

### Discussion Author Manuscript

Here we presented a detailed multi-omics analysis of the cellular, transcriptional, and metabolic responses to the pandemic H5N1 avian influenza vaccine administered with and without the squalene-based emulsion adjuvant AS03 in a cohort of young healthy volunteers. A major goal of our study was the investigation of the molecular mechanisms that underlie durable vaccine-induced antibody responses. Here we identified blood transcriptional signatures of cellular migration associated with a more persistent antibody response to AS03-adjuvanted H5N1 vaccination that were predictive of antibody response durability in an independent study using the same vaccine (Figure 3). Notably, CITE-seq identified platelets as a primary driver of this longevity signature (Figure 3), indicating a potential role for bone marrow megakaryocytes, responsible for platelet production, in maintenance of durable antibody responses. Further experiments in mice demonstrated that TPO-induced

### Author Manuscript

activation of megakaryocytes promoted bone marrow plasma cell survival and durable antibody responses to an AS03-adjuvanted COVID-19 vaccine in vivo and that this occurs in a contact-dependent mechanism between megakaryocytes and plasma cells (Figure 4). In humans, this mechanism appears to be dependent on integrin β1 and β2 mediated cell-cell contact, and the survival factor APRIL and the MIF/CD74 axis (Figure 8). Interestingly, APRIL has also been shown to be released by activated platelets71, thus raising the possibility that platelets themselves may contribute to promotion of plasma cell survival. Whether such a mechanism is equally effective in promoting the survival of both short lived plasmablasts as well as the canonical “long-lived plasma cells,” remains to be

experimentally determined. Nonetheless, the fact that co-culture of human bone marrow

### Author Manuscript

megakaryocytes with bone marrow plasma cells (presumably “long-lived bone marrow plasma cells) from unvaccinated donors, resulted in the enhanced survival of plasma cells and immunoglobulin secretion suggests that megakaryocytes can indeed support the survival and function of long-lived plasma cells. Interestingly, recent work has shown that LT-K63, a detoxified derivative of an E. Coli enterotoxin, and other adjuvants are capable of increasing the frequency and APRIL production of megakaryocytes and other bone marrow cells and promote greater vaccine-induced antibody responses in mice72, 73, raising the possibility that adjuvants designed to activate megakaryocytes may be an effective means to improve the durability of vaccine-induced immunity.

These platelet and cell migration/adhesion signatures were also predictive of durability of antibody responses to a variety of different vaccines, including the RTS,S malaria

### Author Manuscript

vaccine, Pfizer-BioNTech COVID-19 mRNA vaccine, meningococcal polysaccharide vaccine, and pneumococcal conjugate vaccine (Figure 7), suggesting that the phenomenon of megakaryocyte-mediated enhancement of plasma cell survival is conserved across different types of immune responses and is not mediated by a specific vaccine formulation or adjuvant. It did, however, fail to predict responses to some vaccines, notably the yellow fever vaccine YF17D. This vaccine induces highly durable antibody responses and therefore there is little variation in the rate of decay of antibody titers across individuals within the measured window. It should be noted that the currently analyzed studies only contained serology data up to >600 days post vaccination (Figure 7A). Therefore, the extent to which the aforementioned signature can be used to accurately predict the durability of antibody responses beyond this timeframe is unknown. Additionally, it is important to stress that there may also be other unidentified factors that determine whether or not this signature is Author Manuscriptpredictive of durability of responses to a given vaccine that are not yet established from the current analysis.

Notably, this signature was capable of predicting the durability of antibody response across six different vaccines from seven independent trials of 244 total participants suggesting a conserved mechanism and predictor of vaccine durability. Nevertheless, the extent to which this signature could have broad practical utility as a prognostic predictor or the durability of vaccine responses in clinical trials in diverse human populations remains to be determined and will require further analysis of this and other signatures in additional datasets from clinical trials conducted globally.

Although the majority of vaccine datasets for which our platelet-associated signature was predictive were generated from PBMC samples, which are supposed to contain few

### Author Manuscript

platelets, it was also predictive in at least one dataset of whole blood transcriptomic data from the Pfizer COVID-19 vaccine. Furthermore, we showed using paired PBMC and Paxgene samples from seasonal influenza vaccinees that changes in platelet-expressed genes are consistently detectable in both sample types (Extended Data Figure 6). Whether the presence of such information in PBMC samples is simply a result of platelet contamination amongst PBMCs or involves other mechanisms requires further investigation. Indeed, previous studies have shown a correlation between between platelet counts in PBMCs and blood74. Moreover, since newly formed reticulated platelets have a higher RNA content

and density than mature platelets75, they may contribute significantly to gene expression

### Author Manuscript

signals detected in PBMCs. Importantly, while the identified genes predictive of antibody response durability were expressed primarily in platelets, our CITE-seq data indicated that other cell types contribute to this transcriptional signature. This suggests that there may be additional immunological mechanisms contributing to persistence of antibody responses, which deserves further investigation.

In conclusion, our findings reveal a signature that can accurately predict the durability of antibody responses, and previously unappreciated biological mechanisms antibody durability following vaccination in humans, thus underscoring once more the enormous potential of systems biological approaches in accelerating vaccine research and development76, 77, 78.

#### Human subjects, treatment protocol, and specimen collection

This study was conducted in Atlanta, GA. Subjects were healthy males and non-pregnant females aged 21–45 years old who met the eligibility criteria as listed on ClinicalTrials.gov (NCT01910519). #### Definition of persistence of antibody responses

Consistent with our prior analysis of TIV responses13, here we defined antibody persistence using a regression-based approach to estimate the expected decay in titers based on a participants’ peak response. For each dataset, a linear regression was performed on all

### Author Manuscript

subjects using peak (day 21–30 post-last vaccination) titer fold change as the independent variable and the long-term (day 90-day >600) antibody titer fold change as the dependent variable. The distance to this regression line (residual) was then computed for each subject and used as a measure of relative antibody response persistence.

#### Influenza vaccine prediction of antibody response persistence

In order to identify robust signatures of antibody response persistence, we first generated per subject enrichment scores for all BTMs based on gene-level post-vaccination fold changes

using GSEA29. We then selected as potential features BTMs whose enrichment scores on

### Author Manuscript

day 7 (TIV) or 28 (H5N1+AS03) correlated with the antibody persistence metric (HAI residual) (p<0.25) in both the TIV and H5N1+AS03 datasets. Next, these selected BTMs were used as inputs to a linear regression-based classifier to predict antibody response persistence. The classifier was trained using our H5N1+AS03 study as a training set and our previous TIV data13 as a validation set, using the persistence HAI residuals as outputs. A forward selection approach was used for feature selection, in which the classifier is trained using each module separately as the sole input. The module that achieves the highest accuracy is included in the final input set, and then modules are iteratively added to this set. Each iteration, all remaining modules are tested one at a time as an addition to the input set, and only the module that improves the average accuracy the greatest is kept. This process is stopped when the validation accuracy fails to increase in a given iteration. The final model was then used to predict the day 100/42 HAI residuals in the blinded test set56.

#### Multi-vaccine prediction of antibody response persistence

To facilitate equitable comparison across vaccines, in the multi-vaccine model antibody durability residuals were defined within each dataset using peak and long-term antibody responses as described above, and participants with positive and negative residuals were classified as ‘high’ or ‘low’ durability, respectively. For H5N1+AS03 datasets Day 28 (7 days after the second vaccination) titers were used as peak and D100 titers were used as the durability time point. For the BNT162B2 vaccine Day 28 (7 says after the second vaccination) time point was used as a peak and Day 210 as the durability time point. For RTS,S dataset, Day 62 (6 days after the third vaccination) was used as the peak, and Day 236 as the durability time point. For MPSV4 dataset, each subject was evaluated to establish the serotype against which the maximum response was achieved, and the time point at which

### Author Manuscript

the maximum response was observed (either Day 30 or Day 180) was used as the peak time point. The durability time point was chosen for each subject individually based on the peak time point: for those subjects that achieved the peak response at Day 30, Day 180 was used as the durability time point, and for those subjects that achieved the peak response at Day 180, Day 730 was used as the durability time point. Transcriptional data at Day 7 post final vaccination was used to make predictions across all datasets.

Elastic net classification models were generated using platelet and cell adhesion/migration BTMs as input features, with module scores computed as the mean fold change of constituent genes for each module. A training dataset of sufficient size was created by combining the TIV dataset (53 subjects) with the present H5N1+AS03 dataset (26 subjects); an external unpublished H5N1+AS03 dataset (CHI) was used as the validation set. Models

### Author Manuscript

were trained either on the full set of subjects in the training data, or by subsetting the training data to include only 10%, 20%, 30% or 40% of the subjects based on the absolute magnitude of the antibody residual. Elastic net regularization was achieved by iterating over a range of alpha parameters from 0 to 1 in increments of 0.01 and obtaining the best matching value of lambda parameter by 10x cross validation, thus producing a path of solutions for each combination of alpha and lambda. Each resulting model was evaluated in the validation set, and 10 best performing models were reserved for evaluation in the

test sets. Prior to testing, each model was re-trained in the combined set of training and

### Author Manuscript

validation data, and then used to predict the durability classes in the test data.

For significance testing, the probability of obtaining an AUC value equal or greater than the actual value was estimated by a permutation test. In this approach, for each dataset the class labels (durable vs. non-durable responses) were randomly permuted and prediction was done using the same locked model on a permuted dataset. The permutation and prediction cycle was repeated 10,000 times, and for each run the resulting AUC value was recorded. Then the parameters of a normal distribution were estimated based on the distribution of AUC values obtained with permuted labels, and the probability of obtaining a value equal or greater than the actual value was estimated from the resulting probability density function.

#### CITEseq analysis

### Author Manuscript

CITE-seq data was analyzed using Seurat v3.1.560. Cells containing number of feature RNA less than 200 were removed. Filtered RNA counts were normalized using LogNormalize method implemented in Seurat with a scale factor of 10,000. FindIntegrationAnchors and IntegrateData function in Seurat was used to integrate all the samples for further analysis. We selected 30 principal components for clustering and UMAP projection. Clusters were identified using SNN graph and original Louvain algorithm with a resolution of 0.5, resulting in 26 clusters. FindAllMarkers function in Seurat and Cell Markers database enrichment using clusterProfiler were used to identify cluster marker genes (p-value <=

0.05, log fold change (LFC) >= 0.25) and cell type enrichments for cluster annotations89, 90 . FindMarkers function in Seurat and BTMs enrichment using clusterProfiler were used to identify differentially regulated genes (p-value <= 0.05, LFC >= 0.25) and their functional annotations. CITE-seq also provided the antibody-derived read count matrix. We
### Author Manuscript

used a centered log-ratio (CLR) method in Seurat to normalize each feature individually. FindAllMarkers function in Seurat was used to identify cluster specific antibodies (p-value <= 0.05, LFC >=0.25). Further, to compare CITE-seq data with microarray data, we summed the Raw RNA counts of all cells in the sample to generate pseudo-RNA-seq data for that sample. Pseudo-RNA-seq data was normalized using normalization method implemented in DESeq291. All further statistical analysis was conducted using R v4.3. Test details are described in figure legends.
