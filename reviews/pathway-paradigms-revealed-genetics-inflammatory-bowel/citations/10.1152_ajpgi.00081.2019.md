First published June 26, 2019; doi:10.1152/ ajpgi.00081.2019.—Hypoxia is a notable feature of inflammatory bowel disease and chronic induction of hypoxia-inducible factor (HIF)-1 and HIF-2 (endothelial PAS domain protein 1, EPAS1) play important, but opposing, roles in its pathogenesis. While activa- tion of HIF-1 decreases intestinal inflammation and is beneficial in colitis, activation of HIF-2 exacerbates colitis and increases colon carcinogenesis in animal models, primarily due to the role of epithelial HIF-2 in mounting a potent inflammatory response. Previous work from our laboratory showed that mice overexpressing intestinal epi- thelial HIF-2 led to massive intestinal inflammation and decreased survival. As oxygen homeostasis and HIFs are critical in embryonic development, it is not clear whether the observed intestinal inflam- matory response was secondary to developmental defects. To address this question, the present study used a mouse model to temporally modulate expression of intestinal epithelial HIF-2 to assess its role in mediating inflammatory response. Remarkably, activation of HIF-2 in intestinal epithelial cells in adult mice increased expression of proinflammatory mediators; however, no decrease in survival was observed. Furthermore, in an acute model of colitis, activation of HIF-2 was sufficient to exacerbate colitis. These data confirm our previous finding that epithelial HIF-2 mediates inflammatory re- sponse and demonstrates that activation of HIF-2 is sufficient to exacerbate colitis.

**NEW & NOTEWORTHY Inflammatory bowel disease (IBD) is a** chronic relapsing inflammatory disease of the intestinal tract. Hypoxia and activation of its downstream transcription factors hypoxia-induc- ible factor (HIF)-1 and HIF-2 are notable features of IBD. HIF-1 has well-characterized protective roles in IBD; however, the role of HIF-2 has been less studied. Using novel HIF-2 mouse models, we show that activation of HIF-2 in intestinal epithelial cells is suffi- cient to exacerbate colitis.

Although the etiology of IBD is not completely under- stood, a complex interplay of decreased intestinal epithelial barrier integrity, dysregulated mucosal immune response, and gut microbial dysbiosis contribute to IBD pathogenesis (7, 12, 16, 22, 25, 32). These processes ultimately lead to chronic inflammatory response and subsequent tissue injury (15, 35). Hypoxia is a hallmark of the inflammatory foci in IBD (12, 32,
38). Previous studies have shown a robust induction of hypoxia along the intestinal mucosa in a mouse model of colitis, further suggesting that hypoxia participates in the pathogenesis of colitis (17, 33). Hypoxic responses are primarily mediated by hypoxia-in- ducible factor (HIF)-1 and (HIF)-2. HIF-1 is ubiquitously expressed, while HIF-2 expression is more restricted (31). HIF-1 and HIF-2 share extensive homology in structure and heterodimerize with HIF-1 (ARNT) to execute their tran- scriptional activities. Although HIF-1 and HIF-2 bind to the same canonical hypoxia-responsive elements (HREs), numer- ous studies have shown that HIF-1 and HIF-2 have distinct and nonredundant functions (9, 32). Activation of intestinal epithelial HIF-1 regulates genes involved in maintaining the epithelial barrier function and decreases the expression of proinflammatory mediators (1, 17, 19, 20). Moreover, stable expression of intestinal epithelial HIF-1 or pharmacological activation of HIFs by prolyl hydroxylase domain enzymes (PHDs) inhibitors is protective in experimental models of acute colitis (6, 19, 27, 42). The latter protective effects were mainly attributed to the activation of HIF-1 in these models (17, 19). However, activation of intestinal epithelial HIF-2 induces a potent epithelial proinflammatory response by regulating the expression of inflammatory cytokines and chemokines (33, 37,
41). Accordingly, mice with intestinal epithelial HIF-2 defi- ciency exhibit attenuated inflammatory responses (41). These findings suggest that in setting of colitis, HIF-1 and HIF-2 play important, but distinct, roles in resolution and progression of inflammatory responses, respectively. HIF-2 expression was further shown to be important in regulating the barrier function (11, 39). As both HIF-1 and HIF-2 impact colonic inflammatory responses, a critical factor in colon cancer, their activation was shown to distinctly impact colon cancer devel- opment. While HIF-1 had no effect on tumorigenesis (42), activation of HIF-2 resulted in increased tumor burden in mouse models of colon cancer (44). These findings suggested that targeting HIF-2 might be optimal, not only in colitis, but also in colitis-associated colon cancer. These observations hinted a possible role of HIF-2 in embryonic intestinal development, which could have impacted the observed inflammatory pheno- type. Thus, to address this question, we generated a mouse model with a tamoxifen-inducible expression of an oxygen- stable HIF-2, specifically in the intestinal epithelial cells. Temporal activation of intestinal HIF-2 did not lead to any basal intestinal injury; however, it is sufficient to increase the expression of proinflammatory mediators, six-transmembrane epithelial antigen of prostate 4 (Steap4), tumor necrosis fac- tor-(Tnf-), and chemokine (C-X-C motif) ligand 1 (Cxcl1). Importantly, temporal activation of intestinal HIF-2 did not result in spontaneous colitis, as previously reported in mice with constitutive embryonic overexpression of HIF-2. How- ever, these mice were highly susceptible to inflammatory injury in a colitis model. These findings confirm the proinflam- matory role of intestinal epithelial HIF-2 and support the notion that intestinal epithelial HIF-2 expression exacerbates colitis.

**DISCUSSION**

Hypoxia has emerged as a key feature of many diseases, including IBD, and numerous studies have investigated the role of HIFs in affecting the outcome of these pathologies (29). In models of colitis, activation of HIF-1 has been shown to improve barrier function, limit the expression of proinflamma- tory cytokines, and protect against colitis (17, 20), while activation of HIF-2 increases the expression of proinflamma- tory mediators, promoting colitis (41). These studies indicated a complex role of HIF-1 and HIF-2 in affecting the outcome of colitis. These differences in their specificities could be attributed to varying degree of tissue expression, differential coactivators, transcriptional factors, and protein-interactome of HIF subunits, thereby affecting their function (14, 26, 31). A few notable observations of constitutive HIF-2-overexpress- ing mice were that they were smaller in size, body weights were significantly lower, and mice died at less than 6 wk of age, as compared with wild-type littermates (41). Moreover, histological analysis revealed massive intestinal inflammation in these mice (41). The question then arose about whether the observed phenotype of severe colitis in HIF-2-overexpressing mice is due to a specific effect of epithelial HIF-2 in mounting a potent inflammatory response or due to other roles of HIF-2 in embryonic development, thereby impact- ing colitis. To answer this question, we used a mouse model, whereby intestinal HIF-2 levels could be temporally mod- ulated (Vil-ER T2 *Hif2* *LSL/LSL* ). This model allowed us to dissect the role of epithelial HIF-2 expression in colitis without interfering with HIF-2 function in embryonic development. Vil-ER T2 *Hif2* *LSL/LSL* mice did not impact HIF-1 target genes, further allowing us to use this model to specifically evaluate role of HIF-2 in colitis. Remarkably, temporal induction of intestinal epithelial-specific HIF-2 in Vil-ER T2 *Hif2* *LSL/LSL* mice did not result in spontaneous colitis. Vil-ER T2 *Hif2* *LSL/LSL* mice were healthy and sur- vived normally compared with littermate controls. These findings indicate that overexpressing HIF-2 during embry- onic development results in spontaneous colitis and in- creased lethality. Interestingly, in mice with an intestine- specific disruption of HIF-2, the intestines are indistin- guishable from wild-type littermates. Together, these data suggest that HIF-2 is not essential in intestinal develop- ment; however, pathological induction during development may have severe consequences. Currently, we are further assessing the role of HIF-2 in intestinal development. Temporal induction of intestinal epithelium-specific HIF-2 in Vil-ER T2 *Hif2* *LSL/LSL* mice did not lead to any overt histological or gross changes in the mice. However, this was sufficient to mount an inflammatory response by increasing mediators like Steap4, Tnf-, and Cxcl1. These data are in line with previous studies and corroborate that Steap4, Tnf-, and *Cxcl1 are direct targets of HIF-2* (37, 40, 41). These findings suggest that the activation of intestinal epithelial-specific

HIF-2 is sufficient to drive basal inflammation but not suffi- cient to drive experimental colitis. Recently, we reported that intestinal overexpression of Steap4 aggravated experimental colitis by dysregulating mitochondrial iron homeostasis (40). Interestingly, spontaneous colitis did not occur in mice with intestinal epithelium-specific overexpression of Steap4, indi- cating that dysregulating mitochondrial iron homeostasis alone does not promote colitis. These observations are in line with current findings and further reflect the complexity of IBD as a multihit, multifactorial disease. Although the parameters of intestinal mitochondrial iron and oxidative stress remained to be examined in the current model, it is plausible that Vil- ER T2 *Hif2* *LSL/LSL* mice may have dysregulated mitochondrial iron homeostasis via induction of Steap4. Using DSS-induced colitis model, we found that Vil-ER T2 *Hif2* *LSL/LSL* mice were highly susceptible to injury, as evidenced by heightened in- flammatory response and decreased colon length, suggesting that the activation of intestinal HIF-2 is sufficient to exacer- bate experimental colitis under these settings. DSS is known to damage the colonic epithelial monolayer, allowing the dissem- ination of bacteria into underlying tissue (3). Thus, activation of intestinal HIF-2 drives inflammatory response, and to- gether, in the setting of DSS, exacerbates colitis. Considering the fact that a myriad of cellular processes may be affected, directly or indirectly, by HIF-2 through its transcriptional or nontranscriptional mechanisms in colitis, we performed RNA sequencing to obtain unbiased information on global gene signatures in mice with intestinal epithelium-specific knockout of HIF-2,(Hif2 *IE* *) mice. On assessing global gene expres-* sion changes by HIF-2 deficiency, our RNA-sequencing data demonstrated significant changes in numerous previously un- known genes. Although the significance of these changes will require additional work, these findings uncovered underappre- ciated regulation of genes by HIF-2. Moreover, canonical HIF-2 targets were found to be significantly decreased in *Hif2* *IE* mice, including Dmt1 and Steap4. Interestingly, in- flammatory mediators, including *IL-1*, chemokines like *CXCR6 were found to be increased in Hif2* *IE* mice. This may point to a compensatory upregulation of these mediators im- pacted by HIF-2 or differential regulation of these mediators. To investigate the biological processes and cellular pathways affected by loss of HIF-2, we evaluated the differentially expressed genes by KEGG analysis. The KEGG analysis showed enrichment in biological processes associated with signal transduction and regulation of several oncogenes. Al- though our previous studies focused on the effects of HIF-2 in inflammatory responses and iron regulation in driving colitis (41) and colorectal cancer (43, 44), these signaling pathways impacted by HIF-2 suggest that these are also likely to contribute to these phenotypes. Recent studies from our labo- ratory showed that HIF-2 potentiated Yes-associated protein 1 (Yap1) expression and activity, and it increased colon cancer (21). Moreover, the role of *Pi3kca, Wnt8b,* and mTORC1 signaling pathways driving colorectal cancer are well docu- mented (2, 10, 28). These suggest that HIF-2 not only regulates inflammatory processes but also impacts cellular transduction pathways driving cell growth. The present data provide strong evidence that activation of intestinal HIF-2 is sufficient to induce inflammatory response and exacerbate experimental colitis. These findings also provide a unique resource of information for future studies aimed at identifying

signaling pathways affected by HIF-2 in driving colorectal cancer. Previously thought to be an undruggable target, several potential inhibitors of HIF-2 have been characterized and are in clinical trials for renal carcinoma (4, 45). Our data suggest that pharmacological inhibition of HIF-2 may be protective in colitis and colitis-associated colon cancers.
