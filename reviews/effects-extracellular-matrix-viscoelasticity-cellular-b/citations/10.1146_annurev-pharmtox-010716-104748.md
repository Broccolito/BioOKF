**INTRODUCTION**

A key challenge in the development of drugs is the need for a quantitative approach to predict the pharmacokinetic (PK) parameters that govern the absorption, distribution, metabolism, and excretion (ADME) of therapeutics. An effective PK model can determine appropriate doses to be administered when a drug enters clinical trials and whether a particular administration regimen is likely to achieve clinically effective plasma levels. Mathematical modeling of drug PK has evolved from simple compartmental models to distributed and multicompartment physiologically based PK (PBPK) models (1–3). In contrast to compartmental PK models that are typically described by drug concentration balance equations in different compartments, PBPK simulation tools represent the whole body as a network of individual organs that are connected via arterial and venous blood flow (Figure 1a). Construction of PBPK computational models requires estimation of numerous anatomic and physiological parameters, such as cardiac output, organ volumes, and rates of organ blood flow and lymphatic perfusion (1, 2, 4) as well as kinetic constants for equations governing drug ADME behaviors. Whereas physiological flow parameters are relatively well established for humans and animal models, the kinetic constants required for PBPK modeling must be obtained by measuring drug concentrations in plasma and tissues, and ideally at the site of drug action (which is usually not possible in humans). Parameters are sometimes estimated from studies with human cell lines in conventional cultures, but these cells usually fail to retain in vivo differentiated functions. More sophisticated microphysiological systems have been developed, including three-dimensional (3D) cultures of cells (5, 6) or organoids (7, 8), but they lack fluid flow—a critical feature of drug PK. Microfluidic devices that contain individual types of cells (e.g., lung or liver epithelium) cultured under flow within hollow microchambers also have been developed that can maintain higher levels of organ-specific functions in vitro (9–11). PBPK models have been developed using these mi- crofluidic culture devices by flowing medium between two different devices, each lined with one type of organ-specific parenchymal cell (e.g., liver cells in one and intestinal epithelial cells in the other) (12–14). **Bioavailability**

To evaluate new orally administered therapeutic agents, one requirement is that researchers must attain adequate systemic exposure after oral dosing (i.e., F—the fraction of a drug that reaches the systemic circulation intact) (20). A major determinant of oral bioavailability of a drug is its absorption across the small intestine. Drug formulation plays a large role in this, as the physical properties of the molecule are key determinants of absorption (21). However, other factors, includ- ing intestinal epithelial permeability, metabolism, motility, and active transport by efflux/influx transporters such as the permeability glycoprotein-1 (Pgp) transporter, play a large role as well (22). In addition to this first-pass effect due to intestinal absorption and metabolism, bioavailabil- ity is largely governed by hepatic clearance (CLhep) as well as route of administration (e.g, oral, IV, intraportal) (22, 23). Bioavailability is usually determined from studies with animals, typically rodents (see 24–27 for more details). However, animal models often fail to accurately estimate bioavailability in humans (24, 28), largely owing to species differences in body size and circulatory flow rates (25, 26) as well as liver and gut enzymes and transporters (27, 29) or plasma proteins (30).

#### PD Parameters

PD describes the mechanisms of drug action at the target site and quantifies the relationship between drug concentration and effect measured in terms of efficacy and potency. By combining the PK time course of drug concentration in the body with PD, one can define the time course of drug-target interaction kinetics and resulting pharmacological effects. Traditional PD studies use cellular assays to assess drug-target interactions based on measures of drug-target binding affinity and to derive mathematical models of dose-effect (D-E) relationships. Considering that the majority of drugs target a finite number of available cellular receptors, the rate of change of the drug-receptor complex can be described using saturable binding kinetics models in which D-E rela- tionships have been derived for specific drug target actions, such as enzyme inhibition/stimulation and agonist/antagonist actions (38, 39). The parameters for such D-E relationships are typically obtained using in vivo or in vitro drug titration assays. However, these equilibrium analyses are insufficient to predict drug efficacy and selectivity in vivo when the drug concentration at the target site changes over time due to blood flow and the binding process is far from equilibrium. #### PARAMETERS IN VITRO

Preclinical estimates of ADME parameters are commonly determined using static in vitro cul- ture (e.g., well) models. It is difficult to predict the dynamic nature of the human body on a particular drug dosing regimen with static conditions, which results in misleading data and po- tentially leads to drug failures in the clinical setting. Predictions based on use of static Caco2 and MDCK cell culture models to evaluate drugs are questionable. For example, to model oral absorption and bioavailability of drugs, it is important to mimic the permeability of the small intestine, where drugs are typically absorbed (22, 48). Intestinal permeability also can decrease owing to the presence of a mucus layer (46) and fluid shear (49, 50), neither of which are present in these static cultures. #### PBPK Modeling Using Microfluidic Models

Even the most advanced 3D organoid cultures or microphysi- ological systems that fail to reconstitute this physiological tissue-tissue boundary cannot properly replicate the kinetic changes of drug concentrations and cellular level drug-target interactions that occur within organs in vivo. Moreover, the presence of a semipermeable membrane between the vascular and epithelial channels facilitates analysis of drug transport and diffusion and hence is a more realistic way to model and measure drug distribution in an organ. Consequently, data obtained with two-channel vascularized Organ Chips are easier to incorpo- rate into PK models and other extrapolation methods. Also, because fluid is transferred between different vascularized Organ Chips via microchannels that are lined with a similar endothelium, design of a common perfusion medium is also much less of a challenge. Using the Human Body–on–Chips approach (Figure 3a), one can imagine administering an oral drug to the lumen of a Gut Chip, measuring its absorption into the vascular channel, quan- tifying its metabolism by the Liver Chip, exploring its clearance by a Kidney Chip, measuring its toxicity in another chip (e.g., Heart Chip), and, in the future, quantifying efficacy and potency in another Chip (perhaps a Bone Marrow Chip). The same approach could be taken by administering an aerosolized drug into the air space of the Lung Chip, placing a drug in a cream formulation on a Skin Chip, or injecting a drug IV through the vascular channel. Modeling whole-organism or human-level physiology via interconnected Organ Chips in vitro poses several challenges. Serial linkage of different microfluidic Organ Chips can be useful for preliminary analysis of organ interactions, particularly for physiologically relevant coupling of two or three organ systems, such as gut, liver, and kidney (Figure 4). However, the serial approach, which was used in the past for parenchymal-to-parenchymal compartment linking, faces serious drawbacks if scaled to larger numbers of organs. For example, serial linkage will result in drug loss with each organ linkage due to the dilution of medium, which results from sampling and subsequent medium replenishment, and potentially from absorption by the device materials, especially in chips made from polydimethylsiloxane (PDMS). Linking Organ Chips serially also can result in accumulation of errors from fluid handling of small volumes. Serial linkages may thus limit the experimentally tractable range of drug concentrations that can be analyzed, thereby complicating experimental design. As described below, an approach that better mimics how drugs enter the circulation can address some of these experimental challenges while simplifying PK/PD models and subsequent IVIVE. Computational modeling may be used to take into account loss into the chip materials, including PDMS, and thereby circumvent what has been thought to be an unsurmountable limitation of these types of materials (95, 96).

Extrapolation to clinically relevant in vivo parameters is crucial for understanding the value of any multiorgan human or animal Organ Chip system. Some studies have left out this step of extrapolations and analyzed only in vitro experimental data (i.e., focusing only on the PK of the in vitro system) (76, 99). Numerous methods have been proposed for scaling in vitro data to the in vivo setting. One method uses the exponential power relationship to scale organ function to body mass (4). The simplest method scales the experimental conditions so that they match the physiological system as closely as possible within the constraints of microfluidic design and process parameters (121). Perfusion of organs and relative organ masses also can be scaled using in vivo ratios and clinical measurements. This approach is complex to execute experimentally, as one must adjust each Organ Chip and interorgan connection. Changing conditions in a microfluidic setup with different cell types growing over a prolonged period (e.g., 1–4 weeks) is not straightforward and can be further complicated if larger numbers of Organ Chips are included in a fluidically linked model. Also, as allometric coefficients vary for each organ, this method has difficulty capturing limitations of in vitro models compared with their in vivo organ counterparts (4, 123). Computational approaches to model Organ Chip and Human Body–on–Chip systems and methods for cross-platform scaling have been suggested for PK/PD quantitation (96). Such physiologically inspired computational modeling can enable more robust extrapolation of in vitro on-chip outcomes to predict human responses and help optimize in vitro design configurations. Another approach scales organ sizes to their relative volumes and residence times (127). The underlying ideas are that the duration of exposure of cells to drugs in a predesigned culture chamber is similar to that observed in vivo, and that cells cultured in vitro mimic in vivo cell metabolism, so the conversion of both should be similar. This approach can be easy to use, but the origin of the cells could be a source of error; for example, organs (liver and lung) modeled in past studies were based on rat-derived cell lines (H4IIE hepatoma cells and L2 lung epithelial cells). #### FUTURE CONSIDERATIONS

Microfluidic cell culture devices and vascularized Organ Chips offer a potentially exciting new ap- proach to predict PK/PD properties in vitro. Vascularized microfluidic models that reconstitute physiological features and multicellular phenotypes are superior to typical cell lines, plate-based assays, and single-channel microfluidic devices that have been predominantly used until now. Computational abilities offer the capability to run more physiological experiments and to extrap- olate in vivo predictions of drug behaviors in humans from in vitro data obtained with linked vascularized human Organ Chips. This is very different from typical PBPK multiorgan linking, in which the cell compartments are scaled similarly to those in humans using various properties, such as volumetric flow distributions and tissue masses. However, Organ Chip technology re- quires better understanding of the kinetics and drug retention properties of elastomeric materials used to construct these devices. Alternative polymers with reduced drug absorption properties may prove helpful (114, 128). Finally, it will also be necessary to increase the throughput and robustness of this technology by mass-producing Organ Chips and automated instruments that will permit researchers and pharmaceutical scientists without engineering or modeling know-how to effortlessly run and analyze PK/PD experiments using this technology.
