### Author Manuscript Introduction

Macrolipophagy (hereafter, lipophagy) is a conserved mechanism for autophagy of lipid droplets (LDs) in mammals1–3, worms4, flies5, zebrafish6, yeast7, and plants and algae8. Although autophagy receptors exist for mitophagy and ERphagy, the selective receptor for lipophagy remains elusive. Autophagy receptors tether autophagic cargo to autophagosome-bound LC3 via cargo-binding domains and LC3-interacting region (LIR) motifs, respectively; and blocking these interactions prevents cargo engulfment by

#### VPS4A, but not VPS4B, engages with LC3 on LDs to regulate lipophagy.

To distinguish whether VPS4A and/or VPS4B regulate lipophagy, we used super-resolution microscopy to track their colocalization with LC3 and LDs in OA-treated AML12 hepatocytes (Figure 2C). Intriguingly, VPS4A and VPS4B exhibited differences in their association with the LC3-LD complex, in that, VPS4A showed numerous instances of concomitant colocalization with LC3 and LDs reflected by triple-colocalized VPS4A-LC3- BODIPY493/503 pixels on LDs (white arrowheads in Figure 2C, top). In contrast, VPS4B Author Manuscriptonly colocalized with a pool of LC3 not associated with LDs (Figure 2C, bottom). This led to our hypothesis that engagement of VPS4A with LC3 on LDs is reflective of its regulation of lipophagy, while VPS4B modulates LD content via a mechanism independent of LC3/autophagosomes.

Interestingly, and consistent with this hypothesis, structured illumination/3D reconstruction revealed that VPS4A appears to tether LC3/autophagosomes with LDs (Figure 2D top, Movie S1, S2; white arrowheads indicate VPS4A-LD association; yellow arrowheads indicate VPS4A-LC3 association), while VPS4B associates with LC3 alone (Figure 2D **bottom, Movie S1, S2; yellow arrowheads indicate VPS4B-LC3 association). Consistently,** silencing Vps4a or Atg7 each substantially reduced the colocalization of LC3 with BODIPY493/503 and increased LD content, reflecting lipophagy blockage (Figure 2E), while Vps4b loss led to LD accumulation without affecting its sequestration by LC3

#### Pulse-chase experiments confirm the role of VPS4A in lysosomal lipolysis.

To confirm that VPS4A is required for lysosomal LD breakdown, we used LipiDye-M, a C12-fatty acid-based metabolic tracer, which allows temporal quantification of lipolysis. LipiDye-M incorporates a polarity-sensitive solvatochromic 3a-azapyren-4-one dye whose fluorescence depends on the polarity of the solvent/milieu (e.g., green fluorescence in hydrophilic cytosol; yellow in amphipathic membranes/organelles; and red in hydrophobic LDs) (Figure 3A). Upon pulsing AML12 hepatocytes with OA for 5 hours (h) followed by 1 h LipiDye-M staining and then chasing in absence of OA for 12 h (Figure 3B), we observed that while control cells completely deplete their red fluorescent LDs by 12 h, represented by increased GFP/RFP ratio and reduced LD area (% of cell area) (RFP+GFP− area/cell), siVps4a cells resist lipolysis indicated by retention of red fluorescence (Figure 3A–3C).

**liver.** We then sought to test if silencing Vps4a results in lipophagy failure in mouse liver. ### Discussion

Herein, we demonstrate that VPS4A satisfies each criteria for a selective lipophagy receptor. First, we show that VPS4A interacts with both LD and LC3, and using 3D reconstruction, demonstrate that VPS4A and LDs are degraded concomitantly in lysosomes—a hallmark of autophagy receptors. We show that VPS4A utilizes its N-terminal MIT domain residues Thr3-Thr5-Lys6 and its LIR motifs 124IRWNDV129/147IKFPHL152 for LD and LC3 binding, respectively, and that targeting these blocks lipophagy. Consistent with the notion that phosphorylation prior to an LIR motif favors receptor-LC3 interaction10,17, we show that VPS4AS95,S97 phosphorylation is required for VPS4A-LC3 binding and lipophagy. Like Author Manuscript other defined autophagy receptors9–11, loss of Atg7 led to accumulation of VPS4A, but not VPS4B, confirming that VPS4A is an autophagy receptor. Importantly, VPS4A is not a core autophagy component, and its loss had no effect on the number of LC3 puncta or LAMP2 vesicles or generic autophagy flux. Finally, using organelle-specific reporters, we show that VPS4A selectively regulates lipophagy but not mitophagy or ERphagy. SPARTIN was reported as a lipophagy reporter25; however, SPARTIN fails to accumulate in Atg7KO livers, indicating that SPARTIN is not the lipophagy receptor. ORP8 (oxysterol-binding protein- related protein) was also proposed as a lipophagy receptor44; however, ORP8 is localized in ER45 and ER-mitochondria/plasma membrane contacts where it regulates LD biogenesis, phospholipid transport46,47 and mitochondrial function48. Hence, ORP8’s selectivity for lipophagy remains undetermined.

### Author Manuscript

Although the ATPase-deficient VPS4A (VPS4AE228Q) exerts a dominant-negative phagophore closure defect, silencing Vps4a has no effect on autophagosome closure29. Indeed, our time-lapse and TEM studies exclude any role of VPS4A in autophagosome closure, and show that it is the ESCRT-III complex protein CHMP1B, which is required for autophagosome closure. In fact, the VPS4A AAA+ ATPase mutant, which lacks ATP binding, maintains its ability to drive lipophagy. Studies have consistently shown that VPS4 paralogs recycle membrane ESCRT-III complex26–31, and that defects in phagophore closure or MVB/ILV biogenesis are largely due to loss of the ESCRT-III complex32–34 and not VPS4A, as we also demonstrate here.

Since loss of VPS4A and VPS4B each led to marked LD accumulation, we investigated

### Author Manuscript

whether VPS4 paralogs regulate LD degradation via two distinct acidic organelles. Interestingly, depletion of VPS4A selectively blocked LD degradation in LAMP2+ lysosomes; while VPS4B loss blocked LD degradation in RAB7+ endosomes21–23, likely related to microautophagy. This suggests a divergence in the role of VPS4 paralogs in LD catabolism through distinct acidic organelles, and despite the high homology between both mammalian paralogs, it is VPS4A phosphorylation that stimulates LC3 binding and tethering of LDs to autophagosomes for lipophagy.

Previous studies have shown roles for various kinases, including TBK110 and CK215,17, in driving starvation-induced mitophagy and ERphagy through receptor phosphorylation. Consistently, kinase prediction for VPS4A phosphorylation indicates a role of CK2 in supporting lipophagy, as has been shown for other autophagy forms through phosphorylation

### Author Manuscript

of ATG3215 and TEX26417. Taken together, we propose that VPS4A is a selective receptor for lipophagy in mouse and human liver. Revealing the selectivity factor for hepatic lipophagy will allow its targeted modulation to prevent human MASLD where defective lipophagy is implicated3.

#### Limitations of the Study

Despite rigorous validation that VPS4A serves as a lipophagy receptor, there are unresolved questions that will need addressing. First, the differential roles of both VPS4 paralogs need to be dissected further, in particular, the role of VPS4B in LD homeostasis. Second, the pathophysiological relevance of VPS4A to preclinical MASLD/MASH models requires investigation. Finally, in-depth testing of the predicted kinase in fasting-induced VPS4A phosphorylation and lipophagy is required.
