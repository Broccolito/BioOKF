The endosomal sorting complex required for transport (ESCRT) is an evolutionarily conserved protein machinery that plays a critical role in membrane remodeling events (1) including multivesicular body (MVB) formation and extracellular vesicle (EV) biogenesis (2–4), autophagy (5–7), viral budding (8), cytokinesis (9), nuclear envelope maintenance (10, 11), and membrane repair (12–14). The ESCRT machinery consists of four complexes—ESCRT-0, -I, -II, and -III with asso ciated proteins, such as ALG2-interacting protein X (ALIX) and VPS4A/B. ESCRT-0,-I, and -II play a central role in cargo sorting and in the recruitment and activation of ESCRT-III, which is directly involved in the reshaping and severing membranes. In mammals, CHMP1-7 and IST1 proteins form heterogeneous ESCRT-III complexes composed of different combinations of CHMP1-7 and IST1 which function at distinct sites. ESCRT-III complexes are capable of forming polymers that drive membrane defor mation. The AAA ATPase VPS4 binds to ESCRT- + III subunits and disassembles ESCRT-III via adenosine triphosphate (ATP) hydrolysis, generating the force for mem brane scission (15, 16). ESCRT-III proteins can bind to membranes to mediate both reverse-topology membrane remodeling which facilitates vesicle budding away from the cytosol and normal-topology membrane remodeling promoting vesicle budding toward the cytosol (17). While the majority of ESCRT-III proteins assemble into different structures on the membrane with a reverse topology, the IST1-CHMP1B complex can coat positively curved membranes to facilitate normal-topology membrane deformation (18–20). IST1 plays an important role in endosomal tubulation (21, 22). However, IST1 and CHMP1B are also present at sites of reverse-topology membrane scission. The distinct functions of the IST1-CHMP1B complex in various membrane processes remain unclear due to an inability to selectively modulating individual ESCRT complexes during mem brane deformation. Autophagy is an evolutionarily conserved catabolic process in eukaryotic cells. A hallmark of this process is the conjugation of ATG8 proteins to phospholipids on double- vesicles known as autophagosomes. These autophagosomes then fuse with lysosomes to membraned facilitate the degradation of intravesicular cargo. Emerging evidence shows that the autophagy machinery also plays a role in several autophagy-independent processes. During these pro cesses, ATG8s are often conjugated to single-membrane compartments such as phagosomes and endolysosomes (23–32). This noncanonical lipidation of ATG8s occurs independent of certain components of the core autophagy machinery. **Significance**

A group of proteins, the endosomal sorting complex required for transport (ESCRT), plays a crucial role in membrane remodeling—a cellular process involving the rearrangement of cellular membranes. Understanding and controlling specific parts of ESCRT is challenging due to its complex organization. Here, we describe a natural product-like compound Tantalosin that specifically disrupts a particular part of ESCRT called the IST1-CHMP1B complex. Using Tantalosin as a chemical tool, we examine how this complex contributes to different membrane remodeling events. Interestingly, Tantalosin induces noncanonical LC3 lipidation—a process involving noncanonical autophagy (self-eating)—at stalled endosomes. This finding further enriches our understanding of ESCRT function and highlights the importance of small molecules as valuable tools for unraveling complex biological mechanisms.

**PNP Tantalosin Targets the ESCRT-III Component IST1.** Phenotypic high-content screening for small-molecule modulators of EGFP- LC3 lipidation (40, 41) led to the discovery of macrocyclic PNPs that induce LC3 lipidation in cells (42). PNPs are synthesized as an unprecedented combination of natural product fragments, providing inherent biological activity in a chemical space not attainable by nature (43). Tantalosin-I (hereafter referred as Tanta losin) was chosen for subsequent investigation (Fig. 1A). To identify potential protein targets of Tantalosin, we performed

throughput approach based on thermal protein profiling with deep proteome coverage and multiplexed quantitative analysis (44). The PISA assay facilitates target deconvolution and mechanism of action elucidation by measuring changes of proteomic solubility upon short (30 to 90 min) incubations of compounds with cells. HEK293T cells were treated with 4 µM Tantalosin, 4 µM inactive compound (hereafter referred as C803) or vehicle (DMSO) for 1 h. C803, chosen from the same compound class as Tantalosin, lacked the ability to induce LC3 lipidation (SI Appendix, Fig. S1 A and B). We analyzed proteome-wide shifts in solubility of samples treated with Tantalosin, comparing them to C803 or DMSO. In both instances, the ESCRT-III component IST1 emerged as a top candidate that exhibited consistent and substantial engagement (Fig. 1 B and C). Treatment of HeLa cells with Tantalosin led to a time-dependent accumulation of intracellular TfR, indicating an endosomal recycling defect (Fig. 3A). Notably, IST1 colocalized with TfR, suggesting that IST1 is recruited to stalled endosomes but is incapable of performing scission (Fig. 3A). **Tantalosin Induces LC3 Lipidation on Stalled Sorting Endosomes.** In a previous study, we screened a family of 20-membered macrocyclic PNPs in an autophagy induction assay which identified Tantalosin as the most robust inducer of LC3 lipidation (42). To explore a potential correlation between the inhibition of endosomal recycling and LC3 lipidation, we cotreated MCF7 cells stably expressing EGFP-LC3 with fluorescently labeled transferrin and Tantalosin. Interestingly, Tantalosin-induced LC3 accumulated at endosomes marked with Tf-555 (Fig. 4 A and B). This observation was further validated by costaining for endogenous LC3 and TfR in Tantalosin-treated HeLa cells (Fig. 4C). Immunostaining for IST1 confirmed the colocalization of Tf-555, LC3 and IST1 within the same compartment following Tantalosin-mediated recycling inhibition (Fig. 4D). This indicates that impaired IST1 function on stalled sorting endosomes may promote the conjugation of LC3 onto endosomal membranes. Intriguingly, IST1 depletion, previously shown to enhance endosomal tubulation (21), did not lead to LC3 accumulation (SI Appendix, Fig. S9 A and B). This suggests that assembly of CHMP1B-binding deficient IST1 on endosomes may be a prerequisite for LC3 lipidation, rather than impairment of endosomal recycling per se. To further examine the causality between LC3 lipidation and endocytic recycling defects, we used LC3 lipidation-deficient ATG7 knock-out (KO) cells. Robust accumulation of transferrin was observed in ATG7 KO cells treated with Tantalosin (SI Appendix,

Fig. S10), suggesting that LC3 lipidation does not promote the

endocytic recycling defect observed during Tantalosin treatment. Altogether, these data confirm that Tantalosin induces LC3 lipi dation on stalled sorting endosomes, a process associated with aberrant accumulation of IST1 on endosomal membranes.

**Tantalosin Induces V-ATPase -Dependent Noncanonical LC3** **Lipidation.** LC3 lipidation is considered a hallmark of canonical autophagy—a lysosome-dependent catabolic pathway used for intracellular cargo degradation. In our previous study, we observed that inhibition of autophagosome-lysosome fusion does not result in an additive accumulation of lipidated LC3 in Tantalosin-treated cells (42). Therefore, Tantalosin specifically inhibits the formation of the IST1-CHMP1B complex on membranes, while preserving the interaction between IST1 and other ESCRT components. Consequently, our findings establish Tantalosin as a chemical genetic tool for investigating the function of the IST1-CHMP1B complex in diverse biological processes. Perturbation of the ESCRT-III complexes has been achieved by genetic depletion of its components or through overexpression of mutant VPS4 (E228Q) (59). Due to the sophisticated organization of the ESCRT complex, depletion of individual components could potentially interfere with complex assembly, leading to global inhi bition of ESCRT-dependent processes. For example, knockdown of IST1 not only disrupts the IST1-CHMP1B complex but also prevents the recruitment of VPS4, MITD1, and spastin. As a result, IST1 knockdown simultaneously causes defects in cytokinesis, endosomal tubulation, and autophagy (7, 21, 48, 49). Tantalosin enables us to rapidly and precisely inhibit the IST1-CHMP1B complex. We demonstrate that the normal-topology membrane scission mediated by the IST1-CHMP1B copolymer is dispensable for cytokinesis, MVB sorting, and EV biogenesis, as these processes are driven by a reverse-topology membrane scission. The role of IST1 in cytokinesis may be largely attributed to the recruitment and activation of other ESCRT components such as AAA + ATPase VPS4 via the MIT-MIM motif interaction (48, 50). Using Tantalosin as a tool, we demonstrate that IST1-CHMP1B copolymer formation plays an essential role in endosomal recycling. Shown previously to drive membrane tubulation, constriction and thinning (19, 46), the IST1-CHMP1B copolymer may further coat endosomal tubes and collaborate with another AAA + ATPase spastin to facilitate membrane scission (21, 22). Investigating the effect of Tantalosin on other membrane processes, such as lipid droplet transportation to peroxisomes or peroxisome scission from the endoplasmic reticulum will provide insights into the role of the IST1-CHMP1B complex in these processes (60, 61). Tantalosin’s specificity toward inhibiting the IST1-CHMP1B complex and disrupting endosomal recycling highlights its poten tial to target receptors and adhesion molecules essential for cancer development and progression (62). Previously, an endosomal recy cling inhibitor primaquine was shown to interfere with EGFR endosomal trafficking and effectively inhibit proliferation of breast cancer cells (63–65). Expanding the repertoire of chemical mod ulators targeting the endosomal recycling pathway with distinct mechanisms of action can be beneficial for the development of novel anticancer therapeutics. Tantalosin was initially identified as an inducer of LC3 lipidation. We expand upon this finding to show that Tantalosin induces non canonical LC3 lipidation on stalled sorting endosomes containing TfR. Tantalosin-induced LC3 lipidation is dependent on the ATG conjugation systems but occurs independently of FIP200 and ATG9A, both of which are required for canonical autophagy initia tion and autophagosome formation. Furthermore, Tantalosin-induced LC3 lipidation is not sensitive to autophagosome-lysosome inhibi tion, suggesting that LC3 is not conjugated to autophagosomes and serves a lysosome-independent function. Noncanonical LC3 lipidation has been shown to be involved in LAP and LC3-associated endocytosis (LANDO) (23, 26, 66). Rubicon is a negative regulator of autophagy and was found to be essential for both LAP and LANDO (56). However, Rubicon is dispensable for Tantalosin-induced LC3 lipidation, suggesting that Tantalosin induces a CASM process distinct from LAP and LANDO. V-ATPase serves as a common regulator of noncanonical LC3

lipidation by recruiting the E3-like ATG5-ATG12-ATG16L1 complex to membranes (33, 34). We show that Tantalosin-induced LC3 lipidation is dependent on the V-ATPase -ATG16L1 axis, consistent with the induction of CASM. While our data demonstrate that Tantalosin treatment results in an IST1-dependent endosomal recycling defect, and that stalled endosomes become decorated in lipidated LC3, we cannot fully exclude the possibility that Tantalosin-induced CASM occurs independent of the inhibition of the IST1-CHMP1B complex. We also show that the stalled endosomes are not targeted for degradation by macroautophagy or lysosomes (Fig. 5 A and B and *SI Appendix, Fig. S11). The exact function and mechanism of LC3* conjugation to the stalled endosomes remain to be determined. In summary, we report a chemical inhibitor targeting the IST1- CHMP1B interaction within the ESCRT-III complexes. This PNP compound specifically inhibits the IST1-CHMP1B complex-mediated normal-topology membrane scission and induces noncanonical LC3 lipidation on stalled sorting endosomes. Tantalosin will serve as an invaluable tool to investigate the IST1-CHMP1B complex functions in distinct membrane processes and facilitate new insights into the function of noncanonical LC3 lipidation.

### Materials and Methods

**PISA Assay.** HEK293T cells were grown in fifteen 25 cm2 flasks at 37 °C in 5% CO2 using Dulbecco’s Modified Eagle Medium (Lonza) supplemented with 10% FBS (Gibco, Thermo Fisher Scientific), 2 mM l-glutamine (Lonza), and 100 units/ mL penicillin/streptomycin (Gibco, Thermo Fisher Scientific). After reaching a 70% confluence, flasks were treated for 1 h with 4 µM of Tantalosin, 4 µM of the inactive compound, or the same volume of DMSO. For each condition, five flasks were treated, each representing a biological/experimental replicate of each cell culture treatment. A pool composed by the fifteenth part of each flask was made and processed according to protocols including detergents for stronger protein extraction, and not to the PISA assay, to provide a 16th sample included as carrier proteome in the TMTpro 16-plex for improving detection and quantification of low abundant peptides and proteins across the fifteen PISA samples. All PISA samples were processed together, according to the published protocol (44) optimized for higher proteome depth and 16-plex multiplicity using Tandem Mass Tag (TMT) pro (ThermoFisher) and using a gradient for thermal denatura- tion as described previously (67, 68). Details were described in SI Appendix, SI Materials and Methods. The dataset is listed in Dataset S1.

**HDX-MS.** A volume of 3 μL of 70 μM purified recombinant IST1 (see Protein expression and purification in SI  Appendix) was mixed with 7 μL of 10 μM Tantalosin (21 μM of IST1 and 7 μM of Tantalosin in the mixture) and incubated at 4 °C overnight. 1% DMSO was used as control instead of Tantalosin. A volume of 4 μL of incubation solution was mixed with 24 μL of stock buffer (50 mM Tris at pH 8.0 including 300 mM NaCl) in D2O for 15 s, 150 s, 600 s, and 1,500 s. After adding 25 μL of ice-cold 100 mM phosphate pH 2.3 and 3.3 M urea, the samples were analyzed in a semiautomated HDX-MS system in which manually injected samples were automatically digested, cleaned, and separated at 4 °C. Samples were digested using enzymate BEH pepsin column (WATERS) followed by a 6 min desalting step using 0.2% FA at 60 μL/min. Peptic peptides were then separated by a 21 mm I. D × 50-mm length C18/3 μm column (Thermo Fisher Scientific) using a 20 min/5 to 90% acetonitrile gradient in 0.2% formic acid at 60 μL/min. An Orbitrap Q-Exactive mass spectrometer (Thermo Fisher Scientific) operated at 60,000 resolution at m/z 200 was used for analysis. The HDExaminer software (Sierra Analytics, USA) was used to process all HDX-MS data.

**MST Measurement.** Binding affinity measurement of proteins was performed using a Nanotemper Monolith NT.115 instrument. MST experiments were per- formed in assay buffer [50 mM Tris, pH 8.0, 150 mM NaCl, 0.05% Tween 20, and 1 mM dithiothreitol (DTT)] using 80 nM FITC-labeled IST1 or 50 nM FAM-labeled MIM1 mixed with different concentrations of proteins in MST assay buffer. The cap- illary was excited at 480 nm. Dissociation constants were calculated by fitting the changes of fluorescence intensity to a 1:1 binding equation using Nanotemper

**Transferrin Pulse–Chase Assay.** Cells were treated with serum-free medium at 37 °C for 1 h in the presence or absence of Tantalosin to hold receptors at the plasma membrane. Then, 25 μg/mL of Transferrin from Human Serum, Alexa Fluor™ 555 Conjugate (T35352, Invitrogen), was added to medium, and cells were placed on ice for 30 min. After quick wash, medium was changed to the imaging phenol-free medium with or without Tantalosin, and live-cell confocal imaging was immediately started. For VEH and Tantalosin-treated cells, all imag- ing conditions were kept identical. Protein expression and purification, peptide synthesis, evaluation of cytokinesis, EGFR degradation assay, EV purification and nanoparticle tracking analysis, cell culture, western blot, confocal microscopy, and image analysis were described in SI Appendix, SI Materials and Methods.
