# SUN1 facilitates CHMP7 nuclear influx and injury cascades in sporadic amyotrophic

# lateral sclerosis

### 1,2 1,2 1,2 1,2

## Victoria Baskerville, Sampath Rapuri, Emma Mehlhop and Alyssa N. Coyne

We have recently identified the aberrant nuclear accumulation of the ESCRT-III protein CHMP7 as an initiating event that leads to a significant injury to the nuclear pore complex (NPC) characterized by the reduction of specific nucleo porins from the neuronal NPC in sporadic amyotrophic lateral sclerosis (sALS) and C9orf72 ALS/frontotemporal dementia (FTD)-induced pluripotent stem cell-derived neurons (iPSNs), a phenomenon also observed in post-mortem patient tissues. Importantly, this NPC injury is sufficient to contribute to TDP-43 dysfunction and mislocalization, a common pathological hallmark of neurodegenerative diseases. However, the molecular mechanisms and events that give rise to increased nuclear translocation and/or retention of CHMP7 to initiate this pathophysiological cascade re main largely unknown. Here, using an iPSN model of sALS, we demonstrate that impaired NPC permeability barrier integrity and interactions with the LINC complex protein SUN1 facilitate CHMP7 nuclear localization and the subse quent ‘activation’ of NPC injury cascades. Collectively, our data provide mechanistic insights in the pathophysiologic al underpinnings of ALS/FTD and highlight SUN1 as a potent contributor to and modifier of CHMP7-mediated toxicity in sALS pathogenesis.

Mechanistically, we have recently demonstrated that the aber rant nuclear accumulation of the ESCRT-III protein charged multi vesicular body protein 7 or chromatin modifying protein 7 (CHMP7) is sufficient to trigger NPC injury cascades, culminating in TDP-43 dysfunction and impaired neuronal survival in sALS. 15

Confirmatory evidence of this phenomenon in post-mortem hu man CNS tissues 14,15,36 implicates the CHMP7/ESCRT-III nuclear surveillance pathway and subsequent NPC injury as a significant contributor to sALS pathogenesis. Importantly, we have demon strated that antisense oligonucleotide (ASO)-mediated reduction in CHMP7 protein can alleviate NPC injury cascades and restore TDP-43 function, highlighting the therapeutic potential of this pathway in ALS. 15 However, the molecular mechanism(s) that fa cilitate increased nuclear localization of CHMP7 to initiate NPC in jury cascades in ALS neurons remains unknown and is critical to our understanding of ALS/FTD pathogenesis. Here, using an iPSN model of sALS, we demonstrate that im paired NPC permeability barrier integrity leads to increased passive nuclear influx of CHMP7 thereby contributing to observed nuclear accumulation in sALS neurons. Moreover, the LINC complex pro tein SUN1 appears to facilitate the nuclear influx of CHMP7 and the initiation of CHMP7-mediated NPC injury cascades via modula tion of the passive NPC permeability barrier and subsequent perhaps transient interactions with CHMP7 itself. Critically, genetic or pharmacological knockdown of SUN1 is sufficient to mitigate CHMP7 nuclear influx and alleviate the activation of CHMP7-mediated NPC injury and downstream TDP-43 dysfunction in sALS iPSNs. Intriguingly, SUN1 also appears to later become a tar get of CHMP7-mediated nuclear injury itself in sALS iPSNs. Thus, these data highlight SUN1 as a significant CHMP7 interactor and critical contributor to CHMP7-mediated nuclear injury cascades in sALS and suggest that SUN1–CHMP7 interaction may be a viable target for therapeutic intervention.

## Results

### Impaired nuclear pore complex permeability barrier integrity contributes to nuclear influx of CHMP7

Within the central channel of the NPC, FG repeat-containing Nups function to create a size-selective diffusion barrier that restricts the passive nuclear influx and efflux of macromolecules larger than about 40–60 kDa. 23,28,37-39 Under basal cellular conditions, the ∼56 kDa ESCRT-III protein resides predominantly in the cyto plasm. During times of NPC and nuclear envelope surveillance and homeostasis, CHMP7 passively diffuses into the nucleus and nuclear envelope space to ‘activate’ ESCRT-III nuclear sur veillance pathways. 40-46 As a result, we hypothesized that im paired NPC permeability barrier integrity may contribute to increased nuclear localization of CHMP7 we have previously ob served in sALS neurons. 15 To test this, we performed digitonin permeabilized cell assays47 to selectively permeabilize the plas ma membrane of iPSNs and leave the nuclear membrane intact. We observed a significant increase in 70 and 500 kDa dextran fluorescence within the nucleus of sALS iPSNs (Fig. 1A–C). This was abrogated by preincubation with the lectin wheat germ ag glutinin (WGA), which binds to FG repeats that line the central channel of the NPC, thereby blocking passive diffusion through the NPC 48,49 (Fig. 1A–C). Given that both 70 and 500 kDa fluores cent dextran molecules should normally be excluded from the nucleus due to size, 28,39,47,50 this suggested that the permeability barrier of the NPC was altered in sALS iPSNs. We next asked whether disruptions to the NPC permeability barrier influenced the passive diffusion of CHMP7 into sALS iPSN nuclei. To avoid increasing the size of CHMP7 via the addition of bulky fluorescent tags, we conjugated recombinant CHMP7 protein with a small Alexa Fluor 488 dye, thereby largely maintaining the size of CHMP7 protein. Digitonin permeabilized cell assays employ ing Alexa Fluor 488 labelled CHMP7 protein revealed a significant increase in nuclear fluorescence in permeabilized sALS iPSNs (Fig. 1D and E) that was once again diminished by the addition of WGA (Fig. 1D and E). Collectively, these data suggest that increased ‘leakiness’ of the NPC permeability barrier contributes to increased nuclear influx of CHMP7 in sALS neurons.

In non-neuronal mammalian cells, the inner nuclear membrane protein LEMD2 recruits and facilitates the activity of CHMP7 for nu clear surveillance and nuclear envelope resealing events. 40,41,44,46
