**INTRODUCTION**

Macrophages play key roles during mammalian development and exhibit important homeostatic activity in nearly all organ systems of the body by producing growth factors and other mediators that provide trophic support to the tissues in which they reside (1). Resident tissue macrophages along with monocytes recruited from the bone marrow are also important drivers of inflammatory and tissue regenerative responses that develop in response to organ damage induced by infection, autoimmune disorders, mechanical or toxic injuries, and various other causes (2). Following tissue injury, the basement membrane is further disrupted by macrophage-derived matrix metallopro- teinases, which help facilitate the movement of inflammatory cells to the site of tissue injury. They also produce chemotactic factors that guide the initial recruitment of inflammatory cells

(3). Macrophages also function as scavenger cells and phagocytize the cellular debris, invading organisms, neutrophils, and other apoptotic cells that are present following tissue injury (4, 5). Consequently, if macrophage recruitment or activation is disrupted following tissue injury, the early inflammatory response is often diminished (6). However, impaired macrophage recruitment and activation may also reduce wound debridement and prolong exposure to proinflammatory stimuli, which can lead to incomplete tissue regeneration (7). Therefore, the macrophage repair response must be well-orchestrated following tissue injury. In addition to supporting the initial inflammatory response following injury, local tissue macrophages and recruited monocytes respond to stimuli found in the local milieu and quickly transition to a wound repair phenotype. Wound repair macrophages are characterized by the en- hanced production of numerous growth factors, including platelet-derived (PDGF), insulin-like (IGF-1), and vascular endothelial (VEGF-α) growth factors that promote cell proliferation and blood vessel development, which together help alleviate the local hypoxia that develops following injury (8–12). They also produce soluble mediators such as TGF-β1 that stimulate local and re- cruited tissue fibroblasts to differentiate into myofibroblasts that facilitate wound contraction and closure as well as the synthesis of extracellular matrix components (13). The proliferation and ex- pansion of many neighboring parenchymal and stromal cells are also augmented by macrophages, and if the injury is severe or chronic, macrophages may activate stem cell and local progenitor cell populations to participate in the tissue repair response (14). In the final stages of tissue repair, monocytes and macrophages assume an anti-inflammatory or proresolving phenotype (15). These macrophages respond to interleukin-10 (IL-10) and other in- hibitory mediators found in the local microenvironment, preferentially secrete anti-inflammatory mediators such as IL-10 and TGF-β1, and express cell surface receptors such as PD-L2 that dampen the immune system and ameliorate the inflammation that if not controlled effectively, can lead to collateral cell death and prolong or significantly impair the repair process (16–19). In some tissues they may also replace or supplement the resident tissue macrophage population. Thus, the three major stages of tissue repair must be carefully regulated, with monocyte and resident tissue macrophages playing unique and critical roles at each stage (2). The unique contributions of resident tissue macrophages versus recruited monocytes have become an important area of research, as some studies have suggested that the different monocyte and macrophage populations play unique and nonredundant roles in inflammation, tissue repair, and fibrosis (24, 25). ##### MACROPHAGES IN LIVER INJURY, REPAIR, AND FIBROSIS

The liver is the most regenerative organ in the body. When the liver is injured, Kupffer cells, the resident tissue macrophages of the liver, become activated and exhibit proinflammatory, regener- ative, and proresolving activities at different times during the repair response (2).

##### A Macrophage Phenotypic Switch Regulates the Initiation and Resolution

##### of Liver Injury

##### Macrophage-Derived Mediators Initiate Liver Inflammation and Fibrosis

Given that inflammatory macrophages can exacerbate chronic liver disease, a deeper understand- ing of the mechanisms by which macrophages promote inflammation and fibrosis might lead to novel strategies to slow disease progression. For example, in chronic hepatitis C virus (HCV) infection, sustained IL-1β production by resident Kupffer cells promotes liver injury and fibro- sis through HCV-induced inflammasome signaling (41). RNA sequencing studies suggest that viral engagement of the NLRP3 inflammasome directly triggers MyD88-mediated TLR7 sig- naling to induce IL-1β mRNA expression, with NLRP3 inflammasome activation processing IL-1β for secretion. The secreted IL-1β then stimulates proinflammatory cytokine, chemokine, and immunoregulatory gene expression that drives HCV disease severity and fibrosis. Therefore, therapeutic strategies that limit IL-1β expression or NLRP3 activation in macrophages could help mitigate the inflammation and fibrotic tissue remodeling that commonly develops during persistent viral infection. In addition to serving as key sources of proinflammatory and profibrotic mediators, inflamma- tory macrophages also promote tissue remodeling and fibrosis by directly enhancing the survival and activation of myofibroblasts, the key extracellular matrix-producing cells of the body. ##### Regulatory Macrophages Suppress Inflammation and Slow the Progression

##### of Fibrosis

Vannella and colleagues (25) identified distinct roles for resident and recruited alternatively acti- vated macrophages in the progression and resolution of schistosomiasis-induced liver fibrosis, a disease found in tropical countries that is characterized by severe granulomatous inflammation and development of hepatic fibrosis. In studies conducted with cell-specific IL-4Rα-deficient mice, F4/80 + CD11b + Lyz2 hi IL-4Rα + mature tissue macrophages activated by the type 2 cytokines IL-4 and IL-13 were identified as the primary population limiting inflammation in the liver during chronic infection, whereas F4/80 + CD11b + Lyz lo Arg1 + monocytes were largely responsible for the resolution of fibrosis. As such, these studies identified distinct roles for different populations of alternatively activated monocytes and macrophages [M(IL-4)] in the pathogenesis of schisto- somiasis. Mechanistic studies suggested that the M(IL-4) monocytes and macrophages are highly metabolically active and quickly consume critical amino acids in the injury site, which slows the progression of fibrosis because local CD4 + T cell proliferation and myofibroblast activation are suppressed (43, 44). Indeed, nutrient depletion by activated macrophages likely serves as a po- tent immunosuppressive mechanism in many chronic inflammatory and infectious diseases (44–

49). Kupffer cells and other resident tissue macrophages are also believed to exhibit important regulatory activity in the liver during acute liver failure (ALF). During acetaminophen-induced ALF (AALF), a marked increase in inflammatory macrophages is observed in the liver. However, the number of circulating monocytes is generally reduced in AALF, with the lowest numbers observed in patients exhibiting the poorest outcomes (50). In ALF, the resident tissue macrophages are thought to quickly convert to a proresolution tissue repair phenotype following injury, so expanding their numbers through local proliferation or recruitment from the periphery may improve survival. In support of this hypothesis, colony-stimulating factor 1-Fc treatment was recently found to expand the number of macrophages exhibiting a reparative anti-inflammatory phenotype in AALF and in mice undergoing partial hepatectomy, resulting in improved outcomes in both settings (51). Thus, proresolving macrophages could be exploited therapeutically to treat a variety of chronic liver diseases (33, 38, 52–54).

##### Regulating Inflammation and Collateral Tissue Damage in the Central

##### Nervous System

Although microglia, the resident tissue macrophages of the brain, are known to play critical roles during postnatal development of the central nervous system (CNS) (1), they also exhibit

important homeostatic activity and serve as sentinel cells when the brain is injured (55, 56). Recent studies have suggested that microglia primarily self-renew (57, 58), with IL-1R signaling playing a critical role in their turnover in the CNS (59). Moreover, as seen in many other organs, accumulating evidence suggests that microglia exhibit both pathogenic and protective activity in the brain following injury and control the progression of many chronic diseases of the CNS, including Alzheimer’s disease (AD) and multiple sclerosis (MS). For example, following a stroke or other traumatic brain injury, the early inflammatory response activates the vascular endothelium, resulting in breaches to the blood-brain barrier. Postischemic inflammation can also exacerbate brain ischemia-reperfusion injury, with microglia and inflammatory monocytes playing key pathogenic roles by producing proinflammatory mediators and reactive oxygen and nitrogen species that promote neuronal cell death, resulting in significant collateral tissue damage. Activation of the NLRP3 inflammasome in resident microglia by amyloid-beta peptides also drives IL-1β-driven neuroinflammation in AD, with NLPR3 blockade providing significant protection by skewing microglia to a reparative anti-inflammatory phenotype (60). Recently, Shichita and colleagues (61) identified peroxiredoxin (Prx) family proteins released from necrotic brain tissue as danger signals that trigger TLR2- and TLR4-dependent IL-23 production by macrophages, which drives the type 1 inflammation that can augment neural cell death. Blocking Prx proteins early after injury with neutralizing mAbs was found to suppress in- flammatory cytokine production and reduce lesion formation in the brain, suggesting that anti-Prx antibodies could be developed as neuroprotective agents for ischemic brain injury. Extracellular matrix protein matrillin-2 (MATN2) was also recently identified as an endogenous danger signal in the CNS. Mice that are deficient in MATN2 are more resistant to the induction of experi- mental autoimmune encephalomyelitis (EAE), with reductions in disease severity associated with reduced TLR4-mediated activation of macrophages and decreased production of proinflamma- tory cytokines that are known to exacerbate axonal damage. MATN2 is also expressed in lesional brain tissue of human patients with MS, so therapeutics that target MATN2 may help ameliorate the disease by reducing inflammatory cytokine production by macrophages (62). Factors that pre- vent resident microglia and inflammatory monocytes from converting to the reparative phenotype can also regulate healing in the brain. Two such factors are TNF-α, which blocks phagocytosis- mediated conversion of inflammatory macrophages to the reparative M(IL-4)-like phenotype, and iron, which accumulates in local injury-associated macrophages and can prolong TNF-α production (63). Thus, iron accumulation and sustained TNF production by macrophages can substantially delay tissue repair following injury. Neutrophils and monocyte-derived macrophages are also the key producers of IL-1β, which plays a critical pathogenic role in EAE. A recent study identified transmigration of myeloid cells across the inflamed blood–spinal cord barrier as an essential activating stimulus driving IL-1β production and myeloid cell recruitment (64).

##### Regulation of Macrophage Recruitment

How the beneficial or reparative M(IL-4)-like monocyte-derived macrophages are recruited to the CNS has also been an active area of research over the past few years. An interesting study by Shechter and colleagues (10) suggested that proinflammatory M(IFN-γ)-like cells and alternatively activated anti-inflammatory M(IL-4)-like macrophages are distinctly recruited to the spinal cord following injury, with neither population entering through a breached blood-brain barrier. They concluded that Ly6C hi Cx3Cr1 lo M(IFN-γ) monocytes are recruited from adjacent spinal cord leptomeninges via a classical CCL2/CCR2-dependent mechanism, whereas the resolving Ly6C lo Cx3CR1 hi M(IL-4)-like monocytes are recruited from the more remote blood–cerebrospinal fluid barrier, the brain-ventricular choroid plexus, by a VCAM-1-VLA-4/CD73-dependent mechanism (68). These studies were important because they suggested that the route of monocyte entry to the injury site could ultimately dictate their function. Their findings also argue against the simple model of phenotypic conversion in situ and instead suggest that inflammatory and reparative macrophages are recruited from distinct anatomical locations in some organs.

##### Unique Therapeutic Strategies in the Central Nervous System

Given the critical regulatory role of macrophages in the CNS, manipulating their recruitment, ex- pansion, or activation may be a way to improve outcomes following brain injury or during chronic disorders such as AD. Although resolving anti-inflammatory macrophages are often associated with recovery from tissue injury (2), decreases in cell-mediated immunity and macrophage phago- cytic activity in the brains of Alzheimer’s patients have been hypothesized to contribute to the reduction in clearance of amyloid-beta plaques. Baruch and colleagues (72) showed that an im- mune checkpoint blockade directed against the programmed death-1 pathway induces an IFN-γ response that leads to the recruitment and activation of monocyte-derived macrophages that restore the clearance of plaques in the brain. Thus, immune checkpoints may be targeted thera- peutically in AD to augment the restorative function of macrophages. Nevertheless, the phenotype of the resolving macrophage population is likely critical in AD, as another recent study suggested that reducing the number of inflammatory Ly6C + TREM2 + macrophages can protect against AD by decreasing inflammation and preventing the formation of amyloid and tau pathologies (73). Multipotent adult progenitor cells also protect against axonal dieback in spinal cord injury by participating in the expansion of protective M(IL-4)-like macrophages (74). The reparative macrophages in turn establish an anti-inflammatory environment that is more accommodating to the survival and growth of both mesenchymal stem cells and progenitor populations; a related study suggests an important role for macrophage-derived IL-10 in progenitor cell renewal and neuroprotection (75). Therefore, a mutually beneficial feedback loop exists between reparative macrophages and stem cell populations, which together facilitate tissue regeneration in the CNS (76, 77).

##### Polarizing Toward Repair

Other groups have recently investigated the mechanisms by which highly plastic myocardial macrophages take on a reparative phenotype. Horckmans and colleagues (82) showed that although neutrophils can contribute to acute cardiac tissue damage, they are also critical for cardiac repair after acute myocardial infarction (MI) because they help polarize macrophages toward a reparative phenotype. Indeed, although neutrophil depletion following MI reduces IL-1β expression and the number of infiltrating monocytes, cardiac function and fibrosis worsen. These authors found that neutrophils crucially promote polarization of macrophages toward an IL-10/TGF-β1-producing reparative phenotype, which is critical to the clearance of dying cardiomyocytes. Expression of the phagocytosis receptor MertK also increases on the protective cells. Several additional investigations recently emphasized the critical contribution of IL-4- and IL-13-activated macrophages to cardiac repair. Shiraishi and colleagues (83) showed that Trib1- deficient mice that are selectively missing M(IL-4) macrophages frequently experienced cardiac rupture after MI. M(IL-4) macrophages were crucial for fibroblast activation that provided sup- portive fibrous tissues in the infarcts. A recent study by de Couto and colleagues (87) demonstrated that cardiosphere-derived cells (CDCs), a stem-like population derived ex vivo from cardiac biopsies, confers cardioprotection and regeneration in acute MI by reducing the number of CD68 + macrophages and secreting factors that polarize macrophages toward a protective phenotype. The protective macrophages were neither M(IFN-γ)- nor M(IL-4)-like but greatly diminished cardiomyocyte apoptosis and infarct size. In vitro transwell experiments showed that the CDCs caused macrophages to express large amounts of the anti-inflammatory cytokine IL-10. Korf-Klingebiel and colleagues (88) re- cently identified another macrophage-derived growth factor produced in humans following MI that promotes cardiomyocyte survival. The growth factor is encoded by open reading frame on chromosome 19 and was revealed using a bioinformatics secretome analysis. Using mice deficient for the growth factor, the authors showed that it is critical for normal tissue repair and prevention of contractile dysfunction.

##### MACROPHAGES IN SKELETAL MUSCLE INJURY

##### AND REGENERATION

The phenotype of the inflammatory response is also thought to play a decisive role in skeletal mus- cle regeneration following injury. After muscle injury, neutrophils, monocytes, and macrophages accumulate in the damaged area, and these cells are believed to tightly regulate the proliferation of myoblasts derived from muscle stem cells (satellite cells) that regulate muscle regeneration. These myoblasts transiently express G-CSFR (granulocyte colony-stimulating factor receptor) following injury and proliferate in response to G-CSF produced in part by macrophages. Thus, G-CSF and macrophages are thought to contribute to muscle regeneration (89). Some studies also suggest differential roles for pro- and anti-inflammatory macrophages in the chronic muscle wasting observed in Duchenne muscular dystrophy (DMD). Although IFN-γ activity is increased in DMD and associated with the development of M(IFN-γ)-like inflammatory macrophages, surprisingly, MDX mice deficient in Ifn-γ displayed no decrease in muscle fiber damage or im- provement in gross motor function during the onset of disease. However, they developed a marked improvement in motor function in the late regenerative phase that was associated with a skewing of macrophages toward an M(IL-4) anti-inflammatory phenotype, suggesting a possible protective role for reparative macrophages (90). Muscle regeneration and muscle cell proliferation following injury are also associated with the development of anti-inflammatory macrophages, with IL-10 playing a central role in their development in vivo (91). Proinflammatory human M(IFN-γ)-like cells were also shown to directly inhibit myogenic precursor cell (MPC) fusion in vitro, whereas anti-inflammatory macrophages strongly promote MPC differentiation by increasing their com- mitment into differentiated myocytes that form mature myotubes. ##### REGULATION OF PULMONARY INFLAMMATION AND FIBROSIS

##### BY MACROPHAGES

Macrophages have also emerged as master regulators of tissue repair in the lungs (2). Recent studies have helped define the mechanisms by which macrophages exacerbate the inflammatory response following lung injury, switch into a healing phenotype, and drive the development of pathological fibrosis when they become persistently dysregulated.

##### Mechanisms that Control or Fail to Control Acute and Chronic Inflammation

Cai and colleagues (95) revealed how a macrophage phenotype that is normally beneficial for lung tissue repair can become pathogenic. Similar to Horckman and coworkers’ findings in the heart (82), these authors showed that the phagocytosis receptor MertK was essential for macrophages to convert to a tissue repair phenotype and suppress inflammation. In their study, MertK in- creased cytoplasmic 5-lipoxygenase to promote long-chain fatty acid–derived lipids called special- ized proresolving mediators (SPMs), which reduced chronic inflammation in a mouse model of ischemia reperfusion injury. The authors also found that under inflammatory conditions, cleavage of MertK by the metalloproteinase ADAM17 diminished SPM synthesis and delayed resolution. However, although this was detrimental during the repair stage of injury, the authors hypothesized that ADAM17-mediated cleavage of MertK could have an important protective role by promoting important acute inflammatory functions such as wound debridement and antimicrobial defense. Although macrophages, particularly alveolar macrophages (AMs), are important for host de- fense in the lung, they can also contribute to tissue injury. Westphalen and colleagues (96) re- cently identified a unique population of protective AMs anchored to the alveolar wall that limit lipopolysaccharide (LPS)-induced inflammation by sending Ca 2+ waves through the epithelium and activating the prosurvival kinase Akt. ##### Mechanisms of Dysregulation in Fibrosis

Macrophages are also implicated in the development of pulmonary fibrosis. For example, Cao and colleagues (99) identified a population of macrophages that suppresses alveolar repair and con- tributes to lung fibrosis following repeated lung injury. The authors induced repetitive lung injury in mice with bleomycin or hydrochloric acid and found diminished expression of the chemokine receptor CXCR7 on pulmonary capillary endothelial cells. The reduced CXCR7 expression was associated with increased recruitment of vascular endothelial growth factor receptor 1 (VEGFR1)- expressing perivascular macrophages. These macrophages contributed to the persistent activation of perivascular fibroblasts leading to the development of fibrosis. Increased Wnt-Jagged1 signaling in capillary endothelial cells was also identified as an important early trigger. Thus, dysregulated macrophage recruitment can serve as an important driver of pulmonary fibrosis. Macrophage survival also appears to be an important determinant in the development of pul- monary fibrosis. Indeed, Larson-Casey and colleagues (100) showed that increased resistance of AMs to apoptosis is required for the development of pulmonary fibrosis. Using a bleomycin- induced model of pulmonary fibrosis, the authors found that Akt1-mediated macrophage mi- tophagy promoted AM survival, and AM-derived TGF-β1 was critical to the development of fibrosis. Lung injury led Akt1 to form macrophage mitochondrial reactive oxygen species and mitophagy that were critical for TGF-β1 expression and fibroblast differentiation. The authors also found increased TGF-β1 mRNA expression in AMs isolated from patients with idiopathic pulmonary fibrosis (IPF). Finally, Zhou and colleagues (101) carried out a translational study of the chitinase-like pro- tein, chitinase 3-like 1 (CHI3L1), in the context of pulmonary fibrotic disease and also identified a critical role for macrophages in the development of pulmonary fibrosis. In this study, increases in epithelial cell–derived CHI3L1 in IPF patients were linked with the elevated expression of CCL18, a marker of alternatively activated M(IL-4)-like macrophages. Moreover, mice that overexpress Brp39, the mouse homolog of CHI3L1, displayed reduced bleomycin-induced fibrosis if macrophages were depleted.

##### AND REPAIR

Recent studies have shown macrophages have potent roles in promoting inflammation in the gut, slowing inflammation, and fostering wound repair.

##### MACROPHAGE-BASED THERAPEUTIC STRATEGIES FOR ORGAN

##### INJURY AND FIBROSIS

As macrophages are key mediators of organ injury and repair, therapeutics that affect their re- cruitment, egress, proliferation, and/or activation at sites of tissue injury are being investigated as potential therapeutic strategies for a variety of chronic inflammatory and fibrotic diseases.

##### Manipulating Macrophage Proliferation and Turnover

Because monocyte and macrophage proliferation is largely dependent on CSF1/CSF1R signaling (109), CSF1-Fc, neutralizing antibodies to the ligand and receptor, and inhibitors of CSF1R kinase activity are being evaluated for their ability to modulate macrophage function in diseased organs. For example, anti-CSF1R therapy was shown to reduce cutaneous and pulmonary chronic graft- versus-host disease (110), serious complications resulting from stem cell transplantation that are triggered by donor macrophages. These authors showed that donor macrophages are highly de- pendent on CSF1/CSF1R signaling, whereas CCL2/CCR2 and GM-CSF/GM-CSFR signaling were largely dispensable. Related studies by Hume and colleagues (111) suggested that CSF1 pri- marily targets and expands the resident tissue macrophage population. Consequently, anti-CSF1R treatment primarily impairs the maturation and replacement of resident tissue macrophages but does not substantially alter the number of proinflammatory monocytes. As such, they concluded that therapeutic manipulation of CSF1/CSF1R signaling would likely have little to no impact on inflammatory diseases that are dependent on bone marrow–derived monocytes (111). Instead, they theorized that CSF1 signaling would primarily regulate macrophage-driven tissue repair. In support of this hypothesis, Stutchfield and colleagues (51) showed that CSF1-Fc therapy enhances recovery from acute liver injury and accelerates liver regeneration following partial hepatectomy, and in each case the beneficial effects were associated with a marked expansion in the number of resident tissue macrophages and recruited monocytes. Gow et al. (112) showed that CSF1-Fc ther- apy could also augment the proliferation of hepatocytes, leading to extensive hepatosplenomegaly. They determined that the targeted macrophages produce several mediators that directly stimulate hepatocyte proliferation, including urokinase, TNF-α, and IL-6. In addition to regulating liver regeneration, CSF1 also accelerates recovery following acute kidney injury. Here again, expansion of mature tissue macrophages by CSF1 led to the enhanced production of soluble mediators that are known to support epithelial regeneration following injury (7). Therefore, the protective effects of CSF1 may extend beyond the simple expansion of tissue regenerative macrophages.

##### Manipulating Macrophage Movement

Chemokines, chemokine receptors, and other mediators that regulate the recruitment and turnover of monocytes and macrophages at sites of tissue injury have also emerged as potential

therapeutic targets (114–117). For example, the CCL2/CCR2 chemokine signaling axis is acti- vated in the liver following injury. In addition, pharmacological inhibitors of Ccl2 substantially reduce hepatic inflammation and fibrogenesis following acute toxic injury, with the reductions in pathology associated with decreased monocyte and macrophage infiltration and reduced expres- sion of several proinflammatory cytokines (114). The CXCR6/CXCL16 chemokine signaling axis is also induced in nonalcoholic fatty liver disease (NAFLD) that can lead to cirrhosis, and pharma- cological inhibitors of CXCL16 have emerged as a promising therapeutic approach by reducing the accumulation of inflammatory macrophages, which is a hallmark of progressive NAFLD (116). Serum amyloid P, a member of the pentraxin family, was shown to inhibit the accumulation of profibrotic macrophages in the lungs of mice following bleomycin exposure, suggesting it might be developed as a therapeutic strategy for idiopathic pulmonary fibrosis (118). Chronic inflammatory diseases like emphysema might also benefit from therapeutics that impair macrophage survival or facilitate their removal from the lung, as demonstrated recently with alendronate, a bisphos- phonate compound that promotes apoptosis of both recruited and resident alveolar macrophages (119). Macrophages also play a critical role in renal cyst formation, inflammation, and fibrosis in autosomal-dominant polycystic kidney disease, with macrophage migration inhibitory factor (MIF) facilitating the recruitment and retention of profibrotic macrophages in the kidney (115). Therefore, therapeutics that target MIF activity might represent a viable therapeutic strategy for fibrotic disorders.

##### Phenotypically Converted and Genetically Altered Macrophages

##### for Transplantation

Macrophage transplantation techniques employing bone marrow–derived macrophages, pul- monary macrophages, and other monocyte and macrophage populations exhibiting restorative, wound-healing, or anti-inflammatory activity are also being investigated as therapeutic strategies for chronic inflammatory and fibrotic diseases (120, 121). For example, clinical studies of bone marrow and macrophage-based cell therapy for liver cirrhosis are being considered (120). The goal of these studies is to increase the number of macrophages producing matrix-degrading MMPs, such as MMP13 and MMP9, which facilitate the reversal of liver scarring following chronic injury. Interestingly, early results from these studies suggest a protective role for mature differentiated macrophages, whereas macrophage precursors are thought to provide little to no benefit. Trans- plantation of genetically modified mature tissue macrophages, which can self-maintain for months or even years, was also recently shown to help correct a myeloid cell disorder (121). Therefore, cell-specific therapies employing macrophages may be applied to a number of diseases that are associated with impaired macrophage function. Because macrophages can exhibit pathogenic or protective activity depending on their activa- tion status, external conditioning could further enhance the therapeutic potential of macrophages prior to transplantation. ##### SUMMARY AND OUTLOOK

Resident organ tissue macrophages and monocytes from circulation have essential roles in the mammalian response to injury in each of the organs covered in this review. Generally, macrophages have similar functions after organ injury. First, they help to regulate inflammation, then they help direct the inflammatory response toward repair and regeneration and finally to resolve the response. Most studies support the concept that macrophages and monocytes undergo phenotypic conversion in situ to these different functional populations, although there are exceptions to this understanding in the CNS. It is clear that monocytes recruited from the bone marrow can be proinflammatory and that they can quickly transition into prowound-healing or proresolving cells. It is also clear that resident macrophages can make this switch and that these transitions occur in multiple organs. A particular macrophage response may be largely controlled by the type of injury, the specific location of the injury, the magnitude of the injury, and whether it is acute or chronic. In any case, timely conversion of macrophages and monocytes from proinflammatory to reparative and restorative phenotypes seems to be decisive. Otherwise, the phases become dysregulated, and unique diseases develop in each organ system. Although distinct mechanisms of macrophage function are dictated by differences in organ physiology and architecture, the studies highlighted in the review nicely illustrate that many mechanisms are shared across organ systems. For example, whether inflammation is promoted by commensals in the gut or infarction in the heart, macrophage inflammasome-derived IL- 1β and CCR2 + monocytes figure prominently in the response. MertK is a crucial receptor for macrophages directing repair regardless whether cardiomyocytes or alveolar epithelial cells are injured, and IL-33 activates type 2 immune signals to switch inflammatory monocytes toward a reparative phenotype if it is produced by injured hepatocytes or astrocytes. Table 1 lists the mediators involved in the mechanisms highlighted by the studies in this review along with the organs in which they were observed. An improved understanding of the unique and disparate mechanisms by which macrophages regulate tissue regeneration in different organ systems will guide the development of new and improved therapeutic strategies for a variety of inflammatory and fibrotic diseases. However, it is often still unclear how macrophages categorized as proinflammatory, anti-inflammatory, repar- ative, resolving, profibrotic, or antifibrotic function in different settings.
