Interleukin 4 is important in protective immunity to a gastrointestinal nematode infection in mice

ABSTRACT Parasitic helminths typically induce compo- nents of immediate-type hypersensitivity, including elevated serum IgE, eosinophilia, and mucosal mast cells. These re- sponses are T-cell-dependent and associated with rapid expul- sion ofparasitic worms from a sensitized host; existing exper- imental systems have failed to derme the precise role of cytokines in these responses. We report here that anti- interleukin 4 oranti-interleukin 4 receptorantibodiesblockthe polyclonal IgE response to a parasitic nematode, Heligmo- somoides polygyrus, and abrogate protective immunity to the infection. Incontrast, anti-interleukin 5antibodypreventedH. polygyrus-induced eosinophilia but did not prevent protection. These data provide evidence that a specific cytokine affects the physiology and survival of a parasitic nematode in the host.

Resolution of gastrointestinal helminthiasis is a T-cell- dependent process. Parasite expulsion mechanisms do not function in congenitally athymic nude mice or in mice that have been depleted of T cells (1-3). Host responses that modulate parasite physiology, such as worm fecundity, are also known to be T-cell-dependent (4, 5). Because there is no evidence that T cells directly kill nematode worms, immune and inflammatory responses that combine to eliminate par- asitesorimpairtheiractivitymight resultfrom T-cell-derived cytokines. Interleukin 4 (IL-4) and interleukin 5 (IL-5) are two cytokines that could potentially affect immunity to helminths. IL-4 has multiple immunoregulatory functions, including autocrine T-cell growth factor activity, regulation of B-cell IgE isotype expression, and stimulation ofthegrowth and/or differentiation of macrophages, hematopoietic cells, and mast cells (6-12). The complexing of IgE receptors on mucosal mast cells with IgE antibody specific for parasite- derived antigens is postulated to be important in protective immunity because of the subsequent release of mediators of inflammation that could adversely affect parasite survival (13). IL-5 regulates the generation of eosinophil myelocyte precursors in bone marrow and the development of mature eosinophils after helminth infection (14, 15), and there is evidence that eosinophils and IgE may constitute an impor- tant defense mechanism in rats infected with schistosomes (16) or the nematode Trichinella spiralis (17). Treatment of mice with monoclonal antibody (mAb) against IL4 (llB11) in vivo completely suppresses polyclonal IgE responses (18, 19) and limits the expansion of mucosal mast cells (K. B. Madden, J.F.U., H. J. Ziltener, J. W. Schrader, F.D.F., and I.M.K., unpublished work). Anti-IL-5 (TRFK-5) suppresses eosinophilia and eliminates eosinophils from the cellular infiltrates responding to larvae in the lungs of mice infected with the nematode Nippostrongylus brasiliensis (20) and from liver granulomas induced by eggs of the trematode Schistosoma mansoni, without affecting lesion size (15). However, neutralization of endogenously produced IL-4 or IL-5 fails to measurably compromise host immunity in mice infected with S. mansoni (21) or N. brasiliensis (ref. 20; J.F.U., I.M.K., and F.D.F., unpublished data). The absence of a definitive role for IL-4 and IL-5 in host responses to these parasites prompted the study of a nema- tode parasite with a completely enteric and more chronic infection than N. brasiliensis to determine whether mucosal elements important inprotective immunity were regulated by IL-4or IL-5. Parasitic third-stagelarvaeofHeligmosomoides polygyrus (Hp) invade the intestinal mucosa after oral inoc- ulation and molt twice within 8 days before emerging into the intestinal lumen as adults that persist for several weeks (22). The results ofthe studies reported here show that antibodies against IL-4 or the IL-4 receptor (IL-4R), but not antibodies against IL-5, block hostprotectivemechanisms thatnormally limit parasite egg production and reduce the development of new worms inoculated into previously infected mice.

MATERIALS AND METHODS

Animals. Female BALB/c mice were obtained from the Small Animal Division of the National Cancer Institute (Frederick, MD) and were used for experimentation at 8-12 weeks ofage. All experiments were conducted with five mice per group. Blood samples were obtained from the orbital plexus. Serum was separated from clotted blood by centrif- ugation and stored at -80'C until used. An anthelminthic drug, pyrantel pamoate (Strongid T, PfizerDiagnostics), was administered orally at a dose of 1-2 mg per mouse to some parasite-infected mice to remove adult worms from a primary infection. Parasitological Parameters. Infective, ensheathed third- stage larvae of Hp (specimens on file at the U. S. National Parasite Collection, U. S. National Museum Helmintholog- ical Collection no. 81930, Beltsville, MD) were propagated and stored at 40C until used (23). Mice were inoculated orally with 200 larvae by using a ball-tipped feeding tube. Adult worms and total parasite eggs in the intestinal contents were determined at necropsy (23). Worm fecundity was expressed by dividing the total number of eggs recovered from each mouse by the total number of female worms. Antibodies. RESULTS

The kinetics of the serum IgE response to parasite infection was followed to evaluate mAb treatment in vivo (Fig. 1). Serum IgE levels rose 4100-fold in response to a primaryHp infection inmice treated with anti-IL-5mAb, control mAb, or no antibody; a second inoculation withHp produced afurther 5-fold increase in serum IgE levels in these mice. Treatment of Hp-infected mice with anti-IL-4 or a combination of anti-IL4 and anti-IL-5 mAb inhibited the Hp-induced rise in serum IgE by a factor of 100 (Fig. 1). Although anti-IL-4 blocked the IgE response to infection, it did not inhibit all T-dependent antibody responses to Hp because the IgG1 response was not blocked (34). Anti-IL-5 mAb, which totally blocked eosinophilia (Fig. 2), had no effect on either IgG1 or IgE responses (34). Interestingly, anti-IL4 treatment considerably enhanced the eosinophil response to Hp, especially after a secondary challenge in- fection (Fig. 2). The role ofIL-4 and IL-5 in protective immunity toHp was evaluated by measuring adult worm survival and fecundity (eggs perfemale worm) in mice cleared ofa primary infection with an anthelminthic drug and then reinoculated with Hp (Fig. 3). Previously uninfected mice had an average of 131 adult worms at necropsy 17 days after inoculation with Hp, whereas previously infected mice given a secondary infection alone or after treatment with anti-IL-5 or control mAb averaged <10 adult worms. Mice infected similarly but given anti-IL-4 or anti-IL-4 plus anti-IL-5 averaged 46 and 54 adult worms, respectively. Anti-IL-4 suppression ofthe protective immunity resulted in increased worm fecundity as well as adult worm survival in the challenge infection. However, interpretation of this experiment was complicated by the relatively low level of fecundity observed in control mice inoculated only once with Hp, as compared with most ofour other experiments (compare Figs. 3 and 4). Treatment ofmice with both a mAb that blocks the binding of IL-4 to the IL-4 receptor (ml) and anti-IL-4 mAb more fully suppressed protective immunity in a second Hp infec- tion than did anti-IL-4 mAb alone. Mice with a primary Hp infection were injected with anti-IL-4R once weekly starting at day 30 after inoculation and also were injected with anti-IL-4 antibody concurrently at the time ofanthelminthic treatment and throughout a secondary challenge infection. Protective immune responses in these mice were completely blocked in that total adult worms, total eggs per mouse, and worm fecundity levels were comparable to previously unin- fected mice given an initial Hp infection (Fig. 4). Initiating anti-IL-4R antibody treatment at the time ofreinfection also proved effective in blocking protective immunity. The worm burden of reinoculated mice averaged <2, whereas nonim- mune mice inoculated similarly averaged 30 adults. Injection ofimmune mice with various doses ofanti-IL-4R mAb at the time of reinoculation and 1 week later inhibited protective immunity in dose-dependent fashion. However, treatment with anti-IL-4R mAb during repeated infections blocked protective immunity in that increased numbers of worms were seen in the intestines of anti-IL-4R-treated mice (Fig. 5). DISCUSSION

Our studies demonstrate that the cytokine IL-4 contributes to the immune mechanisms that limit adult worm survival and fecundity in mice reinoculated with the nematode parasite Hp. Neutralization ofIL-4 activity during a primary infection did not consistently affect parasite egg production (J.F.U., I.M.K., and F.D.F., unpublished results) in contrast with what had been observed in mice treated with anti-CD4 mAb (23). This result suggests that the role of IL-4 in protective immunity during a primary infection may not be all-inclusive. However, treatment with either anti-IL-4 or anti-IL-4R mAb or with both mAbs substantially increased adult worm bur- dens and fecundity in mice inoculated a second time. Con- siderable inhibition of protective immunity was observed, regardless of whether the antagonistic IL-4 treatment was initiated at the first Hp infection, at the second Hp infection, or at some point in between. Although anthelminthic therapy was used in most experiments to cure the initial infection before a second inoculation and to demonstrate protective immunity, inhibition ofprotective immunity was also clearly demonstrated in one experiment in which mice were serially inoculated three times with Hp without intervening drug therapy. Anti-IL-4R mAb appeared more effective then anti- IL-4 mAb at inhibiting protective immunity; however, use of either antibody gave qualitatively similar results, and varia- tions between different experiments and lack of mAb dose- response studies make it impossible to draw any strong conclusions about apparent quantitative differences. The mechanism by which endogenously produced IL-4 contributes to protective immunity is uncertain. However, preliminary studies indicate that treatment of Hp-infected mice with an anti- mouse IgE mAb, which neutralizes secreted IgE, fails to inhibit protective immunity, whereas doses of anti-IL-4R mAb that inhibit IgE secretion incompletely have a consid- erable blocking effect on protective immunity (I.M.K., K. B. Madden, F.D.F., and J.F.U., unpublished data). These stud- ies are incomplete in that they have not yet evaluated IgE levels at the site of Hp infection in the gut or the role of parasite-specific IgE antibodies; however, they do suggest that other effects regulated by IL-4, such as its stimulation of T cells and mucosal mast cell growth (8), and, possibly, activation of cytotoxic T cells (37), may contribute to the protective immune response to Hp. Although inhibition of IL-4 effectively blocked protective immunity in mice given a second Hp infection, neutralization ofa second cytokine that is associated with worm infections, IL-5, did not affect worm survival or fecundity, even though

it totally blocked Hp-induced eosinophilia. Prevention of eosinophilia, but not protective immunity, by treatment of mice with anti-IL-5 was similar to that reported for mice infected with S. mansoni or N. brasiliensis (15, 20) and appears to lead to a similar conclusion-i.e., eosinophils are not critical in murine immunity to these helminths, despite the fact that studies with S. mansoni and T. spiralis in a rat model have suggested a prominent role for this cell type in protective immunity (16, 17). Interestingly, in our studies, IL-4 antagonist therapy has caused eosinophil levels in Hp-infected mice to rise substantially, possibly because the increased worm and egg burden that resulted from this treatment acted to further stimulate eosinophil production. Nevertheless, neutralization of IL-4 suppressed protective responses to infection in the presence of high eosinophilia, and this suppression was not augmented by treatment with anti-IL-5 in combination with anti-IL-4. The demonstration that IL-4 is important to protective immunity in Hp-infected mice sharply contrasts with evi- dence that endogenous production of the same cytokine strongly inhibits protective immunity in mice infected with the protozoan parasite Leishmania major (38, 39). This dichotomy between protective immunity in the L. major and Hp systems is strengthened by recent studies that show that interferon y, which contributes to protective immunity in L. major-infected mice (39), inhibits protective immunity in mice infected with nematode parasites (J.F.U., K. B. Mad- den, A. Cheever, P. Trotta, I.M.K., and F.D.F., unpublished work). Thus, a single cytokine response pattern can be protective or maladaptive, depending upon the infectious agent involved. Because CD4' T cells, which contribute to protective immunity in both protozoan and nematode sys- tems, can differentiate either into cells that secrete large quantities ofIL-4with littleorno interferon yorinto cells that have the opposite cytokine secretory pattern (40), the mech- anisms that regulate the T cells of an infected host to make the appropriate cytokine response must contribute impor- tantly to host survival.
