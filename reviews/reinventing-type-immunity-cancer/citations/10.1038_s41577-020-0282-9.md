# Plasticity of innate lymphoid cell subsets

*1,2,3* *1,2* *1,2* ✉ *Suzanne M. Bal* *, Korneliusz Golebski* *and Hergen Spits*

Abstract | Innate lymphoid cells (ILCs) are important for tissue homeostasis and for the initiation of immune responses. Based on their transcriptional regulation and cytokine profiles, ILCs can be categorized into five subsets with defined phenotypes and functional profiles, but they also have the ability to adapt to local environmental cues by changing these profiles. This plasticity raises the question of the extent to which the cytokine production profiles of ILCs are pre- programmed or are a reflection of the tissue microenvironment. Here, we review recent advances in research on ILCs, with a focus on the plasticity of these cells. We highlight the ability of ILCs to communicate with the surrounding microenvironment and discuss the possible consequences of ILC plasticity for our understanding of the biological roles of these cells. Finally, we discuss how we might use this knowledge of ILC plasticity to develop or improve options for the treatment of

**Group 1 ILCs** (ILC1s). Group 1 innate lymphoid cells (ILCs) are non-cytotoxic or weakly cytotoxic tissue-resident cells that function as the first line of defence against infections with viruses and certain bacteria. ILC1s produce interferon-γ (IF Nγ) and require T-bet for their function. They express CD127 in humans and CD200R in mice, and express NKp46 in both humans and mice.

### inflammatory diseases.

In 1986, a landmark publication by Tim Mossman + and Robert Coffman described two subsets of CD4 T cells, known as T helper 1 (TH1) cells and TH2 cells, that differed in their cytokine production profiles. TH1 cells produce interferon-γ (IFNγ), whereas T 2 cells 1 H produce IL-4 and IL-5 (ref.). In 2005, a third subset of CD4 + T cells that produce IL-17 was described (known as TH17 cells) 2,3 . In the following years, two further CD4 +

T cell populations that produce IL-22 or IL-9 as signa- ture cytokines were described (T 4–6 H 22 cells and TH9 cells, respectively). This subdivision based on cytokine pro- duction profiles turned out not to be unique to CD4 +

T cells expressing the αβ T cell receptor (TCR) as it was also observed for natural killer T (NKT) cells and T cells expressing the γδ TCR7. In 2009 and 2010, several papers were published describing populations of non-T cell, non-B cell and nat- ural killer (NK) cell-like lymphoid cells that could also be defined by their cytokine production 8–12 . The similar- ities between these ‘innate’ lymphocytes and the various 13 THcell subsets were obvious and, in 2013, it was pro- posed to call this novel cell type innate lymphoid cells (ILCs) 14 . Since then, ILCs have been categorized into five subsets on the basis of their developmental pathways. In addition to NK cells and lymphoid tissue inducer cells, these are the group 1 ILCs (ILC1s), group 2 ILCs (ILC2s) and group 3 ILCs (ILC3s), which in general are non-cytotoxic. ILC1s are characterized by their capacity to pro- duce IFNγ and they depend on the transcription factor T-bet, but not eomesodermin (EOMES). However, as discussed below, the phenotype and functions of ILC1s

depend on the tissue microenvironment and, particu- larly in humans, ILCs with a mixed NK cell and ILC1 phenotype have been observed16. ILC2s depend on GATA-binding protein 3 (GATA3) and can produce IL-4, IL-5 and IL-13. ILC3s depend on retinoic acid receptor-related orphan receptor-γt (RORγt; encoded by RORC) for their development and function, and comprise subsets that can be distinguished on the basis of expression of the natural cytotoxicity receptors NKp46 (also known as NCR1; in mice and humans) and 12,17,18 NKp44 (also known as NCR2; in humans only). The main cytokine produced by ILC3s is IL-22 and a propor- tion of ILC3s also produce IL-17. Whereas the ILC equiv- alents of TH1 cells (ILC1s), TH2 cells (ILC2s) and TH17 or TH22 cells (ILC3s) are reasonably well defined, a FOXP3 +

ILC equivalent of CD4 + CD25 + regulatory T (Treg) cells has not yet been definitively identified. Given the similarities between ILC subsets and THcell subsets, our extensive knowledge of the molecular mechanisms that control THcell subset development and functions has greatly accelerated our understanding of these processes in ILCs. Similarly to THcells, it is evident that the phenotypes of ILC subsets are not stable and that these cells can change their functions and associated phenotype upon changes in their microenvironment 19,20 . This phenomenon is known as plasticity and reflects a broad capacity of developing cells to change their destiny and of mature cells to change functions and phenotype in response to fluctuating environmental cues, triggered by particular physiological and pathophysiological stim- uli (Box 1). Box 1 | Plasticity

T he concept of cellular plasticity was first introduced by Helen B lau in 1985 to denote the capacity of a cell to change its identity136. B ased on studies of the regulation of muscle cell identity, Blau and David Baltimore proposed that cell identity was actively maintained and regulated by regulatory factors, which raised the possibility that by modifying these factors the cell identity could be changed137. This was a novel concept, because it was thought at the time that cell identity was fixed and irreversible. Today, many examples of such plasticity are known. Perhaps the most marked example of plasticity is that of induced pluripotent stem cells, whereby a differentiated cell type, a fibroblast, can be manipulated to become a pluripotent stem cell138. Another example is the epithelial–mesenchymal transition and the reverse mesenchymal–epithelial transition that have roles both in the development of epithelial cells and in cancer139. Examples of plasticity in the immune system, in which a precursor cell changes its predetermined destiny, are also known. For example, the identity of pre-B cells can be changed into that of a multipotent precursor cell by deleting the transcription factor PAX5 (ref.140). T he concept of plasticity induced through modulation of lineage-determining transcription factors is now generally accepted in immunology. Plasticity has been shown for, but is not limited to, T cells20, macrophages141, mast cells and neutrophils142. T he specific definition of plasticity that we use for this Review is the capacity of developing cells to change their destiny and of mature cells to change functions and phenotype in response to environmental cues.

Innate lymphoid cells (ILCs) are the innate counterparts of CD4+T helper cells and are mainly tissue-resident cells that proliferate locally. This suggests that ILCs have greater resemblance to tissue-resident memory T (TRM) cells than to circulating T cells. ### Plasticity of mature ILCs

The regulation of THcell subset polarization driven by lineage-determining transcription factors such as T-bet, GATA3 and RORγt has provided a useful basis for under- standing the complexity of effector functions of polar ized T cell subsets. However, it is now clear that polarized T cells can have considerable plasticity and can co-express different polarizing transcription factors. Recent reviews have extensively discussed the signal transduction path- ways and the (epi)genetic control mechanisms that regulate T cell plasticity 20,36 . The analogous plasticity of mature ILCs was observed for the first time in mice37 and in humans 38,39 in three papers published in 2010. The human studies reported that the cytokines produced by human ILC3s in vitro vary according to the way in which these cells are stimulated 38,39 . The transcriptomes and epigenomes of T cells and ILCs have marked similarities, but transcriptome and reg- ulome analyses of human tonsillar THcells and ILCs have shown that there are also clear differences. As expected, active regulation and expression of genes involved in TCR signalling were observed in TH1 cells and TH17 cells, whereas genetic pathways associated with cytokine sig- nalling and innate immune receptors were prominent in ILC1s and ILC3s40. Comparing the chromatin landscapes of resting and stimulated mouse ILCs showed that all ILC subsets are poised to transcribe the genes associated with their distinct lineages, as the chromatin states of cytokine loci only had limited alterations after activation, as well as between different tissues41. Moreover, most of the subset-defining regulatory elements were already accessible in ILC precursors41. However, the situation might be different in human ILCs as marked chromatin alterations were reported in cytokine-activated ILC2s as compared with resting ILC2s 42,43 . Detailed analysis of mouse ILCs suggested that they pass through a plastic phase before the terminal differentiation of ILC subsets41. Peripheral-blood ILCs in humans might be in this plas- tic stage, as epigenetic analysis showed that these cells share many active and poised regulatory elements with haematopoietic stem cells and that precursor subsets are primed to develop into functionally different ILC sub- 24,28 sets. Here, we review our knowledge of the transdif- ferentiation of particular ILC subsets and the underlying mechanisms involved.

Box 3 | Heterogeneity and plasticity of ILC1s

Group 1 innate lymphoid cells (ILC1s) are a heterogeneous group of interferon-γ (IFNγ)-producing cells with overlapping phenotypes and functions. Unlike natural killer (NK) cells, ILC1s do not depend on the transcription factor eomesodermin (EOMES) and are non-cytotoxic. However, besides this clear distinction, there is a strong resemblance between NK cells and ILC1s in terms of their phenotype. In mice, ILC1s are tissue-resident cells that require T-bet and Hobit (also known as ZNF683) for their development and express CD49a (also known as ITGA1)145,146. ILC1 subsets with slight differences have been described in the liver, intestines and salivary glands88,93,147. The phenotypic characterization of ILC1s is problematic, given that preferential surface expression of molecules such as CD49a and TRAIL (also known as TNFSF10) is often lost upon cell activation and the expression of these molecules is tissue dependent. CD200R has been described as a stable marker for ILC1s that is retained in an infection model146. In humans, two main ILC1 subsets have been reported. The first is a tissue-resident intraepithelial ILC1 subset that has features of transforming growth factor-β (TGFβ) imprinting by expression of CD103, Hobit, CD160 and CD9 (refs57,58,146). Unlike other ILC1 subsets in mice and humans, these intraepithelial cells express both T-bet and EOME S57. A CD127+ ILC1 subset has also been described that is present at low frequency at steady state, in both tissues and the circulation55,148. ### ILC2-to-ILC1 plasticity. In ILC2s, the master transcrip-

tion factor GATA3 binds to the regulatory element that controls Ifng expression, thereby restricting IFNγ pro- duction and plasticity towards ILC1s41. Nonetheless, ILC2s can convert into IFNγ-producing ILC1s under the influence of IL-1β and IL-12 in vitro. In vivo conversion of ILC2s into ILC1s has been shown to occur in the lungs of mice infected with influenza virus62. Observations in the intestines of patients with Crohn’s disease and in the lungs and blood of patients with chronic obstructive pulmonary disease (COPD) have provided evidence that ILC2-to-ILC1 conversion also occurs in humans 62–64 . In contrast to ILC3s, ILC2s do not respond directly to IL-12 but require IL-1β (or IL-1α) to induce expression of the IL-12 receptor (IL-12R) and subsequent IFNγ produc- tion 3,43,64 . IL-1β not only induces IL-12RB2 expression but also upregulates the expression of ST2 (also known as IL-1RL1) and IL-17RB, which are components of the receptors for IL-33 and IL-25, respectively, on peripheral blood-derived ILC2s43. Thus, IL-1β seems to function as a signal to prime ILC2s to respond to local environ- mental cues and induce plasticity. Interestingly, trans- differentiated ex-ILC2s lose the potential to produce IL-5, but retain IL-13 production, therefore becoming co-producers of IL-13 and IFNγ 53,63,64 , which suggests that Il5 and Il13 genes are differentially regulated. The ILC2-to-ILC1 conversion can be reversed by IL-4, a cytokine that is present at high levels in type 2 inflammatory responses63. IL-4, which is produced by basophils, eosinophils and ILC2s themselves, signals through the activation of STAT6, resulting in GATA3 induction and, thereby, CRTH2 expression by ILC2s65. The importance of IL-4 for ILC2 maintenance is fur- ther illustrated by its ability to prevent and reverse ILC2 transdifferentiation into IFNγ-producing cells63 or 66,67 IL-17-producing cells. The mechanisms underlying these reversals are unclear. Although IL-4 induces the reversion of IFNγ-producing ex-ILC2s back to ILC2s, it is unable to induce the differentiation of tonsillar ILC1s and ILC3s to ILC2s29. Similarly, CD117 + NKp46 + ILCs from human peripheral blood can differentiate into ILC1s and ILC3s but not into ILC2s28. It is possible that canonical ILC1s and ILC3s are intrinsically unable to transdifferentiate into ILC2s, but it is also possible that other triggers, perhaps in addition to IL-4, are required for this conversion. In T cells, TH2 cell polarization depends mainly on IL-4-induced GATA3 expression, but other pathways such as Notch–Jagged interactions and mTORC2 signalling make important contributions 68,69 to GATA3 induction. The effects of these pathways on ILC2 plasticity remain to be investigated.

**Inflammatory ILC2s** (iILC2s). These group 2 innate lymphoid cells (ILC2s), which are found predominantly in the circulation, can produce both IL-17 and IL-13, express GATA-binding protein 3 (GATA3) and retinoic acid receptor-related orphan receptor-γt), and respond to IL-25. In mice, iILC2s can be identified by the expression of killer cell lectin-like receptor G1 (KLR G1), CC-chemokine receptor 9 (CCR 9) and the IL-25 receptor (IL-25R), whereas in humans they lack expression of KLR G1 and CCR 9. It should be noted that there is no consensus regarding this terminology in the field and that IL-25-responsive ILC2s can also be found under non-inflammatory conditions.

**Tuft cells** Also known as brush cells. ‘Tuft’ refers to the microvilli that project from the surface of these cells. They are secretory epithelial cells found in the intestines and airways that can produce IL-25. They express DCLK1 and TR PM5, and ATO H1 expression is required for their specification.

**Polychromic reporter mice** A mouse model consisting of two or more combinatorial reporter genes that enables visualization of more than one protein in the same mouse. Using such mice, researchers have been able to readily identify precursor cells on the basis of expression of one or more transcription factors.

### Plasticity of ILC2s into cells that produce IL-17 or IL-10.

There is no direct evidence for the conversion of TH2 cells into TH17 cells, although changing the extracellu- lar cytokine composition can trigger a switch between inflammatory and regulatory cell programmes. For example, TGFβ can induce IL-10 and FOXP3 expression in TH17 cells, and IL-27 promotes IL-10 production in various inflammatory cell subsets 70,71 . Conversely, IL-17 production can be induced in Tregcells in response to IL-1β, IL-6 and IL-23 (ref. 72 ). IL-2 might also have an important role here as this cytokine is crucial for the induction and maintenance of Tregcells and also prevents TH17 cell development 73,74 . In contrast to the apparent inability of TH2 cells to convert into TH17 cells, ILC2s can be induced to pro- duce IL-17. Mouse ILC2s produce IL-13 in response to IL-25 or IL-33, but only the IL-25-responding ILC2s produced IL-17 in vitro following culture with IL-1β, IL-6, IL-23 and TGFβ, which is a culture condition similar to that used for the in vitro differentiation of TH17 cells75. The IL-25-responding IL-17RB + ILC2s, which were referred to as inflammatory ILC2s (iILC2s) 75 , expressed RORγt but also high levels of GATA3, which explains why these cells have the capacity to produce 76 both IL-13 and IL-17 (ref.). An important difference between iILC2s and the IL-33-responding, so-called natural ILC2s (nILC2s) is that nILC2s were tissue resi- dent whereas iILC2s were circulating77. iILC2s could be derived from ILC2 precursors that reside in the small 77 intestinal lamina propria. Upon activation with IL-25, which is produced by tuft cells in the intestinal epithelial layer in mice78, the intestinal ILC2 precursors, which constitutively express the receptor for IL-25 (ref. 35 ), gave rise to circulating activated ILC2s with similar features to the iILC2s described by others. Interestingly, iILC2s can differentiate into nILC2s both in vivo and in vitro76, which indicates that these cells are plastic. It has been suggested that iILC2s and nILC2s are generated from the same precursor, but the identity of such a precur- sor remains unknown. Interestingly, the conversion of unprimed, naive ILCs into activation-experienced or primed memory ILC2s is also accompanied by a con- version from IL-33 responsiveness into IL-25 respon- siveness, but the IL-25-responsive memory ILC2s did not express RORγt and continued to produce both IL-5 and IL-13 (ref. 79 ). The finding that iILC2s can differentiate into nILC2s raised the question of whether the reverse process can also occur. Recent work suggests that this is indeed the case as mouse ILC2s isolated from small intestinal lamina propria already have accessible regulatory ele- 41 ments for Rorc and Il17 (ref.). Correspondingly, mouse nILC2s could differentiate into iILC2s in response to a cytokine mixture containing type 2 activating fac- tors, such as IL-33, in combination with IL-1β, IL-23, 75 TGFβ and a Notch signal. Recent work in humans has provided further evidence for dynamic conversions and reconversions of ILC2s to IL-17 production. Two subsets of human CRTH2 + ILC2s were identified in human peripheral blood based on differential expres- 67,80 sion of CD117 (refs). The gene expression profile of CD117 – ILC2s indicated that they are mature ILC2s,

whereas CD117 + ILC2s also expressed, in addition to a clear ILC2 signature, some features of ILC3s, such as RORγt expression. The CD117 + ILC2s produced only type 2 cytokines when stimulated with IL-33, but pro- duced IL-17 following stimulation with IL-1β and IL-23 (refs 68,80 ). By contrast, CD117 – ILC2s required TGFβ in addition to IL-1β and IL-23 to produce IL-17 (ref. 68 ). These observations may imply that the downregulation of CD117 expression is associated with differentiation to a less plastic, thus more polarized, type of ILC2 (ref. 29 ). CD117 + ILC2s also express CCR6, which is expressed by TH17 cells and IL-17-producing ILC3s, and CCR6 expression remains high when ILC2s acquire the capacity to produce IL-17 (ref. 66 ). The vitamin A metabolite retinoic acid is known to enhance RORγt expression81 and IL-22 production82 by ILC3s, and Il22 gene expression was increased in ILC2s cultured with IL-2, IL-7, IL-33 and retinoic acid83. These culture conditions also promoted IL-10 production by ILC2s while maintaining their IL-13 production84. TGFβ prevented the production of IL-10, as well as IL-5, by ILC2s 63,84 . Thus, retinoic acid and TGFβ may regulate the balance between IL-10 or IL-17 production by ILC2s. The transcription factors GFI1 and BCL-11B, which are expressed in many haematopoietic cell types, may be involved in ILC2-to-ILC3 plasticity. These factors regu late the proliferative expansion of TH2 cells and block the generation of T 17 cells and T cells. GFI1 also has H reg a role in ILC2 specification and regulates the respon- 85,86 siveness of ILC2s to IL-33 (refs). Interestingly, GFI1 deficiency not only results in decreased expression of the IL-33 receptor component ST2 — and, as a conse- quence, of GATA3 — and decreased IL-5 production, but also results in IL-17 production by ILC2s. BCL- 11B functions upstream of GFI1; Bcl11b *–/–* mice had reduced ST2 expression, but the expression of IL-17RB and responsiveness to IL-25 remained unchanged85. That –/– *Gfi1* ILC2s produce both IL-17 and IL-13 might sug- gest that GFI1 is a negative regulator of the shift of ILC2s to IL-17-producing ILCs.

### Plasticity of ILC1s and NK cells. Until recently, ILC1s

and NK cells were classified as two members of the same family of ILCs, as they both depend on T-bet to produce IFNγ14. However, more recent studies suggested that NK cells and ILC1s develop from different precursors 87–90 . NK cells depend on the transcription factor EOMES for their development, the induction of CD122 (also known as IL-2RB; a subunit of the IL-15 receptor) and subse- quent IL-15 responsiveness, as well as to become func- tionally cytotoxic cells75. Mouse ILC1s do not express EOMES, depend on T-bet for their development and are mostly non-cytotoxic. These differences between ILC1s and NK cells resulted in a novel classification that identified these cell types as two different subsets15. However, this model of ILC1 and NK cell development has been challenged recently, as more precise studies using polychromic reporter mice have indicated that ILC precursors, which were previously thought to be una- ble to develop into NK cells, have considerable NK cell 91,92 precursor activity. One argument against the notion that ILC3s are obligatory precursors of NK cells is that RORγt-deficient individuals lack ILC3s but have normal numbers of NK cells24. In summary, it is clear that mature ILCs are plastic but that this plasticity is limited to a certain extent. It seems that there is a high degree of plasticity between ILC3s and ILC1s or NK cells and between NK cells and ILC1s. However, whereas ILC2s can acquire features of ILC1s and ILC3s, they mostly maintain their capacity to produce IL-13. Moreover, there is no evidence for the transdifferentiation of ILC1s and ILC3s to ILC2s. This suggests that ILC1s and ILC3s are more related to each other than they are to ILC2s.

### ILC plasticity in disease

ILCs have been associated with inflammatory diseases in various mouse models. As discussed in the previous sec- tions, the phenotype of ILCs is highly flexible. Given that the local tissue microenvironment in inflamed tissues can be very different from that of healthy non-inflamed tissues, ILCs may change their phenotype and functions as a result of inflammation. Here, we review what is known about ILC plasticity in human diseases, includ- ing some autoimmune or inflammatory diseases and cancer, and discuss the possible contribution of these altered ILCs to the pathology.

### Inflammatory diseases. The activation and selective

accumulation of ILC subsets have been observed in the inflamed tissues of patients with several types of inflam- matory disease, in which the cytokine and chemokine bal- ance is often altered. The total numbers of ILCs in these inflamed tissues often do not change when compared with non-inflamed tissues, so the changes in ILC composition in inflamed tissues may be caused by ILC plasticity105. Examples of such plasticity are provided by studies of individuals with Crohn’s disease, psoriasis and diseases of the upper and lower airways (Tabl e 1). Studies in mice have shown that IL-22 is important for the maintenance of the intestinal epithelial bar- rier, and IL-22-producing NKp44 + ILC3s are the main ILC subset in healthy mouse intestine, where they are involved in containing commensals and immune responses to extracellular bacteria 106–108 . An analogous role of IL-22-producing ILC3s in the human intes- tine can be expected but has not been shown directly. ### Concluding remarks

ILCs have emerged as important players in the regu lation of tissue homeostasis and innate immune responses. They are strategically located at mucosal and barrier surfaces and therefore support the first line of defence against infectious pathogens before the adap- tive immune response is activated. Recent advances in our understanding of ILC biology in humans and mice suggest that ILCs have a role in several inflammatory disorders. The plasticity of ILCs enables them to adapt to changing local tissue conditions, which may be essential to tune responses to different pathogenic stimuli. Given that the canonical ILC subsets are mostly tissue resident under homeostatic conditions, their plasticity enables a prompt response to changes in the microenvironment induced by pathogens without the need for de novo recruitment of ILC subsets. Resolution of inflammation may be accompanied by restoration of the original ILC subset composition. Pro- such as ILC1s in Crohn’s disease, ILC3s in psoriasis and ILC2s in chronic rhinosinusitis, are apparently unable to revert to ‘normal’ ILCs. These cells may therefore contribute to the pathology.
