**INTRODUCTION**

Inthemid-1980sMossmanandCoffmanopenedoureyestotheTcellcontrolofhighlybiasedim- muneresponses,providingmechanisticunderstandingastowhysomeindividualsmountedstrong inflammatory (Th1) responses to infection while others had (Th2) responses dominated by IgE and eosinophils. This paradigm was first applied to infectious disease with the realization that Th1 responses were needed to control intracellular pathogens such as Leishmania, whereas Th2 responses controlled infection with metazoan parasites such as intestinal roundworms. In these early days,Th1 cells were defined by the production of IFN-γ and Th2 cells by the production of IL-4. In the ensuing years, multiple discoveries vastly expanded the scope of these responses with a range of proinflammatory cytokines coming under the umbrella of type 1 immunity, including IL-17 (1). Similarly, type 2 immunity grew to include IL-4’s close relative IL-13 and many more cytokines (e.g., IL-5, IL-9, IL-10) involved in allergy and antihelminth immunity (2, 3). A funda- mental observation that persisted through this expansion of knowledge was the molecular cross regulation of Th1 and Th2 polarization. However, differential responses to infection (bacterial versus helminth) did not provide a satisfactory explanation for the need to avoid simultaneous type 1 and type 2 responses. Tissue repair provides an evolutionary context to explain why Th1 (type 1) and Th2 (type 2) pathways are so often mutually exclusive. Physical injury is by necessity inflammatory, not least because the breaking of tissue barriers brings with it the risk of microbial infection. The influx of inflammatory cells, particularly neutrophils, is needed to contain that infection, but also to scav- enge damaged cells and tissue debris. However, inflammation must be controlled for effective repair and rebuilding of the tissue (4). Type 2 immunity is both anti-inflammatory and tissue re- constructive,providingadirectcontrasttotype1responses.Inthecontextofinfection,type1(and type 17) responses control microbial infections, but they often lead to serious collateral damage due to tissue-damaging antimicrobial mediators such as reactive oxygen and nitrogen species (5). In contrast, metazoan parasites (helminths) that induce type 2 responses cause direct tissue dam- age due to their large size, motility, and release of enzymes that allow them to migrate through the tissues. Thus, in the context of evolution, to protect the host against a large moving target, a wound-reparative type 2 response would be more cost-effective than one involving the release of toxic soluble mediators. It is these ideas and others that led to our consideration of type 2 immu- nity as not only an antihelminth response but also a tissue-repair response (6, 7). These concepts are consistent with ideas that type 1 immunity takes evolutionary precedence over type 2 immu- nity (5), with type 1 immunity an essential response to life-threatening loss of structure while the type 2 response is needed to restore loss of function (8). A transition from type 1 antimicrobial responses to type 2 tissue-reparative responses involves a dynamic shift in many cell types and molecular factors, many of which have yet to be defined. Indeed type 2 immunity encompasses not only IL-4, IL-5, and IL-13 but IL-10, TGF-β,and many other features associated with a regulatory and wound-healing environment. #### WHAT CONSTITUTES TISSUE REPAIR?

Ideally, the process by which a physical injury is healed would result in full regeneration of the original tissue,but in mammals this occurs to only a limited degree and only in certain tissues (23). Instead, damaged tissue is typically replaced with a network of collagenous connective scar tissue. Because the new replacement tissue functions suboptimally, it is responsible for a vast range of chronic disease conditions (24, 25). Too much scar formation leads to fibrosis and organ failure. As such the holy grail of wound repair research is to understand what determines whether a tis- sue regenerates or whether a scar is formed. We are a long way from that answer but do know that macrophages play a central role in scar formation (26) and that type 2 immunity enhances extracellular matrix (ECM) deposition and remodeling associated with fibrosis (27).However,the specific contribution of type 2 cytokines and the cells they activate during the normal tissue repair process remains understudied. The process of tissue repair has been most thoroughly described in the skin. This is under- standable given the ready access to this tissue for experimental analysis in both humans and animal models,aswellasitsclinicalimportance.Indeedchronicwoundsremainanenormoussocietalbur- den,with remarkably little effective treatment available (24).Reviews of skin repair typically break it down into stages (28–30). The first stages involve clotting and the recruitment of inflammatory cells necessary to contain any infection. The next stage involves the deposition of a provisional matrix within the wound (granulation tissue) as well as extensive cell proliferation and growth in- cluding angiogenesis.These two stages occur rapidly,in a matter of a few days.The final stage can take from days to years and involves a highly complex remodeling of both the cellular structure and the ECM that supports it. Although the stages of skin repair provide a useful framework, it is important to recognize that injury at internal sites such as the heart and mucosal surfaces such as the lung and gastrointestinal tract will involve cells,matrix components,and repair factors unique to those sites (18, 29, 31–33).

#### Metabolism, IL-4, and Anti-Inflammation

The anti-inflammatory functions of Mϕ(IL-4) that promote wound healing relate at least in part to the metabolic impact of IL-4 and IL-13 (60). IL-4Rα activation heavily skews macrophage metabolism toward mitochondrial respiration, preferentially utilizing oxidative phosphoryla- tion to generate ATP (50, 61–63). This shift away from glycolysis is considered a hallmark of anti-inflammatory cells (64). Willenborg et al. (65) specifically tracked the metabolic state of macrophages during repair of excisional skin injury and found that as expected, macrophages in the early proinflammatory stages exhibit a higher degree of glycolysis, which switches to ox- idative phosphorylation with enhanced mitochondrial mass in response to IL-4Rα signaling as healing progresses (Figure 3). The metabolic pattern executed in Mϕ(IL-4) avoids the export of proinflammatory metabolic mediators that results from the fragmented Krebs cycle seen in M1 macrophages.Forexample,mitochondrialcitrateproducedbytheKrebscycleinM1macrophages is utilized for reactive oxygen species (ROS) and nitric oxide (NO) production (61). Thus, gener- ation of tissue-damaging ROS is restrained in Mϕ(IL-4), with mitochondrial metabolism instead directed toward ATP production and away from inflammatory ROS production (66). Perhaps the best-studied metabolic pathway regulated by IL-4Rα is the arginine metabolism pathway. Indeed, arginine metabolism in macrophages elegantly demonstrates the cross regula- tion between proinflammatory antimicrobial pathways and anti-inflammatory wound reparative pathways (67). Arginine is a substrate for the enzymes inducible nitric oxide synthase (iNOS) and arginase 1 (Arg1), which must compete for available arginine. These two enzymatic pathway result in opposing outcomes, with iNOS induced by Th1 cytokines, leading to the toxic antimi- crobial mediator NO. In contrast Arg1 metabolism generates metabolites that support collagen production and cell proliferation, which underpin wound healing. However, Arg1 is also an anti- inflammatory mediator in that it is a competitive inhibitor of iNOS (68) (Figure 3). In addition, enhanced uptake of arginine and the high affinity of arginine as substrate for Arg1 mean that IL- 4Rα signaling can effectively deplete local arginine supply (69). #### THE MATRIX-BUILDING ROLE OF IL-4/IL-13 IN TISSUE REPAIR

#### AND REMODELING

The ECM is routinely modified,broken down,and rebuilt,and this remodeling process is central to the outcome of wound repair. Multiple in vitro studies starting back in the 1990s demon- strated that IL-4 and IL-13 in both humans and mice are involved in collagen synthesis by fibroblasts (77–80). In vivo studies also supported a role for type 2 immunity in ECM remod- eling by demonstrating that Th2-polarized mice upregulate genes involved in collagen regulation (81).Understanding of a more specific role for Mϕ(IL-4) in ECM remodeling developed with the finding that they directly produce ECM components such as tenascin-C and collagens as well as ECM-remodeling enzymes (82–86). The contribution of type 2 immunity to tissue remodeling has been strengthened by the evidence that eosinophils have a profound role in tissue remodeling (15, 87, 88). Not only are eosinophils a hallmark of type 2 immunity, but they also both produce (15, 20, 87, 89) and are regulated by IL-4/IL-13 (12, 50, 90). The ECM is an enormously complex structure that makes up over a third of our body mass. It includes fibrillar molecules, such as type I collagen, which provide tissue strength; and the linear polysaccharides [glycosaminoglycans (GAGs) and proteoglycans], which fill and lubricate our ex- tracellular spaces,frequently acting as reservoirs for growth factors and cytokines (91); and mucus, which protects our barrier surfaces. ECM structures are held together and connected to the cell surface with specific adhesive proteins (e.g., fibronectin, laminin) that together form dizzyingly complex structures that differ fundamentally between tissues (92). Thus, wounding involves not only cell death but also damage to these matrix structures, which must be appropriately rebuilt. #### Collagen Remodeling by IL-13

Collagenbreakdownandsynthesisareessentialtotissueremodelingthatoccursduringthewound repair process,and IL-4Rα signaling,particularly through IL-13,appears to be involved at almost every step. Matrix metalloproteases (MMPs) are a family of zinc-dependent endopeptidases that have the capacity to degrade almost every component of the ECM, including collagens. MMP-2, -9,-12,-13,and -14 are all induced by IL-13 (98),with MMP-9,MMP-12,and MMP-13 found to be IL-13 dependent in a variety of in vivo contexts (17, 85, 86, 89, 99). While the literature tends to focus on their roles in tissue damage or aberrant collagen remodeling as seen during emphy- sema or fibrosis, MMPs play an essential role in the normal tissue repair process. For example, regional control of collagen after myocardial infarction is critical for maintaining structural in- tegrity in the damaged region while preventing scar tissue that impairs cardiac function outside the area. MMPs are thus central to ensuring that matrix accumulation does not occur in regions where it is not needed. Notably, when myocardial infarction is induced in IL-13-deficient mice, MMP-9 is reduced and collagen deposition in the scar zone significantly increases relative to wild-type mice (17). In addition to facilitating ECM turnover, MMPs also regulate cell signal- ing through their ability to enzymatically cleave bioactive molecules. For example, proreparative properties of MMP-12 may relate in part to its ability to modify the activity of proinflamma- tory molecules and thus suppress inflammation (100). Together the data demonstrate that IL-13, via MMPs, promotes collagen breakdown and suppresses proinflammatory pathways, which are important in the early stages of repair. #### Vascular Integrity

AfeatureassociatedwithanabsenceofIL-4Rα signalingisexcessivebleedingfollowingwounding. This is seen particularly in the N. brasiliensis nematode lung migration models (Figure 2)where infected mice have increased levels of airway hemorrhage in the absence of IL-4Rα or IL-13 (11,

The type 2–driven processes involved in vascular and matrix remodeling are remarkably similar to many pathways involved in generation of the tumor microenvironment (118). The contribution of IL-4Rα signaling to cancer progression, however, remains relatively unexplored but is highly relevant in the context of increasing use of biologics that block IL-4Rα.

#### Glycosaminoglycans: Hyaluronan

HA is a major structural component of the ECM and like all mammalian GAGs is a linear polysac- charide made up of repeating disaccharide units. HA is an enormous hydrophilic molecule that regulates tissue hydration and exists in a range of highly complex and dynamic structures within both the cell surface glycocalyx and the connective tissue.HA forms cable-like structures that vary in architecture and size depending on the cross-linking or adhesive molecules with which it inter- acts (92) and that change with the inflammatory status of the tissue (126).Mainly through binding to CD44, but also several other receptors including Lyve-1, HA dictates a wide range of cellular interactions.Not surprisingly,HA has proved a central player in the repair process (110),and con- version of provisional matrix from one dominated by HA to a collagen-rich structure is a critical step in determining how a wound heals (95). HA production is tightly balanced and controlled locally through CD44-mediated cellular uptake and degradation by hyaluronidases (127). If HA is not cleared by CD44 + myeloid cells following lung injury, inflammation fails to resolve (128). Accumulation of HA has been implicated in severe lung injury associated with influenza and COVID-19 (119, 129, 130), and recently IL-13 was shown to regulate HA deposition in the lung (Figure 4). In a mouse model of SARS-CoV-2, IL-13 blockade protects mice from severe disease and reduces the enzymes involved in HA synthesis as well as CD44 expression (119). **Mucins**

Mucus is an essential ECM barrier, protecting the epithelial cells of our airways, gut, eyes, and urogenital tract. Mucus is structurally formed from mucins, glycoproteins that are secreted by specialized epithelial cells (goblet cells), which upon hydration form the gel-like structures that lubricate and protect mucosal surfaces. Different mucins dominate different mucosal sites, pre- sumably related to differing functions required at the different mucosal barriers.A range of insults weaken the mucous layer, including nonsteroidal anti-inflammatory drugs such as aspirin and infections such as Helicobacter pylori infection (31) resulting in serious consequences for the un- derlying epithelial cells. Increased secretion of mucus by goblet cells is an important protective response to mucosal injury (31), and IL-13 drives goblet cell hyperplasia and the production of mucus at barrier sites (146) (Figure 4). Mucus is a highly dynamic structure that varies widely in composition and thickness depending on tissue location and the physiological or immune status of the tissue (147, 148). #### SOURCES AND DRIVERS OF IL-4 AND IL-13

This review focuses largely on effector functions of IL-13 and IL-4 in tissue repair, with macrophages,epithelialcells,andfibroblastsaskeytargets.However,IL-4Rα isexpressedonmany morecelltypesthatarelikelyplayers,andonlyaportionofthestudiesdescribedabovehaveclearly defined the target cell. A similar challenge for the field is to define the source of type 2 cytokines that drive key repair events, with the understanding that these processes are rarely if ever linear,

i.e., involving only one source, one cytokine, and one target cell. The type 2 immunity field long grappled with determining innate sources of early IL-4 (e.g., mast cells, eosinophils) involved in initiation of type 2 responses. The discovery of several cytokines (TSLP, IL-25, and IL-33) that are responsible for initiating Th2 responses (161) helped resolve that question. The field was also revolutionized by the discovery of ILC2s with transcription factor and cytokine profiles that largely phenocopy Th2 cells without the requirement for antigen (162). However, we are still left with critical questions about the roles of IL-4 and IL-13 produced by innate cells such as mast cells, basophils, and eosinophils, as well as the relative contributions of ILC2s versus Th2 cells as sources of IL-13 during repair.These questions matter for a comprehensive understanding of the contribution that type 2 immunity makes to repair but are challenging for many reasons including redundancy in repair pathways.
#### IL-33 and ILC2s

IL-33 is a profound inducer of type 2 immunity and is released from the nucleus of damaged cells, particularly blood vessel endothelial cells and epithelial cells of barrier tissues (163), consistent with a fundamental evolutionary role of type 2 immunity in repair of damaged tissue (7, 164). #### Adaptive Immunity and Th2 Cells

The concept that Th2 cells regulate tissue repair is strongly supported by the finding of an in- timate association between tissue-resident Th2 cells and IL-13Rα1 + fibroblasts in subcutaneous tissue (180). Challenges to the skin early in life, such as N. brasiliensis infection, result in expan- sion of Th2 cell and IL-13α1 + fibroblast numbers, which results in accelerated wound closure in adult mice. This robust wound repair response is accompanied by greater numbers of Arg1 + macrophages and eosinophils. A striking role for Th2 cells in repair was found in a study that highlights the link between Th2-mediated repair and ECM composition. In this study decellu- larized ECM scaffolds induced efficient repair of traumatic muscle wounds with functional tissue restoration (16). High-quality repair depended on Th2 cells, which induced Mϕ(IL-4). Consis- tent with this, the mechanical properties of wounded tissue, which are largely determined by the ECM, direct T cells to produce IL-4 and IL-13, with T cell–derived IL-13 more dependent on mechanical force than IL-4 (181). The presence of T cells in a wound suggests the need for immune memory during repair. However, the issue of antigen specificity is complex and ill-defined. Do T cells that infiltrate a wound recognize self-antigens, or pathogen-derived molecules that alert the body to the need for more rapid repair? The latter might be the case with a tissue-migrating nematode, in which one can envision that repairing the damage more quickly on second exposure would have fitness benefits. However, IL-33 can induce IL-13 independent of the T cell receptor, which may help explain the role of T cells in some situations where there is no obvious antigenic target (182). While Th2 cells can function locally without antigen, engagement of T cell receptors is needed in the draining lymph node to license the cells to subsequently respond to IL-33 and produce IL-13 (183). Thus, defining antigens relevant to tissue repair is still of considerable interest. At barriersurfaces,keyantigenictargetsmaycomefromthemicrobiome(184).Withdirectrelevance to type 2 immunity, commensal-specific CD8 + T cells in the epidermis promote tissue repair through release of IL-13 in response to tissue alarmins (185).

#### CONCLUSION AND OUTLOOK

The cytokines IL-4 and IL-13 through IL-4Rα signaling to multiple cells promote an anti- inflammatory and wound-resolving environment. It is particularly striking that many aspects of ECM quantity and quality are specifically regulated by IL-13, consistent with the well-described role of IL-13 as a major profibrotic cytokine (25).These findings suggest that the original purpose of IL-13 is to maintain and support appropriate matrix structure, a function that has expanded

*Allen*

in complexity over evolutionary time. Because unchallenged IL-13-deficient mice are overtly normal,ECM regulation by IL-13 is not a developmental necessity but rather is needed for main- tenance or remodeling of matrix structures after injury.However,it is not clear in many situations how much, if any, of the wound repair response relies specifically on IL-13 or IL-4Rα signal- ing. TGF-β and many other factors may suffice to suppress inflammation in many situations, as well as drive remodeling. Notably, IL-33, which has many overlapping functions with IL-4, can metabolicallyreprogrammacrophagestopromotehealing,independentofIL-4Rα (199).Itthere- fore remains a challenge to unravel the specific contribution of IL-4 and IL-13 in the vast range of repair scenarios where these cytokines have not yet been implicated.A model of excisional skin repair that demonstrated a clear shift from M1-like glycolytic to M2-like oxidative phosphoryla- tion macrophages also revealed enormous heterogeneity at the single-cell level,with only a subset exhibitingastrongIL-4Rα-dependentprofile(116).Thelackofapuretype2signatureinmanyin vivo settings is not surprising considering the complexity of the wound setting, and neither is the inability to detect IL-4, which is rapidly consumed. Instead experiments in mice that utilize time- and cell-specific control of expression,along with human studies involving individuals undergoing treatments with biologics against type 2 responses (200), are needed to refine our understanding of the specific role of IL-4Rα signaling in tissue repair.
