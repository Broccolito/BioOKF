#### Background & Summary

With the worldwide increase in the acquisition of neuroimaging data using magnetic resonance imaging (MRI), the use of big data is becoming a standard practice in the feld through international data-sharing initiatives 1–4 . Data sharing can bring many benefts to neuroscience research and increase sample sizes, which allow for greater precision and improve the statistical power to detect smaller efects, although smaller efects may be associated with either smaller biological efects or potentially confounding factors5. Tis has accelerated many felds into their new stages or even entirely new feld, such as the developmental population neuroscience 6,7 or statistical connectomics8. On the one hand, it can facilitate researchers to conduct test-retest studies when more neuroim- aging public databases are available and improve the reproducibility of research results 9,10 . Data reuse can also make peer review better 9,11 . On the other hand, it encourages cooperation and communication between insti- tutions and organizations, and thus creates a win-win situation for both researchers giving and receiving data12. As the concept of sharing data continues to grow, more and more public databases are emerging to sup- port researchers such as the Human Connectome Project (HCP) 13 and its derivatives across human lifespan14 (e.g., the Adolescent Brain Cognition Development, ABCD15), UK Biobank16, the Autism Brain Imaging Data Exchange (ABIDE) 17,18 , the 1000 Functional Connectomes Project19. Tese eforts on big-data sharing has pro- pelled the cognitive neurology feld forward (see more data projects from our recent reviews 20,21 ). However, most of the existing databases are based on Caucasian populations. Tis preventing us from identifying eth- nic or culture-related diferences in neuroscience associations. Moreover, China has the largest population in the world and there is still a need to expand and improve the public database of the Chinese brain mapping

Much like the genetics feld24, data-sharing faces many challenges in the neuroscience feld. One of the chal- lenges is to ensure that personal information, especially facial information, remains protected when raw MRI data are shared. Tis is especially challenging for high-resolution T1-weighted anatomical images, in which facial information has been fully embedded. Te higher the image resolution is, the clearer the facial informa- tion becomes. It has been possible to restore subject’s facial information through the combination of existing facial recognition and reconstruction technologies. As a result, personal information can easily be disclosed. Terefore, we must frst remove or obscure the facial features in brain images, especially in high-resolution structural MRI images. In response to this, the Health Insurance Portability and Accountability Act (HIPAA) of the United States specifcally stipulates that the subject’s private photos or equivalent images must be removed from the medical images to protect their privacy25. Te European Union has general data protection, which requires research institutions to strengthen the privacy protection of personal data. Terefore, data protection should be considered and implemented during the design phase of scientifc studies 24,26 . In order to ensure participant’s private security, all the aforementioned face masking algorithms are involved in taking a standard brain template as references, so as to blur the facial information in the template space. However, to date, previous studies have not dealt with whether a diferent facial mask will be generated due to a diferent ethnic template used during anonymization. Tis may refect the situation that the existing public brain imaging databases rarely contain non-western samples. In this data descriptor, we release the’I See Your Brains’ database for imaging Chinese young brains and demonstrate the template efects on face masking using head templates of diferent races. Specifcally, ethnically diverse brain templates are employed to anonymize Chinese multimodal MRI data independently. Te discrepancy between Chinese brain images anonymized using dif- ferent templates of race is quantitatively examined. We hypothesized that the use of diferent (Chinese versus Western) templates will cause signifcant diferences in the face masking performance. Use of the corresponding ethnic template will achieve a better performance in face anonymization.

#### Methods

**Participant recruitment.** Te database, Imaging Chinese Young Brains (namely, “I See Your Brains”, ISYB), is generated by the Institute of Psychology, Chinese Academy of Sciences (IPCAS), which is one contributing site of the Chinese imaging genetics cohort (CHIMGEN) 27 to enhance cross-ethnic and cross-geographic brain research. It is the largest prospective neuroimaging genetic cohort for Chinese Han adults with natural and soci- oeconomic measurements obtained from remote sensing. In accordance with CHIMGEN, all participants in ISYB were recruited by advertisements posted in colleges and communities. Participants were excluded if they met any of the following criteria: regular smoker, pregnancy, abnormal color discrimination, a history of alcohol or drug abuse, currently any medication, MRI contraindications, neuropsychiatric or severe somatic disorder and sedative-hypnotic medication within a month or any medication for major neuropsychiatric disorders. Te ISYB study was approved by the IPCAS ethical committee and written informed consent was obtained from each participant.

**Image acquisition.** ISYB contains multi-modal neuroimaging data from 241 right-handed Chinese

healthy volunteers. Each participant received MRI scans of the brain images at a 3.0 Tesla MRI scanner (GE MR750) at the IPCAS MRI Research Center including: (1) the high-resolution T1-weighted structural MRI (sMRI, matrix = 256 × 256, number of slices = 188, feld of view = 256 × 256 mm, repetition time = 8.16 ms, echo time = 3.18 ms, flip angle = 12°, inversion time = 450 ms), (2) resting-state functional MRI (rfMRI, matrix = 64 × 64, number of slices = 36, field of view = 220 × 220 mm, repetition time = 2000ms, echo time = 30 ms, fip angle = 90°), (3) difusion tensor MRI (dMRI, matrix = 128 × 128, number of slices = 50, feld of view = 256 × 256 mm, repetition time = 6000 ms, echo time = 65 ms, fip angle = 90°, 64 difusion-sensitisation directions at b = 1000 with b = 0), and (4) arterial spin labeling MRI (aMRI, matrix = 128 × 128, number of slices = 50, feld of view = 240 × 240 mm, repetition time = 5046 ms, echo time = 11.09 ms, fip angle = 111°, inversion time = 2025 ms).

**Quality assurance.** All participants were included in the frst-step quality assessments of the neuroimaging data. Teir anatomical T1 sMRI images were visually inspected to ensure no substantial head motion and struc- tural abnormalities. Two participants were excluded for excessive head motion. In the second-step quality assess- ments, the T1 sMRI and rfMRI image quality of ISYB datasets were examined using the MRIQC toolkit (https:// github.com/poldracklab/mriqc), which incorporated a series of quantitative metrics as in the following list. More details of these metrics can be found in the previous work 28,29 .

##### •	 Spatial Metrics (sMRI, rfMRI):

1. Signal-to-Noise Ratio (SNR)
30 : Te mean value of gray matter divided by the standard deviation (SD) of the air.

2. Foreground to Background Energy Ratio (FBER): Mean energy of image values (i.e., mean of squares) within the head relative to outside the head.
3. Contrast-to-Noise Ratio (CNR) (only sMRI) : Calculated as the mean of the gray matter values minus the mean of the white matter values, divided by the standard deviation of the air values.
4. Entropy Focus Criteria (EFC) : Shannon’s entropy is used to summarize the principal directions distribution.
5. Smoothness of Voxels32: Te full-width half maximum (FWHM) of the spatial distribution of image intensities.

**Standard brain templates.** Taking into consideration on the global scope of the open science commu- nity and data-sharing initiatives, it is critical to realize that the face masking procedure initially uses Western head template as reference. Extensive research has shown that the race factor played a major role when com- paring the group diferences in brain morphology metrics. For example, previous studies have indicated signif- cant morphological diferences between Chinese and Western brains in size35, shape36, and anatomical volume37.

Caucasian brains are signifcantly longer but present a smaller width and a decreased height compared with Chinese brains 23,38 . Tus, taking templates of diferent races as reference during registration exerts a signifcant impact on the results of the registration, which may lead to systematic diferences in subsequent morphological measurements39. Selecting ethnicity-matched templates to analyze structural MRI data may be preferable, which could improve the accuracy of the registration and obtain robust morphological results40. Tis is attributed to the diferences in the brain structure between China and the West population, which cause some systematic bias in the registration between individual images and template images and reduce the accuracy in morphological meas- urements. Such bias has been reproduced in diverse samples across imaging protocols and lifespan studies 41–44 . Ethnicity-specifc brain templates are discrepant regarding the diversity in the brain structures between the East and the West. To test the template efects on face masking, we chose two Chinese brain templates (CN200 and Chinese2020): (1) CN200 is a head template assembled by MRI T1 images using the 3 T Siemens Prisma scanner from 200 healthy Chinese Han participants from the Chinese Human Connectome Project23; (2) Chinese2020 is a head template generated based on the MRI T1 images under multiple 1.5 T magnets collected from more than 2,000 healthy participants across multiple centers and regions45. Te MNI152 head template was used as the Western template, which is the default head template as in the face masking toolkit.

**Face anonymization.** In order to efectively blur the part of the face in images and protect privacy as much as possible, researchers have developed several toolkits to remove facial information. For example, Quickshear segments brain images into facial features and brain, and confgures the voxels of the facial features to 0, thus resulting in identifable facial features cut efectively25. FreeSurfer determines the location of facial information and creates a brain template afer manually separating brain tissue from non-brain tissue. In the subsequent images, the voxel position of non-brain tissue is obtained through the established linear transformation relation- ship and the voxel value of the facial information position is eliminated46. Although these two methods efectively remove facial privacy information, they also lose some areas of the original image at the same time (e.g., intrac- ranial volume and cerebrospinal fuid volume), which may cause the problem of lacking important anatomical features when calculating certain structural metrics. To address this privacy concern, Milchenko and colleagues have developed a deface toolkit, namely face masking, which extracts the anatomical surface of the face in the volume space and then obscures it47. Te recognition rate of the image is reduced by changing the image resolu- tion, leading to ideal anonymization of the data without disturbing reliability and validity of subsequent tissue extraction and measurements of brain morphology 48–51 . Tis procedure has become a built-in function of the Extensible Neuroimaging Archive Toolkit52 for facial anonymization and been incorporated into the Human Connectome Project53. For these reasons mentioned above, when data in the ISYB database were anonymized for facial information, we adopted the CN200 Chinese template. Te purpose of which is to remove more privacy and obtain a better anonymization efect for facial information in Chinese brain data. Te results and discussion of data anonymization based on diferent ethnic brain templates are included in Usage Notes session. Te ISYB data are processed by the face masking toolkit.

#### Data Records

As supports of the open sciences, we have released the multimodal neuroimaging data of the 215 participants in the ISYB database for both research and education in human brain mapping as well as culture association studies. Specifcally, as demonstrated in the previous sections, we frst applied the face masking pipeline based on CN200 brain template to anonymize the MRI data by blurring facial information while all the participants agreed to share their anonymized data to the public. We then organized the data according to the Brain Imaging Data Structure (BIDS: [https://bids.neuroimaging.io](https://bids.neuroimaging.io)) 54 and employed the MRIQC toolkit for monitoring the quality of all structural and functional images. Tis toolkit generated comprehensive reports on the data quality, which were presented as in the Supplementary Fig. 1 and Supplementary Fig. 2 at group level. All the BIDS-formatted data and related fles on the QC reports have been publicly shared through the Science Data Bank (SciDB) at the National Basic Science Data Center ([https://doi.org/10.11922/sciencedb.00740](https://doi.org/10.11922/sciencedb.00740)) 55 . Te ISYB_DATA folder contains 215 folders labeled with participant ID numbers (e.g., sub-0002). Each par- ticipant’s folder contains subfolders labeled func containing the rfMRI images (.nii.gz), anat containing defaced T1-weighted sMRI images (.nii.gz), perf containing aMRI images (.nii.gz), dwi containing dMRI images (.nii. gz,.bval,.bvec). Te ISYB_QC folder contains QC reports generated by MRIQC toolkit for each participant. All QC results were integrated into webpages, and linked in an overview page on group analysis. Of note, the cur- rent ISYB release contained the demographic information (age and sex) but did not include any behavioral and clinical measurements.

#### T echnical Validation

As in the Consortium for Reliability and Reproducibility (CoRR) 9 , we demonstrate the utility of the released ISYB multi-modal MRI data. All the anonymized and quality-controlled data were preprocessed using the Connectome Computation System (CCS56, [https://github.com/zuoxinian/CCS](https://github.com/zuoxinian/CCS)) with the most recent updates57. Tis pipeline integrates multiple analytical sofware packages to achieve imaging processing of multi-modal MRI data. Considering the advantages of surface-based functional brain mapping58, we reconstructed cortical surface models to generate NIFTI fle for structural and functional metrics on the cortical surface. Te structural image went through the following preprocessing steps: (1) spatially adaptive non-local means denoising, (2) rough inhomogeneity correction, (3) spatial normalization into the MNI standard brain space, (4) inhomogeneity correction, (5) intensity normalization, (6) brain extraction by non-local intracranial cavity extraction (NICE), and (7) gray and white matter segmentation, surface reconstruction. Te rfMRI image preprocessing included

(1) dropping of the frst 5 EPI volumes, (2) removing and interpolating temporal spikes, (3) correcting acqui- sition timing among image slices and head motion among image volumes, (4) normalizing the 4D global mean

intensity to 10,000, (5) regressing out head motion artifacts and other spurious noise by using ICA-AROMA 59,60 , and (6) removing linear and quadratic trends from the rfMRI signals to mitigate the scanner-related infuences56. Te preprocessed NIFTI images were converted into the GIFTI format using the Cifify toolbox61. Te sMRI images were further preprocessed using the custom pipeline to generate highly refned cortical surface meshes which were spatially normalized to the MNI space and re-sampled to have 32k vertices, i.e., the fsaverage_LR32 cortical surface. Individual dMRI and aMRI images were preprocessed in individual volume space and then transformed onto the fsaverage_LR32 space. Individual surface-mapped rfMRI signals were then brought into the register across participants using a multi-modal surface matching algorithm to the and vectorized into the CIFTI format. Tis maps each surface vertex to an index in a vector by the Cifify toolbox. A group-level surface mask was fnally established by including every vertex where all the 215 participants obtained the full rfMRI signals. Based on the surface-based data, we derived two sets of brain imaging metrics using CCS for both struc- tural and functional characteristics, respectively. Specifcally, following derivatives were calculated:

• Cortical Tickness (CT) 62 : Te average distance between the gray/white boundary and the pial surface within each ROI. • Surface Area (SA) 62 : Te sum of the areas of each tessellation falling within each ROI. • Gray Matter Volume (GMV): Te measure of the density of brain cell body in a particular region. • Local Gyrifcation Index (LGI) 63 : Te ratio of a regional surface area for the pial surface to a smooth surface contour estimated to wraps around the pial surface. • Fractional Anisotropy (FA) 64 : An index for the amount of difusion asymmetry within a voxel, defned in terms of its eigenvalues. • Mean Difusivity (MD) 64 : Te mean amount of difusion in each of the principal directions calculated in the tensor. • Regional Homogeneity (ReHo) 58 : Te synchronicity of a voxel’s time series and that of its nearest neighbors based on Kendall’s coefcient of concordance to measure the local brain functional homogeneity. • Voxel-Mirrored Homotopic Connectivity (VMHC) 65 : Te functional connectivity between a pair of geomet- rically symmetric, inter-hemispheric voxels. • Amplitude of Low-Frequency Fluctuations (ALFF) 66 : Te total power in the low frequency range (0.01–

0.1 Hz) of an fMRI image, normalized by the total power across all frequencies measured in that same image.
• Cerebral Blood Flow (CBF) 67 : Te rate of delivery of arterial blood to the capillary bed in brain tissue and is quantifed in milliliters of blood per 100 g of brain tissue per minute.

We note that each metric was calculated within the group-level surface mask. Te CT metric was gener- ated and extracted from the structural outcomes of cifify in the fsaverage_LR32k space. Te LGI metric was produced by the FreeSurfer scripts ([https://surfer.nmr.mgh.harvard.edu/fswiki/LGI](https://surfer.nmr.mgh.harvard.edu/fswiki/LGI)). Te SA, ReHo, ALFF and VMHC metrics were calculated by the CCS scripts from the sMRI and rfMRI outcomes of cifify. CCS also provided a computational workfow for dMRI data to derive both FA and MD for gray matter and white mat- ter voxels in individual volume spaces. Te results were named as FA-WM, MD-WM, FA-GM and MD-GM, respectively. Te bbregister command in FreeSurfer was then applied to the native individual maps of CBF, SA, FA and MD to register these individual volume maps onto the fsaverage surface template. Te wb_command **-metric-resample command in the HCP workbench was used to map the native individual CBF, SA, FA and** MD metrics to fs_LR and resample them to the 32k space. Te vertex-wise group-level maps across all qualifed participants are shown on the fsLR_32k surface as in Fig. 3 (mean) and Fig. 4 (SD).

**Guidance and implication.** With the continuous development of neuroimaging, the distinction in the brain structure across diferent races can become more refned. Te comparison of templates of diferent races provides a basis for more in-depth research on the distinctions of separate races. However, many challenges have also emerged, especially when the private information is easily leaked through images. At the same time, due to the continuous development and maturity of deep learning technology, combined with big data, the entire face can be reconstructed and refabricated using a small portion of facial information. Tis forces us to pay special attention to protection of sub- jects’ private information, especially facial information. Masking the face of the data by using the face masking toolkit efectively blur the subjects’ faces, thereby protecting privacy. Meanwhile, it protects the integrity of the brain structure, which makes the structure MRI easier to interface with mainstream brain image analysis sofware. Follow-up analysis is more convenient without degenerating image quality. Since skull shape difers by racial and ethnic groups in length and width68, systematic biases may be introduced when images of diferent races are registered to the same template in the face masking toolkit. Tis kind of registration diference based on data and templates which are not in the same race has been emphasized in preceding articles, which is inevitable due to genetic factors41. Tis study assumed that using difer- ent human brain templates to anonymize facial data would yield diferent results69. We selected 215 data from Chinese database and three brain templates from two races, and used ethnicity-matched and ethnicity-mismatched templates to blur the facial information of the data, explored the impact of diferent ethnic templates on data anonymization.

(2) Choosing a template that matches the race of the data will achieve better results in comparison to with a mismatched template when using diferent templates to anonymize data. In other words, the template selected during anonymization should correspond to the race of the data so that better private infor- mation anonymity results can be achieved. In the process of masking the face, the surface mask algorithm pro- posed by Budin and colleagues was used and performed70. Te face area was identifed and positioned, and then smoothed by using a blur algorithm to obtain a contaminated face image. However, in the face mask algorithm, the data need to be registered on the specifc template, and then the brain tissue position and skull boundary information could be found through the binary image, thereby blurring the part between the brain and skull. Te registration efect varies from race to race, resulting in diferent positions of facial features and causing disparate phenomena based on diferent templates when using templates of diferent races as a reference to registration. Te efect of facial anonymization varies by brain templates to a large extent, the results demonstrate that there exist a diference in the efect of facial masking based on two Chinese templates, the diference may attributed to the fact that the two Chinese templates are constructed using data obtained with diferent feld strengths (1.5 T and

3.0 T). What we want to emphasis is that the anonymization efect is determined by the ethnicity of the templates. Te brain structure of individuals from diferent ethnic group is quite diferent. Although diferent templates can be used to anonymize structure MRI data, the use of ethnicity-matched templates will lead to the best anonymi- zation efect. Tis also demonstrated that the accuracy of brain image data processing could be enhanced by using the ethnicity-appropriate templates. Improved facial anonymization, conserving less information, has been demon- strated when the Chinese head template is applied to obscure the faces of data acquired in a Chinese population. Te customized pipeline using a native Chinese head template is thus recommended for the anonymization of sharing Chinese neuroimaging data. International data-sharing and the relevant large-scale team work on brain imaging such as building brain charts for the human lifespan (see a recent review21 and progress71) must consider this efect care- fully. Its impact on the generalizability of brain-mind association studies
10,72 is also an interesting topic in the future.
