#### THE STANDARD WORKFLOW

A general framework for using WGS in epidemiology has been developed for culturable bacte- ria (Figure 2) (63, 71). We provide a brief overview and then highlight steps that are critical for drawing well-supported conclusions. We focus primarily on using WGS to infer relationships of bacterial strains collected over short periods of time to provide information about direct trans- mission routes, e.g., between sites, but touch on differences in approaches for studying strains collected over long periods of time, e.g., historical studies or global spread of disease. Sexually reproducing, obligate, or unculturable pathogens introduce additional challenges that we briefly discuss below. Currently, the general workflow is based around short-read (∼150 nucleotides) se- quencing technology. Standard software tools are available for filtering individual reads on the basis of quality and removing adapter and barcode sequences, leaving only the most confidently called sequences of a read ([https://sourceforge.net/projects/bbmap/](https://sourceforge.net/projects/bbmap/); 10). The next task is to identify single nucleotide polymorphisms (SNPs) to determine differences between genome sequences (see sidebar titled Variations in Mutation Rates Among Pathogenic Bacteria). Reference-based methods align reads from each sample to a common genome se- quence (55, 56). Sequence differences, relative to the reference, are identified and thresholds in coverage at that position or statistical models are used to call SNPs that are significant (57, 61). Pairwise genetic distances between strains are inferred by determining the number of SNPs, rel- ative to the reference sequence, that differentiates them. These pairwise distances are finally used to assign strains to genotypes on the basis of a maximum threshold of SNP differences.Minimum spanning networks of genotypes or phylogenies of strains can then be generated to visualize rela- tionships (48). #### CHALLENGES FOR USES IN AGRICULTURE

Use of genomic epidemiology in agriculture is promising, but there remain challenges that need to be overcome. Genetic links are insufficient by themselves to provide information about trans- mission patterns. In healthcare settings, patients and individuals that may have been contacted can be interviewed to gather more information to inform on genomic patterns. In agricultural settings, growers can be interviewed but they may not be able to keep track of all the interactions with plants because of the volume they handle. Metadata, such as location of isolation, original source, current location of plant materials, and time of isolation, are necessary to draw any conclusions on pathogen movement or spread. Nurseries selling plant material may be required to maintain sales records for a period of time. Even so, genetic data cannot be used to conclude a direct link or the directionality of transmission, only that strains of the same genotype are found in separate locations. Genomic epidemiology in agriculture must be coupled with meticulous record keeping and diagnostics that can be done in real-time to provide sufficient resolution to guide epidemiological studies. Culturable pathogenic bacteria provide excellent opportunities to guide best practices when implementing genomic epidemiology and diagnostics but are only a fraction of the organisms that cause disease in plants. We need to develop methods for using WGS to trace and diagnose unculturable bacteria,viruses,fungi,and oomycetes.For unculturable bacteria,metagenomics has potential in mitigating dependencies on culturing and can be used to support diagnostics. Extra steps need to be taken to enrich for microbes and reduce the amount of biomass from the host. Moreover, new computational methods and statistical tests are necessary to classify reads, asso- ciate them with candidate pathogens, and provide measures of uncertainty. This is an open area of investigation with tremendous opportunities. Viruses are challenging not only because they cannot be cultured but also because they have higher mutation rates, and many plant-associated viruses have RNA-based genomes.Fortunately,significant progress has been made in refining the Downloaded from www.annualreviews.org. Fungal and oomycete pathogen genomes are substantially larger than those of bacteria and tend to have more repeated sequences,which are challenges that can be mitigated by long-read se- quencing technology.But currently,costs and error rates of these technologies are too high for use in disease epidemiology and diagnostics of these organisms.Nonetheless,analyses of their whole- genomesequenceshaveledtothedevelopmentofpossibleapproachesforaddressingfeaturesthat make use of WGS in diagnosis more difficult than for diagnosis of bacteria.These approaches can

be implemented once diagnosis of fungi and oomycetes becomes cost-effective. WGS has been used to identify possible origins and likely transmission routes of fungal and oomycete pathogens (18, 29, 33, 41, 51, 89). These studies relied on the analysis of multiple marker genes or core genomic regions that provide information about ancestry. As previously discussed, although suit- able for uncovering large spatiotemporal transmission patterns, this approach has less utility for identifying epidemiological links in real-time. Another challenge is the variation in ploidy that can be present within a species. The depth of read coverage can be used to infer copy number variation and has been used to understand relationships among reproductive strategies, disease, and geographical distribution (51). Some fungal phytopathogens exhibit presence/absence poly- morphisms of entire chromosomes,some of which have been implicated in virulence (31,68).We have already discussed how variation in presence/absence can impact WGS-based epidemiology (Figure 3). This instability of genomes is analogous to gain and loss of mobile genetic elements in bacteria and can be analyzed in a similar fashion.

#### FUTURE STEPS

There are some important barriers that need to be overcome to adopt WGS as a standard method in plant disease epidemiology and diagnostics. Correct and careful interpretation of whole-genome data is critical when used for actionable insights.Diagnostic plant clinics currently operate according to standard protocols to ensure high-quality,reliable results.Similar guidelines and protocols need to be developed for approaches that generate and use WGS data.Laboratories that trace human pathogens have developed standards for preparing samples (4,28,39,54,63,92). Some of these can be adopted for use in agriculture. However, different pathogen groups require different solutions, many of which are as yet outside the realm of implementation in a diagnos- tic setting. Importantly, correct and careful interpretation of data requires experts cross-trained in data sciences and plant pathology. One expertise cannot replace the other and the two need to be integrated. This should be addressed at multiple levels. Undergraduate and graduate plant pathology programs need to provide students more opportunities to learn skills in data sciences to gain familiarity in working with large genomic data sets and communicating effectively with experts in other disciplines. Universities need to develop new structures that promote alliances between diagnostics laboratories and those with expertise in data sciences. Regulatory agencies should invest in infrastructure to promote interdisciplinary collaborations. Other important barriers that need to be addressed are data sharing and the infrastructure to interact with the data. In the United States, data privacy is ensured by constraints agreed upon by the allied diagnostic laboratories and state regulatory agencies. These constraints would presumably apply to WGS data generated by diagnostic laboratories. The value of these data would be even greater if housed in databases available to the public in open-science platforms (46,

99). Ideally, they would become available with common standards for sharing data in real-time about each pathogen group. Crowdsourcing can help grow databases, which is particularly
Downloaded from www.annualreviews.org. Guest (guest) IP: 104.52.5.246 On: Fri, 26 Jun 2026 06:45:53 important when making inferences about newly emerging strains. Background populations need to be substantially sampled to make inferences about patterns and processes involved in migration and emergence. Open science and crowdsourcing can facilitate the recruitment of experts in diverse disciplines who bring new perspectives and new solutions to problems. Large databases require curators and an integrated cyberinfrastructure framework that allows end users easy access via integrated workflows. The workflows also need tools that allow users to easily visualize data as well as methods to automate flagging of newly emerging plant pathogens or reemerging pathogens. But making the data publicly available can compromise data privacy. Stripping away

identifying information can address data privacy but doing so can significantly compromise the utility of the data. A hierarchical approach for data access sharing, where entities would have access to some data and not others, would help alleviate fears of misuse of identifying data but would require agreement across multiple entities who may have conflicting interests.

#### SUMMARY POINTS

1. WGS data are used in healthcare settings and by a network of labs to mitigate risks of diseases to humans.
2. Genome-based approaches have been used to retrospectively uncover local and global transmission patterns of plant pathogens.
3. Genome-based approaches have potential uses in the diagnostics of plant diseases.
4. Understanding evolutionary processes that shape pathogen genomes and populations is fundamental for disease epidemiology and diagnostics.
5. Defining goals of studies and knowing limitations of methods are critical for properly translating findings from WGS to application.
6. An evolutionary framework helps minimize errors in using WGS to trace pathogen transmission patterns.
7. Each pathogen group may require unique approaches, standards, and technologies for WGS-based epidemiology and diagnostics.
#### FUTURE ISSUES

1. Evolutionary and ecological relationships among nonpathogenic and pathogenic mem- bers of species in agricultural settings must be resolved.
2. Investments must be made to facilitate changes at all levels of training to effectively use WGS in agriculture.
3. There are needs for statistical methods to replace ad hoc methods currently used in WGS-based epidemiology.
4. There are needs for new computational and statistical workflows for unculturable bac- teria, viruses, fungi, and oomycetes.
5. There are needs for standards in workflow, data quality, and data sharing.
6. Communities of experts need to form to develop databases and workflows for each pathogen group.
