### Abstract

Plant height (PH) is an essential trait in maize (Zea mays) that is tightly associated with planting density, biomass, lodging resistance, and grain yield in the field. Dissecting the dynamics of maize plant architecture will be beneficial for ideotype-based maize breeding and prediction, as the genetic basis controlling PH in maize remains largely unknown. In this study, we developed an automated high-throughput phenotyping platform (HTP) to systematically and noninvasively quantify 77 image-based traits (i-traits) and 20 field traits (f-traits) for 228 maize inbred lines across all developmental stages. Time-resolved i-traits with novel digital phenotypes and complex correlations with agronomic traits were characterized to reveal the dynamics of maize growth. ### INTRODUCTION

Maize (Zea mays) is one of the most important crops for food, feed, and fuel worldwide. Although maize production has increased eight-fold in the past century (Haley, 2011; Xiao et al., 2017), accommodating world population growth and global climate change will require a yield increase in global cereal production. Plant architecture is one of the important agronomic traits that directly influence grain yield in maize (Li et al., 2020b; Le et al.,

2022). Plant height (PH)-associated traits, such as ear height, node number, and internode length, which correlate closely with biomass, grain yield, lodging resistance, and stress tolerance, have been largely determined (Zheng and Liu, 2013; Li et al., 2014b; Ku et al., 2015; Zhou et al., 2016). For example, the development of wheat and rice varieties with semi-dwarf genes (rht1 and sd1) in the first Green Revolution increased crop yields dramatically (Peng et al., 1999; Khush, 2001; Sasaki et al., 2002). It has been reported that improvements in maize PH and architecture are expected to increase productivity and grain yield (Lima et al., 2006; Zhang et al., 2006). Therefore, identification of genes related to PH will enable targeted genetic modifications. However, it is very challenging to map the natural PH-associated variation in maize because of the complexity of PH-related traits. PH-related loci have been cloned and characterized by molecular tagging of mutagenesis- induced, mostly large-effect, alleles (Salas Fernandez et al., 2009; Andorf et al., 2010). More than 50 maize genes are known to participate in PH regulation (Maize GDB). Most genes affect the synthesis, transport, and signaling of the plant hormones gibberellin (GA), abscisic acid, and brassinosteroid (Winkler and Helentjaris, 1995; Multani et al., 2003; Wang and Li, 2008; Lawit et al., 2010; Hartwig et al., 2011). By quantitative trait locus (QTL) mapping, relevant genomic regions associated with PH and ear height traits have been identified. However, only a few genes have been cloned, including ZmRPH1 (Reducing Plant Height
1), ZmGA3ox2 (Gibberellin 3-beta-dioxygenase 2), ZmTSH4 (Tasselsheath4), and ZmACS7 Mol Plant. Author manuscript; available in PMC 2025 February 06.

(1-aminocyclopropane-1-carboxylate synthase 7) (Teng et al., 2013; Chuck et al., 2014;

### Author Manuscript

Li et al., 2020a, 2020b). PH growth rates vary throughout development, especially at the important seedling, jointing, flowering, and mature stages. Although some genes or QTLs associated with PH have been cloned based on phenotypes measured manually at the mature stage, comprehensive, systematic characterization of dynamic PH-related traits across all growth phases has not yet been performed, and the genetic mechanisms that underlie maize

### Author Manuscript

PH diversity are still largely unknown.

Next-generation sequencing, QTL mapping, and genome-wide association studies (GWAS) are powerful tools for elucidating the genetic architecture of complex traits in plants (Li et al., 2013; Wen et al., 2014). However, lack of phenotypic data has prevented genetic

### Author Manuscriptdissection of key multi-gene agronomic traits in crop genomes. Plant phenomics has relied

on non-destructive and accurate acquisition of large-scale, high-dimensional phenotypic data throughout plant development (Houle et al., 2010). Combined with GWAS and QTL mapping, high-throughput phenotyping has proven to be an effective tool for investigating the genetic basis of abiotic/biotic stress and yield assessment at the population level in Author Manuscript different plant species (Chen et al., 2014; Honsdorf et al., 2014; Klukas et al., 2014). A high- throughput plant phenotyping platform (HTP) and open-source image analysis systems were developed to quantify phenotypic traits at the vegetative growth stage in maize (Muraya et al., 2017; Zhang et al., 2017; Wu et al., 2021). However, the dynamic genetic architecture of plant growth throughout all developmental stages and its contributions to final PH and yield in maize remain to be investigated.

Machine learning is a subset of artificial intelligence and has emerged with big data technologies and high-performance computing to create new opportunities in the multi- disciplinary agri-technologies domain that will revolutionize crop improvement (Hickey et al., 2017; Grinberg et al., 2020; Tong and Nikoloski, 2021; Yan and Wang, 2022). Predictive models built using various computational methodologies have been used as an effective approach to facilitate data-driven decision-making for phenotype prediction (Desta and Ortiz, 2014; Gonzalez-Recio et al., 2014). Therefore, considerable attempts need to be made to address the bottlenecks associated with phenotyping of complex traits through computer-aided image acquisition for final phenotype prediction in maize breeding (Xiao et al., 2021).

To further understand the dynamics of PH-related traits in maize, we combined high- throughput phenotyping data collected from 228 inbred lines across all developmental stages with genotyping data from GWAS analysis to dissect the genetic architecture of complex agronomic traits. A PH-association network, established from the GWAS results, revealed the i-trait hub and the hub genes. The haplotypes of nine traits for seven candidate genes were found in maize. Transcriptome profiles of specific internodes indicate that these hub genes, including ZmVATE (V-type proton ATPase subunit E), ZmPHD1 (PHD-type zinc finger family protein 1), ZmHmg2 (High mobility group box protein2), and ZmTBL10 (Protein trichome birefringence-like 10), may play vital roles during rapid growth. The 49 novel i-traits measured in multiple growth stages may be used as indicators for final PH in maize. Functional analysis of one candidate gene, ZmVATE, in regulation of maize PH development using an ethyl methane sulfonate (EMS) mutation further confirmed the power

of the prediction provided in this research. In addition, machine learning-based predictive

### Author Manuscript

models using i-traits acquired from an HTP were built to predict final PH from early S4, with rapidly increasing prediction accuracy along developmental stages. Taken together, our data demonstrate that combining HTP and GWAS with a predictive model is a novel approach for dissecting the dynamic genetic architecture of maize throughout all growth phases and will be useful for design breeding and genetic improvement in maize.

### Author Manuscript RESULTS

#### Automated HTP for maize across all growth stages

An HTP with a 456-pot capacity was built and used to measure whole-plant phenotypes

### Author Manuscript

across all developmental phases of maize. We extracted morphological traits and biomass information through visible light imaging and high-capacity computing (Figure 1A and Supplemental Video 1). To understand the genetic basis of maize growth throughout development, we obtained 228 accessions from natural maize populations under greenhouse conditions and used the HTP to monitor individual plants (Supplemental Table 1). Each Author Manuscriptplant was inspected in front and side-view (90° from the front view) images and one top-view image using a Basler RGB camera (Basler AG, Ahrensburg, Germany; Pilot piA2400–17gc) in an image chamber (Figure 1A and 1B). To acquire traits at all stages for all investigated accessions, two replicates of each accession were examined once daily from seed germination to plant maturity, resulting in ~244 000 images and yielding ~487 Gb of data. Processed images of 77 image-based digital traits (i-traits) were collected (Figure 1C and 1D). These traits were further divided into 3 categories: 49 morphological i-traits, 20 biomass-related i-traits, and 8 growth-related i-traits (Supplemental Table 2). The 49 morphological traits were divided into two subgroups. Traditional morphological traits (19) had been identified as associated with PH in previous field experiments (Wei et al.,

2018), including image-based PH (i-PH), number of internodes (i-NI), number of leaves (i-NL), length of tassel (i-LT), ear height (i-EH), length of the first internode above the ear (i-LUpL), length of the first internode under the ear (i-LUnL), length of each internode (i-LI1–11), and mean length of all internodes (i-MLI). Among the 49 morphological traits, 30 were novel plant morphological traits, e.g., image-based object extent Y (i-OEY), object extent X (i-OEX), caliper length (i-CL), convex hull circumference (i-CHC), and minimum enclosing circle diameter (i-MECD), as listed in Supplemental Table 2. In addition, we manually measured 20 field traits (f-traits) from all investigated maize accessions at the maturation stage as defined in Supplemental Table 3. These i-traits and f-traits that were further analyzed by GWAS reflect changes in plant architecture during development.
#### Phenotypic variation of plant architecture traits in maize

To investigate i-trait variation related to plant architecture, we collected images of all maize accessions at nine representative time points during the growth phase, stage 1 (S1) to S9, which corresponded to the vegetative 3 (V3, third leaf), V5 (fifth leaf), V7 (seventh leaf), V10 (tenth leaf), V14 (rapid growth), VT (tassel), reproductive 1 (R1, silking), R3 (milk), and R6 (maturity) maize stages, respectively (Figure 2A, Supplemental Table 1, and Supplemental Videos 2, 3, 4, and 5). The extracted images of two inbred maize lines (B73 and Mo17) illustrate the overall developmental process (Supplemental Videos 2 and 3). We

first evaluated plant architecture dynamic variance in the tallest inbred line, the shortest

### Author Manuscript

inbred line, and inbred line B73, which is widely used in maize genetic and genomic studies (Figure 2A and Supplemental Figure 4A). The values of numerous i-traits in all inbred lines differed significantly across the nine growth stages, as shown in the large heatmap (Figure 2B). For example, i-OEY, defined by the length in the Y direction of the maize plant used to quantify the PH, differed significantly across multiple time points (p values

ranging from 0.46 to 2.22 × 10−16, paired-samples t-test) (Figure 2C). Notably, we found that the mean value of i-OEY increased quickly by 2-fold from S2 to S3 and 1.37-fold from S5 to S6 (Figure 2C). However, i-OEY increased slowly in a range from 1.00 to 1.28 at other stages, indicating that S2 and S3 and S5 and S6 might be key stages for rapid height development. To further explore the potential of i-traits to reflect fast growth, we

These results suggest that these i-traits may be effective indicators of rapid maize growth.

#### Spatiotemporal developmental patterns and maize growth rate

By analyzing data from the time-series images and the height changes across nine time points, we found that the most rapid increases in PH occurred from S2 to S3 (24 cm) and S5 to S6 (41 cm) (Figure 2C). Consistent with this observation, we noticed that some accessions, such as Mo17, grew faster at the early developmental stage, and others, such as B73, grew faster at the late developmental stage (Supplemental Figure 3 and Supplemental Videos 2 and 3). Most accessions underwent the greatest elongation and most rapid growth at S5 to S6 (Figure 2C), suggesting that specific growth patterns and faster growth occurred from S5 to S6. All internodes make some contribution to mature maize PH (Ganesan et al., 2012; Alem et al., 2015; Silva et al., 2019). To determine the primary internode(s) that were elongating during S5 to S6, we analyzed the Pearson correlation coefficients (PCCs) among the image-based elongated PH (i-PHS6-S5) and the elongated length of each internode (S6 minus S5) (Supplemental Figure 4B). We found that the correlation coefficients of i-LI5(S6-S5), i-LI6(S6-S5), and i-LI7(S6-S5) with i-PH(S6-S5) were markedly higher than the others, suggesting that the elongation of these three internodes (LI5–LI7) made the greatest contribution to the increase in PH (Supplemental Figure 4B). Notably, these three internodes were near the ear position, suggesting that the specific internode patterning could be used as an effective predictor of final PH in maize.

#### Identification of novel PH-related traits

We verified the congruence of the automatic digital measurements of i-OEY and the manual measurements of i-PH by making a manual measurement immediately after imaging. The best model for predicting PH was first determined using 100 randomly selected accessions. Four models, linear, exponential, quadratic, and logarithmic, were evaluated

using the adjusted coefficient of determination (adjusted R2) (Supplemental Table 4).

R 2 values between i-PH and i-OEY ranged from 0.75 to 0.93. Cross-validation results of other samples showed that R2 was increased to 0.94 (Supplemental Table 4). The quadratic model had the highest i-PH and i-OEY R2 values (Supplemental Table 4) and was therefore selected to build the PH predictive model. Scatterplots showed that the R2 between manual and automatic measurements for PH was not less than 0.94 throughout

all stages (Supplemental Figure 5A). Similarly, i-OEX also showed an apparent correlation with adjusted PH (p < 2.2 × 10−16) (Supplemental Figure 5B), and the R2 between i-PH and i-OEY from the correlation analysis was greater than 0.96 at the same developmental stages (Figure 3B). In addition, the R2 was 0.87 for i-OEY based on data collected in two different years (Supplemental Figure 6A). The replicate datasets from two consecutive

years showed that fast growth occurred at two identical stages (Supplemental Figure 6B), further demonstrating the reliability and robustness of the HTP methodology. Together, these results suggest that automatic image-based measurements are accurate and effective. More importantly, HTP demonstrated high efficiency and throughput with accurate acquisition and analysis of multi-dimensional phenotypes on an organism-wide scale during crop growth in

different years.

PH-related traits are determinants of plant fitness and agricultural performance. Prediction of final PH from maize traits measured at early developmental stages could be helpful for maize breeding. Both traditional morphological traits and novel i-traits might be utilized to assess plant architecture. As shown in Figure 3A and Supplemental Table 5, correlation analysis of i-traits in three categories revealed that some novel i-traits had clear correlations with traditional morphological traits. In addition, i-PH was significantly correlated with image-based growth rate (i-GR, R = 0.94) (Figure 3C). The PH acquired from the field experiment (f-PH) had a robust relationship with the traditional PH-related traits, except for f-LUpl (length of the first internode above the ear) and f-LUnl (length of the first internode under the ear) in the field experiment (Figure 3D). Finally, correlation networks of PH and other traits from the HTP or major f-traits were constructed using R ≥ 0.3 as the cutoff value based on a previous study (Figure 3E–3G) (Guo et al., 2018). Interestingly, the PCCs of some novel traits with PH were higher than those of traditional PH-related traits (Figure 3F). These results suggest that these novel traits could be used in PH prediction.

#### Genetic basis of i-traits and f-traits in maize

To investigate the genetic and molecular basis of plant architecture-, biomass-, and growth rate-related traits, 1.25M SNPs with MAF ≥ 5% were selected from previous reports for GWAS analysis (Li et al., 2013; Liu et al., 2017; Yang et al., 2019). Genomic variants were identified by GWAS using a mixed linear model that took population structure and relative kinship into account in the TASSEL v5.0 package. The strict significant genome-wide p value threshold was set at 7.97 × 10−7 (p ≤ 1/effective number [EN], where EN is 1 253

814) (Liu et al., 2020). In total, 6797 trait-associated SNPs (4945 unique SNPs without duplicates) were identified (Supplemental Table 6). In these maize lines, overall linkage disequilibrium (LD) decay was estimated to be 10 kb (r2 = 0.2) (Supplemental Figure 7) (Li et al., 2013). Considering the rapid LD decay and abundant diversity in maize (Gore et al., 2009; Yang et al., 2011; Xiao et al., 2021), adjacent SNPs within a region less than

100 kb were defined as a genetic locus. In total, 1685, 1638, and 1 genetic locus were

### Author Manuscript

detected for plant architecture-, biomass-, and growth rate-related traits measured with the HTP, respectively.

The GWAS identified 857, 739, 377, 131, 465, 408, 608, 892, and 1769 SNPs associated with each of the nine growth stages. Interestingly, the last two stages (S8 and S9) had Author Manuscriptmore SNPs than the first two stages (S1 and S2), and the first two stages had more SNPs than the five intermediate stages (S3–S7) (Supplemental Table 7). The S9 stage had the highest number of SNPs associated with i-traits among all investigated stages (Supplemental

Table 7). We next performed an UpSet analysis and found that more genes were associated

with i-traits at individual stages than at multiple overlapping stages (Supplemental Figure Author Manuscript8). In the overlapping groups, pairs of adjacent stages displayed more SNPs, such as S5 overlapping with S6 (44 SNPs), S8 overlapping with S9 (34 SNPs), and S7 overlapping with S8 (23 SNPs). In addition, we found that five i-traits (i-MPAR, i-Cit, i-R, i-Rt, and i-SCt) had significant hits across various growth stages (Supplemental Table 7).

We detected 305 genetic loci that were associated with f-traits in the field experiment (Supplemental Table 6). Excluding overlapping genetic loci for i-traits and f-traits, 2603 unique genetic loci were identified and were not randomly distributed across the genome. Both terminals of all 10 chromosomes were locus hotspots (Figure 4A). We next identified candidate genes associated with the significant SNPs. In total, 1974 candidate genes related to 4945 significant associations were identified and annotated (Supplemental Tables 6 and 7). We constructed a trait-related association network from the GWAS results (Figure 4D) and found that each SNP was associated with an average of 1.37 traits (ranging from 1 to 13) and each trait with an average of 38.6 SNPs (ranging from 1 to 675). We defined SNPs with ≥6 associations and traits with ≥44 associations as hub SNPs and hub traits based on 5% of the total number of SNPs and i-traits, respectively (Guo et al.,

2018). The SNP–trait association network indicated distinct genetic control of different types of i-traits (Figure 4D). All these data suggest that hub genes associated with hub traits and SNPs potentially have central roles in regulating plant architecture in maize.

#### Identification of novel PH-related genes

We found that i-LI5(S6-S5), i-LI6(S6-S5), and i-LI7(S6-S5) were closely correlated with i-PH(S6-S5), and the elongation of these three internodes (LI5–LI7), particularly LI7, contributes most to the rapid increase in PH (Supplemental Figure 4). To gain further insight into the dynamic gene expression profiles of fast-growing internodes during maize stalk development we performed RNA sequencing (RNA-seq)-based transcriptional analysis using data from the representative seventh internode at the elongation stage (ES) (stage S5) and maturation stage (MS) (stage S8). B73 was selected for transcriptome analysis because it grows faster than Mo17 in the ES (Supplemental Video 2). Two replicates per group displayed a strong correlation (Supplemental Figure 13A). Compared with the ES group, 3821 genes were upregulated and 5047 genes were downregulated in the MS group (Supplemental Figure 13B and 13C). Moreover, 13.7% (271/1947) of the GWAS- associated genes were upregulated and 18.7% (369/1947) were downregulated in the MS group (Supplemental Figure 13B and 13D, Supplemental Table 9). Gene ontology (GO) analysis of these differentially expressed genes (DEGs) revealed that genes related to system development, organic substance catabolic processes, nitrogen compound transport, flower development, and embryo development were overrepresented in the developmental stage from elongation to maturation (Supplemental Figure 13E). To validate the strong relationship between hub genes identified by GWAS analysis and rapid elongation of specific internodes, we examined the expression of plant architecture-related candidate genes, including ZmTBL10, ZmMMP37, ZmVATE, ZmPHD1, ZmHmg2, and ZmPri2, in the ES and MS groups. We found that these hub genes were highly expressed in the ES, but their expression decreased in the MS (Supplemental Figure 13F–13K), which was closely related to the rapid growth of maize internodes at specific developmental stages observed in

HTP. These results indicate that higher expression of these hub genes at the elongation stage

### Author Manuscript

is vital for the rapid growth of specific internodes in maize.

#### Functional verification of the candidate PH-related gene ZmVATE

As shown in Figure 4F, plants with the T allele of the ZmVATE gene showed significantly higher levels of i-MPAR, a novel PH-related trait. Phylogenetic and MEME analysis showed

### Author Manuscript

that ZmVATE (with two transcripts) was conserved in both monocot and dicot plant species (Figure 5A) (Bailey and Elkan, 1995), suggesting that it may have similar functions in regulating plant development. ZmVATE encodes a V-type proton ATPase subunit E, which is responsible for acidifying a variety of intracellular compartments in eukaryotic cells. To confirm the function of ZmVATE in the regulation of maize plant architecture, a B73

### Author Manuscript

EMS mutant (zmvate, Mut_sample EMS3–03361f) with a G to A base mutation at 864 aa, resulting in an early stop codon, was obtained from a maize EMS mutant library (http:// www.elabcaas.cn/memd) (Figure 5B). The decrease in ZmVATE expression was detected by quantitative reverse-transcription PCR (qRT–PCR) in the zmvate mutant plants (Figure 5D and Supplemental Table 10). The zmvate mutants displayed reductions in PH, ear height,

### Author Manuscript

tassel length, and internode length but did not differ in leaf length, number, and angle compared with the corresponding wild-type (WT) B73 plants (Figure 5C–5L). The i-MPAR value was lower in the zmvate mutants than in B73 WT plants, demonstrating a critical role for ZmVATE in regulation of the i-trait MPAR (Figure 5M).

To investigate the role of ZmVATE in regulating plant architecture, we performed RNA-seq analysis of zmvate mutant and WT plants. Fragments per kilobase per million mapped reads (FPKM) values for all maize genes were used to construct a Pearson’s distance correlation matrix to compare the zmvate mutant and WT transcriptomes. A heatmap showed that zmvate and WT groups could be distinguished, and the three biological replicates were highly correlated (Figure 6A). Using the RNA-seq data, we identified 4152 upregulated and 2433 downregulated DEGs (|log2 fold change| ≥ 1 and FDR ≤ 5%) (Supplemental

Table 11 and Figure 6B). GO analysis indicated that the DEGs were mainly involved in

photosynthesis, the cell cycle, and response to abiotic stimulus (Figure 6C). The zmvate mutation induced the upregulation of cell cyclin-related and tubulin family genes (Figure 6D). Interestingly, about 18.8% (1239/6585) of the ZmVATE-regulated DEGs overlapped with DEGs in the seventh internode at the ES and MS (Figure 6E). Fifty plant architecture- related genes identified in the HTP were also changed in the seventh internode at the different stages of zmvate mutants (Figure 6E and 6F). qRT–PCR was used to confirm the expression of six selected genes involved in the short phenotypes observed in zmvate mutants (Figure 6G). Expression of beta expansin 8 (EXPB8) AP2-EREBP-transcription factor 114 (EREB114), and glutathione transferase 9 (GST9) was significantly increased, whereas that of vanishing tassel2 (VT2), trehalose-6-phosphate phosphatase 2 (TRPP2), and pyruvate orthophosphate dikinase 1 (PDK1) was decreased in zmvate mutants compared with WT plants (Figure 6G). These results demonstrate the essential roles of ZmVATE in regulating plant architecture-related traits and the predictive power of potential key genes identified in our analysis for ideal plant architecture breeding in maize.

#### Machine learning-based predictive models for maize PH using i-traits

### Author Manuscript

Machine learning can automatically learn data patterns and optimize model parameters, and it has been widely used in Big Data analytics in plants (Ma et al., 2014). Early i-trait measurements could also be beneficial for predicting final PH in maize using machine learning. To assess the performance of the final PH prediction based on early i-traits, we

### Author Manuscript

performed 10-fold cross-validation within seven stages using a random forest (RF) algorithm (Breiman, 2001). The PCCs of each model were calculated for each fold with instant accuracy to evaluate model performance, with weak to very strong correlations ranging from 0.2 to 0.9 across the developmental stages (Zhou et al., 2017) (Figure 7A). The early stages (S1, S2, and S3) in which maize plants had just begun to grow exhibited weak

### Author Manuscript

correlations between the predictions and the experimental dataset (Figure 7A). Moderate and very strong correlations were quickly achieved when using i-traits at the early S4 (R =

0.49, p = 3.7 × 10−15) and S6 (R = 0.80, p < 2.2 × 10−16) stages, respectively (Figure 7B and 7C), consistent with the two rapid height development stages in maize (Figure 2C). The correlation coefficient increased to 0.51 when a combination of S3 and S4 i-traits was used and to 0.82 when S5 and S6 i-traits were used (Figure 7D–7F), suggesting greater prediction
### Author Manuscript

precision and computing efficiency. The relative error was less than 20% for most maize accessions at the S4 stage and less than 10% at the S6 stage (Figure 7G). These results suggest that predictions at the early stage will be a potential robotic approach for ideal plant selection and crop breeding.

To discover the major i-trait determinant of PH in predictive models, we recursively selected features from among all i-traits on the basis of their feature importance in the full RF model (Figure 7H). Among the top 30 i-traits, 13 plant-related i-traits (9 novel identified i-traits) at the S4 stage and 21 (17 novel identified i-traits) at the S6 stage made the greatest contribution (Figure 7H), consistent with our previous findings (Figure 3F). Together, these results indicate that these novel plant-related i-traits could be used as indicators of final PH. We then selected different numbers of i-traits and PH-related traits based on feature importance to validate the predictive models. The predictive accuracy was highest when only i-traits with top-30 feature importance were used or when only top-20 PH-related i-traits were used at the S4 stage (Figure 7I and Supplemental Table 12). The i-traits with top-5 feature importance included i-OEY and i-CMY (Y coordinate of the center of mass of the maize plant) at both the S4 and S6 stages (Figure 7H). Although it did not significantly increase the predictive correlation coefficient when selecting PH-related i-traits, the predictive correlation coefficient was greater than 0.8 when only the top 5 i-traits were used (Figure 7J and Supplemental Table 12). Therefore, PH could be partially predicted using only five i-traits from our HTP with an RF model, highlighting their potential utilization as key biomarkers for analysis of complex PH traits in maize.

### DISCUSSION

PH has been thought to be a critical agronomic trait controlled by many small-effect loci (Hill, 2010; Wang et al., 2019). PH is a commonly studied complex trait because it is relatively simple to measure its relationship with biomass (Freeman et al., 2007), lodging resistance, and grain yield (Duvick, 2005). Previous studies performed conventional

QTL mapping of PH (Austin and Lee, 1996; Tang et al., 2007). Current phenotyping

### Author Manuscript

methods are labor-intensive, costly, and time-consuming with low throughput compared with genomic technology, and most previous QTL studies have examined a limited number of primarily mature stages and traits (Osman et al., 2013; Zhang et al., 2013). HTP facilitates the measurement of traditional traits and new digital traits associated with plant architecture (Yang et al., 2014b; Wu et al., 2019). Here, we used an efficient HTP to acquire

### Author Manuscript

multi-dimensional image-based traits for 228 maize accessions. Unlike a previous study (Zhang et al., 2017), we screened maize phenotypes across all growth stages (Figure 2 and Supplemental Videos 2, 3, 4, and 5). Therefore, this HTP system could obtain more comprehensive and effective plant architecture traits of maize across all developmental stages than those reported in previous studies (Guo et al., 2018; Wu et al., 2021).

### Author Manuscript

Interestingly, B73 grows more slowly than Mo17 in the early stage but more rapidly in the later stage, although the height of mature B73 is almost the same as that of Mo17 (Supplemental Figure 2 and Supplemental Videos 2 and 3). This is consistent with the two stages of rapid increase in PH (Figure 2).

### Author Manuscript Maize breeding would be facilitated if traits measured in early developmental stages could

be used to predict final PH and yield. We identified 49 morphological i-traits, 20 biomass- related i-traits, and 8 growth-related i-traits (Figure 2). Among the 49 morphological traits, 30 novel plant architecture-related traits identified in this study, e.g., i-OEY, i-CL, i-CHC, and i-MECD, were strongly correlated with i-PH and could be used to monitor PH dynamics (Figure 2).

In our study, hundreds of plant architecture-related loci were identified based on a large number of i-traits, which, to our knowledge, are the first temporal dynamic genetic architectures of maize plant growth across all developmental stages to date. Consistent with a previous study (Zhang et al., 2017), our results identified different genes at different stages and a large number of genes for most traits (Buckler et al., 2009; Xiao et al., 2016). In summary, 4945 SNPs were associated with 1974 unique candidate genes (Supplemental Tables 6 and 7). One recent paper has dissected marker–trait associations for multiple traits published in maize (Mural et al., 2022). We found that 246 (12.5%) of our genetic loci overlapped with the public data reported in Mural et al. (Supplemental

Table 8). Moreover, only 14% of the candidate genes for f-traits were also associated with

i-traits (Figure 4C). However, at least 86% of the unique genes were specific to f-traits, suggesting that the different candidate genes in the two groups may exhibit specific genetic architecture under different growth conditions (Xu et al., 2012; Yuan et al., 2019). It will be interesting to determine and explore how these candidate genes play key roles at specific stages to regulate plant architecture. The information gathered here will be of particular interest for future studies on the evolution of hub gene-mediated mechanisms and the divergence of functionality in crop plants. In addition to the potential roles of candidate genes in influencing plant architecture, technical innovations such as synthetic biology and genome editing will enable greater control of plant growth for crop improvement in future agriculture.

Four candidate genes, ZmVATE, ZmPHD1, ZmHmg2, and ZmTBL10, were identified as having high correlations with PH-related traits. Interestingly, expression of these

genes increased during seventh internode elongation and then decreased at maturation

### Author Manuscript

(Supplemental Figure 13). These data suggest that higher expression of these hub genes may contribute to the rapid increase in PH at elongation and that the i-MPAR trait could be used as a potential new image-based marker for prediction of maize PH. EMS mutagenesis has been widely used to produce plant materials for functional studies and breeding (Lu et al., 2018). Our study of zmvate mutants demonstrated the role of ZmVATE gene in PH

### Author Manuscript

regulation through the use of EMS-induced mutations. ZmVATE mutation resulted in the downregulation of VT2, which is an essential gene in maize PH development. ZmVT2 encodes an enzyme that converts Trp to indole-3-pyruvic acid and plays a vital role in regulation of maize PH (Phillips et al., 2011). Therefore, the short PH caused by ZmVATE mutation may be due to decreased VT2 gene expression. More importantly, the EMS mutant

### Author Manuscript

zmvate exhibited lower ear height and PH, thereby changing plant architecture, which may facilitate lodging resistance and grain yield. Thus, orchestrating ZmVATE expression by tissue- or stage-specific promoters may help maize adaptation to higher plant density, thereby increasing maize grain yield per unit area.

Author Manuscript In addition, plants with the A allele of chr5.S_194097377 in the gene body of ZmHmg2 showed significantly shorter tassels (Figure 4H). ZmHmg2 encodes a protein from a subgroup of the HMGB gene family with a distinctive DNA-binding motif, the HMG- box domain. This motif confers non-sequence-specific interaction with linear DNA and structure-specific binding to distorted DNA sites. HMGB proteins are involved in the assembly of nucleoprotein complexes and can be phosphorylated by CK2alpha in Arabidopsis (Riechmann et al., 2000). Ectopic expression of maize HMGB1 in tobacco seedlings resulted in a shorter primary root (Lichota et al., 2004), suggesting a potential role for HMGB protein in building plant architecture. ZmTBL10 encodes trichome birefringence-like 10, a member of the TBL (Trichome Birefringence-Like) family containing a plant-specific DUF231 (domain of unknown function) domain. The TBL gene family has 46 members in Arabidopsis. TBR/AT5G06700 is involved in synthesizing and depositing secondary wall cellulose. TBL3/AT5G01360 mutant seedlings have shorter hypocotyls (Bischoff et al., 2010). In our study, plants with the C allele of chr10.S_4692637 in the promoter region of ZmTBL10 exhibited slower growth (Supplemental Figure 11D), implying a key role for ZmTBL10 in maize PH. The ZmPHD1 gene variant (chr6.S_1538130) associated with tassel length (Figure 4G) is a member of the large ZFP plant gene family. Characterized ZFPs are involved in GA signaling pathways that alter PH. Mutants with impaired GA signaling are dwarf, whereas those with constitutively active GA responses display a slender phenotype (Sun and Gubler, 2004; Wang et al., 2015). ZmPHD1 is widely distributed in maize tissues at different stages (Sekhon et al., 2011), suggesting a broad effect of this gene on maize development. All these hub genes may be good candidates for subsequent studies to expand our understanding of the genetic control of plant architecture, including height, in maize.

Plant architecture-related genes reported in previous studies were found and co-localized in our GWAS results, including Zm00001d040650 (ZmPme19), Zm00001d012746 (Wrky111), Zm00001d011611 (clumped tassel1, clt1), Zm00001d026289 (Potassium transporter, ZmPT), and Zm00001d034045 (ZmMADS4) (Hu et al., 2017; Wu et al., 2018; Sun et al., 2020; Yang et al., 2020; Strable, 2021). For example, ZmPme19 encodes a

pectinesterase involved in cell wall modification, cell size and shape determination, growth

### Author Manuscript

and development, intercellular communication, and interactions with the environment (Wu et al., 2018; Zhang et al., 2019). Here, ZmPme19 was associated with the number of leaves in the mature plant (Supplemental Table 7). Interestingly, ZmPme19 gene expression was significantly decreased in the seventh internode of mature maize plants (Supplemental Table

9). This implies that ZmPme19 is important at different developmental stages. In short,
### Author Manuscript

our results provide many novel traits and previously undetected genetic loci. The HTP has distinct advantages for identifying traits and SNPs.

More importantly, most traits, including newly defined i-traits identified in HTP that are very difficult to measure by traditional methods in the field, can now be detected

### Author Manuscriptautomatically in early maize growth stages and used for PH prediction based on machine

learning (Figure 7). We built a predictive model and included a series of assessments of all i-traits that influenced the precision of maize PH prediction. The PCCs of predictive models gradually increased from 0.2 to 0.9 during maize development as PH reached its final value. A moderate correlation between experimental data and PH prediction was achieved using Author Manuscript i-traits at the early S4 stage after the plants had undergone the first rapid growth stage (S2 to S3), and a very strong correlation was quickly achieved at the S6 stage after the second stage of rapid growth (S5 to S6). Thus, these assessments are critically important, and we highly recommend performing predictions at specific developmental stages, such as early S4, to make the prediction precision and actual samples optimally balanced in crop breeding. Interestingly, we found that the correlation of PH prediction could be significant using only five i-traits in an RF model in our HTP (Figure 7). Therefore, our results demonstrate previously undescribed machine learning-based predictive models for final PH based on i-traits across all developmental stages of maize, which may provide a theoretical basis for implementing data-driven phenotyping design for accelerated breeding in crop species.

In summary, combining high-throughput phenotyping technology and GWAS analysis with predictive models revealed the genetic internode patterning of PH in maize, providing valuable clues for maize breeding by optimizing plant architecture.

### METHODS

#### Plant materials and experiment design

Two hundred twenty-eight diverse maize accessions (Supplemental Table 1) in an association panel, including landraces and elite varieties representative of temperate, subtropical, and tropical germplasm (Yang et al., 2011; Li et al., 2013), were planted in a greenhouse in China’s Hebei province in two consecutive summers. The seeds were sown in pots 25 cm tall and 25 cm in diameter situated on the ground. Two seeds per inbred line were sown in each pot. Only the normal and strong seedlings were retained in the V3 stage. At least four plants of each accession grew well, and two replicates of each were retained for further phenotypic processing and analysis. All maize accessions were screened every day across all growth stages. Images were selected to extract i-traits at 9 representative developmental time points (approximately every 8 days from day 1 to day 113 after sowing; e.g., S1–S9 represent 25, 32, 50, 59, 70, 78, 85, 91, and 113 days after sowing and correspond to V3 [third leaf], V5 [fifth leaf], V7 [seventh leaf], V10 [tenth leaf],

V14 [rapid growth], VT [tassel], R1 [silking], R3 [milk], and R6 [maturity], respectively)

### Author Manuscript

(Eroglu et al., 2019). The same accessions were also planted in the same summers in Hebei province, China. Every accession was planted in one-row plots in an incomplete randomized block design with two replicates with a line length of 5 m, a line width of 60 cm, and a row spacing of 20 cm.

### Author Manuscript

#### Phenotyping platform

The phenotyping facility used in this study contained two sections: the maize automatic HTP and the field experiment. The HTP, with 8 transporting pipelines and 57 conveyers in each pipeline, can accommodate 456 pots for plant cultivation and imaging at the same time. It required approximately 40 s to acquire images for each pot. After the analysis, a flowchart

### Author Manuscript

was designed and tested. The maize plants were transported to the HTP, which captured six images from three different views (front, side, and top) using a Basler piA 2400 camera (Basler AG, Ahrensburg, Germany) with a 2/3″ CCD progressive scan that can generate a 2456 × 2058-pixel image with a 3.45 × 3.45-mm pixel size. Zooming parameters appropriate for the growth phase were used when imaging to obtain a better, fully displayed image. All

### Author Manuscript

calculated parameters were scaled relative to the width of the pot, and 77 i-traits (49 plant architecture-related i-traits, 20 biomass-related i-traits, and 8 growth-related i-traits) were extracted through image analysis.

Field traits such as PH, ear height, tassel length, and individual node lengths (Supplemental

Table 3) were manually measured on 10 plants from each accession at the maturation stage.

**Image analysis and trait extraction**

Image analysis was carried out as shown in Figure 1 using LemnaGrid software developed

by LemnaTec (Aachen, Germany) to address these specific phenotyping needs. First, all raw images were reconstructed in color with the Bayer color pattern. Then the RGB images were converted to the lightness color components of the LAB color space system. After this initial processing, images were transformed to binary and the plants separated from the background. The plant region was picked up using the region of interest free form maker process. Then, a fill area filter was used to remove unwanted small holes on the plant structure to obtain a better extraction of the plant. The skeleton of the plant was extracted using the skeleton filter. Finally, morphological traits were calculated from this well-established digital plant.

#### Definition and calculation of i-traits from front-view images

Area: number of foreground pixels attributed to the maize plant.

Boundary point count: number of pixels on the edges of the maize plant.

Circumference: sum of the direction-corrected length of the outer boundary of the maize plant.

Object extent Y (OEY): the maximum number of pixels in the Y direction. This measures the height of the plant.

Caliper length (CL): the maximum distance between two points in the boundary of the

### Author Manuscript

maize plant. When the front view is more or less round, this length refers to the manually much less stringently definable maximum “diameter” often measured with a caliper.

Convex hull circumference: the sum of the direction-corrected lengths of the outer boundary of the convex hull in the maize plant. This parameter indicates the compactness of the whole

### Author Manuscriptplant.

Minimum enclosing circle diameter: the pixel number of the diameter of the minimum enclosing circle of the whole maize plant. This radius refers to the manually much less stringently definable maximum diameter, which is similar to iCL but computed with a

### Author Manuscriptdifferent algorithm.

#### Roundness: roundness of the maize plant.

Object extent X (OEX): maximum number of pixels in the X direction, which represents plant width of maize.

### Author Manuscript

Second moments principal axis ratio (MPAR): the ratio of the second moments of the principal axis.

Other plant architecture-related traits and growth rate-related traits and i-traits from the top-view images are detailed in Supplemental Table 2.

#### Analysis and comparison of i-traits and f-traits

PCCs between i-traits were calculated using the package Hmisc ([https://github.com/harrelfe/](https://github.com/harrelfe/) Hmisc/) in R (www.r-project.org). To test the accuracy of the HTP measurement, we performed PCC analysis of i-OEY, which represented PH measured by HTP, and iPH, which represented the manual measurement of PH at nine stages. The PCCs of i-PH and growth rate at different stages were also calculated, as were PCCs between traits measured in the field. We selected a high PCC threshold (phenotypic Pearson correlation coefficient R ≥ 0.3, p < 0.001) (Guo et al., 2018) between i-PH and other i-traits to construct an i-PH correlation network using Cytoscape software ([http://cytoscape.org/](http://cytoscape.org/)). The raw data were normalized as y = (x − min)/(max − min) (x, raw data; y, normalized data; max, maximum; min, minimum) for heatmap visualization. All heatmap plots were visualized with the R package pheatmap ([https://www.rdocumentation.org/packages/pheatmap/](https://www.rdocumentation.org/packages/pheatmap/)).

**GWAS** Genotype data for the inbred lines were obtained from previous studies (Yang et al., 2014a; Liu et al., 2017). An integrated variant map with much higher density (2.65M loci) combining genotypes from previous RNA-seq, high-density array, and GBS studies was downloaded from the MaizeGo website ([http://www.maizego.org/Resources.html](http://www.maizego.org/Resources.html)). Among these variants, more than half (1.4M, 52.8%) were rare (minor allelic frequency [MAF] < 5%), whereas 1.25M had a MAF ≥ 5% and were used for this study. GWAS was performed with a mixed linear model that took into account population structure and relative kinship and was implemented in the TASSEL v5.0 package. The same trait at 9 different stages

was regarded as a different trait (77 i-traits) for the association analyses. In total, we

### Author Manuscript

performed GWAS for 720 traits, including i-traits measured by HTP and traditional traits from manual measurements. To better understand the underlying regulatory network of maize development, we classified the traits into three categories (PH-related traits, biomass- related traits, and growth rate-related traits) to perform GWAS and identify candidate genes.

Author ManuscriptThe EN of independent SNP markers was calculated using the GEC tool (Li et al., 2012). The calculated EN was 1 253 814 and was used to determine the global p threshold (p ≤ 1/EN of independent markers). To reveal the unique candidate gene underlying association signals, we performed LD analysis of the significant SNP on the same chromosome and used a cutoff of <0.2 for the LD statistic r2. A gene with a significant SNP in or near (within

### Author Manuscript2 kb up- and downstream) was identified as a candidate gene.

Haplotypes were determined based on the genotypes of the SNPs (MAF ≥ 0.05), and the LD statistic r2 was calculated using the R package InAssoPlot (He et al., 2020). Comparisons of groups of accessions with different haplotypes were performed using the Wilcoxon test in

### Author Manuscript

the R package ggsignif ([https://github.com/const-ae/ggsignif/](https://github.com/const-ae/ggsignif/)).

#### Identification of new PH-related genes

We downloaded 459 genes identified in previous studies of maize PH GWAS from the GWAS Atlas ([https://ngdc.cncb.ac.cn/gwas/](https://ngdc.cncb.ac.cn/gwas/)). We checked for overlap between these genes and genes identified in this study and found 11 common genes.

The EMS mutant zmvate (Mut_Sample EMS3–03361f), in which the tryptophan codon TGG at 864 aa is changed into an early stop codon (TGA), was obtained from the Maize EMS-induced Mutant Database ([http://www.elabcaas.cn/memd/index.php](http://www.elabcaas.cn/memd/index.php)) (Lu et al., 2018). The EMS mutant zmvate was then sequenced to confirm the mutation (Supplemental Table

9). Homozygous mutants were purified by backcrossing for two generations and were then propagated in Hebei and Hainan province, China. Homozygous mutant zmvate and WT plants were identified and used for detailed phenotyping and further analysis.
#### Gene expression and quantification

The maize B73 inbred line was planted in the HTP. RNA was extracted from fresh B73 seventh internodes at S5 (elongation stage) and S8 (maturation stage) (two replicates for each group) with the RNeasy Plus Mini Kit (Vazyme Biotech, China). The cDNA libraries were generated using the NEBNext Ultra RNA Library Prep Kit (NEB, USA). The quantified libraries were prepared for sequencing on the Illumina HiSeq X-ten sequencing platform (Illumina, USA). After filtering of low-quality reads (Q < 20) and trimming of adapter sequences, the clean data were aligned to the maize v4 reference genome ([http://plants.ensembl.org/](http://plants.ensembl.org/)) using HISAT2 (Kim et al., 2019). Gene expression levels were calculated with featureCounts (Liao et al., 2014) using default parameters and converted to FPKM values. PCCs between sample replicates were calculated using R and visualized with the R package pheatmap.

#### Differential expression and GO enrichment analysis

### Author Manuscript

DEGs, defined as genes with at least a two-fold expression difference between N7_ES (control sample) and N7_MS (treated sample), were identified with the R package DESeq (Love et al., 2014) using a corrected p value <0.05. The GO annotations and GO enrichment of the DEGs were analyzed with the topGO R package ([http://www.bioconductor.org/](http://www.bioconductor.org/)

### Author Manuscript

packages/release/bioc/html/topGO.html) (Alexa et al., 2006) and visualized using ggplot2 (Ito and Murphy, 2013).

#### Prediction of maize final PH using i-traits based on machine learning

RF, a machine learning algorithm, is a tree model that uses the bagging technique, which is

### Author Manuscriptmore suitable for complex data processing and prediction (Breiman, 2001). We performed

an algorithm using the caret package ([https://CRAN.R-project.org/package=caret](https://CRAN.R-project.org/package=caret)) in R and constructed the model. The replicates of maize accessions were merged using the average value, and the raw data were then scaled. The 10-fold cross-validation strategy was also used to construct and validate the model. The average PCC of 10 models was calculated to

### Author Manuscript

evaluate model performance. All plots were created with the ggplot2 package in R.

#### Statistical analysis of plant phenotypes

ANOVA was first used to determine whether there were any significant differences among multiple groups. If the p value for the ANOVA was less than 0.05, two-tailed Student’s t-test was used to determine the p values for pairwise comparisons.
