ABSTRACT

Plants contain a large number of cell types and exhibit complex regulatory mechanisms. Studies at the single-cell level have gradually become more common in plant science. Single-cell transcriptomics, spatial transcriptomics, and spatial metabolomics techniques have been combined to analyze plant development. INTRODUCTION Differentiation during plant cell development produces heteroge- neity among cells and cell types, which then associate with each other during development. Expression levels of specific genes that determine the course of cell development differ significantly among cell developmental stages (Liu et al., 2022b), making it important to study this process at the single-cell level.

Many recent studies have therefore made use of single-cell RNA sequencing (scRNA-seq) (Butler et al., 2018; Wu et al., 2019; Moncada et al., 2020), in which the transcriptomes of isolated single cells are amplified and sequenced. The ability to obtain gene expression profiles of single cells is a key advantage of scRNA-seq relative to previous transcriptomic techniques (Melnekoff and Lagana`,2022). Although scRNA-seq enables the study of transcriptomic heterogeneity among cells and the identifi- cation of specific cell types, its requirement for tissue dissociation to isolate single cells means that information on the original spatial locations of the cells is lost. This drawback limits the study of cell development in both temporal and spatial dimensions. To address this limitation, a novel sequencing technology (spatial transcrip- tomics) that can retain both gene expression and location informa- tion has been developed (Crosetto et al., 2015). Such techniques include spatially resolved transcriptome profiling (Giacomello

et al., 2017), 10x Genomics Visium, and the recently introduced single-cell spatial enhanced resolution omics sequencing (Ste- reo-seq) (Xia et al., 2022). The latter approach combines spatial information with transcriptome sequencing to enable *in situ* observation of gene expression in cells with unprecedented high resolution, as high as 500 nm (Giacomello and Lundeberg, 2018; Maynard et al., 2021). Thus, it is now possible to develop a comprehensive spatial atlas of plant gene expression using spatial transcriptomics and scRNA-seq technologies, which will benefit the plant research community as a whole.

Metabolites are also essential for the regulation of plant develop- ment (Pang et al., 2021). Spatial information on metabolites is required to fully understand their roles in the regulation of specific biological processes in individual plant tissues or cells. Mass spectrometry imaging (MSI) is a molecular imaging technology for determining the spatial distribution of large numbers of metabolites in specific tissues (Roempp and Spengler, 2013; Peng et al., 2020). LCM uses laser pulses to cut out single target cells identified under a microscope (Simone et al., 1998); it is a labor-intensive tech- nique that allows only very limited throughput (Hodne and Weltzien, 2015). Droplet-encapsulated single cells obtained us- ing microfluidic technology and microwell-array-based single- cell separation have greatly reduced the cost of single-cell sep- aration and sequencing (Huo et al., 2016; Birey et al., 2017), enabling the creation of a cell atlas through scRNA-seq (Figure 1). SINGLE-CELL EPIGENOMIC TECHNOLOGIES AND THEIR APPLICATIONS IN PLANTS Epigenomics is the study of epigenetic modifications at the genomic level, including investigation of chromatin accessibility, 3D genomic architecture, DNA methylation, and histone modifica- tions (Wen and Tang, 2022). Single-cell epigenome sequencing technology has developed rapidly in recent years. DNA methylation research has enabled the development of bisulfite-converted randomly integrated fragments sequencing (BRIF-seq) (Li et al.,

2019). Compared with reads generated by traditional single-cell bisulfite sequencing, those generated by BRIF-seq are more evenly distributed throughout the genome, including in regions with a high degree of DNA methylation (Li et al., 2019). Chromatin accessibility is detectable by both deoxyribonuclease I hypersensitive site sequencing (DNase-seq) and assay for transposase-accessible chromatin with high-throughput sequencing (ATAC-seq). DNase- seq uses sensitivity to DNase I enzymatic digestion to determine the DNA accessibility of sites, whereas ATAC-seq uses Tn5 trans- posase for this purpose (Buenrostro et al., 2015a; Jin et al., 2015). Single-cell ATAC-seq (scATAC-seq) uses ATAC-seq of sorted sin- gle nuclei to study open chromatin regions at the single-cell level (Buenrostro et al., 2015b). scATAC-seq has also been used to mea- sure chromatin accessibility in single cells by combining scATAC- seq with combinatorial indexing of intact nuclei (Cusanovich et al., 2015). Single nuclei can be sorted by microfluidics-based 10x Genomics Chromium single-cell ATAC using barcoded gel beads to bind the nuclei (Thibivilliers et al., 2021). Compared with single-cell DNase-seq, indexing single-cell DNase-seq uses the activities of terminal DNA transferase and T4 DNA ligase to add unique cell barcodes to DNase-digested chromatin ends, re- sulting in higher read coverage and better prediction of gene expression (Gao et al., 2021). 3D genomic architecture has been characterized using single-cell combinatorial indexed high-throughput/resolution chromosome conformation capture (Ramani et al., 2017). This technique uses nucleic acid barcodes from successive rounds to label DNA in intact nuclei and enable high-throughput analyses (Ramani et al., 2017). The advent of single-cell epigenomic sequencing has enabled the study of chromatin heterogeneity in single plant cells. BRIF-seq was used to study single microspores of maize, which have a highly methylated and repetitive genome, and the technique pro- duced high rates of read mapping and genome coverage, enabling the identification of heterogeneous sites (Li et al.,
2019). scATAC-seq was used to measure chromatin accessibility in the nuclei of six maize organs (Marand et al., 2021). scATAC- seq, combined with sNucRNA-seq, enabled the construction and analysis of a cis-regulatory element map from multiple maize organs, thereby revealing cell-type-specific cis-regulatory ele- ments that are closely associated with phenotypic variation (Marand et al., 2021). Dorrity et al. created single-cell maps of open chromatin in *Arabidopsis* roots using scATAC-seq and identified three different developmental states of endodermal cells by integrating scATAC-seq and scRNA-seq data (Dorrity et al., 2021). Farmer et al. used nuclear and protoplast transcriptomes of *Arabidopsis* root cells to study dynamic changes in chromatin accessibility during cell development by combining sNucRNA-seq and single-nucleus ATAC-seq, thereby
discovering additional root-cell-type transcriptomes (Farmer et al., 2021).

Continued advances in single-cell epigenomic sequencing tech- niques will allow us to gain a better understanding of plant chro- matin states at the single-cell level. Combining scRNA-seq and scATAC-seq to simultaneously capture the transcriptomes and epigenomes of individual cells provides sufficient information to characterize gene expression patterns and cell types and to determine how different transcription factors cooperate in the regulation of gene expression networks.

SPATIAL TRANSCRIPTOMIC TECHNOLOGIES AND THEIR APPLICATIONS IN PLANTS One problem with scRNA-seq is that spatial information for indi- vidual cells in a tissue is lost during cell isolation (Longo et al.,

2021). Thus, various early methods such as in situ hybridization (ISH), single-molecule RNA fluorescence ISH, and LCM have been used to visualize gene expression of cells within their tissue context (Huang et al., 2020; Zollner et al., 2021 €; Yang et al., 2022). ISH uses labeled DNA or RNA probes to hybridize nucleic acid fragments in tissue sections, followed by visualization of the label (Jiang and Gill, 1994). However, ISH is inefficient and cannot detect the expression locations of all genes in the transcriptome owing to limitations associated with probe design (described in Veselinyova´ et al., 2021). Nakazono et al. isolated maize cells and successfully localized gene expression in maize epidermal and vascular cells by combining LCM and microarray technology (Nakazono et al., 2003). The use of LCM is still limited because it is time consuming and complex (Craven and Banks, 2001). Development of new technologies, such as 10x Visium and Slide-seq, has promoted the use of spatial transcriptomics (Sta˚ hl et al., 2016). In the Slide-seq tech- nique, 10-mm beads are attached to a glass coverslip with unique barcoded reverse transcription primers; tissue section permeabi- lization is then performed to transfer mRNA from inside the cells to the surface beads for library construction (Rodriques et al.,
2019). Owing to the small bead diameter, Slide-seq achieves single-cell resolution compared with the lower resolution of 10x Visium (Rodriques et al., 2019). The bead diameter is further reduced to 2 mm in high-definition spatial transcriptomics, and the 2-mm beads also contain barcoded reverse transcription primers from an ordered bead array for mRNA capture (Vickovic et al., 2019). When collecting and pretreating samples for spatial transcriptomics, some researchers have used optimal cutting temperature compound gel to rapidly embed fresh tissue after quick freezing to maintain tissue structure (Giacomello and Lundeberg, 2018; Su et al., 2021). Because of the multiple air spaces in plant tissues, Giacomello et al. preserved the morphology of tissue sections by applying an ultrathin layer of Tissue-Tek optimal cutting temperature com- pound (a formulation of water-soluble glycols and resins) to the specimen surface before cryosectioning (Giacomello and Lundeberg, 2018). MSI AND ITS APPLICATION FOR ANALYZING SPATIAL DISTRIBUTION OF PLANT METABOLITES Plant metabolomics research focuses on qualitative and quanti- tative investigation of plant metabolites and their metabolic path- ways (Tugizimana et al., 2018). Gas chromatography MS has been the most extensively used analytical technology for metab- olomics because of its high resolution, high sensitivity, broad application range, and excellent specificity (Spa´ nik and Machyna´ kova´, 2018). However, information on the spatial distribution of metabolites in tissues is lost during extraction, purification, and enrichment, thus limiting the use of gas chromatography MS (Kira´ ly et al., 2016).

MSI is a novel molecular imaging approach that directly obtains large amounts of information on the structure, content, and spatial distribution of molecules in biological tissues, including known and undiscovered endogenous metabolites, and it thus produces tissue molecular imaging maps (Caprioli, 2014).

Figure 4 summarizes the use of SM analysis in plant research.

Three MSI techniques have been developed based on different ion sources: secondary ion MS (SIMS), desorption electrospray ionization MSI (DESI-MSI), and matrix-assisted laser desorption or ionization MSI (MALDI-MSI) (Oppenheimer and Drexler,

2012). SIMS uses an ion beam as the ionization source to ionize molecules on the sample surface and generate secondary ions for detection, and it is thus a hard ionization method (Nishinomiya et al., 2016). However, fragments of molecules are generated during SIMS ionization, resulting in some ions that cannot be detected, and most detected molecular masses are less than 1000 Da (Stoeckli et al., 2001; Zhu et al., 2021). SIMS is therefore typically used to detect small metabolites. SIMS also has significant advantages, as it provides high spatial resolution close to the sub-mm (0.1-mm) level, which allows for analysis of intracellular metabolites (Vickerman, 2011). DESI- MSI enables direct analysis of unprocessed frozen sample sec- tions, which simplifies sample preparation (Taka´ ts et al., 2005). DESI-MSI uses a solvent spray to perform soft ionization of sam- ple tissues (Taka´ ts et al., 2004). It detects metabolites with larger molecular masses (0–2000 Da) than SIMS, but its spatial resolution is lower than that of SIMS (about 50 mm) (Svato,
2010). The most widely used method is MALDI-MSI, which uses UV or infrared lasers to ionize the sample surface. Similar to DESI-MSI, MALDI-MSI uses soft ionization (Zhu et al., 2021), and it detects masses up to 100 000 Da (Stoeckli et al., 2001). However, sample ionization requires deposition of an organic matrix, making the experimental operating conditions more complex than those of SIMS or DESI-MSI (Li et al., 2022a). MALDI-MSI usually achieves a resolution of about 5 mm (Feenstra et al., 2017). Ion information, such as mass-to-charge ratio and intensity, can be obtained after isolating ions from tissue samples by MS. Processing of these large ionic datasets, com- bined with a knowledge of the metabolome, is key to SM analysis. Suitable platforms have been developed for visual imaging, such as MSiReader for reading and verifying MSI data (Robichaud et al., 2013) and mass2adduct software for metabolite data analysis (Janda et al., 2021). Advances in sample preparation steps and mass spectrometers have contributed to the development of MSI technology. For example, the use of adhesive film in the preparation of sample cryosections effectively improved DESI-MSI analysis of amino acids, sugars, organic acids, and specialized metabolites such as anthocyanins and flavan-3-ols in strawberry (Enomoto,
2021). Likewise, the newly developed imaging technique of desorption ionization using through-hole alumina membrane, in which a prefabricated nanostructured film is placed over the sec- tion, achieves higher signal uniformity and contrast than MALDI- MSI (Mueller et al., 2021). Liu et al. showed that use of caffeic acid as a standard organic acid matrix enhances endogenous protein imaging of soybean seeds with MALDI-MSI, demonstrating that a well-established and appropriate sample preparation method is crucial for subsequent MS analysis (Liu et al., 2021a). Another improvement to the mass spectrometer is the introduction of a new fiber probe laser ablation system for atmospheric pressure MSI, which ablates the material at a high spatial resolution of 5 mm to detect the distribution of dye molecules such as methyl green zinc chloride salt (Lu et al., 2021). Studies have also been performed to improve efficiency by combining MS with other instruments. For example, the combination of ion mobility spectrometry with nanospray DESI MS has been used to detect
isomeric lipids in tissues (Unsihuay et al., 2021). Interference by isomers is effectively eliminated with the help of nanospray DESI combined with an ion mobility spectrometer-mass spectrometer, and the molecular specificity of MSI detection is improved (Unsihuay et al., 2021). The trade-off between high sensitivity and spatial resolution of ion detection during MS has hindered the development of MSI (Scupa´ kova´ et al., 2020). However, the distribution of lipids in tissue sections can be measured with a spatial resolution of 5 mm and an imaging speed of 18 pixels/s using a high-repetition-rate laser in an ion source combined with an orbital capture mass spectrometer (Muller € et al., 2020). The successful establishment of this technique has improved the sensitivity of MSI analysis.

Nonetheless, MSI has many technical limitations. For example, the diameter of the mass spectrometer laser spot is closely related to spatial resolution, but a decrease in spot size also de- creases the ion yield obtained by ionization (McDonnell and Heeren, 2007). In addition, a special matrix is needed for ionization during MALDI-MSI, but the matrix crystallization pro- cess is easily affected by the environment, which reduces the repeatability and reliability of the experimental method, and new matrix systems must therefore be developed (Scupa´ kova´ et al., 2020).

MSI has been used to study metabolite dynamics in higher plants. As components of cell membranes, lipids play an important role in energy supply, and their localization in plants has frequently been studied (Han et al., 2012). Sarabia et al. combined MALDI-MSI, liquid chromatography MS, and inductively coupled plasma MS to study lipid distribution in the roots of barley seedlings during salt stress (Sarabia et al., 2018). MSI can also be used to study metabolite changes in plants in response to biotic stress. MALDI- MS has been used to image esters, glycerophospholipids, pep- tides, and proteins produced by nematode infection in tomato roots and to analyze the molecular processes responsible for infection and maintenance of feeding sites during nematode parasitism (Barbosa et al., 2018). Because phytohormones play an important role in plant growth and development, the use of MSI technology to determine the distribution and concentration of phytohormones in tissues will expand our understanding of plant developmental processes. Shiono et al. used nanoparticle- assisted laser desorption/ionization to image several hormones, including ABA, auxin, brassinosteroid, jasmonic acid, salicylic acid, 1-aminocyclopropane-1-carboxylic acid, and heavy hydrogen-labeled ABA in rice roots for the first time (Shiono and Taira, 2020). This approach provided new insight into the role of hormone signals in crop development and stress response (Shiono and Taira, 2020). MSI also has potential for use in plant nutrition research. For instance, Li et al. discovered a new tomato vitamin D synthetic pathway using MALDI-MSI and a bio- fortification strategy, providing a solution to the problem of vitamin Ddeficiency(Li et al., 2022b).

Isomeric compounds have always been difficult to distinguish in plant tissues, but a study using tandem MSI analysis of disaccha- ride matrix droplet spots demonstrated the feasibility of distin- guishing isomeric species in onion bulb tissues by combining tan- dem MS and MSI to discover new plant saccharide species (Lingpeng et al., 2021). Nonetheless, metabolite imaging of the whole plant remains difficult. In a breakthrough for whole-plant

MSI, Yun et al. combined direct analysis in real-time time-of-flight MS and ambient ionization MS to analyze alkaloids in the above- ground parts of Ephedra (Yun et al., 2021). The applicability of MSI for such plant studies makes it a powerful technique for metabolomic research.

Air flow-assisted ionization imaging MSI (AFAI-MSI) is an imaging technique based on DESI-MSI (Mao et al., 2016). It uses high- speed airflow during sample ionization to achieve longer distance ion transport compared with DESI-MSI, and it is thus suitable for large tissue samples (Mao et al., 2016). AFAI-MSI has been used for drug imaging in rats (He et al., 2011; Tang et al., 2015), and this research strategy may lead to the application of AFAI-MSI to plant species.

MSI is a well-developed technology that has been used extensively for analysis of metabolites in various plant tissues (Yoshinaga et al., 2016; Bednarz et al., 2019; Hu et al., 2021; Samarah et al., 2021). Plant growth, development, and reproduction depend on the production of primary and secondary metabolites, and secondary metabolites also act as defense molecules in plant–environment in- teractions, helping plants to withstand environmental stress (Rui et al., 2020). Use of MSI technology to detect primary and secondary metabolites in plant tissues and infer their biological functions will help to further characterize the regulatory mechanisms that affect plant growth.

SPATIAL MULTI-OMICS STUDIES IN PLANTS: CHALLENGES AND PERSPECTIVES Studies in many fields of biology have made use of scRNA-seq and scATAC-seq technologies as experimental tools. Nonethe- less, the use of single-cell sequencing technologies in research remains limited, and the most significant limitation is the inability to obtain cell spatial information (Seyfferth et al., 2021). Spatial imaging maps of tissue sections can be combined with spatial transcriptomics to explore how cells interact within tissues and where specific genes are expressed (Giacomello et al., 2017). Transcription is not the only factor that affects plant development: levels of proteins and metabolites also closely regulate developmental processes (Geng et al., 2014; Bhambhani et al., 2017). Key factors in plant development can be accurately identified only by comprehensively studying multiple cellular components. Thus, multi-omics techniques are increasingly being combined in botanical research. A number of studies have combined genomic and metabolomic data to analyze plant developmental processes. Marques et al. discov- ered a previously undetected metabolic pathway in Podophyllum *hexandrum* and *Podophyllum peltatum* tissues by combining LCM, RNA-seq, and MALDI-MSI (Marques et al., 2014). Likewise, a recent study combined MSI and RNA-seq to analyze the transcriptome and metabolome of elm (Ulmus pumila) seeds (Qi et al., 2022), providing information on gene and metabolite changes during late seed maturation (Qi et al., 2022). These studies suggest that combined multi-omics analysis is an effec- tive method for elucidating complex metabolic pathways in plants. In Figure 2 and Supplemental Table 1, we summarize a number of plant research studies published since that used MSI, spatial transcriptomics, and scRNA-seq.

Few studies have combined high-throughput spatial transcrip- tomics, scRNA-seq, scATAC-seq, and MSI in plant tissues. How- ever, such a combined analysis is now widely used in animal developmental research. For instance, Fawkner-Corbett et al. created a single-cell spatiotemporal map of the development of the human intestine using scRNA-seq and spatial transcriptomics and documented the process of intestinal morphogenesis (Fawkner-Corbett et al., 2021). Deng et al. proposed a spatial CUT&Tag approach to spatially resolve genome-wide histone modification by combining in situ CUT&Tag chemistry, microflui- dic deterministic barcoding, and NGS (Deng et al., 2022). These techniques have been used to analyze mouse embryo and brain development at spatial and genome-wide levels, as well as underlying epigenetic mechanisms (Deng et al., 2022). A novel mechanism by which plant growth-promoting bacteria induce DNA methylation in the roots of Phytolacca americana L. to promote plant growth has been characterized by transcrip- tome sequencing and whole-genome bisulfite sequencing, providing insight into the metabolites produced by the bacteria to regulate DNA methylation levels in roots (Chen et al., 2022b). Recent studies have more accurately identified the composition of immunized mouse lung cell types by combining the advantages of 10x Visium spatial transcriptomics with scRNA- seq and scATAC-seq (Xu et al., 2022). Analysis of these techniques in animal research can provide us with guidance for experimental design in plant research.

Microarray-based sample preparation or LCM technology can also be used to continuously characterize the metabolic hetero- geneity of single cells (Krismer et al., 2020; Yamada et al.,

2022). Key genes and metabolites identified from epigenomic, transcriptomic, and metabolomic data at the single-cell level, together with development of user-friendly software packages, can enable prediction of interaction mechanisms, thereby revealing the unique molecular characteristics of individual cells in plants. We therefore propose a possible multi-omics analysis pipeline combining multiple techniques such as 10x Visium spatial transcriptomics, Stereo-seq, scRNA-seq, and AFAI- MSI-based SM (Figure 5). Unlike traditional single-omics techniques for studying biological processes from the perspective of metabolomics, genomics, or transcriptomics, spatial multi-omics technology integrates meta- bolic and genetic information during development, enabling anal- ysis of correlations between key metabolites and gene expres- sion at a single-cell resolution. By observing interactions between cells with spatial multi-omics, a more comprehensive understanding of cell functions will be revealed, and rare cell groups and complex metabolites will be discovered for further research on plant development. Future spatial multi-omics tech- nologies will bring new research breakthroughs, deepening our understanding of the regulatory mechanisms of multiple cell and tissue types.
