**INTRODUCTION**

At present, we are facing challenges from the triple burden of nutrition: elimination of hunger, malnutrition related to hidden hunger, and increasing obesity, with accompanying higher inci- dence of type-2 diabetes and other noncommunicable chronic diseases (NCDs) (Martin & Li 2017, Parween et al. 2020). Breeding programs have centered on generating high-yielding vari- eties of staple foods to ensure food security for the growing global population.However,the yield has often been pursued at the expense of nutritional quality.The opportunity to tap the enormous diversity of nutritional content present in crop landraces or wild relatives has been missed. In- deed, for almost all our crops, genetic bottlenecks imposed during early domestication through modern breeding mean that cultivated crop varieties carry only a fraction of the variation present in the gene pool (Fernie et al. 2006). As a result, breeders have missed the opportunity to ad- dress nutritional security issues and focused on core food security alone.Fortunately,the diversity presentinmajorcropsandtheirdistantrelativeshasbeencollectedandstoredinexsitugenebanks (Figures 1 and 2 and Supplemental Table 1) to be tapped to enhance the nutritional value of the foods they produce. On the completion of the first crop genome—that of rice in 2006—it was barely conceivable that a mere 14 years later, whole-genome sequences for some 30 crop species of plants would be available, many at chromosome-level resolution. Similarly, that resequencing of hundreds of accessions would become commonplace so rapidly was equally unforeseen. In parallel, the advent of metabolomics rendered it possible to simultaneously profile the presence and quantify the abundance of a wide range of metabolites, macronutrients, vitamins, and phytonutrients, and techniques for similar analyses of minerals have also been established. These techniques have been applied to large collections of cultivars, landraces, and wild relatives of crops. However, to date, fingerprinting efforts have not been undertaken at the scale of entire genebank collections to assess the levels of health-promoting compounds. #### DIETS MEETING THE REQUIREMENT OF HUMAN NUTRITION

Diets rich in nutrients have always shaped human health. To maintain optimum health, per day we need 225 g of high-quality carbohydrates, 50 g of protein with essential amino acids, 75 g of fat with not more than 20 g of saturated fatty acids, 30 g of dietary fiber, and additional nutrients, including essential vitamins and minerals and phytonutrients.In the past three decades,diets have shifted toward highly processed foods with excessive calories and inadequate nutritional content (Reynolds et al. 2019). Continued global urbanization has led to people leading more sedentary lives. The broad adoption of Western diets has created an imbalance between energy intake and expenditure, leading to the obesity epidemic and a global pandemic of noncommunicable dis- eases.More than three billion people are afflicted by malnutrition,resulting in a daily mortality of 24,000 people (Tulchinsky 2010). The effects of malnutrition, such as deficiencies in micronutri- ents, are not always apparent clinically but are associated with life-threatening complications that can seriously damage health and quality of life (Tulchinsky 2010). #### GLOBAL SEEDBANKS: AN UNTAPPED RESOURCE

#### FOR NUTRITIONALLY BENEFICIAL ALLELES

Humans have developed a mutualistic relationship to crop plants over millennia. Domesticated crops require human intervention to complete their life cycles,and the metabolism of humans also depends on the sustained supply of carbohydrates and nutrients from domesticated crops. This relationship has resulted in genetic alterations in both human and domesticated crop genomes. The human genome shows genetic footprints in genes related to specific diets (Raj et al. 2019), and the domestication of crop plants and their continuous adaptation to our needs resulted in the loss of genetic diversity. Modern elite varieties, which are the result of cross-breeding, show yet lower genetic diversity when compared to their landrace progenitors (Raj et al. 2019). Consequently, contemporary plant breeding depends on a continuous influx of genetic di- versity; otherwise, genetic gains stall. For many thousands of years, farmers have been the conservationists of genetic diversity by growing locally adapted landraces in their fields and pass- ing the seeds on to their descendants. At the turn of the twentieth century, with the onset of the development of genetically uniform cultivars and hybrids, farmers ceased to act as gatekeepers of genetic diversity, and they were replaced by ex situ genebanks founded in the 1920s based on the visionary concepts of the Russian geneticist Nicolai Vavilov. Today, ex situ genebanks play a crucial role in conserving the genetic diversity of crop plants and their utilization. At present, an estimated 7.4 million accessions are maintained in 1,750 national and inter- national genebanks worldwide (Lee et al. 2020). Of these, more than 6.5 million accessions are housed in national genebanks, of which only 123 hold more than 10,000 accessions each (FAO

2019). Some 760,000 accessions are maintained in the nine Consultative Group of International Agricultural Research (CGIAR) genebanks (CGIAR & Crop Trust 2019).Each accession in these genebanks has a “passport”description that details information such as collection sites,taxonomic status,and the provenance of the material.Approximately 95% of the genetic resources are stored as seed samples at low temperatures. At the same time, other accessions are maintained vegeta- tively wherever the corresponding species give rise to recalcitrant seeds or do not breed true to type or do not set seeds.Hence,many tropical species are not amenable to long-term conservation at low temperatures. Clonal conservation of plants is a costly undertaking jeopardized by pests, diseases, and adverse environmental conditions. Hence, several genebanks operate laboratories for in vitro culture of such accessions. In addition, pretreated tissue samples of selected species, including potato, banana, and garlic, are stored at ultra-low temperatures in liquid nitrogen. Botan- ical gardens strive to maximize the number of species to ensure the representativeness of their collections is high. Consequently, more than 50% of the ex situ accessions with known biological status are categorized as landraces or wild relatives, and less than 10% are advanced cultivars (FAO 2022b). There is no clear metric defining how many samples are required to represent a given species’ genetic diversity adequately. As a first approxima- tion, ex situ representation of a given crop is a function of its agricultural importance because the acreage fetched by a given crop is significantly correlated with the number of ex situ accessions. However, this correlation holds only for seed-borne crop species (Figure 2). Hence, further ef- forts are needed to identify gaps in existing collections and to better represent wild relatives and neglected crop species because of their potential value for trait improvement.

#### SEQUENCING GENEBANK ACCESSIONS AND GENOME-WIDE

#### ASSOCIATION MAPPING

Emergent sequencing technology has provided novel and valuable insights into ancestry, geno- typic, and phenotypic variation, improving genebanks’ operation (Milner et al. 2019). Owing to their cost-effective and high-throughput nature, many single nucleotide polymorphisms (SNPs) have been identified that define the characteristics of specific accessions. These genetic profiles, which are becoming available for many genetically diverse genebank accessions, serve to comple- ment or even supplant traditional passport records (Mascher et al. 2019). Advances in the speed and accuracy of DNA sequencing technologies blazed the trail for high-density fingerprinting of a comprehensive core collection of rice comprising 3,000 accessions (3000 Rice Genomes Proj.

2014) as well as comprehensive sets of maize inbred accessions, which are maintained in national collections in the United States and France and the USDA germplasm collection of 19,448 soy- bean accessions (Song et al. 2015). Using a low-coverage sequencing approach (genotyping by sequencing), the entire barley collection housed at the German Federal ex situ genebank, com- prising >20,000 accessions, was fingerprinted by analyzing 171,000 SNPs (Milner et al. 2019).

Recently, exploration of the genetic diversity of genebank collections by whole-genome shotgun sequencing has been initiated (Varshney et al. 2019). #### TARGETED ANALYSIS, METABOLOMICS, LIPIDOMICS,

#### AND IONOMICS

Targeted analysis of the chemical composition of crops has been carried out for decades, with a prominent example being the Illinois Long-Term Selection Experiment for grain protein and oil content in maize (Moose et al. 2004). By 2004, this project had undergone 103 cycles of selection, which produced nine related populations exhibiting extremes of grain composition, in- dicating the power of breeding to produce (reversible) changes in food characteristics related to health. Although this study utilized simple phenotyping methods, within the past 20 years, such studies have been augmented by high-throughput, unbiased analyses, including metabolomics, lipidomics,and ionomics (Alseekh & Fernie 2018,Huang & Salt 2016,Tsugawa et al.2020).Early metabolomic analysis relied on gas chromatography–mass spectrometry (GC-MS),which is capa- ble of detecting a few hundred mass features corresponding to 100–200 metabolites (Alseekh & Fernie 2018).Given that the plant kingdom is estimated to contain more than one million unique small molecules (Wang et al. 2019), such methods were far from comprehensive and limited to low-molecular-weight compounds. Although nuclear magnetic resonance remains the gold stan- dard in terms of structural elucidation, its relative insensitivity renders it less useful than GC-MS for metabolomic analysis of plant tissues (Fernie et al. 2004). Liquid chromatography–mass spec- trometry (LC-MS) has, in recent years, become the method of choice for plant metabolomics, given its more extensive dynamic range and broader coverage. Current analytic platforms can de- tect tens of thousands of mass features corresponding to a couple of thousand metabolites with known chemical structures.LC-MS has,therefore,been championed as the technique most likely to improve the comprehensiveness of the metabolome.Lipidomics has been used to classify more than 300 plant lipids, predominantly for characterizing oil seed composition. Multiparallel mea- surements of ion contents based on flame photometer (Munns et al. 2010) or the more sensitive liquidchromatography(Cataldietal.2003)andinductivelycoupledplasmamassspectrometrycan now measure a few to more than ten ions, respectively. More details can be found in an extensive review of ionomics in plants (Salt et al. 2008). As of mid-September 2020, nearly 53,032 compounds from 129,662 different plant species have been listed in the KnapSac database. Moreover, metabolomic studies have been published for all 16 major crops listed in Supplemental Table 3. #### DIET RICH IN PROTEIN AND ESSENTIAL AMINO ACIDS

A wealth of analysis has been undertaken on the protein content of crops. We exemplify this with studies in rice and maize before outlining studies on a broader range of species, including

high-protein crops such as legumes. We also describe analyses of free amino acids, paying particular attention to the essential amino acids that we cannot synthesize ourselves and thus need to obtain in our diet (Galili et al. 2016). An incredibly long-running experiment with 115 recurrent selections has provided considerable insight into wide genetic variation in protein and oil content in maize. This experiment, which has been running continuously since 1986, re- vealed that reversible increases and decreases in protein and oil content of up to 20 and 4 standard deviations, respectively, were achievable by breeding (Moose et al. 2004). In rice, genes encoding an amino acid transporter OsAAP6 (qPC1) and a glutelin type A2 precursor (qGPC-10) have been fine-mapped and shown to contribute to elevating grain protein content (Yang et al. 2018). Similarly, for wheat, both QTL and GWAS approaches have proven effective in defining ge- nomic regions underlying grain protein content (Muqaddasi et al. 2020). In legumes, QTLs for seed protein and oil content have also been reported (Lu et al. 2013), and GWASs also indicate broad variation in these traits across panels of up to 570 accessions (Leamy et al. 2017). Protein is seen widely as being of high nutritional relevance, and protein content has been well character- ized in legumes, which, alongside nuts, are vital sources of nonmeat protein (Galili et al. 2016). However, excess animal-based protein in the diet has been reported to have deleterious effects on bone structure (Martin et al. 2011). The protein content of many staple crops is relatively low, meaningdietaryproteinexcessisgenerallynotaproblem.Indeed,theWorldHealthOrganization estimates that 30% of the population suffers from protein-energy malnutrition, with symptoms including kidney malfunction, retarded mental and physical development of young children, and poor musculature and dull skin (Galili et al. 2016). Protein deficiency can, to some extent, be overcome by a mixed diet of cereals (deficient in Lys,Thr,Trp,and Tyr) and legumes (deficient in Met and Cys).The development of high-protein and high-essential-amino-acid cultivars has been attempted for several different crops (Galili et al. 2016). Recently, several metabolomic GWASs and QTL studies in rice, maize, wheat, barley, sorghum, and tomato have identified considerable variation in the levels of free amino acids (Supplemental Table 4). Although breeding falls short of the massive increases in Lys content possible using transgenic approaches (Yang et al.2021),ex- periments have demonstrated that it is an effective route to increase protein content (Moose et al.

2004).Furthermore,the genetics underlying amino acid content suggests that breeding could also improve free amino acid content (Schauer et al. 2008).
#### OIL ENRICHED WITH POLYUNSATURATED FATTY ACIDS

Considerable effort has been invested in improving lipid content and composition. The lipids of nutritional importance are triglycerides (fats and oils), phospholipids (e.g., lecithin), and sterols (e.g., cholesterol). Lipids in the diet transport the four fat-soluble vitamins (vitamins A, D, E, and K) and assist in their absorption in the small intestine (Broun et al. 1999). They also carry with them substances that impart sensory appeal and palatability to food and provide satiety value. Recent studies of human neonates have shown that the intake of long-chain PUFAs has beneficial effects on cardiovascular disease, renal function, and the development of the normal function of the retina and brain (Jasani et al. 2017). Notably, there is great natural variation in oil content of maize (Li et al. 2014) and rice (Goffman et al. 2003, Zaplin et al. 2013) as well as in Brassica napus (B. Wang et al. 2018), Elaeis guineensis, and soybean (Liu et al. 2020) (Supplemental Table 4). Crude rice oil consists of approximately 70% triglyceride, with various other lipids making up the remainder, including antioxidants such as γ-oryzanol. On the other

hand, refined rice oil consists of roughly 20% saturated fatty acids, 37% monounsaturated fatty acids, and 37% PUFAs. Capturing natural variation in fatty acid composition in different rice accessions led to identifying key candidate genes such as FAD2 (Goffman et al. 2003), the sup- pression of which leads to an increase in oleic acid (Zaplin et al. 2013). World rice production is around 700 million metric tons, of which approximately 10% is bran, and of the bran, 20% is oil. Most rice bran is treated as waste due to rancidification and a lack of large-scale oil extraction pro- cesses.In contrast,the seminal study of Li et al.(2014) used a GWAS to analyze 368 inbred maize lines, including high-oil lines, identifying 74 loci significantly associated with kernel oil content and fatty acid composition, which defined 26 regions underlying maize kernel oil content, thus providing considerable resources for breeding of this trait (Li et al.2014). Because higher plants do not synthesize EPA and DHA, transgenic approaches are necessary for elevating the omega-3 long-chain PUFA content of oilseeds (Rani et al. 2018).

#### TWEAKING THE QUALITY OF STARCH TO ADDRESS

**HYPERGLYCEMIA**

Cereal grains store their primary energy reserves in the form of starch (composed of two types of α-glucan:amyloseandamylopectin)intheendosperm.Starchstaplesinvokeahighglycemicindex (GI)whenconsumed,butvariationintheresistanceofstarchtodigestion,particularlytoamylases, exists in nature (Figure 4); thus, starch quality needs to be improved by increasing the content of resistant starch (RS). The exploitation of natural and induced variation in starch biosynthetic genes has allowed the selection of traits impacting the fine structures of starch molecules, which improve cooking and eating quality (Butardo et al. 2017). An increase in the ratio of amylose to amylopectin can be obtained in different cereal mutants with an increase in the composition of RS. RS is known to protect against colon cancer, diabetes, and obesity (Birt et al. 2013). In rice, a mutant derived from the synergistic effect of a null mutation in Starch Synthase IIIa (SSIIIa)and high expression of the Waxy (Wx *a* ) allele (Waxy encodes a granule-bound starch synthase) leads to a dramatic increase in RS. Similarly, mutations in Starch Synthase IIa (SSIIa)andStarch Branching *EnzymeII (SBEII) increase the apparent amylose content in other crops such as barley,maize,pea,* potato (Jobling et al. 1999), and wheat. Clinical trials and animal studies investigating diets rich in RS have curtailed fat deposition, improved insulin sensitivity, and established the metabolic benefits of RS. For example, human subjects fed with BARLEYmax TM , a high RS line, promote butyric acid–producing bacteria and improve gut health (Akagawa et al. 2021).

#### DIVERSIFIED HEALTHY FOOD RICH IN VITAMINS

Vitamins, by definition, are essential within the human diet because of our inability to synthesize these compounds de novo. The term vitamin was coined by the Polish biochemist Casimir Funk in 1912 when he isolated a substance present in rice bran but not in polished rice (Oryza sativa) that could alleviate the deficiency disease beriberi that is endemic to many Asian countries

(Fitzpatrick et al. 2012). Thirteen different compounds have been classified as vitamins (Supplemental Table 6). Most vitamins are made in plants, but their availability in the ed- ible parts of many staple crops such as rice, maize, wheat, cassava, and potato is below minimum requirements. This problem is exacerbated by the fact that levels of vitamins are often sig- nificantly depleted by postharvest processing, such that vitamin status is far from adequate in many countries and results in severe health consequences (see Supplemental Table 6). This is particularly true in areas where many people do not have the means to consume a diverse diet and rely heavily on single staple crops such as rice or cassava (FAO 2022c). Although there is considerable access to diverse foods that provide all required vitamins in either fresh or fortified form, vitamin deficiencies remain a considerable problem globally. The following sections review attempts to assess the natural variation in vitamin content in crops. We describe only the best-characterized vitamins—A, B, C, and E— and considerable work is required to better understand the production and abundance of vitamins D and K. Addressing vitamin A deficiency has been the subject of considerable metabolic engineering, with a range of high–vitamin A crops being developed by transgenic approaches (Paine et al.

2005), the most famous being Golden rice, which has recently gained regulatory approval for consumption and processing in some countries. Golden rice is rich in β-carotene, the best plant source of provitamin A because it can be metabolized to two molecules of retinal (vitamin A) in contrast to other plant carotenoids such as α-carotene, which can be converted to only one. Although natural variation in β-carotene content (which confers the orange color associated with provitamin A) is absent in rice cultivars, it is present in maize, where the level of this compound varies by a factor of 190-fold. Moreover, several provitamin A–dense maize varieties with near- target levels of β-carotene with local and regional adaptation and relevance have been developed (Wurtzel et al. 2012). Additionally,severalQTLshavebeenidentifiedinmaizethatenhancethelevelofprovitaminA by more than threefold (Azmach et al.2013).The combination of association-based mapping and linkage analysis in maize identified variations in the genes encoding lycopene epsilon cyclase and β-carotene hydrolase (both involved in the catabolism of β-carotene), which are responsible for high β-carotene content. Hence, it allowed the development of inexpensive molecular markers to select varieties replete with this vitamin (Harjes et al. 2008). Interestingly, in cassava, allelic polymorphisminoneofthetwoexpressedphytoenesynthasegenescanenhancethefluxofcarbon through carotenogenesis,thus leading to the accumulation of colored provitamin A carotenoids in storage roots,suggesting that the levels of provitamin A can be elevated via conventional breeding in this crop (Welsch et al. 2010). The vitamin B complex constitutes eight vitamins, with B1, B6, and B9 arguably the most im- portant for human nutrition. Several studies have assessed natural variation in staple crops. QTL mapping identified genomic regions responsible for thiamine (B1) and riboflavin (B2) content in wheat (Li et al. 2018). Similarly, wild potato relatives that display more than twofold higher thi- amine content in tubers have been proposed as valuable breeding stock (Goyer & Sweek 2011).

Screening of natural genetic variation in vitamin C (ascorbate) content is arguably the most advanced of all vitamins, with considerable work being carried out in the screening of natural variation, QTL analysis, and, most recently, association studies (e.g., GWASs) in a wide range of species, including tomato, apples, kiwi, and strawberry. #### CHALLENGES AND PERSPECTIVES

The health of future generations will be determined by the nutrient per calorie density of their diets. Generally, promoting sustainable plant-based foods constituted with dense nutrients while meetingthecaloricneedsofhumanpopulationsislikelytotransformsocieties,regardlessoffinan- cial status, race, or nationality. From a health perspective, food must provide macronutrients and micronutrients adequate to meet the human body’s daily requirements without toxic elements and antinutrients. When we eat high-nutrient foods (rich in antioxidants, micronutrients, vita- mins, and dietary fiber), the body ages more slowly, Type 2 diabetes may be reversed, obesity reduced, NCDs prevented, and immunity to infectious diseases may be higher. When consuming carbohydrate-rich food, we need to eat low-GI food with enhanced quality of complex carbohy- drates, for example, by selecting whole grains over processed foods. Meeting these daily dietary requirements can be achieved through dietary diversification, including consuming superfoods with high contents of vitamins,minerals,and bioactive compounds.These nutrients have demon- stratedthepositiveimpactofaplant-richdietonhumanphysiology.However,meetingthedietary needs of low- and middle-income target groups requires strategies to enrich nutrient density in staple foods and increase the diversity of foods consumed. The importance of plant-based foods in preventing and reducing chronic disease is well recog- nized. However, there is a need to better compile data on bioactive compounds using up-to-date data standards and classifications and develop a sustainable infrastructure to support users, in- cluding researchers,policymakers,industry,and consumers.Information on bioactive compounds is available in databases, such as eBasis/ePlantLIBRA, Phenol Explorer, and the USDA databases forflavonoids,isoflavones,andproanthocyanidins.However,thesehavesignificantlimitationsthat impact the ability of researchers to produce reliable and comparable estimates of bioactive intake for use in epidemiological studies. Examples of the limitations include (a) data source and data quality issues; (b) food component variability within and between varieties, locations, and crops;

(c) reduced geographical ranges of foods reported upon; (d) issues with compiler decisions, often due to a lack of common standards and ontologies; (e) data access issues, e.g., some data sets are subscriber based and should be open access; ( f) lack of common user-friendly interfaces and asso- ciated rationale databases; (g) breadth of crops studied; (h) lack of information and detail on crop varieties and local/indigenous crops and foods; and, finally, (i) lack of coordination of national and international efforts for sustainable production of healthy and nutritious food with improved value chains. Developing an online data management platform with a user-friendly interface is desperately needed to address these problems.
