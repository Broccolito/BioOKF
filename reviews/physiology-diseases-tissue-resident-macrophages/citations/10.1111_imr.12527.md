Signal regulatory proteins (SIRP) are a multigene family of immuno- receptors encoded in humans by a cluster of genes on chromosome 20p13. 15 Basically, the family encompasses five members with vari- able levels of amino acid sequence homology, including SIRPα, SIRPβ1, SIRPγ, SIRPβ2, and SIRPδ (Figure 1A). 15,16 The glycoprotein SIRPα, also known as CD172a, SHPS-1, BIT (rat homologue), p84 (mouse homo- logue), MFR, MYD-1, or PTPNS1, is the primordial and also the best conserved member of the family, with other genes and pseudogenes often being the result of independent SIRPα duplications, thereby giv- ing rise to different compositions of SIRP families in other species, including rodents. 15 Structurally, SIRPα consists of three extracellu- lar Ig superfamily (IgSF) domains, including an NH2-terminal CD47- ligand binding V-domain and two C1-type IgSF domains, that are connected through a single transmembrane region to an intracellular tail with inhibitory signaling capacity17 (Figure 1B) (see also below). #### 4 | CD47 AND SIRPα

#### EXPRESSION AND REGULATION

CD47 is broadly expressed on the membrane of essentially all cell types. On the contrary, the expression of SIRPα is much more restricted to particularly myeloid cells, including monocytes, mac- rophages, granulocytes, and CD4-positive dendritic cells, and also in variable levels on neuronal cells in the central nervous system. 29,33 Although the expression levels of SIRPα appears to remain rather sta- ble during several inflammatory conditions, 33 its function can poten- tially be influenced by the proteolytic cleavage of its cytoplasmic tail. This has been shown to occur, for instance, following exposure of THP-1 monocytic leukemia cells to pro-inflammatory stimuli, such as LPS or TNF-α, 49 or in vivo in neutrophils during experimentally induced colitis in mice. 50 This cleavage of the cytoplasmic tail may, under such conditions, lead to a SIRPα that lacks inhibitory signaling and it may perhaps even function as a decoy receptor, as the remain- ing extracellular part was found to still effectively bind CD47. 50 In contrast with the relatively stable expression of SIRPα, the expression levels of CD47 appear to be more variable, and this may be relevant when considering CD47-SIRPα interactions as an immune checkpoint in cancer. Another aspect that also appears to be relevant in this context is an intriguing mechanism of macrophage “education” that has been identified, which is apparently tuning the sensitivity of macrophages toward target cells 55 lacking CD47 (see also below). The functional relevance of CD47 on cells may be affected by other positive or negative signals that act in concert with CD47. Among such positive signals are opsonins, eg, antibodies and/or complement. 56 In the context of cancer, different types of cancer cells have been reported to overexpress CD47, and this may contribute to the prevention of their phagocytic destruction, and thereby promote their survival. 57–59 Indeed, in many of these stud- ies overexpressed CD47 was implicated to function as a “don’t eat me” signal on cancer cells, inhibiting phagocytosis and clearance by macrophages and enhancing tumor cell survival in vivo, 51 which will be further discussed below. Furthermore, a number of patient stud- ies have described the expression level of CD47 on cancer cells to be negatively associated with the response to therapy, including both chemotherapy and antibody therapy. This includes patients with either hematological or solid cancers, and low expression of CD47 on tumor cells was in many cases associated with a better prognosis 24,57–59 and an improved survival of patients. 60 It is known that cancer devel- opment and metastasis formation are primarily driven by so-called tumor-initiating cells and/or cancer stem cells (CSC), populations of cancer cells with strong capacity for self-renewal, which are often also more resistant to many conventional cancer therapies. 61 Of interest, CSC self-renewal appears to be regulated by CD47-TSP-1 interactions [reviewed in (62)]. In particular, a high expression of CD47 on CSC and functional CD47-TSP-1 interactions are associated with an increased capacity for self-renewal, whereas in normal stem cells such interac- tions are rather limiting self-renewal instead. Collectively, the high expression and functional role of CD47 on survival of both differen- tiated cancer cells of several types and on cancer stem cells provides a basis to target CD47-SIRPα interactions in a therapeutic context, which will be further discussed below.

#### 5.1 | CD47 as a “don’t eat me” signal

The basis for its functioning as an innate immune checkpoint in can- cer can be found in the normal homeostatic function of CD47-SIRPα interactions in the body. The first evidence for this came from com- pelling experiments in which CD47-deficient red blood cells (RBC) that were infused into normal recipients were shown to be cleared within hours because of an accelerated phagocytosis by macrophages in the spleen. 67 In contrast, normal erythrocytes survive for ~45 days in mice. 68 This essentially demonstrated that CD47 functions as a sign of “self” or a “don’t eat me” signal. The involvement of SIRPα and that of CD47-SIRPα interactions in RBC phagocytosis and clearance in vivo was confirmed 69,70 using mice with a mutant SIRPα receptor lack- ing the cytoplasmic tail. 71 Further mechanistic studies implicated that binding of CD47 to SIRPα leads to an RBC phagocytosis-associated deactivation of myosin IIA, which is an important contributor of phago- cytosis because of its participation in macrophage actino-myosin con- traction. 72 CD47-SIRPα interactions also suppress the clearance by macrophages of antibody- or complement- opsonized erythrocytes, through Fcγ- or complement- receptors on macrophages, 56 and this could be relevant in the context of autoimmune hemolytic anemia (AIHA). 73,74 The findings described above inspired the important studies by the group of Weissman with leukemic cells to show that also in the context of cancer cells CD47 can function as a “don’t eat me” signal and consequently escape immune surveillance and destruction by phagocytes 51,57 (also see Figure 2A). #### 5.3 | Targeting CD47-SIRPα interactions in vivo:

#### studies in xenogeneic and syngeneic mouse models

The majority of the in vivo experiments related to CD47-SIRPα checkpoint inhibition in cancer have been performed in xenogeneic immunodeficient mouse models (ie, either NOD/SCID or NOD/ RAG/common γ-chain-double deficient mice, ie NSG mice) in which human tumor cells were injected in conjunction with intact CD47 blocking antibodies, such as the B6H12 antibody, often with quite compelling results on tumor growth reduction and frequently on survival too, and this has already been shown for a striking vari- ety of cancers. 57–59,79–86 While these xenogeneic models are widely employed and have proven very useful for preclinical testing of drugs on human cancer cells in an in vivo context, there are also a few issues that deserve some discussion. Firstly, NOD/SCID mice or NSG mice, which have most commonly been used as the recipients of human cancer cells in these studies, lack both T and B lympho- cytes and NSG mice also NK cells. So it should be realized that such models represent quite strongly immunocompromised and there- fore oversimplified immune systems, without adaptive immunity. This may perhaps show some resemblance to the immunocompro- mised condition in cancer patients that receive chemotherapy, but it is also rather different from the desired immunocompetent situation in patients in which immunotherapeutic intervention with check- point inhibitors is expected to provide optimal results. Furthermore,

**|**

it should be realized that these xenogeneic models may not allow proper functioning of other often species-specific potentially rel- evant checkpoints that could under physiological syngeneic con- ditions act in conjunction with CD47-SIRPα interactions. Another point to bear in mind is that xenogeneic NOD models typically allow for extraordinary potent interactions between human CD47 and NOD mouse SIRPα, which is known to be ~10-fold stronger than the endogenous human CD47-human SIRPα interaction. 103 This is in fact the principal reason why immunodeficient mice on the NOD background are superior in the engraftment of human cells to begin with, and are so widely used in human engraftment studies. 25 Anyway, because of the stronger binding of human CD47 to NOD mouse SIRPα the role of CD47-SIRPα might be somewhat overem- phasized in such xenogeneic models. Finally, it should be realized that when using a human-selective CD47 antibody, like, eg, B6H12, in such xenogeneic cancer models that it bypasses the putative ‘anti- genic sink’ of the broadly expressed CD47 and also avoids potential side-effects by binding to normal cells that might occur in a synge- neic situation, like in a cancer patient. In spite of all these limitations, the number of studies in which beneficial effects of CD47-SIRPα checkpoint blockade in vivo has already been shown remains quite impressive. So can interference with the CD47-SIRPα checkpoint also cause tumor elimination in a syngeneic immunocompetent context? There is certainly evidence to support this and it is clearly growing. The first evidence for this came from our own studies in which we used mice which lack the SIRPα cytoplasmic tail and therefore its inhibitory sig- naling capacity. 24 When subjected to the well-established metastatic B16F10 melanoma model there is in the absence of cancer-opsonizing antibodies no difference in cancer metastasis and outgrowth between wildtype and SIRPα-mutant mice, but in the presence of TA99 anti- bodies against gp75, a tumor antigen overexpressed on melanoma cells, a much more pronounced and essentially complete elimina- tion of the cancer cells in the SIRPα-mutant animals occurs. A recent study also reported in the same syngeneic melanoma model beneficial effects of a high affinity single-chain blocking alpaca antibody against mouse CD47, in particular in combination with anti-PD-L1 antibod- ies. 76 Although in these experiments the effects of the CD47 antibody alone and even in the presence of TA99 were limited, there was some doubt whether sufficient levels of saturation had been achieved by the alpaca CD47 antibody to see proper effects at all. 104 Moreover, it could be that the PD-L1 antibody used was not only blocking, but was rather, or simultaneously, opsonizing the cancer cells as shown before. 105 Several other studies showed that the targeting of mouse CD47, using conventional blocking antibodies, in the absence of other partner antibodies, can indeed be effective in syngeneic mod- els of breast cancer, colon carcinoma and B cell lymphoma, and these studies also revealed an adaptive immune contribution to the anti- 59,106 cancer response (see also below). Thus, indeed it appears that also targeting CD47 in a syngeneic immunocompetent mice can have beneficial effects, but clearly more work is needed to be able to see this for more models and types of cancer, and also with other relevant targeting agents.

#### 5.4 | Mechanism(s) of action of CD47-SIRPα innate

#### checkpoint inhibition

The findings described above using intact CD47 antibodies (see Tables 1 and 2) and many of the other studies as well 24,75 suggest that blocking CD47 alone may perhaps not be sufficient for inducing tumor cell destruction, but rather that a second stimulus such as an opsonizing antibody or another pro-phagocytic stimulus107 may be required (Figure 2A). However, it should also be emphasized that it cannot be excluded that at least some of the many reported effects of the CD47 antibodies in these experiments are due to the pure blocking of CD47-SIRPα interactions. But is there other evidence to support the idea that CD47-SIRPα blockade can be sufficient for cancer cell elimination? Well, there is perhaps some, 57,59,108 albeit still somewhat circumstantial and also controversial, 87,89,109 because it is based on downregulation of cancer cell CD47 by RNAi, which may also affect other functions of CD47 that are unrelated to SIRPα binding. Clearly, the difficulty to distinguish between CD47-SIRPα check- point blockade and opsonization in some of the experimental set- tings has created conceptual confusion and discussions in the field. Although in principle the two-hit signal exerted by an intact CD47 blocking antibody may constitute a powerful approach in the destruc- tion of cancer cells too, it may not be desirable at all with respect to the potential side effects toward healthy cells, which of course gen- erally express high levels of CD47. This view is also supported by the relatively strong side effects seen with, eg, the affinity engineered SIRPα-IgG4 Fc construct targeting CD47 in cynomolgus monkeys. 75 While an enhanced macrophage phagocytosis has generally been implicated to explain the anti-cancer effects of CD47-SIRPα check- point blockade, it should be emphasized that not only macrophages, but also neutrophils express SIRPα and these cells can mediate strong ADCC toward cancer cells (Figure 2A). Furthermore, neutrophils have indeed been shown to directly contribute to in vivo effects of anti- body therapy as seen in both syngeneic as well as xenogeneic mouse cancer models. 110–112 In vitro, blocking either CD47 or SIRPα with antagonistic intact or F(ab)2antibodies in combination with trastu- zumab lead to a synergistic enhancement of neutrophil-mediated killing of breast cancer cells. 24 Also here, the effects were absolutely dependent on the presence of a cancer targeting opsonizing anti- body, as no effects were observed when CD47-SIRPα interactions were blocked alone. It should be noted that the exact mechanism by which neutrophils mediate these cytotoxic effects towards antibody- opsonized cancer cells and how this process is influenced by SIRPα signaling is still largely unknown and this therefore needs further research as well.

#### 5.7 | Combination of targeting CD47-SIRPα and

#### other immune checkpoints

While it is clear that CD47-SIRPα interactions are a promising innate immune checkpoint in the context of cancer treatment, other immune checkpoint inhibitors targeting, eg, PD-L1, present on the membrane of cancer cells, and PD-1, expressed on the surface of activated lym- phocytes, are already in clinical use for the treatment of several types of cancer, including melanoma and NSCLC. 3–12 Interestingly, both the expression of CD47 and PD-L1 on several types of cancer have shown to be upregulated through activity of the important oncogenic tran- scription factor MYC, 66 and the combined activity of these apparently helps the cancer cells to evade both innate and adaptive anti-tumor responses. 66 Of interest, the combined targeting of CD47 and PD-L1 is exactly what is addressed in an exciting recent study by Sockolosky et al. 76 , which provides the first evidence that blocking CD47 and PD-L1 antibodies synergize, which can indeed be beneficial for can- cer elimination. #### 5.8 | Inhibition of CD47-SIRPα in combination with

#### other anti-cancer agents

In hepatocellular cancer (HCC) an upregulation of CD47 was shown to be associated with an increased resistance toward the chemotherapeutic drugs sorafenib, doxorubicin, and cisplatin through selection of tumor-initiating cells, which could be reversed 120,121 after knock-down of CD47 in several HCC cell lines in vitro (for more details see Tables 1 and 2). Furthermore, there is evi- dence that chemotherapeutics, in particular cyclophosphamide and paclitaxel, when administered shortly before CD47 antibod- ies in syngeneic cancer models, can synergize in the establishment of anti-tumor immunity and also support the formation of mem- ory responses. 106 When such chemotherapeutics were, however, administered after the CD47 antibodies they rather compromised immune-mediated cancer elimination, thereby demonstrating that timing is a critical issue in this context, and also confirming that a functional immune system is important for the anti-cancer activity of CD47 antibodies.

It is known that clinical application of some of the registered immune checkpoint inhibitors can cause significant toxicity. 122 However, each checkpoint is anticipated to have unique biological properties, and until the results of a number of ongoing phase I clinical trials in hematologic and solid cancers with the CD47 blocking antibod- ies Hu5F9-G4 92 (www.clinicaltrials.gov identifiers: NCT02216409; NCT02678338), CC-90002 (NCT02641002; NCT02367196), and the recombinant SIRPα-Fc protein96 (NCT02890368; NCT02663518) will become available it remains difficult to predict potential side effects of CD47-SIRPα checkpoint inhibition in cancer. #### 6.1 | RBC clearance and anemia

As already mentioned above, one of the best established homeo- static functions of the CD47-SIRPα interaction is its role as a nega- tive regulator of RBC clearance. 67 The experiments performed with RBC from CD47-deficient mice67 demonstrated a compelling and very rapid clearance of RBC that occurred within hours. Strikingly, as said both CD47- and SIRPα signaling-deficient mice show only very mild symptoms of anemia and thrombocytopenia 71,73 (J. Alvarez Zarate and

T.K. van den Berg, unpublished data), which is in apparent contrast to the rapid clearance of CD47 null erythrocytes upon infusion into wildtype animals.
67 An interesting observation in the original study by Oldenborg et al. was that when CD47-deficient RBC were injected into CD47-deficient recipients the clearance of erythrocytes was nor- mal. This suggested that the presence of CD47 was somehow instruct- ing macrophages to recognize CD47 null cells. This was further addresses by the elegant study of Wang et al. 55 , where various types of bone marrow chimeras were used, in which CD47 expression was either missing from hematopoietic or non-hematopoietic cells and clearance of CD47 null erythrocytes was analyzed. As it turned out, absence of CD47 expression on non-hematopoietic cells was a neces- sary disposition for inducing tolerance of macrophages to CD47 null cells, implying a process of macrophage “education” in the host that is dependent on the presence of CD47 during macrophage develop- ment. 55 While the underlying mechanism(s) of this macrophage edu- cation are not understood, it could perhaps imply that when CD47 is normally present, as would be expected in most individuals, that the effect of CD47-SIRPα with respect to anemia could be much more pronounced. Nevertheless, the fact that the available evidence so far only points to a very mild anemia upon treatment with CD47-SIRPα antagonists in mice 51,75,76,124 and monkeys 75,92 is indeed promising.

#### 6.2 | Autoimmunity

One of the most prevalent side effects in therapies utilizing immune checkpoint inhibitors is the triggering of, or exacerbation of, auto- immune reactions, 122 which is perhaps not really surprising given the fact that the normal physiological function of such checkpoints is to dampen immune activity. Can we expect a similar effect when CD47- SIRPα interactions are disrupted? Based on the available evidence it appears that the answer may depend on the type of autoimmunity. First of all, CD47 KO and SIRPα signaling-deficient mice show no signs of overt autoimmunity under steady state conditions to begin with. 36 Furthermore, protective effects of interference with either CD47 or SIRPα were rather observed in various T-cell mediated mod- els of autoimmunity, including colitis, 125 rheumatoid arthritis, 126 or experimental autoimmune encephalomyelitis. Mechanistically this was explained by the requirement of CD47-SIRPα interactions and signaling during antigen presentation, because using either genetically modified animals or blocking agents a strong decrease in generation

of autoreactive T cells was observed in these models. 125,127 However, it should be mentioned that in the context of experimental autoim- mune encephalomyelitis (as a model for multiple sclerosis), there is also some evidence that disruption of CD47-SIRPα interactions may worsen the symptoms at the peak of the disease due to an increased uptake and presentation of myelin by APC. 128 Furthermore, in a mouse model of systemic lupus erythematosus genetic ablation of CD47 caused a suppression of autoreactive IgG antibodies and there- fore also the clinical symptoms of the disease. 129 In complex condi- tion such as diabetes mellitus type I the interpretation of CD47-SIRPα interactions become less clear, as they may regulate insulin produc- tion and responsiveness. 130 However, available evidence from mouse studies suggests that the role of CD47-SIRPα interactions in antibody-mediated autoim- mune diseases may be quite different as compared to the situation in T cell-mediated autoimmunity. In particular, both AIHA or idiopathic thrombocytopenic purpura (ITP) appear exacerbated when exper- imental models of these autoimmune conditions are performed in CD47-deficient mice. 56,73,131 Furthermore, blocking of CD47-SIRPα interactions promotes the phagocytosis of antibody-opsonized eryth- rocytes. 56,132 Thus, it appears that CD47-SIRPα interactions may play a protective role in these conditions.

#### 6.3 | Infection and inflammation

Considering the importance of CD47-SIRPα signaling for normal immune homeostasis, it is quite surprising how mild the phenotype of CD47 or SIRPα deficient mice appears to be under steady state conditions. However, oncological patients may be treated with neo- adjuvant or adjuvant chemotherapy often rendering them neutropenic and highly susceptible to infections. 133 They may also experience signs of disease- or treatment-associated systemic inflammation. 134 So the question is how this may be affected by the disruption of sign- aling via CD47-SIRPα axis? Both CD47 and SIRPα have been implicated in regulation of leu- kocyte migration 36,39,135,136 an obvious question arose to what extent interference with their signaling can affect migration of immune cells to the site of inflammation. It seems most feasible that the effects originally observed with respect to the migration of neutrophils in CD47-deficient mice during bacterial infection were caused by an impaired neutrophil integrin function rather than to a disruption of CD47-SIRPα interactions. 36 At least in models of sterile inflamma- tion CD47 and SIRPα seem to be largely dispensable for leukocyte extravasation and therefore only modest effects can be observed in CD47-KO or SIRPα-mutant animals. 137,138 On the other hand, manip- ulation of CD47-SIRPα signaling in mouse model of allergic inflamma- tion showed protective effects, like in T cell-mediated autoimmunity, probably due to changes in DC-migration. 139,140 Relatively little is still known about the role of CD47-SIRPα sig- naling in infection. #### 7 | CONCLUSIONS AND

#### FUTURE PERSPECTIVES

Collectively, a series of studies published in recent years have iden- tified the CD47-SIRPα interaction as a promising innate immune checkpoint in cancer. With multiple CD47 blocking agents now in clinical trials and a number of other agents in preclinical development it should become clear in the coming years whether CD47-SIRPα checkpoint inhibition is indeed safe and effective, which is currently a major outstanding question in this area. However, there are other important mechanistic issues still to be resolved as well. For instance, there is considerable confusion with respect to the exact molecular and cellular requirements for cancer cell destruction by CD47-SIRPα inhibition, in particular relating to the role of antibody opsonization. Conceptual clarity will obviously be important for designing the most effective and safe CD47-SIRPα checkpoint inhibitors and treatment regimens. Finally, can better effects be reached by using combinations of checkpoint inhibitors and perhaps even other immunotherapeutic approaches, and if so, what are the optimal combinations for what types of cancer? It can be expected that we will be learning much more about these and other relevant issues in this exciting and rap- idly expanding and developing field which is expected to have a major impact in cancer treatment.
