## Abstract

Embryo-derived tissue-resident macrophages are the first representatives of the haematopoietic lineage to emerge in metazoans. In mammals, resident macrophages originate from early yolk sac progenitors and are specified into tissue-specific subsets during organogenesis—establishing stable spatial and functional relationships with specialized tissue cells—and persist in adults. Resident macrophages are an integral part of tissues together with specialized cells: for instance, microglia reside with neurons in brain, osteoclasts reside with osteoblasts in bone, and fat-associated macrophages reside with white adipocytes in adipose tissue. This ancillary cell type, which is developmentally and functionally distinct from haematopoietic stem cell and monocyte-derived macrophages, senses and integrates local and systemic information to provide specialized tissue cells with the growth factors, nutrient recycling and waste removal that are critical for tissue growth, homeostasis and repair. Resident macrophages contribute to organogenesis, promote tissue regeneration following damage and contribute to tissue metabolism and defence against infectious disease. A correlate is that genetic or environment-driven resident macrophage dysfunction is a cause of degenerative, metabolic and possibly inflammatory and tumoural diseases. In this Review, we aim to provide a conceptual outline of our current understanding of macrophage physiology and its importance in human diseases, which may inform and serve the design of future studies.

1.  Wolfgang Liedtke 29 June 2023, 03:44

    **Where is TRPV4 ?**

    This is a tremendously helpful and timely review.
    Yet here is one shortcoming worth pointing out.

    Under "Sensors and Effectors of Tissue Homeostasis", reference is made to macrophages' capability to sense relevant physiologic cues in their microenvironment, such as thermal, osmotic and mechanical cues (with Figure 2 illustrating the concept), toward their proper effector function.

    This important macrophage function is reviewed with incomplete attention to relevant findings.
    A sensor molecule that is surprisingly absent from review is TPRV4, the first vertebrate osmoreceptor \[1, 2\]. Undoubtedly TRPV4 is expressed and functional in macrophages - first reported in high pressure ventilation-induced lung injury which is attenuated in Trpv4-/- mice and restored to full impact by restitution of Trpv4+/+ macrophages \[3\]. Since then, there is a rich paper trail of 82 papers "TRPV4 macrophage" in PubMed, which not only represents impact in numbers, but more importantly in relevance (please do compare with mentioned Piezo1, TRPV2 (!)): for example, TRPV4, via calcium influx, impacts macrophage function in response to urate crystals, foreign materials and, more generally, stiffness of the extracellular matrix, a fundamental mechanical cue for the resident or infiltrating macrophage \[4-8\].
    Many more functions have been observed via rigorous experimental approaches.
    Macrophage TRPV4 expression has been documented in human macrophages, and its role has been defined in macrophage-selective cKO mice \[8-10\], with unambiguous results.
    TRPV4 is polymodally activated, there is osmo-mechano activation amongst activating cues, also including thermal cues, also including direct chemical activation via lysolipids \[8, 11, 12\]. These are relevant for macrophage effector functions and should have been prioritized for discussion.
    Also, receptor systems with endowed responsiveness to physical and basic homeostatic cues (mechano, osmo, pH, thermo, oxygen) might be pleiotropically activated, such as clearly so for TRPV4, and possibly so for Piezo1 which regulates iron homeostasis via expression and function in macrophages \[13\]. This function of Piezo1 in macrophages appears independent of the "classic" mechanical responsiveness of Piezo1.
    In addition, the TRPV2-macrophage literature trail goes back to a paper where TRPV4 expression was not interrogated at the starting point \[14\].
