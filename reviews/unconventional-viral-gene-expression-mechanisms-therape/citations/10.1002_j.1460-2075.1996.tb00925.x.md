Novel exploitation of a nuclear function by influenza virus: the cellular SF2/ASF splicing factor controls the amount of the essential viral M2 ion channel protein in infected cells

Shin-Ru Shihl and Robert M.Krug2

Rutgers University, Department of Molecular Biology and Biochemistry, Piscataway, NJ 08855, USA

'Present address: Department of Medical Technology, Chang Jung College of Medicine and Technology. Kwei-Shar, Tao-Yuan, Taiwan, R.O.C.

2Corresponding author

We show that a cellular nuclear protein, the SR splicing factor SF2/ASF, controls the level of production of an essential influenza virus protein, the M2 ion channel protein. The M2 mRNA that encodes the ion channel protein is produced by alternative splicing of another viral mRNA, Ml mRNA. The production ofM2 mRNA is controlled in two ways. First, a distal (stronger) 5' splice site in Ml mRNA is blocked by the complex of viral polymerase proteins synthesized during infection, allowing the cellular splicing machinery to switch to the proximal (weaker) M2 5' splice site. Second, utilization of the weak M2 5' splice site requires its activation by the cellular SF2/ASF protein. This activation is mediated by the binding of the SF2/ASF protein to a purine-rich splicing enhancer sequence that is located in the 3' exon of Ml mRNA. We demonstrate that activation of the M2 5' splice site is controlled by the SF2/ASF protein in vivo during influenza virus infection. Utilizing four cell lines that differ in their levels of production of the SF2/ASF protein, we show that during virus infection of these cell lines both M2 mRNA and the M2 ion channel protein are produced in amounts that are proportional to the different expression levels of the SF2/ASF protein. Keywords: alternative splicing/influenza virus/M2 ion channel protein/SF2 (ASF) splicing factor/splicing enhancer sequence

Introduction

Viruses are obligate parasites that need to utilize host cell functions for their replication. The identity ofthese cellular functions depends largely, though not totally, on whether the viral genome is DNA or RNA. Among animal viruses with RNA genomes, influenza virus, which contains a negative-stranded genome, has the most extensive depend- ence on host cell functions in the nucleus. This virus is unique in that it cannibalizes cellular capped RNAs in the nucleus to produce the primers needed for the initiation of viral mRNA synthesis (Krug et al., 1989). Specifically, a viral endonuclease cleaves the host nuclear, capped RNAs to generate capped fragments 10-13 nucleotides in length that serve as primers for viral mRNA synthesis. Because this process, termed 'cap-snatching', occurs in the nucleus, where the pool of cellular capped RNAs is continually being depleted by export to the cytoplasm and possibly by degradation, the virus requires the continuous functioning of the cellular RNA polymerase II to provide an adequate pool of cellular capped RNAs. In addition, influenza virus, like human immuno- deficiency virus 1 (HIV-1), encodes a protein that most likely interacts with one or more components of the cellular system for the nuclear export of mRNAs. The influenza virus NS 1 protein (non-structural protein 1) inhibits the nuclear export of poly(A)-containing mRNAs (Alonso-Caplen et al., 1992; Fortes et al., 1994; Qiu and Krug, 1994), whereas the HIV-1 Rev protein facilitates the nuclear export of unspliced and partially spliced viral mRNAs (Felber et al., 1989; Hadzopoulou-Cladaras et al., 1989; Malim et al., 1989a). Both proteins contain two important functional domains: an RNA-binding domain and an effector domain that is presumed to interact with one or more host cell nuclear proteins (Malim et al., 1989b; Qian et al., 1994). Recently the effector domain of the HIV-1 Rev protein has been shown to interact with a cellular nucleoporin-like protein (Bogerd et al., 1995; Fritz et al., 1995; Stutz et al., 1995). The cellular nuclear protein that interacts specifically with the effector domain of the influenza virus NS 1 protein has not yet been identified. Influenza virus also exploits the host nuclear splicing machinery to splice two of its mRNAs (Krug et al., 1989; Nemeroff et al., 1992; Shih et al., 1995). The NSL and Ml (matrix protein) mRNAs are spliced to form mRNAs coding for two other proteins, NS2 (non-structural protein 2) and M2 (the viral ion channel protein), respectively. This splicing is undoubtedly carried out by host cell nuclear enzymes. As is the case for the splicing of retrovirus pre-mRNAs (O'Reilly et al., 1995 and refer- ences therein), only a portion of the influenza viral precursor NS 1 and Ml mRNAs is spliced, and both the unspliced and spliced mRNAs are exported to the cytoplasm (Krug et al., 1989). Influenza viral MI mRNA also undergoes alternative splicing (Lamb and Lai, 1982; Krug et al., 1989; Shih et al., 1995), a process that, as shown here, involves an additional exploitation of a host cell nuclear function. Ml mRNA contains two 5' splice sites. The distal 5' splice site fits the consensus 5' splice site sequence more closely than the proximal 5' splice site, and presumably as a consequence only the distal but not the proximal 5' splice site is used when the M 1 gene is transfected into uninfected cells (Lamb and Lai, 1982; Shih et al., 1995). The use of the distal 5' splice site in uninfected cells generates mRNA3, which has a reading frame for only a nine amino acid peptide. In contrast, use of the proximal 5' splice site, which occurs only in influenza virus-infected cells,

In infected cells the complex of the three viral polymerase proteins, the same complex that transcribes and replicates the viral genome (Krug et al., 1989), binds to the 5' end of Ml mRNA in a sequence- specific and cap-dependent fashion, thereby blocking the distal, or mRNA3, 5' splice site (Shih et al., 1995). The cellular splicing machinery then switches to the proximal M2 5' splice site. Hence the production of M2 mRNA is delayed until sufficient amounts of the polymerase com- plex are synthesized, so that the time course of production ofthe viral polymerase complex controls the time at which the M2 5' splice site can be utilized in infected cells. The role of the influenza viral polymerase complex can be compared with that of the Drosophila melanogaster sex lethal (Sxl) protein in controlling the choice of a 3' splice site in tra pre-mRNA. By blocking the constitutive 3' splice site, the Sxl protein causes the splicing machinery to switch to an alternative 3' splice site, resulting in a spliced mRNA encoding the tra protein (Sosnowski et al., 1989; Inoue, 1990; Valcarcel et al., 1993). This report demonstrates that the alternative splicing of influenza viral Ml RNA has an additional, surprising feature: after the mRNA3 5' splice site is blocked by the viral polymerase complex, the cellular nuclear SF2/ASF splicing factor is exploited to control the usage of the M2 5' splice site in Ml mRNA and hence the production of the M2 ion channel protein. The Ml mRNA molecules that are not spliced are exported to the cytoplasm to direct the synthesis of the Ml matrix protein. SF2/ASF is an SR protein, a member of a family of proteins which contain a domain rich in serine and arginine and which function in both alternative and constitutive splicing (Ge et al., 1991; Krainer et al., 1991; Zahler et al., 1992; Birney et al., 1993; Fu, 1995). Cellular extracts that lack SR proteins (S100 extracts) are inactive in pre-mRNA splicing and in fact are incapable offorming any detectable specific splicing complexes (Krainer et al., 1990a; Zahler et al., 1993). The addition of individual SR proteins restores splicing activity to these extracts, indicating that SR proteins are essential splicing factors (Krainer et al., 1990a; Zahler et al., 1992; Fu, 1993). These observations also suggest that the functions of the various SR factors are at least partially redundant. How- ever, evidence has been obtained that individual SR proteins do display some specificity for different pre- mRNAs (Fu, 1993; Zahler et al., 1993). In addition, several SR proteins, e.g. SF2/ASF, affect 5' splice site selection in a concentration-dependent manner (Ge and Manley, 1990; Krainer et al., 1990a,b). Thus, as the concentration of SF2/ASF is increased, the use of the proximal rather than the distal 5' splice site in an SV40 pre-mRNA is favored (Ge and Manley, 1990). Several SR proteins have been shown to bind to purine-rich splicing enhancer elements in the pre-mRNA, and this interaction most likely mediates many of the activities of the SR proteins in splicing (Lavigueur et al., 1993; Sun et al., 1993a,b; Staknis and Reed, 1994; Dirksen et al., 1995; Tacke and Manley, 1995). We demonstrate in this report that the 3' exon of the influenza viral MI mRNA contains a purine-rich splicing enhancer sequence thatbinds the cellularSF2/ASF splicing factor, and we prove that this binding is responsible for controlling the production of M2 mRNA by splicing and hence the production of the M2 ion channel protein. As a consequence, influenza virus, a negative-strand RNA virus, has ceded the control of the level of expression of one of its gene products, the M2 ion channel protein, to a cellular nuclear protein, the SF2/ASF splicing factor. For this purpose the virus has evolved a sequence in the exon of one of its pre-mRNAs, MI mRNA, that enables this RNA to interact with SF2/ASF.

Conditions affording efficient utilization of the weaker M2 5' splice site in influenza virus Ml mRNA In influenza viral Ml mRNA the mRNA3 and M2 mRNA 5' splice sites are located 11 and 51 nucleotides down- stream, respectively, from the beginning of the viral- encoded sequence (Figure IA). Like all the viral mRNAs, MI mRNA contains 10-13 host-coded nucleotides at its 5' end as a result of capped RNA-primed initiation of viral RNA transcription (Krug et al., 1989), so that the mRNA3 and M2 mRNA 5' splice sites are actually 21- 24 and 61-64 nucleotides downstream from the 5' end of the MI mRNA molecule, respectively. Where indicated below, the mRNA3 or the M2 5' splice site was inactivated by mutating the/GU sequence at the splicejunction to/CU. Only the mRNA3 5' splice site is utilized in uninfected cell nuclear extracts and in uninfected cells transfected with Ml DNA (Shih et al., 1995). Thus, an Ml mRNA construct (lacking both part of the intron and part of the 3' exon) is spliced in vitro, though inefficiently, to produce mRNA3, but not M2 mRNA (Figure iB, lane 1). In contrast, in infected cells the viral polymerase complex blocks the mRNA3 5' splice site, and as a consequence the M2 5' splice site is used (Shih et al., 1995). This switch in 5' splice site usage occurs in vitro when the viral polymerase complex is added to splicing reactions catalyzed by uninfected cell nuclear extracts. The same switch occurs when the mRNA3 5' splice site is blocked by mutation, rather than by the viral polymerase complex (compare lanes 1 and 2), as also shown previously (Shih et al., 1995). Regardless of the method of blocking the mRNA3 5' splice site, the amount ofM2 mRNA produced was much greater (5- to 15-fold in various experiments) than the amount of mRNA3 produced during the splicing of the wild-type Ml mRNA construct in the absence of the viral polymerase complex (Shih et al., 1995). It was conceivable that the M2 5' splice site was used more efficiently when the mRNA3 5' splice site was inactivated simply because the M2 5' splice site was the only 5' splice site present in this MI mRNA molecule. However, this was not the case: when the M2, rather than the mRNA3, 5' splice site was inactivated by mutation, the efficiency of using the remaining mRNA3 5' splice site was not significantly improved (compare lanes 1 and 3). Based on its closer fit with the consensus 5' splice site sequence, it has been presumed that the mRNA3 5' splice site is a stronger 5' splice site than the M2 5' splice site. In light of the above results, it was important to determine whether this presumption is correct. To this end, competi- tion experiments between the two 5' splice sites were carried out (Figure IC). Our results indicate that influenza virus most likely utilizes the cellular SF2/ASF splicing factor in infected cells to control the level of production of the viral M2 mRNA and hence of the viral M2 ion channel protein. For our experiments we took advantage ofthe observation that SR proteins are expressed at different levels in different cell types (Zahler et al., 1993). We identified four established cell lines that differed in their levels of SF2/ASF expression. For example, 293 cells expressed the greatest amount of SF2/ASF, in confirmation of Ge and Manley (1990), whereas CV- 1 cells expressed the least amount. We showed that after influenza virus infection of these four cell lines, the amounts of both M2 mRNA and the M2 ion channel protein were proportional to the expression level of SF2/ASF. This explains previous observations that the amount of the M2 protein varied in different cell lines infected by the same influenza virus strain (Inglis and Brown, 1984; Lau and Scholtissek, 1995). Thus, the cellular SF2/ASF splicing factor controls the production of the viral M2 ion channel protein in infected cells by activating the M2 5' splice site in viral M I mRNA. Therefore, the number of MI mRNA molecules that acquire an activated M2 5' splice site is determined by the number of SF2/ASF molecules that are present in the cell. It should be noted that in all four cell lines the majority of the Ml mRNA molecules were not spliced, but instead were transported to the cytoplasm to direct the synthesis of the Ml matrix protein. Our results demonstrate that, as hypothesized (Zahler et al., 1993), the intracellular level of an SR protein can regulate gene expression, in this case the expression ofa gene introduced by a virus. It should, however, be pointed out that our experiments do not rule out the possibility that other cellular splicing factors in addition to SF2/ASF participate in the regulation of the amount of the viral M2 protein produced in virus-infected cells. First, what are the consequences ofdifferent levels ofexpression of the viral M2 ion channel protein? This protein has been shown to function during the uncoating of the virus: it causes a decrease in the pH of the infecting virus particles, thereby dissociating viral nucleocapsids from viral MI protein molecules (Pinto et al., 1992). Although only a small number of M2 protein molecules are incorporated into virus particles (Zebedee and Lamb, 1988), the M2 protein is expressed at relatively high levels in the plasma membrane of infected cells (Lamb et al., 1985). One possibility is that the M2 protein has a function in virus assembly and budding without being incorporated into progeny virus particles (Zebedee and Lamb, 1988; Hughey et al., 1995), e.g. an interaction between the M2 protein and the Ml matrix protein has been inferred from genetic evidence (Zebedee and Lamb, 1988). In addition, it is conceivable that the ion channel activity ofthe M2 proteins which are associated with the plasma membrane plays a role in the cytopathic effect of the virus. These functions of the M2 protein would be expected to be regulated largely by controlling the amount of the M2 protein that

is produced. A second, related question stems from the implications of our results concerning the relative roles ofthe virus and the cell incontrolling viral gene expression. We have shown that influenza virus has ceded the control of the level of expression of the viral M2 ion channel protein to a cellular gene product, SF2/ASF, which is expressed at different levels in different cells. What advantage does the virus gain from this transfer of the control of viral gene expression to this particular, variably expressed cellular gene product? Surely, if there were no advantage to influenza virus, this mechanism would not have evolved. It is clear that we need to know more about the functions of the viral M2 ion channel protein during infection before we can answer this question. However, we can speculate that the virus has evolved this mechanism in order to achieve a successful infectious cycle in its vertebrate hosts. This mechanism may for example be important for tissue tropism and/or pathogenesis. Our mutational analysis directly identified the splicing enhancer sequence in the 3' exon of influenza viral MI mRNA, i.e. the functional binding site for SF2/ASE Changing the purines to pyrimidines in the GAAACGA 3' exon sequence (nucleotides 749-755, see Figure 6) reduced binding of SF2/ASF by 75%. All detectable binding was eliminated by additional purine to pyrimidine mutations in an adjacent purine-rich region, the GGGGUG sequence at nucleotides 759-764. These mutations also eliminated the SF2/ASF-dependent enhancement of the utilization of the M2 5' splice site. This specific binding occurs in the presence of SF2/ASF alone, indicating that SF2/ASF does not require accessory factors to bind to this defined region of the Ml mRNA 3' exon. These results establish that the purine-rich (81% purine) sequence extending from nucleotide 749 to 764 constitutes the functional binding site for SF2/ASF. Though previous studies have shown that SF2/ASF binds to a purine-rich sequence in the exon of other pre-mRNAs (Sun et al., 1993a,b; Dirksen et al., 1994; Staknis and Reed, 1994), these studies did not identify the exact binding site in these pre-mRNAs. In vitro experiments designed to identify the high affinity binding sites of SF2/ASF yielded the purine- rich consensus sequence RGAAGAAC (where R is a purine) (Tacke and Manley, 1995). This sequence has four mismatches with the GAAACGA sequence that constitutes the major part of the SF2/ASF binding site in the 3' exon of MI mRNA, and essentially no match with the GGGGUG sequence which is also part of this binding

site. Consequently, the SF2/ASF protein probably binds to more than one purine-rich sequence in pre-mRNAs and/or binding may require a particular RNA structure as well as a purine-rich sequence. It is likely that the splicing enhancer sequence in the 3' exon ofM 1 mRNA is specific for SF2/ASF, because two other SR proteins, SRp4O and SRp55, did not activate the M2 5' splice site (our unpublished results). SR splicing factors have been implicated in the regula- tion of alternative splicing in various biological systems (reviewed by Fu, 1995). Several studies have reported that SR proteins are involved in the alternative splicing of pre-mRNAs encoded by viruses. In fact, the first study that demonstrated that SF2/ASF was responsible for the choice of alternative splice sites was carried out with a viral pre-mRNA, the early pre-mRNA of the DNA tumor virus SV40 (Ge and Manley, 1990). SF2/ASF causes the switch from the distal large T 5' splice site to the proximal small t 5' splice site of this pre-mRNA in a concentration- dependent fashion. Subsequently it was shown that the RNA-binding domain of SF2/ASF binds to the purine- rich small t 5' splice site, suggesting that in this case SF2/ ASF interacts directly with the 5' splice site (Zuo and Manley, 1994). Binding of SF2/ASF to a purine-rich 5' splice site was also observed with another viral pre- mRNA, adenovirus EIA pre-mRNA (Zuo and Manley, 1994), and several SR proteins have been implicated in the regulation of the alternative splicing of this viral pre- mRNA in infected cells (Himmelspach et al., 1995). In addition, SF2/ASF likely has a role in the splicing of the

tat pre-mRNA of HIV-1, because the splicing of this viral pre-mRNA by nuclear extracts has been shown to depend on the addition of SF2/ASF (Krainer, 1990b; Zahler et al., 1993). It has been postulated that SF2/ASF interacts with a purine-rich sequence in the 3' exon of tat pre-mRNA, but this has not yet been established. Thus, to our knowledge, the present study constitutes the first identi- fication of a functional splicing enhancer in the exon of a pre-mRNA encoded by an animal virus, notably a virus with an RNA genome. The mechanism of action of SR proteins in splicing has not yet been delineated. Several studies have shown that SR proteins function at early stages of splicing in the absence of ATP, and that these proteins are involved in multiple interactions with components of the splicing machinery (Wu and Maniatis, 1993; Amrein et al., 1994; Kohtz et al., 1994; Staknis and Reed, 1994; Zahler and Roth, 1995). Most pertinent to the present study is the observation that SF2/ASF can interact simultaneously with the U170K protein, which is at the 5' splice site, and the U2AF35 protein subunit, which is at the 3' splice site (Wu and Maniatis, 1993). This suggests that one of the ways that SF2/ASF can function is by bridging 5' and 3' splice sites during the initial stages of spliceosome assem-

bly. Our working hypothesis is that this is in fact the way

Materials and methods

In vitro splicing assays The Ml mRNAs used for the splicing assays contained a large deletion ofthe intron and only 74 nucleotides of the 3' exon, and were synthesized as described previously (Shih et al., 1995). These MI mRNAs also contained an additional 13 nucleotides (derived from the plasmid) 5' to the virus-encoded Ml sequences to mimic the MI mRNA that is synthesized in infected cells. Splicing reactions were carried out for 2 h

at 30°C as described previously (Nemeroff et al., 1992). The final volume of the reaction was 20 p1, containing 6 pl of a HeLa cell nuclear extract (20 mg/ml of nuclear protein). After incubation RNA was extracted and analyzed by electrophoresis on 7% polyacrylamide-8 M urea gels.

Purification of SF2/ASF SF2/ASF purified from HeLa cell nuclear extracts was provided by Adrian Krainer (Krainer, 1990a,b). For the purification of recombinant SF2/ASF, the pDS6H-ASF plasmid (provided by James Manley, Ge

et al., 1991) was transformed into Escherichia. coli strain JM101, and the synthesis of the SF2/ASF protein containing six amino-terminal histidines was induced with 1 mM isopropyl 3-D-thiogalactoside. The SF2/ASF protein was purified to homogeneity as described by Ge

et al. (1991).

UV crosslinking assays The DNAs corresponding to nucleotides 740-814, 740-780 or 781-814 of MI mRNA were inserted into the HindIllI and BacmHI sites ofpGEM 1, and after linearization by BainiHI the inserted sequences were transcribed using SP6 polymerase in the presence of [Vz-32P]UTP. For the preparation of labeled RNA corresponding to the M 1 mRNA sequence extending from nucleotides 630-738, the DNA corresponding to nucleotides 630- 1027 of MI mRNA was inserted into the EcoRI and HindIll sites of pGEM1, and after linearization with Stlil the 630-738 sequence was transcribed using T7 polymerase. The labeled RNAs were incubated, in a final volume of 20 p1, with either HeLa cell nuclear extract (6 p1 of a 20 mg/ml preparation), HeLa cell S10 extract (6 pl of a 20 mg/ml preparation), or purified SF2/ASF (0.45 pg) for 30 min at 30°C in splicing buffer. The mixtures were irradiated with UV light (305 nm) for 7 min at 4°C at a distance of 5 cm, followed by digestion with RNase A (1 mg/ml final concentration) for 10 min at 37C. Where indicated, samples were immunoprecipitated with an anti-SF2/ASF monoclonal antibody provided by Adrian Krainer (Sun et al., 1993a). Labeled proteins were resolved by electrophoresis on 14% SDS- polyacrylamide gels for 14 h at 70 V.

Quantitation of the amount of Ml and M2 mRNAs synthesized in influenza virus-infected cells Four different cell lines, 293, Cos- 1, CV-1 and Hela, were infected with 50 p.f.u. of influenza virus.(Udorn strain) for 5 h (Shih et al., 1995). Equal aliquots of the total RNA from the infected cells were subjected

to quantitative RT-PCR as described by Noonan et al., (1990) and Salomon et al. (1992). Briefly, these RNA samples were reverse transcribed for 1 h at 37°C in a 20 R1 reaction containg 100 units of Moloney Murine leukemia virus reverse transcriptase, a deoxynucleotide mixture (10 mM dTTP, dCTP and dGTP; 0.5 mM dATP) containing [oL-32P]dATP, and an oligonucleotide primer complementary either to nucleotides 709-690 of Ml mRNA or to nucleotides 840-812 of M2 mRNA. Control experiments established that these were the optimum conditions for reverse transcription. For PCR expansion, I unit of Taq DNA polymerase and an oligonucleotide primer complementary either

to nucleotides 408-428 of Ml mRNA or to nucleotides 31-51 of M2 mRNA were added to the cDNA present in the 20 p1 reverse transcriptase reaction. This mixture (final volume of 100 p1) was subjected to 25 cycles of amplification. At this stage of the amplification each cycle was shown to yield an exponential increase in product. As controls for setting up an internal standard curve, known amounts of in vitro synthesized MI and M2 mRNAs were subjected to the same amplification protocol. PCR products were resolved by electrophoresis on 2.5% agarose gels. The cDNA bands were excised from the gel, and the amount of radioactivity was determined.

Other methods The preparation of the influenza viral polymerase complex and the procedure for iinmunoblots (Western blots) have been described pre- viously (Shih et al., 1995).
