# With the global population predicted to grow by at least 25% by 2050,

the need for sustainable production of nutritious foods is critical for human and environmental well-being. Recent advances show that

specialized plant transporters can be utilized to enhance yields of staple crops, increase nutritious content and might expand available arable

# land.

Of the present global population of seven billion people, almost one billion are undernourished and lack sufficient protein, fats and carbohydrates in their diets 1 . An additional billion people are malnourished because their diets lack required micronutrients such as iron, zinc and vitamin A (ref. 2 ). These dietary deficiencies have an enormous negative impact on global health resulting in increased susceptibility to infection and diseases, as well as increasing the risk of significant mental impairment 3 . During the next four decades, an expected additional two billion humans will require nutritious food. Along with growing urbanisation, increased demand for protein in developing countries coupled with impending climate change, population growth will impose further pressures on agricultural production 4 , with the result that global demand for food will increase by 50% by 2030 1 . Innovative solutions are required to increase production on the land currently used for agriculture, as we are already close to the sustainable limit of 15% of the Earth’s surface that can be exploited for crop production 5 .

Analysis of crop yields globally shows that in those developing regions most susceptible to human malnutrition, the availabilities of inorganic nutrients and water are the principal factors that determine productivity 6,7 . Simply increasing inorganic fertilizer use and water supply or applying organic farming systems to agriculture 8 will be unable to satisfy the joint requirements of increased yield and environmental sustainability. Increasing food production on limited land resources will rely on innovative agronomic practices coupled to the genetic improvement of crops 9 .

Transport proteins embedded within membranes are key targets for improving the efficiency with which plants take up and use water and nutrients. These proteins not only transport mineral nutrients and control drought tolerance but are also essential for moving sucrose, the energy currency of plants, to where it is needed. Furthermore, transporters are also central to mechanisms that allow plants to tolerate adverse environments such as saline or acid soils.

Advances driven by genetics, physiology and biophysics over the last 20 years have dramatically advanced our understanding of the molecular basis of plant nutrition and how plants respond to stress. Genome sequencing and the development of experimental systems for studying transporter function have allowed many of the major families of membrane transporters to be characterized. Next generation sequencing is leading to an understanding of how natural genetic diversity of plant membrane transporters can be exploited for agriculture, whether by marker-assisted breeding or through genetic engineering. Breakthrough approaches involving TAL effectors and genome editing 10 can provide non-GM but molecularly-directed rapid solutions to crop improvement.

In this article we report on findings demonstrating that understanding of the biology of plant transporters can be a key contributor to the goal of global food security. We discuss examples where fundamental research is already being translated into practical applications such as enhancing the micronutrient content of grain and improving plant tolerance of saline and acidic soils. We further discuss potential applications linked to breakthroughs in basic research that are yet to be applied to crop plants. This Perspective reviews the extent to which the rapid advances in plant transport research address global aspects of food security, and how we can potentially reduce the time between trait identification in the laboratory and exploitation in the field.

## TRANSPORTERS THAT MEDIATE STRESS RESISTANCE AND ENHANCE YIELDS

## Aluminum Tolerant Crops for Acid Soils

Acid soils comprise 30% of the earth's ice-free land and pose a serious constraint to agricultural production, as only a small proportion of these soils is suitable for crops 11 . Soil acidity liberates normally innocuous aluminium as toxic Al 3+ ions. Once in the soil solution, Al 3+ damages the root tips of susceptible plants and inhibits root growth which impairs the uptake of water and nutrients. Natural genetic variation in Al 3+ tolerance exists within major cereal crops. Efflux of organic anions from roots was discovered as a naturally occurring tolerance mechanism of several species 12 . Transport proteins are central to this mechanism with members of two families of proteins responsible for exporting the organic anions from inside root cells to the external medium surrounding roots. The organic anions secreted by roots chelate Al 3+ into a non-toxic form, thus protecting the sensitive tips and allowing the roots to grow unimpeded (Fig. 1).

In wheat, the TaALMT1 gene encodes an Al 3+ -gated anion channel that facilitates malate efflux from roots 13 (Fig. 1). Molecular markers based on the TaALMT1 gene can be implemented in marker-assisted breeding to select for Al 3+ tolerance in wheat germplasm. Similarly, a unique sub-group of the large family of plant MATE transporters mediates citrate efflux from root cells . While genes encoding organic anion transporters are already established as a means of enhancing the Al 3+ tolerance of crops, other recently discovered mechanisms provide additional options. For instance, rice is the most Al 3+ tolerant of the cereal crops and employs mechanisms distinct from the efflux of organic anions. One of these mechanisms involves transporters working in concert ultimately to sequester Al 3+ into the vacuole, thus

removing Al 3+ from mainstream metabolism 19,20 . Genetic analysis has identified susceptible and tolerant variants of one of the transporters (Nrat1) that explains a large proportion of the variation in Al 3+ tolerance within the rice aus subpopulation. This finding provides a promising tool for marker-assisted breeding 21 .

The discoveries of transporters involved in Al 3+ tolerance has identified two principal strategies that plants use to deal with this toxic cation. In one strategy, Al 3+ is excluded from cells by chelating the toxic ion external to plants and in the other Al 3+ is sequestered within cells in the vacuole. The genes encoding these transporters can be used to develop Al 3+ - tolerant crops and represent an important component, along with management practices such as liming, for improving yields on acid soils.

## HKT Transporters Improve Salt Tolerance

Approximately 7% of the world's land including agricultural lands is affected by either salinity or sodium toxicity (Fig. 2a). Production in over 30 % of irrigated crops and 7 % of dryland agriculture worldwide is limited by salinity stress (Fig. 2a). Crop irrigation is increasing soil salinity, due to trace amounts of salt in irrigation waters. Plant plasma membrane transporters in the HKT family transport Na + and K + 22 and play an essential role in salt tolerance 23 . Research in the reference plant Arabidopsis showed that the “Class 1” HKT transporters (HKT1s) are Na + selective and protect plant leaves from salinity stress by prohibiting toxic sodium over-accumulation in leaves 23 . Class 1 HKT transporters are expressed in veins 23 that connect nutrient flux between roots and leaves. These transporters are expressed in the living cells surrounding the xylem, which are vessels that carry nutrients and water to the leaves. Class 1 HKT transporter remove excess Na + from the xylem in Arabidopsis and rice, thereby keeping Na + below toxic levels in the photosynthetic leaf tissues 24-26 (Fig. 2b). Analogous mechanisms have been demonstrated in wheat for the HKT1;4 and HKT1;5 genes 27,28 (Fig. 2b). Remarkably, the recent introgression of an ancestral form of the HKT1;5 gene from the more Na + tolerant wheat relative Triticum monococcum into susceptible commercial durum wheat (Triticum turgidum ssp durum) increased grain yields on saline soil by 25% in the field, illustrating the immense potential of this mechanism 28 .

Some crops are salt tolerant through effective sequestration of Na + in leaf vacuoles by Na + /H + antiporters 29 . Specific class 2 HKT transporters (HKT2s) 30 mediate cation influx into roots 31 . These HKT2 transporters, together with transporters that sequester sodium and potassium into the vacuole 32,33 have the potential to improve the production of cereals such as barley, a species that copes with high Na + loads in leaves by compartmentation in the vacuole 34 . Thus combining (pyramiding) HKT transporter traits with vacuolar Na +

sequestration mechanisms provides a potentially powerful platform for molecular breeding and transgenic approaches to improve the salinity tolerance of crops.

## “SWEET” Sugar Transporters and Pathogen Resistance

Photosynthesis in leaves produces sugars, which are distributed through the veins to support growth of roots, meristems and seeds. The translocation rates and relative distribution critically determine yield potential of crops. Sucrose is a key energy carrying

molecule that also acts as the driver for translocation of all other nutrients and signaling molecules in veins, but until recently, the molecular players were unknown. In the early 1990s the sucrose-proton co-transporter SUT1 was identified as the key transporter that 35 loads sugar into veins , yet the mechanism for sucrose release from leaf cells that synthesize it remained elusive. Recently, “SWEET” sugar transporters were identified with 36,37 the help of sugar sensors based on Förster Resonance Energy Transfer technology . Known crop genomes possess ~20 SWEET genes. SWEETs are plasma membrane proteins located in the phloem parenchyma, a cell type inside the veins that exports sucrose to the SUT1 sugar loaders (Fig. 3). The import and export of sucrose from vein cells is 38 controlled by hormones, turgor feedback and sugar levels . Knowledge of this machinery now provides a new starting point to engineer yield by modifying energy and carbon distribution within the plant.

Notably, SWEET sugar efflux transporters have been identified as pathogen resistance loci, 39-42 leading to a new paradigm in the understanding of disease development in plants . Growth of pathogens in leaves and stems depends on nutrient supply from their plant hosts. Blight bacteria directly induce SWEET gene expression in rice in infected cells through TAL effectors (bacterial transcription factors that directly target SWEET promoters). Inhibiting induction of SWEET genes with an innovative technology such as chromosomal editing of the promoters of SWEETs with TALENs (artificial TAL effector-nucleases) or through cell- specific expression of micro RNAs in cells outside the phloem has now enabled blight 43,44 resistance to be engineered in rice . The discovery of these key players in combination with TALENs promises new ways of engineering both crop yield and pathogen resistance, without introduction of foreign genetic material, to produce plants with significantly improved performance in the field.

## IMPROVING HUMAN HEALTH THROUGH IMPROVED PLANT NUTRITION

## Pumping Iron and Zinc

Over 2 billion people suffer from iron and zinc deficiencies because their plant-based diets are not a sufficiently rich source of these essential elements. Developing crop cultivars with increased micronutrient concentrations, an approach known as biofortification, is challenging as metal ion concentrations in various tissues and compartments are maintained within narrow physiological limits by coordinated uptake, translocation and storage. Furthermore, for crops like rice, removal of the outer layers of the grain during polishing essentially removes all of the micronutrients, leaving only the starchy endosperm. By expressing key genes involved in the mobilization of micronutrients from the soil to the seed, scientists have biofortified rice, a staple food consumed by half the world population every day (Fig. 4). Enhancing iron translocation through overproduction of the metal chelator nicotianamine 45-47 (NA) and phytosiderophores or enhancing iron flux into the endosperm by means of the 48 iron-NA transporter OsYSL2 , has resulted in greenhouse-grown rice with 3-4 fold higher levels of iron (Fe) in polished grain. Combining overproduction of NA with enhanced 49 expression of the iron storage protein ferritin increased the iron content more than 6-fold , and combining all three approaches has resulted in paddy field-grown polished rice with 4.4-

fold higher Fe concentrations than those found in non-transgenic seeds, with no yield penalty 50 . Although these results are impressive and bring iron levels close to those recommended by nutritionists, only a handful of studies have tested whether these enhanced levels of nutrients are bioavailable. With respect to this, it is encouraging that, enhancing NA levels did increase the levels of bioavailable iron 46 and zinc 51 in polished rice.

Vacuolar sequestration is another mechanism to enhance the concentrations of iron and zinc (Zn) in seeds 52 . Transporters belonging to several different families transport metals between the cytoplasm and the vacuole 53-55 , including the Arabidopsis VIT1 protein that is highly expressed in developing seeds and transports Fe and manganese into the vacuole 54 . Disruption of the rice VIT orthologues (OsVIT1 and OsVIT2) leads to increased Fe/Zn accumulation in rice seeds and a corresponding decrease in the source organ flag leaves, probably because VIT genes are highly expressed in rice flag leaves. Without a sink, there is enhanced Fe/Zn translocation to the seed, providing another strategy to biofortify Fe/Zn in staple foods 56 . MTP1 also transports divalent cations into the vacuole and is another promising candidate for use in biofortification 57 . Thus several strategies are resulting in enhancement of iron and zinc micronutrients in edible plant tissues, a crucial goal in light of the growing world population and the central roles of staple crops in human nutrition.

## Essential Plant Nutrients, Phosphate and Nitrogen

Phosphorus (P) is a macro-element essential for plant growth and is of vital importance to crop yield. The availability of orthophosphate (Pi), the only form of P directly accessible to plants, is influenced by the biogeochemical properties of the soil and limits crop production on nearly 70% of the world’s agricultural soils 58 . Consequently, global crop production depends on Pi fertilizers, which are produced from rock phosphate, a finite, non-renewable mineral resource (Fig. 5a). Only 20%-30% of the P fertilizer applied is utilized by cultivated plants and at the current rate of use, it is estimated that rock phosphate reserves will be consumed within the next 70-200 years 59 , therefore improvements to ensure sustainable use of Pi are of paramount importance for human nutrition. Strategies to enhance Pi acquisition and Pi use-efficiency in plants are multifaceted and modifications to root growth 60 , root architecture 61 and novel genetic engineering strategies to use alternative P sources 59 are among recently identified approaches to improve Pi use-efficiency. Understanding of plant transporter proteins that function to acquire Pi from the soil and to distribute it throughout the plant is an essential component of such strategies. Plants possess several families of Pi transporter proteins, and both high- and low- affinity transporters are important for Pi uptake into roots 62,63 . Pi transporters are also critical for Pi distribution throughout the plant, and for remobilization between source and sink tissues 64,65 . PHO1, an efflux transporter essential for Pi transfer to the shoot, is a major player in the regulation of Pi homeostasis 66 and might provide strategies to optimize Pi distribution within plants.

In addition to direct acquisition of Pi from soil, most crop species have the capacity to form symbiotic associations (called arbuscular mycorrhizae) with soil fungi. These fungi capture Pi through extensive hyphal networks and deliver it to symbiotic compartments in the root where plant Pi transporters transfer this ion into the root cells. Plant symbiotic Pi transporter function is essential for this process and is also required to maintain the symbiosis .

Phosphate transporters are important targets for breeding plants with improved Pi acquisition and use-efficiency, and that benefit maximally from their fungal symbionts.

Application of nitrogen fertilizers has greatly increased crop yields and alleviated hunger over the past five decades. However, N fertilizer production consumes 1% of global energy usage and poses the highest input cost for many crops. Nevertheless, only 30% to 50% of the N fertilizer applied is utilized by plants. The remainder can lead to production of the greenhouse gas N2O, or to eutrophication of aquatic ecosystems through water run-off. 68 Therefore, enhancing crop nitrogen utilization efficiency (NUE) is an important goal . For most crops, nitrate is the primary nitrogen source and enhancing nitrate uptake is one strategy for improving NUE.

Multiple nitrate uptake transporters, in the NRT1 and NRT2 families, work together to enable 69,70 nitrogen uptake in plants . Most NRT1 transporters are low-affinity nitrate transporters, meaning they function mainly when nitrate is abundantly available. In contrast, CHL1 71,72 (NRT1.1) is a dual-affinity nitrate transporter involved in nitrate acquisition . A recent study found that CHL1 also functions as a nitrate sensor to regulate the nitrate-induced 73 transcriptional response , implying that plants use this transporter to monitor changes in external nitrate concentration to trigger proper metabolic acclimation. CHl1 therefore has become the paradigm for how nutrient transporters may also serve as nutrient sensors, and how optimization of transport and signaling can be used simultaneously to improve nutrient efficiency.

Using dual-affinity binding, with the help of two protein kinases CIPK23 and CIPK8, CHL1 senses a wide range of nitrate concentration changes in the soil to alter its own transport 73,74 properties and induce different levels of transcriptional response (Fig. 5b) . In addition, 75,76 CHL1 and NRT2.1 are also important for nitrate-regulated root development . Vigorous 60,77 root development is important for plants to compete for nutrients and sustain crop yield . Therefore, nitrate transporters and other proteins that regulate nitrate uptake and sensing provide new potential tools for engineering crops with tailored N uptake activity, N metabolism and improved root growth for enhanced NUE and reduced N fertilizer requirements.

## FUTURE OUTLOOK

Our knowledge of the molecular nature and regulation of transporters has expanded vastly over the last twenty years. As shown in the examples here, fundamental research into transport mechanisms in plants is leading to rapid innovations for improving yields, extending the range of arable land that can be used for crops and improving the performance of plants under stress. This research is also pointing to new solutions for more sustainable use of limited soil nutrients and for enhanced human nutrition through biofortification.

Recent advances on other plant transporters add to the list of potentially innovative applications in agriculture. For example, plants in the Brassica family, which includes oilseed

rape (Canola) and mustard, produce glucosinolates, which are potent defense compounds against herbivores and plant pathogens. A transporter controls the distribution of glucosinolates in Arabidopsis 78 and could be engineered to enhance herbivore resistance, as a way of reducing the application of pesticides. In another example, toxic heavy metal and arsenic accumulation in edible plant tissues, including rice in the United States, poses a threat to human health 79 . Plant transporters have been recently identified that control toxic metal and arsenic accumulation in seeds and other tissues 80,81 , pointing directly to potential applications for developing plants with reduced levels of toxicant accumulation in grain and other edible tissues. Rice with nearly cadmium-free grain has been produced by identifying the transporter responsible for cadmium uptake and using a molecular marker to select for genotypes that accumulate low cadmium concentrations 82 .

Drought tolerance and desiccation avoidance of plants is critical for conserving water during drought periods. Plants lose over 90% of their water by transpiration from stomatal pores in the epidermis of leaves. Stomatal pores are also the gateways for CO2 intake into plants for photosynthesis and play a key role in determining the water use efficiency of plants. Research has shown that modification in the expression level of ion channels in guard cells that control the opening and closing of stomatal pores can be used to reduce water loss, enhance water use efficiency and regulate efficient CO2 intake for photosynthesis 83-85 . Moreover, transporters for the plant abiotic stress resistance hormone, abscisic acid (ABA), have been identified 86,87 , and these may be used to target drought resistance responses. Targeting drought tolerance to particularly drought sensitive tissues or organs during particularly susceptible stages of reproductive development (e.g. grain filling and pollen meiosis) could become an important strategy in light of prolonged droughts and other predicted consequences of climate change.

Transport processes are key to photosynthesis. Since Peter Mitchell’s groundbreaking ‘chemiosmotic hypothesis’, the relevance of transporters in photosynthesis has become abundantly clear. Major advances have been made in identifying metabolite transporters across chloroplast membranes 88 . Yet many of the key transporters in chloroplast membranes remain unidentified. Discovery of the many predicted transporters in subcellular compartments, specifically in chloroplasts has potential for improving energy capture.

A major challenge in future agriculture, is establishing which genetic traits can be combined, or “pyramided”, without adversely affecting yield. As many transport processes reviewed here enhance plant performance via defined functions in specific tissues or cell types, these may be particularly amenable to pyramiding. For instance, salinity tolerance that operates by removal of toxic sodium ions from the xylem sap 24,25,28 could be combined with traits that enhance sequestration of sodium into vacuoles 32,33 , to confer additive salt tolerance. However, many fundamental mechanisms for essential transport processes remain to be uncovered and many essential transporters undoubtedly remain to be discovered. Therefore, knowledge-targeted pyramiding of traits will require future advances in fundamental research into plant membrane transport processes.

Recent advances have identified plant membrane transporters and underlying mechanisms that increase stress resistance and yield of staple crops as reviewed here. Based on these advances, we expect that research into fundamental mechanisms of plant membrane

transport processes will continue to spring new surprises and breakthroughs that will provide new avenues towards a more sustainable and productive agriculture in the face of impending challenges in the future.
