# MATERIALS AND METHODS

**BIOLOGY**

Media and buffer components, kits, and enzymes were used as received from the specified commercial supplier. All expression media, buffers and antibiotics were prepared using purified H2O (Nanopure system, Barnstedt) and autoclaved at 120 °C for 20 min or filter-sterilized, as appropriate.

**Cloning** All oligonucleotide primers (table S7) and all commercial enzymes were purchased from Microsynth AG (Switzerland) and New England Biolabs (NEB), respectively. Phusion HF polymerase (NEB) and supplied Phusion GC buffer were used according to the manufacturer’s protocol for all polymerase chain reactions (50 µL total volume containing 10 µL GC buffer, 50- 200 ng DNA template, 0.5 µM primer, 0.2 mM dNTPs (each, Sigma), 1 µL Phusion) except for error-prone PCR. All PCR fragments were gel-purified using the Zymoclean gel DNA recovery kit (Zymo Research). Full-length PCR fragments were digested with BamHI and HindIII (NEB) and purified using the DNA Clean and Concentrator-5 kit (Zymo Research). The vector backbone pQE_H6MBP (11) (derived from pQE-80L, Qiagen) containing a stuffer fragment was digested accordingly and dephosphorylated with Antarctic phosphatase (NEB) before gel purification. The digested PCR fragments and vector backbone were ligated with T4 DNA ligase (NEB), purified using the DNA Clean and Concentrator-5 kit (Zymo Research), and used to transform electrocompetent E. coli BL21 gold(DE3) cells (Agilent Technologies). The transformation mixture was immediately rescued with super optimal broth containing 20 mM glucose (SOC, 6 mL) and incubated at 37 °C for 1 h before plating on LB agar containing 100 µg/mL ampicillin (Sigma). Pre-cultures were grown in LB Miller broth (Merck) from selected single colonies (5-10 for libraries) containing ampicillin (100 µg/mL final concentration). After harvesting cells by centrifugation, plasmid DNA was isolated using a ZR Plasmid Miniprep Classic kit (Zymo Research) and verified by Sanger sequencing (Microsynth, Switzerland) using the standard primer pQE-30 rev.

## Single-chain MID1sc* variant

A single-chain variant of MID1 (PDB: 3V1C) was generated by connecting the two chains of the symmetric dimer using a glycine-serine linker. Briefly, zero to four alternating glycine and serine residues were manually appended to the C-terminus of one of the chains. The loopmodel Rosetta application (39) was then used to model loop conformations connecting these terminal residues to the N-terminal glycine and serine of the second chain, resulting in models with glycine-serine loops ranging from two to six residues in length. The five-residue loop was found to yield the best overall energies, and two conformations of this loop were selected for use in later simulations.

## Single active-site MID1sc variant

Fixed-backbone design with side chain minimization was used to redesign one zinc site of the single-chain MID1 model (40). The three zinc-binding residues farthest from the flexible linker were allowed to mutate, and the remaining three zinc-binding residues were held in their current rotamers. All other residues’ identities were held fixed, but they were allowed to sample different

rotamers. Twenty-five simulations were performed on each of two models with different possible loop conformations. Mutations with the lowest predicted energy and highest rate of occurrence were selected for testing.

The single active-site variant MID1sc was created by introducing the selected mutations (H12L, H16W, H88K) into the MID1sc* gene using site-directed mutagenesis. The codons for the metal-coordinating histidines at position 12 and 16 were mutated by overlap extension PCR (41) using the cloning primers SST-003 and SST-004 (containing a BamHI and HindII restriction site, respectively) and the mutagenesis primers SST-005 and SST-006 (table S7). The resulting full- length PCR fragment served as template for the introduction of the H88K mutation by site- directed mutagenesis using the cloning primers and the mutagenesis primers SST-007 and SST- 008 (table S7). The gene was reassembled by PCR, digested, ligated into pre-cut pQE-H6MBP, electroporated into E. coli BL21 gold(DE3) cells, and verified by Sanger sequencing (Microsynth AG).

## Cassette mutagenesis

Residues replacing the second zinc site, close to the zinc-binding site, lining the primitive substrate binding pocket, and/or prone to be covalently modified were targeted by cassette mutagenesis. Two library primers were designed for each targeted residue for randomization with degenerate NNK codons (N: A, T, G, C; K: G, T). Initially, two mutations, M38W and Q80R, identified as beneficial for esterase activity in a “test evolution” of MID1sc* were introduced by site-directed mutagenesis using the cloning primers SST-003 and SST-004 and the mutagenesis primers SST-009 and SST-010 (fig. S3, table S7). All DNA libraries were constructed by overlap extension PCR (41) using the cloning primers SST-003 and SST-004 with the corresponding library primers (fig. S3, table S7). The two short fragments generated in the first PCR reaction were gel-purified and mixed in equimolar amounts to serve as a template for the subsequent PCR using only SST-003 and SST-004 as flanking primers. After reassembling the full-length gene, the DNA libraries were digested, ligated into cut pQE- H6MBP, electroporated into E. coli BL21 gold(DE3) cells. Library quality was assessed by Sanger sequencing (Microsynth AG).

## Shuffling by overlap extension PCR

Beneficial mutations identified in focused libraries were shuffled by overlap extension PCR (41) using regular primers (round 2) or primers containing degenerate codons (round 4). Sets of degenerate shuffling primers were designed to encode the parent and mutant amino acids, and mixed in appropriate ratios so that the frequency of each codon was equal. The shuffling libraries were created by overlap extension PCR (41) using the cloning and shuffling primers (fig. S3, table S7). The short fragments generated (up to 5) in the first PCR reaction were gel purified and mixed in equimolar amounts to serve as template for the subsequent PCR using only SST-003 and SST-004 as flanking primers. The full-length DNA libraries were constructed as described before.

## Random mutagenesis using error-prone PCR

Random mutagenesis libraries were created by error-prone PCR (42) of the entire gene using the JBS Error-Prone Kit (Jena Bioscience) according to the manufacturer’s protocol with cloning primers SST-001 and SST-002 (table S7) and 2.8 ng PCR fragment or 32 ng plasmid DNA to afford in average 1-2 amino acid changes per variant. The full-length DNA libraries were

constructed as described above. The first random mutagenesis library (round 7) was based on an assembly PCR fragment that included the K68R substitution. Briefly, K68R was introduced by overlap extension PCR using the cloning primers SST-003 and SST-004 and the mutagenesis primers SST-058 and SST-059 (fig. S3, table S7) with MID1sc6a-h genes as templates (fig. S3). Assembly of the short PCR fragments generated in the first PCR step additionally shuffled the 5’ and 3’ fragments adjacent to position 68.

## Screening

For each focused library based on a single randomized residue, approximately 100 clones were screened. For shuffling libraries, the number screened was increased to 800 to 1600 clones. Single colony transformants were used to inoculate 150 µL LB medium containing 100 µg/mL ampicillin. After incubation at 30 °C and 250 rpm overnight, the pre-cultures were used to generate a replica plate and inoculate 1.8 mL pre-warmed (37 °C) expression cultures in 96-deep well plates. After incubating the resulting cultures at 37 °C and 250 rpm for 3.5 h, the temperature was reduced to 18 °C and, 30 min later, protein production was induced with 0.3 mM isopropyl β-D-1-thiogalactopyranoside (IPTG) and 0.5 mM zinc sulfate. Following incubation at 18 °C for 16 h, the cells were harvested by centrifugation and stored at -20 °C for 1 h. After thawing for 30 min and refreezing for another hour at -20 °C, the cell pellets were resuspended in assay buffer (40 mM HEPES, 50 mM NaCl, pH 8.0) containing egg white lysozyme (Sigma, 0.2 mg/mL) and DNase I (Roche), and incubated for 4 h at room temperature. The cell lysate was cleared by centrifugation and the soluble protein fraction (200 µL) transferred to a 96-well plate containing a TEV protease solution (0.02 mg/mL) in each well. The plates were sealed with a AlumnaSeal 96 film (Sigma) and incubated at 4 °C and 750 rpm overnight. After TEV cleavage, zinc sulfate (200 µM final concentration) was added to each well before the protein solution (undiluted, rounds 1-3; 5-fold diluted, rounds 4-6; 50-fold diluted, rounds 7-8; 500-fold diluted, round 9) were transferred to a black fluorescence 96-well plate (Thermo Scientific) containing (±)-ester 1 (2.5 µM, rounds 1-6, 9; 400 µM, rounds 4-6; 250 µM, rounds 7-8; 50 µM, round 9). Product formation was monitored at 25 °C in a Varioskan flush multimode reader (Thermo Scientific, λex = 380 nm, λem = 470 nm). For reference, several controls were included on each plate: 1) the best variant from the previous round, 2) EGFP to assess cell lysate background, and 3) a negative control as buffer background. Plasmids encoding the most active variants were isolated, sequenced, and used as templates for shuffling or another round of focused or random mutagenesis.

## Protein production and purification of MID1 variants

MID1sc variants were produced as fusion proteins with an N-terminal His6-tag followed by the maltose binding protein and a linker containing a TEV cleavage site in E. coli BL21 gold (DE3) cells and purified by affinity and size-exclusion chromatography as previously described (11). 800 mL of LB medium containing ampicillin (100 µg/mL final concentration) were inoculated with pre-cultures (800 µL) prepared from a single colony and incubated at 37 °C and 230 rpm until the cell density reached an OD600 of 0.4. Subsequently, the temperature was decreased to 18 °C and protein expression was induced after 30 min with IPTG (0.3 mM final concentration) and the cell culture was incubated for 16 h at 18 °C and 230 rpm before harvesting. The cell pellets were stored at -20 °C prior to purification. Upon thawing, the pellets were resuspended in lysis buffer (20 mM Tris-HCl, 100 mM NaCl, 10% glycerol, pH 8.0) containing egg white lysozyme (Sigma, 1 mg/mL final concentration) and incubated at 4° C for 30 min. The cells were

sonicated, and cell debris was removed by centrifugation. After adding imidazole (20 mM final concentration), the soluble protein fraction was loaded onto Ni-NTA beads (Qiagen) equilibrated with wash buffer (20 mM Tris base, 100 mM NaCl, 20 mM imidazole, pH 8.0), and washed. Protein was eluted with elution buffer (20 mM Tris base, 100 mM NaCl, 250 mM imidazole, pH 8.0). The eluted fusion protein was further processed with TEV protease, incubating the fusion protein with TEV protease, DTT and EDTA (0.05 mg/mL, 1mM and 1mM final concentration, respectively) overnight at 4° C. After addition of zinc sulfate (2 mM final concentration), the TEV cleavage reaction mix was purified by a second Ni-NTA column to remove cleaved His-tagged MBP and TEV protease, followed by size-exclusion chromatography on a Superdex-75 column (GE Healthcare, HiLoad 26/60 prep grade) using 40 mM HEPES, 150 mM NaCl pH 8.0 as eluent. Appropriate fractions were pooled and concentrated using a 3 kDa Amicon Ultra-15 Centrifugal Filter Unit (Merck Millipore). Protein purity was assessed by SDS- PAGE and, if necessary, remaining MBP impurities were removed using amylose resin (NEB) equilibrated with assay buffer (40 mM HEPES, 50 mM NaCl, pH 8). Protein concentration was determined by absorption using calculated extinction coefficients from the ExPASy Bioinformatics Resource Portal ([http://expasy.org/tools/protparam.html](http://expasy.org/tools/protparam.html)).

## Protein production and purification of TEV protease

The pRK793-TEVS219V plasmid containing the TEV protease gene (43) was obtained from Dr.

D. S. Waugh (National Cancer Institute at Frederick, Maryland, USA). TEV protease was produced in E. coli BL21(DE3) CodonPlus-RIL cells (Stratagene) and purified by affinity chromatography as described above. Exceptions included addition of chloramphenicol (Sigma, 30 µg/mL final concentration) to cell cultures and phosphate buffers (20 mM potassium phosphate, 150 mM NaCl, 10% (w/v) glycerol, pH 8) for purification, whereas wash and elution buffer additionally contained imidazole (25 mM and 250 mM, respectively). After Ni-NTA purification the eluted protein fraction was dialyzed three times against phosphate buffer supplemented with DTT and EDTA (1 mM final concentration each) at 4 °C in SnakeSkin dialysis tubing (ThermoFisher Scientific), concentrated to 2.5 mg/mL using 3 kDa Amicon Ultra-15 Centrifugal Filter Unit (Merck Millipore), aliquoted, flash-frozen in liquid nitrogen, and stored at -20 °C.
## Identification of acylated residues

To identify residues that are covalently self-acylated by ester 1, enzymatic reactions in assay buffer (40 mM HEPES, 50 mM NaCl, pH 8.0) containing enzyme (MID1sc*: 20 µM, MID1sc8:

2.5 µM), ZnSO4 (MID1sc*: 40 µM, MID1sc8: 2.5 µM) and (±)-ester 1 (MID1sc*: 400 µM, MID1sc8: 200 µM) were incubated at room temperature overnight (MID1sc8: 1 h). The enzyme was separated from the reactants by elution from a dextran column (illustra NAP-5 column, GE Healthcare) using 0.1% acetic acid. The enzyme was washed with 4 volumes of 0.1% acetic acid and concentrated to 15 µM using a 3 kDa Amicon Ultra-15 Centrifugal Filter Unit (Merck Millipore). The unmodified enzyme was treated analogously to provide a reference standard. MALDI-MS/MS data of trypsin or AspN-digested protein was recorded and analyzed by the Functional Genomics Center Zürich. Briefly, the enzyme samples were digested with trypsin or AspN (10 ng/µL and 2 ng/µL final concentration, respectively) in 20 mM Tris-HCl, 0.2 mM CaCl2, pH 8.2 for 10 minutes in an ultrasonic bath. The digests were spotted after mixing 1:1 with matrix solution (0.7 mg/mL α-cyano-4-hydroxycinnamic acid in 85% acetonitrile, 0.1% TFA, 1 mM NH4H2PO4) and MALDI-MS/MS data were acquired.

## Stopped-flow kinetic assay

All stopped-flow experiments were performed at 25 °C on an Applied Photophysics model SX18 stopped-flow spectrometer with a path length of 1 cm. Reactions were initiated by 1:1 mixing of MID1sc10 (100 nM) in assay buffer containing zinc sulfate and casein (50 µM and 0.2 mg/mL, respectively) with (S)-1 ester (0.69 – 55 µM) in assay buffer containing zinc sulfate (50 µM). The absorbance change was monitored at 377 nm for 2 to 5 s. Initial rates were corrected for the zinc-catalyzed background reaction. The steady-state parameters kcat and KM were determined from six measurements of three independent protein batches by fitting all data points to the Michaelis-Menten equation as described above. Standard deviations were calculated from the standard error of the fit considering each replicate value as individual data point.

## Total turnover number

A reaction containing enzyme (50 nM), zinc sulfate (50 μM), and (S)-configured ester 1 (250 μM) was performed at 25 °C in assay buffer and monitored spectroscopically at 377 nm. The amount of product formed by the zinc-catalyzed background reaction was determined theoretically. For each time point, the remaining amount of substrate and the corresponding zinc- catalyzed reaction rate were calculated to determine the amount of product formed in the following time interval (1 s). The sum of product formed in each interval was subtracted from the total product formed in the enzymatic reaction.

## Competitive inhibition by phosphonate 4 and (S)-acid 2

MID1sc10 (100 nM) was preincubated with different concentrations of (±)-phosphonate 4 (0 to 10 µM) or (S)-acid 2 (0 to 50 µM) in assay buffer containing zinc sulfate and casein (50 µM and

0.2 mg/mL, respectively) for at least 10 min. Reactions were initiated by 1:1 mixing of different concentrations of (S)-1 ester (0.69 to 27.5 µM) in assay buffer containing zinc sulfate (50 µM). ## Circular dichroism (CD) spectroscopy

Thermal denaturation curves were recorded in aqueous buffer (5 mM HEPES, 15 mM NaCl, pH

8.0) containing enzyme (10 µM) and zinc sulfate (50 µM) or EDTA (50 µM) using an Aviv 202 spectropolarometer (Aviv Association) and a sealed 0.2 cm quartz cuvette. Thermal denaturation curves were recorded at 222 nm (bandwidth = 1 nm) between 20 and 95 °C in 0.5 °C steps with 30 s averaging time at a rate of 5 °C min
-1 . The experimental data was corrected for the enzyme concentration using the following relationship [Θ] = (Θ·100·MW)/(l·C·n), where [Θ] denotes the mean residue ellipticity, Θ the observed ellipticity, n the number of amino acids, l the path length, c the concentration of the protein, and MW the molecular mass. The melting temperature (Tm) was obtained by determining the inflexion point using a sigmoidal fit: [Θ] = a+b/[1+e (T-Tm)·c ].

## X-ray crystallography

MID1sc10 was crystallized at 20 °C using the sitting drop vapor diffusion method. Crystals of MID1sc10 with racemic phosphonate 4 appeared after two days by mixing 0.2 µL protein solution (13.8 mg/mL) in 40 mM HEPES, 150 mM NaCl, pH 8.0 containing 2.8 equivalents phosphonate 4 (4.2 mM) and zinc sulfate (1.8 mM) with 0.1 µL reservoir solution containing 200 mM HEPES pH 8.6 and 14% (w/v) polyethylene glycol 3350. Crystals were soaked in reservoir

solution containing 20% (w/v) glycerol and 4 mM phosphonate 4 before flash-freezing in liquid nitrogen. X-ray diffraction data (table S4) were collected at the Swiss Light Source (PSI, Villigen, Switzerland) beamline X06DA at a wavelength of 1 Å using the EIGER detector system (Dectris, Switzerland) and processed with XDS (44). The outer resolution limit was estimated using the CC1/2 and I/σ(I) criteria (45, 46). According to these criteria, diffraction data for MID1sc10 were cut at 1.34 Å (CC1/2 0.727 and I/σ(I) 1.28 for the resolution range 1.39 to

1.34 Å). The structure of MID1sc10 was solved by molecular replacement using chain A from 5OD9 with the program Molrep (47) and refined using the program Refmac5 (48). Refinement restraints for heteroatoms were calculated using the prodrg server (49).
