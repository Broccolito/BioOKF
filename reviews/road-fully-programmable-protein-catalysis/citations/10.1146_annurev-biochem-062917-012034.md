**Abstract**

Directed evolution is a powerful technique for generating tailor-made en- zymes for a wide range of biocatalytic applications. Following the principles of natural evolution, iterative cycles of mutagenesis and screening or selec- tion are applied to modify protein properties, enhance catalytic activities, or develop completely new protein catalysts for non-natural chemical trans- formations. This review briefly surveys the experimental methods used to generate genetic diversity and screen or select for improved enzyme vari- ants. Emphasis is placed on a key challenge, namely how to generate novel catalytic activities that expand the scope of natural reactions. Two particu- larly effective strategies, exploiting catalytic promiscuity and rational design, are illustrated by representative examples of successfully evolved enzymes. Opportunities for extending these approaches to more complex biocatalytic systems are also considered.

#### 1. INTRODUCTION

Nature’s biocatalytic repertoire arose over billions of years of evolutionary optimization. Starting from generalist ancestral enzymes with low catalytic activity and broad substrate and reaction scope, gene duplication and diversification via iterative cycles of mutation and natural selec- tion yielded the plethora of efficient, highly specialized protein catalysts responsible for cellular metabolism (1). Over the last two decades, this approach has been successfully transferred to the laboratory where it has become a powerful tool for protein engineering. Here, we briefly review the general strategies underpinning the directed evolution of enzymes. After highlighting a few illustrative examples of natural enzymes that have been fine-tuned for biocatalytic applications, we focus on protein catalysts that have been evolved to perform novel chemistries.

#### 2. GENERAL STRATEGIES

A directed evolution experiment mimics the process of natural evolution, albeit on a much- shortened timescale (Figure 1). The starting point for this process can be any protein that exhibits measurable activity for a chemical transformation of interest. This may be an existing enzyme, for which improved or altered stability, catalytic activity, stereoselectivity, or substrate preference is desired. For reactions outside the biological repertoire, low promiscuous activity of a natural enzyme might be exploited. Alternatively, the target activity might be installed in an existing active site by rational engineering or, more ambitiously, by de novo design. In the past, random DNA damage was induced by chemical or physical means, such as alkylation, deamination, or UV radiation. Since single mutations are seldom sufficient to achieve high activity, recombining beneficial substitutions is a key step in the directed evolution of protein catalysts. Various methods that mimic the natural process of homologous recombination have been developed. DNA shuffling, for example, involves cutting a gene with DNase into random fragments, which are subsequently reassembled in a PCR reaction without external primers (7). Alternatively, shuffled libraries are often generated by a staggered extension process (StEP), a PCR protocol with significantly short- ened elongation steps (8). Since the choices made during library generation dictate the range of possible evolutionary trajectories, the best strategy for gene diversification depends on the task at hand. Most routes through a fitness landscape may lead into the vast desert of inactivity rather than up the desired peak, so systematic approaches for navigating sequence space, such as iterative saturation mutagen- esis (9) or ProSAR (10), a statistical method for analyzing protein sequence–activity relationships, can be helpful for increasing the efficiency of evolutionary searches.

#### 4. LABORATORY EVOLUTION OF NEW CATALYTIC ACTIVITIES

Compared with specificity changes, which have almost become routine, creating reactivities un- related to the native function of an enzyme can be quite challenging, even with the power of directed evolution. A prerequisite for a successful evolution experiment is sufficient starting activ- ity to initiate cycles of mutagenesis and screening or selection. To return to the fitness landscape metaphor, a starting point near the peak to be climbed increases the likelihood that the first steps will be productive, leading upward rather than toward a dead end. Without an amplifiable signal, discovering beneficial mutations may be difficult or even impossible. Choosing an appropriate starting catalyst with at least weak activity is therefore essential.

#### 4.1. Finding a Starting Point: Catalytic Promiscuity Versus Design

Catalytic promiscuity, or promotion of reactions other than those associated with native func- tion, is an inherent property of natural enzymes (53). The ability of enzymes to accept alternative substrates and catalyze secondary reactions is a valuable source of novel reactivity, both in nature and in the laboratory (54, 55). In recent years, ancestral sequence reconstruction has emerged as one strategy to generate promiscuous biocatalysts that can serve as starting points for directed evolution (56). Phylogenetic analysis of the wealth of DNA sequence information available to- day, together with inexpensive gene synthesis, allows the resurrection of evolutionarily ancient enzymes, which often exhibit high thermostability, broad substrate scope, and substantial catalytic promiscuity (57–59). These generalist enzymes appear to be more evolvable than their younger, Downloaded from www.annualreviews.org. Guest (guest) IP: 104.52.5.246 On: Fri, 26 Jun 2026 07:03:15 highly specialized counterparts. Promiscuity may also be induced by external stimuli, as seen in the radical dehalogenation of lactones triggered by photoexcitation of a nicotinamide-dependent ketoreductase (60). If the desired activity is not available naturally, protein engineering by rational design may come to the rescue. For example, minimal active site modification has been exploited to expand the catalytic repertoire of many enzymes (61). Computational methods can extend this approach, permitting exploration of more extensive changes and offering much greater control over active site chemistry.

Enzymes that hydrolyze organophosphate pesticides and nerve agents have been developed from both promiscuous precursors and computationally redesigned active sites. Serum paraox- onase, a calcium-dependent lactonase, and phosphotriesterase-like lactonases, which possess a binuclear metal center, were found to have low promiscuous activity with aryl esters and phos- photriesters. This secondary reactivity was exploited to evolve highly efficient organophosphate hydrolases via multiple rounds of mutagenesis and screening (22, 62). The best variants exhib- ited catalytic efficiencies up to 10 6 M −1 s −1 (22) and even protected mice against lethal doses of organophosphate agents (62). Complementarily, computational methods were used to convert adenosine deaminase, a zinc-containing metalloenzyme that does not cleave phosphate esters, into a phosphotriesterase (63). Although the starting activity conferred by the design mutations was low (kcat/Km= 4M −1 s −1 ), it could also be enhanced by more than three orders of magnitude via directed evolution. Owing to their orthogonal character, promiscuity and design can sometimes be profitably combined. The intrinsic promiscuous activities of natural catalysts can be augmented through design, whereas de novo designed enzymes may exhibit useful promiscuous activities in their own right. The following sections discuss selected examples of enzymes that have been evolved to promote novel chemistries, using promiscuity, design, or a combination of both to generate starting activity.

#### 4.2. Exploiting Promiscuous Activities of Natural Enzymes

Although enzymes may have evolved to promote specific reactions in nature, their catalytic ma- chinery is often suited for alternative chemistries. Making use of this catalytic promiscuity and improving the low starting activities by laboratory evolution provides access to novel protein catalysts, as illustrated exemplarily by squalene-hopene cyclases (SHCs) and heme proteins.

**4.2.1. Squalene-hopene cyclases for non-natural, protonation-driven transformations.** Brønsted acid catalysis triggers a number of synthetically valuable C–C bond-forming trans- formations, including Diels–Alder cycloadditions, Friedel–Crafts alkylations, Prins reactions, and Nazarov cyclizations. An enzyme possessing an unusually reactive carboxylic acid might therefore be expected to accelerate these types of reactions. The reactive intermediates generated could then be channeled along specific reaction pathways by the chiral environment of the active site. SHCs utilize Brønsted acid catalysis to promote cyclization of the triterpene squalene to give the pentacyclic products hopene and hopanol (Figure 3a) (64, 65). The native reaction is initiated by protonation of an olefin by an aspartic acid residue, with participation of a neighboring π-bond in the substrate. After ensuing cyclizations, the final cationic intermediate is quenched by loss of a proton or by addition of water. The protonation machinery in SHC, oriented and activated by a hydrogen bond network within the active site, is also capable of cyclizing diverse non- native substrates with high stereoselectivity (66–69). Replacing iron porphyrin with an artificial cofactor containing other noble metals, such as iridium, has the potential to further extend the catalytic capabilities of these natural scaffolds. Modified myoglobins and P450 enzymes containing an iridium porphyrin cofactor have been prepared that catalyze various carbene and nitrene insertion reactions (91–94), including insertion of carbenes into unactivated C–H bonds, albeit with only 31 turnovers for this challenging reaction (91). As for natural heme proteins, mutagenizing residues that line the active site enhanced both the activity and stereoselectivity of these artificial metalloenzymes. In a complementary approach, noncanonical amino acids may be used to fine-tune the reac- tivity of metal centers. In a recent example, replacement of the proximal heme-binding histidine ligand in myoglobin with N-methylhistidine (NMH) enhanced the enzyme’s promiscuous peroxi- dase activity substantially (95). Further optimization by a combination of protein engineering and several rounds of laboratory evolution afforded NMH-containing variants with catalytic efficien- cies comparable to those of natural peroxidases (kcat= 690 s −1 ,kcat/Km= 1.5 × 10 6 M −1 s −1 for Amplex Red oxidation). Such enzymes could conceivably be used to promote carbene and nitrene insertion reactions as well. The ability to evolve recombinant proteins containing noncanonical active site functionality provides exciting new design opportunities. The unique reactivity of iron centers is not restricted to heme proteins. Nature exploits this metal ion in another highly diverse class of biocatalysts, namely nonheme iron enzymes (96). These enzymes are also interesting targets for enzyme engineering and directed evolution. For instance, Rieske nonheme iron oxygenases have been engineered for the asymmetric dihydroxylation of dif- ferent alkenes (97). Furthermore, the selectivity between rebound hydroxylation and halogenation in α-ketoglutarate-dependent nonheme iron enzymes has been investigated (98, 99). An improved mechanistic understanding of these enzymes may foster practical applications in the future.

#### 4.3. Rational Design and Directed Evolution of Artificial Protein Catalysts

Rational design represents an attractive route toward novel enzyme activity. Non-natural metal- containing cofactors have been successfully incorporated into otherwise inert protein scaffolds, yielding highly stereoselective protein catalysts. **4.3.1. Artificial metalloenzymes.** The ability of metal ions to promote redox chemistry, radical reactions, and complex functional group interconversions is quite general. As a consequence, considerable effort has been devoted to the design of artificial metalloproteins (100, 101). The catalytic versatility of metal ions makes this approach particularly attractive for the acquisition of novel activity. Strategies based on covalent anchoring or supramolecular docking of metal complexes in pro- tein binding pockets have afforded catalysts for a wide range of non-natural reactions, including

hydrogenation of alkenes, transfer hydrogenation of ketones, and olefin metathesis (101). By com- bining the intrinsic reactivity of an artificial metal cofactor with the specificity of the host protein, such systems often exhibit high enantioselectivities. However, with rare exceptions (102), rate en- hancements tend to be orders of magnitude lower compared to those achieved by natural enzymes. This effect can be attributed to the substantial influence that second and third coordination spheres have on catalysis. Such distant interactions are hardly ever optimized in artificial metalloenzymes. In some cases, the resulting metal–protein complexes have been successfully improved by evolu- tionary strategies (103). For instance, an artificial di-rhodium metalloenzyme that catalyzes olefin cyclopropanation was constructed by covalently linking an alkyne-substituted di-rhodium com- plex to a prolyl oligopeptidase containing a genetically encoded L-4-azidophenylalanine residue (104). The enantioselectivity of the metalloenzyme was subsequently improved via several rounds of mutagenesis and screening. The best variant not only achieved high enantioselectivity [enan- tiomeric excess (ee) > 92%] for styrene cyclopropanation but also exhibited improved substrate scope and water tolerance compared with free di-rhodium complexes. Artificial metalloenzymes for enantioselective Friedel–Crafts alkylations have been constructed by formation of a supramolecular Cu(II) complex with a dimeric protein (105) or genetic incorpora- tion of the copper-binding amino acid (2,2 ′ -bipyridin-5yl)alanine (106). Similar to the di-rhodium catalyst, both yield and enantioselectivity of these systems could be improved by mutation. How- ever, because the metal–protein complexes are formed in vitro, they require protein purification before screening, making high-throughput evolution difficult. Streptavidin has been used extensively as a scaffold for creating artificial metalloenzymes (107,

108). Binding of biotinylated rhodium, ruthenium, or iridium complexes has provided stereose- lective catalysts for many different non-natural reactions, including asymmetric C–H activation (102). Using a smart library of 335 streptavidin variants that targeted 28 different sites for muta- genesis, it was possible to screen for artificial transfer hydrogenases and metathesis catalysts with improved activities and selectivities (109). To assay larger libraries, however, higher throughputs would be needed. To that end, an in vivo compartmentalization strategy has been developed in which streptavidin variants are secreted to the periplasm (110, 111), enabling formation of a com- plex with a biotinylated metal cofactor and subsequent catalysis in the absence of cytoplasmic inhibitors such as glutathione (Figure 5). Proof-of-concept was demonstrated for an intramolec- ular olefin metathesis reaction that forms a fluorescent product. However, directed evolution of the respective biocatalyst yielded only a two-fold increase in kcat/Kmcompared with the starting variant (110). Because the fluorescent product quickly diffused from the periplasm, the genotype– phenotype linkage was maintained by prior spatial separation of cells in microtiter plates. The use of microfluidic-based single-cell encapsulation and droplet sorting may be an option to realize higher throughput in the future.

However, high activity was achieved at the expense of the originally designed catalytic apparatus, which was replaced by a sophisticated hydrogen bond network of four active site residues that arose one residue at a time over the course of evolution (Figure 6a). #### 4.4. Complex Biocatalytic Systems

Owing to continuous improvements in directed evolution methodology, more complex biocat- alytic systems can be tackled. Protein engineers now have the appropriate tools in hand to modify and optimize metabolic pathways as well as multimodular enzymatic assembly lines.

**4.4.1. Metabolic pathway engineering. Assembling sequentially acting enzymes into synthetic** pathways, both in cells and in the test tube, is an effective strategy for producing complex, high-

Natural biosynthetic pathways have been successfully engineered to optimize the production of complex natural products like artemisinin (141). It is even possible to bring together biocatalysts from various organisms to create hybrid pathways (142). Merging pathway engineering, enzyme design, and directed evolution promises to propel this area of research, providing the tools needed to create artificial reaction cascades that are efficient and robust. Individual enzymes in such pathways may be optimized or engineered by directed evolution or even replaced with artificial catalysts that have been designed and evolved for completely new functions (143).

Diversification of the carotenoid biosynthetic pathway by directed evolution provided an early example of this strategy’s potential to expand nature’s repertoire of bioactive compounds (144). Mutagenesis and screening of specific enzymes, such as carotenoid synthase (145), engendered **PKS:** polyketide changes in specificity that led to novel carotenoid end products, which are unavailable from synthase biological sources. In the ensuing years, methods for manipulating biosynthetic pathways have **NRPS:** nonribosomal multiplied. For the design of a hybrid pathway to overproduce levopimaradiene, a diterpenoid peptide synthetase precursor of ginkolides, for instance, several engineering approaches were used to amplify the flux of key biosynthetic precursors in Escherichia coli and to tailor the enzymes geranylgeranyl diphosphate synthase and levopimaradiene synthase to augment overall yield and selectivity (146). The complexity of the reaction networks that can be designed today is strikingly demonstrated by an artificial CO2 fixation pathway that was constructed by assembling 17 enzymes from 9 different organisms (147). A notable feature of this synthetic cycle is the use of an efficient enoyl- CoA carboxylase/reductase for the central carboxylation step, which circumvented the efficiency bottleneck of natural RuBisCo-based systems. By transplanting this system into a living organism, it may ultimately be possible to develop robust whole-cell biocatalysts capable of recapturing atmospheric CO2 for use as a carbon feedstock. In the future, designer enzymes may extend these forays into synthetic biology. In a proof- of-principle study, computational design has been combined with site-directed mutagenesis to transform benzaldehyde lyase into an artificial formolase that catalyzes the carboligation of three formaldehyde molecules to give dihydroxyacetone (148). Despite its low catalytic efficiency (kcat/Km= 4.7 M −1 s −1 ), the engineered enzyme could be employed in a simple metabolic pathway for assimilating formaldehyde into central metabolism. As these technologies mature, they will present engineers with many new options for designing biocatalytic routes to diverse natural and unnatural products.

#### 5. FUTURE CHALLENGES AND OPPORTUNITIES

Creating state-of-the-art protein catalysts for reactions unknown in nature, long a dream of chemists and biochemists, is gradually becoming reality. The same evolutionary processes that shaped the natural biocatalytic world are being effectively exploited in the laboratory today to modulate the activities and selectivities of existing enzymes. Efforts to exploit the promiscuous properties of these natural systems for new chemistry, or to augment the efficiency of artificial enzymes, similarly benefit from efficient methods for exploring sequence space. Nevertheless, directed evolution remains a laborious and time-consuming undertaking. Im- Downloaded from www.annualreviews.org. Guest (guest) IP: 104.52.5.246 On: Fri, 26 Jun 2026 07:03:15 proving the efficiency of a protein catalyst by several orders of magnitude can easily take years and numerous rounds of evolution. Further methods development is thus needed to make its im- plementation faster, more efficient, and amenable to more intricate and challenging systems. On the experimental side, advances in gene synthesis (161) and sequencing (162) promise to influence how we design and analyze libraries. The methods described above to screen libraries in ultrahigh- throughput fashion, or conduct evolution experiments continuously, are already speeding the pace of discovery, and novel assay formats continue to appear. Computational methods represent a particularly powerful ally to laboratory evolution, provid- ing a means of exploring regions of sequence space that are not accessible within a few random

mutations. As computational methods improve, the synergy between these approaches is only likely to intensify. Remarkable recent progress on designing atomically accurate protein scaffolds points to exciting opportunities ahead (163). Extending these approaches to the design of more accurate, and hence more active, functional sites—perhaps in combination with classical molecular dynam- ics (MD) or combined quantum mechanics/molecular mechanics (QM/MM) simulations—may present a shortcut to circumvent the currently arduous experimental trek through sequence space (164, 165). In this regard, the ability of computation to sample vastly larger numbers of sequence variants than will ever be possible experimentally represents a potentially decisive advantage. Evo- lutionary optimization of first-generation computational enzymes will remain important for the foreseeable future, yet its role may shift from repairing or replacing imperfect designs to polishing and refining their properties. The progress made over the last few decades in the field of protein design and evolution heralds exciting opportunities for the future. As enzyme engineers experience success in tackling increas- ingly challenging chemical transformations, biocatalytic reaction sequences only imaginable in the present day will be applied to living cells and manufacturing concerns alike. Sophisticated catalysts and molecular machines capable of synthesizing new antibiotics, editing our genomes, or degrading the mountains of plastic waste in the oceans may not be far behind.

**SUMMARY POINTS**

1. Iterative cycles of mutagenesis and screening or selection, performed in the laboratory, mimic the process of natural evolution. This approach enables optimization of enzyme properties as well as the development of novel protein catalysts possessing non-natural function.
2. Smart strategies to design gene libraries combined with high-throughput screening tech- niques facilitate efficient laboratory evolution of enzymes catalyzing a wide range of reactions.
3. Catalytic promiscuity and rational design are valuable sources of catalysts for a desired reaction, which can be subsequently optimized by directed evolution.
4. Both natural and artificial metalloenzymes can be engineered to catalyze non-natural transformations efficiently.
5. Computational enzyme design combined with laboratory evolution is a successful strategy for generating novel protein catalysts with true enzyme-like activities.
