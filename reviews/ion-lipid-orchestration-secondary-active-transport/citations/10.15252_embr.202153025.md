## Review

# How physical forces drive the process of helical membrane protein folding

#### Karolina Corin & James U Bowie*

### Abstract

Protein folding is a fundamental process of life with important implications throughout biology. Indeed, tens of thousands of muta- tions have been associated with diseases, and most of these muta- tions are believed to affect protein folding rather than function. Correct folding is also a key element of design. These factors have motivated decades of research on protein folding. Unfortunately, knowledge of membrane protein folding lags that of soluble proteins. This gap is partly caused by the greater technical chal- lenges associated with membrane protein studies, but also because of additional complexities. While soluble proteins fold in a homoge- nous water environment, membrane proteins fold in a setting that ranges from bulk water to highly charged to apolar. Thus, the forces that drive folding vary in different regions of the protein, and this complexity needs to be incorporated into our understanding of the folding process. Here, we review our understanding of membrane protein folding biophysics. Despite the greater challenge, better model systems and new experimental techniques are starting to unravel the forces and pathways in membrane protein folding.

Introduction

Membrane proteins can be divided into two major classes: the helix bundle class comprised of assembled transmembrane helices and the b-barrel class comprised of transmembrane b-strands that form the staves of the barrel. Both classes satisfy backbone hydrogen bonds in different ways. The helical class satisfies backbone hydro- gen bonds internally within the helix, whereas the b-barrel class satisfies hydrogen bonds by bringing together separate strands. As a result, the biogenesis and folding of the two classes are completely different (Bowie, 2004). To limit the scope of this review, we will

focus on the helix-bundle class, which is by far the largest class of membrane proteins. However, physical principles garnered from both classes will be included. The biogenesis of helical membrane proteins has long been a challenging field of study. The hydrophobic transmembrane domains of these proteins must partition into the bilayer core to avoid aggregation, while the extramembrane domains rely on an aqueous environment to maintain a functional fold. These polar opposite environmental requirements make studying membrane protein folding far more challenging compared to soluble proteins. Pioneering work on cleaved bacteriorhodopsin (Popot et al, 1987) yielded tantalizing clues about the folding mechanism for helical membrane proteins. These initial experiments supported a two-stage model that could be broken into two primary steps. (Popot & Engel- man, 1990; Fig 1A). In the first step, transmembrane helices are inserted and an initial topology is defined. For most membrane proteins, this step is handled by the Sec translocon complex (Cymer et al, 2015), although some helical membrane proteins are inserted by other more specialized machinery (Borgese et al, 2019; De Geyter et al, 2019; Drwesh & Rapaport, 2020). Once inserted, the protein can complete folding. This second step involves the assembly of trans- membrane helices, folding of the extramembrane domains, and, when relevant, oligomerization with other subunits. While separation into two stages is useful conceptually, it has become clear that these stages cannot be so neatly divided. Topology is not necessarily fixed, and insertion and folding can be coupled (Fig 1B). This review will focus on the physical forces that shape this tortuous pathway to a folded helical bundle membrane protein.

Transmembrane helix amino acid composition and bilayer properties

In essence, membrane protein folding is determined by the interac- tions between the protein residues themselves and their surrounding environment. Any discussion of folding must thus begin with helix residue composition and lipid membrane characteristics. Membrane bilayers are apolar in the center and polar in the headgroup region. To avoid aggregation, the amino acid composition of transmem- brane helices varies across the bilayer thickness to reflect those dif- ferences (Ulmschneider & Sansom, 2001; Ulmschneider et al, 2005; Senes et al, 2007; Schramm et al, 2012a; Fig 2, propensity). The thermodynamics, in turn, are dominated by the hydrophobic effect and reflect how well a helix’s amino acid sequence can match the properties of the lipid membrane (Cymer et al, 2015). As such, numerous hydrophobicity scales have been developed to quantify the probability of insertion in the membrane. While initial scales were modeled based on the transfer free energy between a solvent and water (Wimley & White, 1996), more membrane-specific and biologically relevant scales were subse- quently developed (Wimley & White, 1996; Hessa et al, 2005, 2007; Moon & Fleming, 2011; Marx & Fleming, 2021). Hessa et al (2005) enabled this development through an elegant assay quantifying insertion propensities in a biological context: Their assay measured the insertion probability of a 19 amino acid test segment into an endoplasmic reticulum membrane. Their biological hydrophobicity scale is similar to the physical hydrophobicity scales that measure the transfer free energy between a solvent and water, but reflects the apparent transfer free energy from the translocon to the bilayer. To account for the dramatic physical and chemical variation across the thickness of the bilayer, this scale was later refined to describe the contribution of each side chain at each position along a trans- membrane helix (Hessa et al, 2007). Figure 2 shows the biological hydrophobicity scale profiles for each amino acid as a function of bilayer depth. The apparent free energy scales nicely match the observed amino acid distributions in transmembrane helices. Recent work on hydrophobicity scales has been able to quantify transfer free energies as a function of bilayer depth using direct physical measurements of the coupled insertion and folding of a b- barrel membrane protein (Marx & Fleming, 2021). Marx and Flem- ing were able to show how the modulation of transfer free energy correlated with water content at different bilayer positions. Their analysis allows for a quantitative description of the energetics of side chain movements along the bilayer normal, an important feature of any description of membrane protein folding. In an inter- esting observation, they note that the biological hydrophobicity scale seems to reflect, quantitatively, the energetics of transfer from a bilayer interface rather than bulk water. This suggests that biologi- cal insertion decisions catalyzed by the translocon may effectively evaluate an apparent equilibrium between the bilayer core and the bilayer interfacial region (Cymer et al, 2015). Although hydrophobicity scales can be used to predict the likeli- hood of insertion, they do not capture the whole picture. For exam- ple, some helices in multi-spanning membrane proteins are only marginally hydrophobic and do not insert efficiently on their own (Sato et al, 2002; Sadlish & Skach, 2004; Pitonzo & Skach, 2006). Yet, they are still able to insert and yield properly folded proteins. Clearly, factors other than hydrophobicity must influence trans- membrane helix insertion. Transmembrane helix length is one factor that can affect insertion across the membrane. Helix lengths typically vary from ~14 to 36 residues (Bowie, 1997) and several experimental and computational studies suggest that a minimum of ~10-12 residues are required for efficient insertion (Jaud et al,2009;Ulmschneideret al,2010).The energetic cost of inserting helices with fewer residues is likely due to hydrophobic mismatch. While the hydrocarbon core of a bilayer is ~30 A thick, a 10-residue helix only spans ~15 A, requiring either the protein or bilayer to deform. Interestingly, the insertion efficiency of

shorter peptides can be increased by flanking them with lysines on either end. The lysines likely reduce the energetic barrier to insertion by hydrogen bonding with the lipid phosphate groups or water (Jaud et al, 2009). Several computer simulations mirrored these findings, indicating that charged residues can either pull down phosphate headgroups, or “snorkel” up toward the headgroup interface in order to form stabilizing hydrogen bonds (White & Vonheijne, 2005; von Heijne, 2007; Ulmschneider et al, 2014; O¨ jemalm et al,2016). Interactions with, or the position of, neighboring helices or domains can also influence transmembrane helix insertion. Inter- helix interactions between polar residues can reduce the energetic barrier to insertion (Meindl-Beinker et al,2006).Anefficiently- inserting preceding or subsequent helix alone can also significantly increase the insertion efficiency of a marginally hydrophobic helix (Hedin et al, 2010). Further, the orientational preference of a down- stream helix can facilitate or impede insertion by altering the hydrophobicity threshold required for efficient insertion (O¨ jemalm et al, 2012). The loops flanking marginally hydrophobic helices can also reduce the energetic barrier, typically by adding positive charges to the cytosolic side (Hedin et al, 2010). Finally, previously inserted helices can literally pull newly synthesized segments into the bilayer, a move that is indicative of co-translational folding (Cymer & von Heijne, 2013). Clearly, translocon-catalyzed insertion assesses the local environment, which, in addition to the bilayer, includes the translocon complex and any previously inserted protein chains.

##### Forces driving helical membrane protein folding

Hydrogen bonds Hydrogen bonds are important interactions in membrane protein folding, but their contributions are as complex as the bilayer envi- ronment in which they reside. In water-soluble proteins, the contri- bution of hydrogen bonds is reduced by the high dielectric of water and the ability of water to make hydrogen bonds that compete with the hydrogen bonds in the folded protein (Klotz & Franzen, 1962; Mitchell & Price, 1990; Eberhardt & Raines, 1994; Ben-Tal et al, 1997; Bowie, 2011). Thus, it might be expected that the low dielec- tric of the membrane core and the low concentration of water would result in strong hydrogen bonds in membrane proteins. Yet existing experimental data reveal that the intrinsic contribu- tion of hydrogen bonds is not much different than that observed in soluble protein structures. For example, Joh et al. found that eight hydrogen bonds in bacteriorhodopsin only contributed an average of ~0.6 kcal/mol (Joh et al, 2008). Hydrogen bonds made by a vari- ety of polar side chains across a designed transmembrane helix trimer contributed a maximum of 1.8 kcal/mol/monomer (Grat- kowski et al, 2001). A hydrogen bond between Gln residues across a b-barrel membrane protein dimer was found to contribute

0.5 kcal/mol/monomer (Stanley & Fleming, 2007). In the glyco- phorin A transmembrane helix dimer, a hydrogen bond made by a Thr side chain contributes only 0.4 kcal/mol/monomer in detergent (Fleming & Engelman, 2001b; Hong et al, 2010) and ~1 kcal/mol in natural bilayers (Hong et al, 2010). A hydrogen bond made by a Glu side chain in an FGF transmembrane dimer contributes only
0.7 kcal/mol/monomer (Li et al, 2006). Thus, while the experimen- tal data reveal favorable hydrogen bond contributions, they are comparable to those in soluble proteins (Bowie, 2011).

Why aren’t side chain hydrogen bonds stronger in membrane proteins (Bowie, 2011)? First, most of the interhelical hydrogen bonds are actually not made in a low dielectric, low water environment (Joh et al, 2008). Membrane protein cores can be quite polar and contain many water molecules. Indeed, access to aqueous compartments is vital to the function of many transporters and channels. Consequently, water can often compete with intra-protein hydrogen bonds (Fig 3A). Second, polar groups in membrane proteins are usually there to provide function and facilitate stable interactions with substrates, and not necessarily to stabilize the protein. As such, there would be no evolutionary pressure to make strong hydrogen bonds within a membrane protein. Third, hydrogen bonds in folded proteins must compete with other possible hydrogen bonds. In helices, polar side chains can readily make hydrogen bonds to the backbone (Chamber- lain & Bowie, 2004). Consequently, inter- and intra-helix hydrogen bonds must compete with each other, diminishing their net contribu- tion to stabilizing the folded state (Fig 3A). Backbone hydrogen bond contributions are more difficult to measure as they cannot be removed by mutation (Gao & Kelly,

2008). They can, however, be assessed by H/D exchange measure- ments since weaker hydrogen bonds prefer D over H (Loh & Markley, 1994; Bowers & Klevit, 1996; LiWang & Bax, 1996; Khare et al, 1999; Shi et al, 2002; Cao & Bowie, 2014; Cao et al, 2017; Lessen et al, 2020). Through model compound studies, it is possible to assign a free energy to a hydrogen bond using the observed H/D preferences (Cao & Bowie, 2014). Cao et al. and Lessen et al. measured H/D fractionation factors for the helical amyloid precursor protein and the b-barrel OmpW, respectively (Cao et al, 2017; Lessen et al, 2020). After correcting the Cao et al. values for kinetic isotope effects, Lessen et al concluded that the intrinsic strength of hydrogen bonds is similar in membrane proteins and soluble proteins. While experimental data so far indicate that most backbone hydro- gen bonds in membrane proteins are not unusually strong, we do not wish to suggest that they are not important. Indeed, membrane proteins can be designed with hydrogen bond formation as the orga- nizing principle (Lu et al, 2018a). These bonds are still favorable and some can actually be extremely strong (up to 4.6 kcal/mol). It can be particularly unfavorable to break a hydrogen bond without making an alternative hydrogen bond. Indeed, it is rare to find unsatisfied hydrogen bonds in proteins (Fleming & Rose, 2005). Breaking hydro- gen bonds in the bilayer core is unfavorable without the ability to make a compensating hydrogen bond to water or a side chain. As a result, the hydrocarbon core region of the bilayer is strongly helix favoring and makes transmembrane helices resistant to denaturation (Brouillette et al, 1987; Lau & Bowie, 1997; Riley et al, 1997). If the energetic cost of breaking hydrogen bonds is so high in the membrane, it might be expected that transmembrane helices would be extremely rigid. Yet that is not the case. They are able to undergo considerable twisting and bending during functional cycles. How is this possible? One way to break backbone hydrogen bonds is to replace them with alternative hydrogen bonds to water molecules (Cao & Bowie, 2012). Another way is to shift hydrogen bonds between i + 3, i + 4, and i + 5 partners in the backbone to allow helix distortion without a loss of hydrogen bonds. Such shifting is actually quite common during conformational changes (Hildebrand et al, 2004; Cao & Bowie, 2012) and can result in helix unwinding or facilitate binding interactions with substrates (Screpanti & Hunte, 2007; Merkle et al, 2018). Finally, backbone hydrogen bonds can
vary dramatically in strength, creating weak points in the helix (Cao et al, 2017). For example, for unknown reasons, there is at least one backbone hydrogen bond in the amyloid precursor protein that appears to be destabilizing. Variation in backbone hydrogen bond strengths could play an important role in defining proteolytic sites and breaking points for conformational change in transmembrane helices (Valiyaveetil et al, 2006; Cao & Bowie, 2012; Cao et al, 2017; Go¨ tz et al, 2019a, 2019b). How the transmembrane protein sequence determines backbone hydrogen bond strength and confor- mational flexibility remains unclear and requires further investiga- tion (Ho¨ gel et al, 2018; Go¨ tz et al, 2019a, 2019b). Non-canonical C-H O hydrogen bonds can also stabilize membrane proteins (Senes et al, 2001). The clearest examples are in transmembrane helix dimers stabilized by the GxxxG motif (Senes et al, 2000; Mueller et al, 2014). In this common motif, the two Gly side chains make Ca-H O hydrogen bonds to the backbone carbonyl oxygens. Theoretical calculations suggest that the Ca-H O hydrogen bond is a particularly strong carbon–hydrogen bond, perhaps half the strength of a polar hydrogen bond (Sandoval-Lira et al, 2015). Indeed, the Ca-H O hydrogen bond in the GxxxG motif of glycophorin A was estimated to be worth ~0.9 kcal/mol based on vibrational frequency shifts (Arbely & Arkin, 2004). Though they can be stabilizing, it remains unclear how important carbon–hydrogen bonds might be in general. Many apparent carbon–hydrogen bonds seen in crystal structures are simply inci- dental occurrences from close packing (Chamberlain & Bowie,

2002). Also, an apparent Ca-H O hydrogen bond between a Thr side chain oxygen and a Ca-H seen in the crystal structure of bacteri- orhodopsin was even found to be slightly destabilizing (Yohannan et al, 2004). Nevertheless, even if these interactions make small contributions, their potentially large numbers can combine to make significant contributions toward stability (Chamberlain & Bowie, 2002; Loll et al, 2003). Other electrostatic interactions Like hydrogen bonds, other electrostatic interactions have the potential to be stronger in the low dielectric membrane environ- ment, though the same mitigating factors described above apply. Many membrane proteins require salt bridges to function properly (Lee et al, 1992; Call et al, 2002; Kim et al, 2004; Hertel et al, 2010; Cui et al, 2013), but the strength of these interactions has largely gone unstudied. One group was able to probe a set of salt bridges in the b-barrel membrane protein OmpA (Hong et al, 2006) and found interaction free energies varying from ~ 0.6 to 5 kcal/mol. These measurements, along with those of other buried salt bridges in soluble proteins (Singh, 1988; Anderson et al, 1990; Hendsch & Tidor, 1994; Marqusee & Sauer, 1994; Tissot et al, 1996), suggest that these interactions are likely to be mildly to strongly stabilizing. It has been hypothesized that their characteristics may allow them to control longer range conformational changes (Hong, 2014). Interactions involving aromatic residues are also likely to affect membrane protein folding. Cation?p interactions can act over a longer distance than salt bridges or hydrogen bonds and are less sensitive to the effects of solvents (Gallivan & Dougherty, 1999). Pi- pi interactions are also long-range, acting up to ~7 A (Burley & Petsko, 1985, 1986; Makwana & Mahalakshmi, 2015). Aromatic interactions may also drive or stabi- lize helix dimerization (Ridder et al, 2005; Sal-Man et al, 2007; Unterreitmeier et al, 2007).

Van der Waals interactions Van der Waals packing interactions are clearly a major factor stabi- lizing membrane proteins. Stable helix association does not require polar residues (Fleming & Engelman, 2001a; Ptacek et al, 2007; Heim et al, 2015) and stable membrane protein structures can be designed using only apolar side chains (Mravic et al, 2019). More- over, the most stable helix dimers formed via the GxxxG motif have increased van der Waals interactions (Anderson et al, 2017). Conversely, when packing interactions are disrupted by the intro- duction of steric clashes or empty pockets, proteins are destabilized (Fleming et al, 1997; Fleming & Engelman, 2001b; Doura et al, 2004; Joh et al, 2009; Baker & Urban, 2012). This begs the question: Do membrane proteins maximize van der Waals contributions through better, more efficient packing? Some early crystal structure analyses suggested that membrane proteins are indeed better packed than soluble proteins (Gerstein & Chothia, 1999; Eilers et al, 2000), but later analyses indicate that they aren’t and actually contain more cavities (Adamian & Liang, 2001; Hilde- brand et al, 2005). These cavities can be essential for membrane proteins to function properly (Guo et al, 2020). Moreover, cavities introduced into bacteriorhodopsin and T4 lysozyme had similar energetic contributions from van der Waals packing interactions: ~20 cal mol -1 A -2 for lysozyme and ~18 cal mol -1 A -2 for bacteri- orhodopsin (Joh et al, 2009). Altogether, this suggests that if the quality of packing is higher in membrane proteins, it is a subtle dif- ference at best. If membrane proteins do not increase van der Waals contribu- tions by optimizing packing energetics, are there other tricks that they can use? Oberai et al found that transmembrane regions of membrane proteins bury considerably more surface area than soluble proteins, thereby increasing the total amount of packing (Oberai et al, 2009). This suggests that membrane proteins achieve more, rather than better, van der Waals packing. Because mutations in buried residues are more likely to be deleterious, one conse- quence of higher packing in the transmembrane domains is an increased sensitivity to disease-causing mutations (Oberai et al,

2009). Another consequence as well as corollary explains an odd finding in protein evolution: transmembrane regions evolve at a slower rate than soluble proteins, while the extramembrane regions evolve at a much faster rate (Tourasse & Li, 2000). Evolutionary pressure to avoid harmful mutations in the sensitive highly buried regions likely leads to their slower mutation rate. Conversely, a higher tolerance for mutations in the loosely buried extramembrane loops allows for faster evolution. (Fig 3B). Entropy Fixing a polypeptide chain into a well-defined structure involves an enormous loss of entropy (Baxa et al, 2014). For helical membrane
proteins, much of the conformational entropic cost occurs during the first stage of folding. Upon insertion into the bilayer, much of the secondary structure is fixed and the orientation of transmem- brane helices is restricted. Fixing the secondary structure has the additional effect of reducing side chain rotomeric freedom (Cham- berlain & Bowie, 2004; Baxa et al, 2014). This entropy loss is compensated by the hydrophobic effect and favorable bilayer inter- actions that occur upon insertion. Thus, for the second stage of fold- ing, substantial conformational entropy cost has been effectively pre-paid in the first stage. Still, anything that reduces entropic losses during second-stage folding will help thermodynamically stabilize the folded protein. One way membrane proteins may reduce the entropic cost of folding is to use small side chains with fewer rotomeric possibili- ties (MacKenzie & Engelman, 1998). Indeed, common helix pack- ing motifs (Moore et al, 2008) like GxxxG (Senes et al, 2000), glycine zippers (Kim et al, 2005), GASRight, GASLeft(Walters & DeGrado, 2006), and serine zippers (Adamian & Liang, 2002) involve small side chains. These motifs not only allow for hydro- gen bonding and close packing but also involve minimal entropic losses. Further, small side chains like Gly and Ala allow membrane proteins to bury more surface area by enabling closer approach of the helices (Eilers et al, 2000; Jiang & Vakser, 2000; Oberai et al, 2009). Another unexpected trick membrane proteins use to reduce entropic losses was only discovered recently when the Wand group was able to measure the fast internal motions of membrane protein side chains (O’Brien et al, 2020; Fig 3C). Surprisingly, the most rigid class of motions that is observed in soluble proteins was nearly absent in the tested proteins, and a new highly dynamic class was observed. This new class, dubbed J0, suggests fluid motion and extensive sampling between different rotameric states. This remark- able finding was observed in two disparate membrane proteins, suggesting that high conformational entropy might be a general feature of membrane proteins. Somehow, it appears that membrane proteins can maintain a defined folded state with significantly fewer side chain restrictions. Maintaining high conformational entropy in this manner may reduce the penalty for folding in the membrane. How membrane proteins can maintain higher side chain flexibility while soluble proteins do not remains an interesting question for future research. A third factor that contributes toward membrane protein fold- ing, albeit modestly, is solvent entropy. Although the hydropho- bic effect cannot drive the assembly of transmembrane helices in the bilayer core (Haltia & Freire, 1995; MacKenzie & Engelman, 1998; DeGrado, 2003; Lomize et al, 2009), a process akin to the hydrophobic effect in soluble protein folding occurs. The associa- tion of membrane proteins or transmembrane helices reduces the amount of lipid-exposed surface area, thereby releasing previ- ously retained lipids back to the disordered bilayer (Helms, 2002) and allowing them to assume less conformationally defined states (Chadda et al, 2021; Fig 3D). This likely increases the entropy of the membrane and may partially explain why many membrane proteins favor oligomeric structures (Kahraman & Haselwandter, 2016; Chadda et al, 2021). This solvation entropy may contribute up to 4 kcal/mol to the free energy of helix dimerization in SDS and probably contributes more in a lipid environment (Fisher et al, 1999).

##### Lipid bilayer forces

The lipid bilayer is an ordered medium and plays an important role in defining membrane protein structure. Indeed, lipid headgroup size and charge, as well as alkyl chain number and length, can alter membrane protein folding, stability, topology, and function (Corin & Bowie, 2020). For example, optimizing lipid chain length to mini- mize hydrophobic mismatch can enhance protein activity (Lee, 1998; Dumas et al, 2000; Pilot et al, 2001; Hong et al, 2006), likely by affecting helix tilting or packing (Lee, 1998; Pilot et al, 2001). The interactions and collisions between lipid headgroups and alkyl chains create a pressure gradient through the bilayer that can affect membrane protein folding (Fig 3E). Natural bilayers are typically overpacked in the hydrocarbon core and under- packed in the interfacial region, leading to high lateral pressure in the center and low lateral pressure at the interface. Several stud- ies have shown that high lateral pressureinthebilayercorecan impede membrane protein insertion (Curran et al, 1999; Meijberg & Booth, 2002; Curnow et al,2004;Findlay&Booth,2017), though this effect is protein-dependent (van Dalen et al, 2002; Findlay et al,2010;Harriset al, 2017; Choi et al, 2019). In some cases, when a lateral pressure increase simultaneously leads to an increase in curvature stress, the barrier to insertion can actually decrease (van Dalen et al,2002;Choiet al, 2019). Once inserted into the bilayer, high lateral pressure can facilitate the packing of transmembrane helices (Miller et al, 2009), as well as oligomer- ization (Keller et al, 1993; Hong & Bowie, 2011). It can also affect the rate of formation and relative population of folding intermedi- ates (Allen et al, 2004a, 2004b). Moreover, high bilayer lateral pressure or curvature stress can increase the thermostability or half-life of proteins (Keller et al, 1993; Allen et al, 2004b; Hong et al, 2006; Findlay & Booth, 2017) and stabilize oligomeric states (van Dalen et al, 2002; Hong & Bowie, 2011). Interestingly, the bilayer may also stabilize oligomeric states in order to reduce defects or localized changes in curvature that could be caused by hydrophobic mismatch at monomer–monomer interfaces (Chadda et al, 2021). In addition to pressure, bilayer lipid charge can also significantly affect membrane protein structure. A high concentration of anionic lipids has been found to induce topological inversion of many membrane protein domains (Hu & King, 1998; Bogdanov et al, 2002; Zhang et al, 2003, 2005; Vitrac et al, 2011, 2013, 2017a; Tunu- guntla et al, 2013). Negative lipid charge may also play an important role in improving membrane protein insertion and folding rates (Seddon et al, 2008; Harris et al, 2018; Choi et al, 2019). In some cases, negative lipid charge may be necessary for membrane protein stability or oligomerization (Nussberger et al, 1993; van Dalen et al, 2002; Henrich et al, 2017; Sanders et al, 2018). The mechanisms by which lipid headgroup charge affects membrane protein structure are likely diverse. Membrane proteins can interact with bilayer lipids via hydrogen bonds (Nogi et al, 2000; Lange et al, 2001; Wiener, 2006; Hakizimana et al, 2008; Martens et al, 2016), and aromatic residues in the interfacial region are capable of p-interactions with membrane lipids (Roderick et al, 2002; Shinzawa-Itoh et al, 2007; McIlwain et al, 2015). These inter- actions, and perhaps others, can regulate protein activity, orienta- tion, and helical tilt (Miller et al, 2009; Hong & Bowie, 2011; McIlwain et al, 2015). They can also help stabilize specific

conformations (Martens et al, 2016) and alter the pKa of side chains (Gbaguidi et al, 2007; Martens et al, 2016).

##### Post-translational topology adjustments

The traditional model of membrane protein folding assumes that transmembrane helices are inserted co-translationally across the membrane before coalescing into their final tertiary structure (Popot & Engelman, 1990). Implicit in this model is that the final topology of the protein is largely fixed upon insertion. While this model may describe insertion for many proteins, the reality for many others is likely far more nuanced and complex. Indeed, transmembrane helices are not necessarily inserted sequentially, with some helices inserting only after later-synthesized helices have been inserted (Lu et al, 2000; Pitonzo & Skach, 2006; Skach, 2009). They can also initi- ally insert in the wrong direction (Lu et al, 2000), or can pop in and out of the bilayer prior to folding (Lu et al, 2000; Kanki et al, 2002; Seurig et al, 2019). In addition to these local topology adjustments, large domains and entire proteins can flip in the bilayer (Fig 4; Bogdanov et al, 2014; Woodall et al, 2017; Dowhan et al, 2019; Corin & Bowie, 2020). For example, an entire domain of lac perme- ase can flip upon a change in lipid composition (Bogdanov et al,

2002), and the so-called dual topology transporters are highly topo- logically malleable. Dual topology transporters have proved to be key instruments in furthering knowledge of the role of topological change during membrane protein biogenesis. Dual-topology transporters are oligomers with identical polypeptide chains adopting opposite orientations (Fig 4; Rapp et al, 2006). This finding begs the ques- tion: how can the same polypeptide generate two inverse topolo- gies? In one extreme model, the protein inserts in both topologies in equal proportion. In another extreme model, the protein topol- ogy is completely malleable and flips continuously until it finds an oppositely oriented subunit allowing it to form a stable oligomer (Woodall et al, 2015). Current evidence suggests both mechanisms can occur. The dual topology protein EmrE can initially insert in either orientation, but can also flip completely in the bilayer (Woodall et al, 2015, 2017). While EmrE can flip during biogene- sis, it remains controversial how important topological flipping is during normal biogenesis (Fluman et al, 2017; Woodall et al,
2017). Understanding how topological change is enabled by the polypeptide chain is an important issue going forward in membrane protein folding and biogenesis. The fact that transmem- brane helices and entire protein domains can flip in a membrane is quite remarkable when one considers that even simple lipid flip- flop between bilayer leaflets can be on the order of hours to days (Anglin et al, 2007). Computational modeling of dual-topology transporters suggests that large domains do not need to invert in a concerted fashion (Van Lehn et al, 2015). Instead, individual helices probably invert one at a time until the final topology is achieved (Fig 4). For dual topology membrane proteins, the approach to the final orientation is likely governed by the loop with the slowest flipping rate (Van Lehn et al,2015).Experimental observations are consistent with these modeling predictions. ##### Second stage folding pathways in detergent

For many years, the second stage of membrane protein folding had to be studied in detergent solutions. This was typically accom- plished by refolding from an SDS-denatured state as this state displays some key features expected for an unfolded protein in the membrane. Specifically, much of the helical secondary structure in a variety of membrane proteins is normally maintained in SDS (Booth et al, 1995; Lau & Bowie, 1997; Otzen, 2003; Krishnamani et al, 2012). To our knowledge, the first effort to study membrane protein folding kinetics was performed by Paula Booth and coworkers, who examined bacteriorhodopsin folding kinetics by diluting the SDS- unfolded state into native detergent micelles (Booth et al, 1995). Refolding studies of bacterioopsin have identified intermediate states (Booth et al, 1995; Booth et al, 1995; Booth & Farooq, 1997;

Curnow & Booth, 2007; Pan et al, 2010; Krishnamani & Lanyi, 2011), but there has been limited data on their corresponding structures. To obtain information on the formation of structure in the folding transition state, /-value analysis has been performed on a handful of membrane proteins. These /-values analyze how mutations alter the folding transition state free energy (Zarrine-Afsar & Davidson, 2004). The results of these analyses are somewhat murky. One study found that residues in the second transmembrane helix of bacteriorhodopsin become structured in the transition state, suggesting that structure formation toward the N terminus nucleates folding (Curnow et al,

2011). This was consistent with pulsed oxidative labeling experiments indicating that the N-terminal end of the protein may become struc- tured early (Pan et al, 2010). However, another study found very little ordered structure in the bacteriorhodopsin transition state (Schlebach et al, 2014). /-value analysis of DsbB indicates that the transition state involves interactions between residues from disparate helices at the same end of the final helix bundle. This suggests a more broadly structured transition state in which the tips of the helices initially coalesce, leading to further consolidation of the structure (Otzen,
2011). A /-value analysis of GlpG was particularly extensive and interesting (Paslawski et al, 2015). In this work, high /-values were observed in helices 1 and 2, indicating that folding nucleates near the N terminus. Intriguingly, some mutations nearby had negative /- values, indicating that mutations destabilizing the protein thermody- namically could nevertheless speed folding. A subsequent computa- tional analysis of the folding pathway suggested an interesting explanation for this phenomenon (Schafer et al, 2016). Namely, fold- ing can occur from either the N or C terminus, but folding to the tran- sition state via the favored pathway requires breaking interactions formed in an intermediate state. Weakening these interactions by mutations makes this “backtracking” more facile, thereby explaining the negative /-values. This model predicts a smoother pathway to the folded state in bilayers, however, where the unfolded state is presum- ably more organized. Overall, pathway investigations of detergent refolding leave a rather muddled picture. Several studies indicate the possibility that N-terminal structure is key for nucleating folding, while others see more long-range structure formation. Of course, it is entirely possi- ble that different proteins employ diverse folding mechanisms, and much more work is needed to extract patterns. However, it is also possible that folding in bilayers cannot be modeled as well as we would like by using detergents.
##### Second stage folding pathways in bilayers

Folding studies within true bilayers are critical to understand membrane protein biogenesis, but can be tricky to implement. Stan- dard chemical and thermal denaturation approaches do not work because they are ineffective in unfolding membrane proteins, or they alter or destroy the bilayer. Luckily, alternative techniques are emerg- ing and are yielding considerable insight into the folding process. One approach used to drive unfolding in bilayers utilizes steric trapping (Hong et al, 2010). In this method, two biotin tags are introduced into the protein at points that are close in space in the folded structure. Monovalent streptavidin can freely bind to one biotin, but a second streptavidin can only bind when the protein is unfolded due to steric overlap. With this arrangement, streptavidin

concentration can effectively force unfolding. This method has successfully been used to study the thermodynamics of folding and unfolding within natural bilayers and bicelles (bilayer-like disks; Hong et al, 2010; Hong & Bowie, 2011; Chang & Bowie, 2014; Nadeau et al, 2015; Guo et al, 2016, 2020). In theory, a method to rapidly remove the biotin tags would allow for kinetic studies of refolding. However, steric trapping has not been applied to mecha- nistic folding studies thus far. Another approach is to employ single molecule forced unfolding. Mechanical force is a particularly attractive denaturant for membrane proteins because it is completely independent of the chemical forces driving folding. It is also uncoupled from the vari- able, heterogeneous environment of the lipid bilayer. Pioneering work used an atomic force microscope to pull individual bacteri- orhodopsin molecules out of membranes by pulling normal to the bilayer plane (Oesterhelt et al, 2000). Numerous studies since then have used the technique to explore the pathways of extraction, ener- getics of extraction, pathways of insertion, catalysis of insertion, and even ligand binding (Oesterhelt et al, 2000; Muller € et al, 2002; Kedrov et al, 2004; Cisneros et al, 2005; Bippes et al, 2013; Serdiuk et al, 2016, 2019; Yu et al, 2017, 2019, 2020; Sapra et al, 2019; Jacobson & Perkins, 2021). In a particularly remarkable series of experiments, the Muller group examined the process of polypeptide € insertion catalyzed by the SecYEG translocon and the insertase chaperone YidC (Serdiuk et al, 2016, 2019). They managed to pull a lac permease molecule out of the bilayer, dangle it over YidC and/or SecYEG, and then watch the insertion process. They found that SecYEG is particularly adept at inserting transmembrane segments sequentially, while YidC is not particularly concerned about order and inserts segments more randomly. Together SecYEG and YidC cooperate to effectively insert lac permease. While the AFM method has provided fascinating insight into membrane protein architecture and allows for physical mechanistic studies of the insertion process, it cannot capture the essence of second stage folding, that is, folding within the context of a bilayer. Indeed the overall energetics is dominated by removal from the bilayer rather than folding within the bilayer (Jefferson et al, 2017). An alternative single molecule approach using magnetic tweezers provides an opportunity to pull and fold within the plane of the bilayer (Min et al, 2015, 2018; Lu et al, 2018a; Choi et al, 2019). As shown in Fig 5A, the protein is tethered to DNA handles which are then attached between a surface and a magnetic bead. Lowering a magnet allows force to be applied to the protein. As the protein unfolds, the increasing bead height can be monitored with a micro- scope. Bicelles and true bilayers in liposomes have successfully been used with this technique (Min et al, 2015, 2018; Choi et al, 2019). The first application of magnetic tweezers to the rhomboid protease GlpG exposed some limitations of the technique (Min et al,

2015). First, unfolding occurs in an experimentally accessible time frame only at forces so high that the protein is ripped out of the bicelle (Fig 5B, topmost panel). Nevertheless, it is possible to extrapolate that the passage through the transition state involves only a small ~15 A expansion. Thus, unfolding to the transition state occurs within a bicelle, and the system can report on unfolding prior to extraction. A second limitation was that refolding occurred with reasonable probability only at low forces where noise from Brownian motion of the bead hides any folding events that occur. The improve- ments made it possible to observe folding and unfolding from a so- called zig-zag conformation within a bilayer (Fig 5B), much like what was envisioned in the original two-stage model. Remarkably, the experimental result for GlpG was foreshadowed in a pioneering study which computationally modeled membrane protein folding under force (Lu et al, 2018b). The ability to compu- tationally model the folding process is an ultimate test of our under- standing, and the congruence of experimental and computational results for GlpG folding bodes well for the future. The new GlpG and b2-adrenergic receptor folding results, combined with the more speculative results implying that a ClC transporter may fold from the N terminus, lead to a rather appealing model for membrane protein biogenesis. In particular, the results suggest that membrane proteins have evolved to fold during biosyn- thesis and insertion. If so, it is not necessary for the entire polypep- tide to be synthesized before the folding process can begin in earnest. As soon as two helices emerge from the ribosome, they can begin folding and accrue structure as new polypeptides emerge. This idea is consistent with work showing that the insertion of prior transmembrane helices can enhance the probability of subsequent helix insertion, and that interactions between previously inserted helices can actually physically pull in subsequent helices via residue-specific interactions (Cymer & von Heijne, 2013; Nicolaus et al, 2021). This idea is appealing because unfolded proteins are typically prone to aggregation, and concurrent insertion and folding would minimize these potentially deleterious issues.

##### Oligomer and complex assembly paths

Oligomerization is important for membrane protein structure and function. Many membrane proteins function as oligomers (Meng

et al, 2009; Clarke & Gulbis, 2012; Reading et al, 2015), with some exhibiting multiple multimeric states (Kahraman & Haselwandter, 2016; Henrich et al, 2017). Further, mutations that disrupt oligomer- ization, perhaps even without changing monomeric structure, can cause disease (Ng et al, 2012; Ye et al, 2014b). It is thus important to understand how membrane proteins associate into complexes. Although oligomerization plays an important biological and struc- tural role, only a handful of studies have explicitly examined oligomer assembly. Interestingly, almost all suggest that oligomerization is a cooperative rather than sequential process. In unfolding and refolding studies of the tetrameric KscA using triflouroethanol (Valiyaveetil et al, 2002; Barrera et al, 2008) and of the trimeric AcrB using SDS and urea (Ye et al, 2014a), no intermediates were observed. Unfolding and refolding studies of AcrB and the ABC transporter BtuCD even suggested that oligomerization might be coupled with folding. For AcrB, trimer dissociation and monomer unfolding appeared to occur simultaneously, and a mutant incapable of trimerization could only achieve a fold similar to the native conformation (Ye et al, 2014a). Further, unfolding of the monomer caused trimer dissociation. For BtuCD, assembly from unfolded subunits resulted in activity closer to that of the native protein than assembly from folded subunits (Di Bartolo et al, 2011). Of note, a dimeric intermediate was observed during unfolding of the tetrameric aquaglycoporin GlpF (Veerappan et al, 2011). Even so, its oligomerization process does not completely deviate from the pattern observed with the other transmembrane proteins. Intermediates with 3 monomers were not observed, suggest- ing some level of cooperativity. Although the assembly of few oligomeric helical membrane proteins has been studied, the consistency of the results suggests that there might be a common underlying principle governing oligomerization. Based on assembly in soluble proteins, diBartolo et al suggested that the interface between monomers determines whether oligomer assembly is a cooperative or independent process (Di Bartolo et al, 2011). Specifically, interfaces that are larger, better packed, or sufficiently hydrophobic are more likely to exhibit cooperative association. This makes sense because a larger surface area would confer a larger contribution toward fold stabilization upon oligomerization. The proteins studied thus far seem to follow this general rule. The surface area buried in the BtuCD interface is ~1500A2, and hydrophobic interactions may be necessary for association (Di Bartolo et al, 2011). The surface areas buried by AcrB trimerization or KcsA tetramerization are ~3,500 A2 (Yu et al, 2011; Fang et al, 2013) and ~3,600 A2 ([https://www.ebi.ac.uk/pdbe/pisa/](https://www.ebi.ac.uk/pdbe/pisa/)), respectively. In both cases, about half of the buried surface area is hydrophobic. Indeed, an analysis of 62 oligomeric membrane proteins demonstrated that nearly all of their interaction surfaces were >900 A2 and had a preference for small or hydrophobic residues (Duarte et al, 2013). This principle may even account for the dimer intermediates observed during GlpF tetramer formation, as a large fraction of the surface area that is eventually buried in the tetramer is actually buried in the monomer–dimer transition (Veerappan et al, 2011). Further, the interface between GlpF monomers is almost as hydrophobic as the lipid-exposed area (Fu, 2000). In each case, it would appear that the oligomerization pathway attempts to bury as much interface area as quickly as possible (Fig 6A). higher order complexes typically seem to assemble in a sequential or ordered manner. The cytochrome bo3 complex is a classic example. When its four subunits come together, only two intermediates are observed. This suggests assembly in an ordered sequence because if their assembly occurred in a random order, equal amounts of 10 dif- ferent intermediates would be expected (Fig 6B) (Stenberg et al, 2007; Daley, 2008). Sequential assembly has been observed in other complexes as well, like the proteins involved in E. coli cell division (Daley, 2008), and the F1FoATP synthase (Dalbey et al, 2011; Ruhle & € Leister, 2015; Song et al, 2018). Assembly of higher order complexes is likely regulated because each subunit can have a different function. Random assembly could lead to the formation of intermediates with unconstrained or deleterious functionality (Daley, 2008; Ruhle & Leis-€ ter, 2015), such as the generation of reactive oxygen species toxic to the cell (Herrmann & Funes, 2005). Controlled assembly, with the aid of chaperones, likely prevents the formation of inactive, toxic, or “dead-end” intermediates (Daley, 2008).

Conclusion

Exploration of the physical forces governing membrane protein fold- ing has been a slow and tortuous pursuit. Thanks to new and evolv- ing techniques, we are now able to tackle topics in this challenging field of study with greater precision and depth. Physical and biologi- cal insertion assays have given us a much better thermodynamic picture of insertion into different layers of the bilayer (Hessa et al,

begin as soon as a helical hairpin emerges from the translocon and possibly even in the ribosome exit tunnel (Cymer et al,2015).Further, transmembrane helices can interact during membrane integration (Cymer & von Heijne, 2013; Nicolaus et al, 2021). Indeed, folding can partially drive the insertion of some protein segments, and even surface or re-entrant helices may play a role in membrane integration (Nicolaus et al, 2021). Such co-translational folding is likely favored in order to prevent the deleterious consequences of misfolding or aggregation. In cases where local folding is not possible, the protein may adopt a “place-holding” fold that must later be corrected though “backtracking” or other topological adjustments (Schafer et al, 2016). A particular surprise is the possibility that oligomerization and inser- tion may be coupled as well, which begs the question of how subunits being inserted by distinct translocons find each other during biosyn- thesis. To the extent that insertion and folding are coupled, it will be important to develop methods to watch the complete process in real time. There are hints that such a dream may be possible, and exciting new developments are sure to come.
