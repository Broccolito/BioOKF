## May the proton motive force be with you: A plant transporter review

### Camilla Gottlieb Andersen, Laust Bavnhøj and Bjørn Panyella Pedersen

Abstract As our ecosystems experience challenges associated with climate change, an improved understanding of the funda- mental biochemical processes governing plant physiology is needed. Strikingly, current structural information on plant membrane transporters is severely limited compared to other kingdoms of life, with only 18 unique structures in total. To advance future breakthroughs and insight in plant cell molec- ular biology, structural knowledge of membrane transporters is indispensable. This review summarizes the current status of structural knowledge in the plant membrane transporter field. Plants utilize the proton motive force (PMF) to drive secondary active transport. We discuss the PMF, how it relates to secondary active transport and provide a classification of PMF driven secondary active transport, discussing recently published structures of symporters, antiporters, and uniporters from plants.

While 390 of these structures are of human origin, only 51 unique membrane protein structures have been deter- mined from terrestrial plants (land plants (Embry- ophyta)), as tallied by the authors November 2022. As in all other kingdoms of life, transmembrane trans- port is essential in plants; providing them with the ability to sense, respond and adapt to changing envi- ronments [2e5]. However, plant transporters are currently represented by a meager 18 unique structures (Table 1). The lack of structural knowledge in the plant transporter field hampers our understanding of a wide range of fundamental biological processes in plants such as organ development, tissue patterning, hormone dis- tribution, vascular function, uptake of nutrients, and interaction with microbes [2e5].

Active transport and the proton motive force Active transport requires an energy source to drive a substrate across a cell membrane against its concentra- tion gradient and is traditionally divided into primary and secondary active transport. Primary active transporters are driven by redox potential, light energy or chemical energy often in the form of ATP hydrolysis. A central purpose of many primary active transporters is to generate an electrochemical gradient across membranes. Second- ary active transporters exploit this electrochemical gradient, allowing them to drive transport of their “pri- mary substrate” against its concentration gradient.

In most organisms, protons are used to create an elec- trochemical membrane gradient called the proton motive force (PMF), with the notable exception of animals that utilize a sodium gradient across the plasma membrane [6,7]. In plants, primary active transporters generate and maintain the PMF which is the combination of a chemical proton gradient (DpH or D[H þ ]) and the electric mem- brane potential (DJ). The plant plasma membrane potential is typically far more negative than the 40 to 95 mV resting mem- brane potential found across the animal plasma mem- brane [9]. Plants can build up significant DJ gradients with cell membrane potentials around 110 to 160 mV, and potentials as high as 250 mV have been measured [10e12]. Notably, compared to the rather fixed sodium concentrations and resting membrane potential found in animal cells, plants adjust their non- cytosolic pH and membrane potential to accommodate physiological needs [13,14]. Assuming reasonable values (e.g. DpH = 2, DJ = 200 mV), the free energy yield from one proton passing the membrane can be calcu- lated to 30.5 kJ mol 1 with respective contributions from DpH of 11.2 kJ mol 1 and DJ of 19.3 kJ mol 1

The first structure of a plant transporter was the primary active transporter AHA2 from Arabidopsis thaliana,an ATP-driven proton pump that creates and maintains the

PMF across the plasma membrane [15,16]. Besides AHA2, structures of PMF-generating primary active transporters now also include the inorganic pyrophos- phatases (PPases) and the vacuolar ATPase (V-type ATPase) that both energize and acidify vacu- oles [17e20].

Classification of secondary active transport Secondary active transporters are classified into three categories according to how they utilize the PMF: symport, antiport and uniport. Symport and antiport use protons to drive transport of the primary substrate. Symporters need two substrate binding sites, one for their primary substrate and one for protons. Here sub- strate coupling is paramount; transport of the primary substrate and protons are coordinated to ensure efficient energy usage from the PMF [21]. Two bindings sites are also possible in antiporters, but often antiport involves a single binding site that is used first to transport one or more protons-into the cytosol-and subsequently the primary substrate-out of the cytosol. Uniport differs from symport and antiport by transporting only a primary substrate. If the primary substrate is charged, uniport can be classified as secondary active transport due to the contribution from the electrical component (DJ) of the PMF. Here we summarize recent structural advances made in the field of plant transporters, focusing on secondary active transporters. We show examples of symporters, antiporters, and uniporters, and discuss how the indi- vidual transporters utilize the PMF to drive transport.

Examples of symport The Major Facilitator Superfamily (MFS) is the largest superfamily of secondary active membrane transporters known and for instance the genome of A. thaliana contains more than 200 MFS transporters, clustered into 22 families [29]. Within the plant kingdom, structural in- formation is however only available on two MFS trans- porters: The nitrate transporter NRT1.1 and the sugar transporter STP10, which are both symporters and both from A. thaliana [30e33]. Since STP10 has been solved in its two main conformations (outward and inward), it has provided valuable knowledge on how plant symporters utilize the PMF to drive transport. STP10 illustrates a design principle with tight coupling between the primary substrate and protons [32,33]. The STPs are best known as high affinity glucose/proton symporters, primarily responsible for the movement of glucose into the cytosol [34e36]. They belong to the Sugar Porter (SP) family, which is the largest known family among MFSs [29]. Transporters within the MFS all share a fold with 12 transmembrane helices (TM1-12) distributed equally between two domains called the N- and the C-domain that are related by a pseudo-twofold symmetry (Figure 2). Broadly, transport is described by the rocker- switch model, where the two structurally similar domains rock around the binding site for the primary substrate at the center of the transporter [21,37,38]. The structures of STP10 display the protein in an outward occluded state and an inward open state, both featuring a glucose molecule in the binding site found in a central cavity. The glucose is primarily recognized and bound by residues of the C-domain, while the N-domain features the proton binding site. This substrate binding scheme represents a canonical example of asymmetrical substrate coordina- tion, commonly found in the MFS [39e45]. In STP10 an aspartate localized in TM1 carries the proton and thus functions as the proton donor/acceptor. Nearby, in TM4, an arginine controls proton binding and release by directing pKa changes of the aspartate [46]. The pro- tonation of the aspartate directs movement of TM1, making it close in on the glucose to ensure a state of tight binding. Hence, in STP10, protonation is directly coupled to the high affinity binding of the primary

substrate through TM1, displaying an excellent example of substrate coupling in symport.

Another recent example of insights into PMF coupled symport in plants comes from the structure of the

A. thaliana transporter SULTR4; 1, solved in an inward facing conformation [47]. SULTR4; 1 is a member of the sulfate/proton symporters (SULTRs) that transports sulfate (SO42) from the vacuolar lumen into the cytosol [48,49]. SULTRs belong to the Sulfate permease (SulP) family within the structurally diverse amino acid- polyamine-organocation (APC) superfamily. In the APC superfamily, individual families are characterized by either 5 þ 5or7þ 7 transmembrane topologies. Proteins adopting the 5 þ 5 topology are often described as having a “LeuT-fold” and assumed to function by the rocking bundle mechanism first described for the Leucine Transporter (LeuT) from the hyperthermophilic bacte- ria Aquifex aeolicus [50,51]. In contrast, SULTRs are structurally related to APC transporters of the mamma- lian solute carrier 4/23/26 families (SLC4, SLC23 and SLC26) that adopt the 7 þ 7 topology [27,52e55] (Figure 2). The 7 þ 7 topology can be described as a 7- TM inverted repeat (7TMIR) and these proteins are believed to function by a mechanism bearing traits from both the elevator and the rocking bundle mechanism, highlighting the overlap between different transport schemes [27,53,56]. Although the SULTR4; 1 structure displays a dimeric assembly, individual monomers are thought to independently mediate transport. The monomer is comprised of 14 TM helices assembled into two distinct domains which intermix elements of the 7- TM inverted repeat: the larger core domain (TM1-4 and 8e11) and the smaller gate domain (TM5-7 and 12e14). The SO42 binding site is localized in the core domain in proximity to the gate domain. A glutamate found in TM8, w7A
˚ from the SO42 binding site, is believed to be the proton carrier, but exactly how the two sites are mechanistically coupled is unknown. Since SULTR4; 1 is believed to transport two protons per SO42, there is no net charge transfer across the mem- brane and the overall transport process is described as electroneutral. It is unclear exactly how proton-coupling works in SULTR4; 1, but it is suggested that the gluta- mate facilitates transport of one proton, while transport of the second proton may involve water or other residues of the protein [47]. Regardless, the experimentally observed electroneutrality suggests that the chemical component (D[H þ ]) of the PMF is the primary driving force of SULTR4; 1.

Examples of antiport Examples of PMF driven antiport comes both from plant MATE transporters as well as the recently published structure of the vacuolar iron transporter (VIT1) from Eucalyptus grandis [59e62]. The mechanism of plant MATEs has been described in several papers, most recently by Tanaka et al. regarding the Nicotiana tabacum MATE2 that transports nicotine into vacuoles in ex- change for a proton [60e62]. The work on MATE2 sug- gests that plant MATEs operate by the conventional antiport mechanism where a single binding site is shared between the primary substrate and the proton (Figure 2)[60].

VIT1 is an iron(II)/proton antiporter responsible for the transport of ferrous iron ions (Fe 2þ ) into vacuoles [63]. VIT1 has a unique structural fold, which we will refer to as the VIT-fold, making its antiporter mechanism intriguing (Table 1)[59]. The VIT1 structure repre- sents a homodimer, likely displaying the transporter in an inward open conformation. Each monomer comprises two domains, a transmembrane domain (TMD) and a cytosolic metal binding domain (MBD) hypothesized to increase the local concentration of Fe 2þ and thus to facilitate transport through the TMD (Figure 2). The TMD contains five TM helices (TM1-5). TM2 pro- trudes into the cytosol where it is connected to three short cytosolic helices (H1eH3). TM2 cytosolic parts, H1 and H3 make up the MBD. The Fe 2þ /H þ trans- location pathway is found at the dimer interface be- tween the TMD of each monomer, thus, dimerization is essential to the function of this transporter. The pro- posed transport model of VIT1 suggests a relay mecha- nism, involving three highly conserved residues located along the translocation pathway in each monomer: an aspartate, a glutamate, and a methionine. In the dimeric form, these are suggested to form two separate binding sites, a vacuolar localized site, formed by the glutamate of each protomer, and a cytosolic facing site, formed by the aspartate/methionine (D/M-site) pairs-both hy- pothesized to be occupied by two protons. VIT1 trans- port is suggested to take place in three steps: 1) The cytosolic D/M-site binds Fe 2þ from the MBD, releasing two protons to the cytosol. 2) Fe 2þ is then relayed to the vacuolar glutamate site and two protons are transferred to the cytosolic D/M-site. 3) Fe 2þ is released into the vacuolar lumen and is replaced by two vacuolar protons, restoring the initial conformational state. Currently, the

precise stoichiometry of Fe 2þ /H þ is unknown, but this model posits two protons per Fe 2þ transported [59]. The proposed VIT1 mechanism is unusual for anti- porters. Instead of the conventional mechanism, as described above for MATEs, VIT1 is proposed to contain two separate binding sites that both accommo- date either Fe 2þ or protons, reminiscent of the knock-on mechanisms of ion-channels, but there are still open questions regarding VIT1 gating and occlusion.

Examples of uniport Finally, we turn to uniporters and show a recently published example of how they utilize the electrical membrane potential component (DJ) of the PMF to drive transport. The PIN protein family displays the only example of active uniport in plants, with the recently published structures of A. thaliana PIN1, PIN3, and PIN8 involved in export of the plant hormone auxin from the cytosol [64e66]. All solved structures display the protein as a dimer and depict both apo and substrate bound forms. They reveal that PINs belong to the bile/arsenite/ riboflavin transporter (BART) superfamily. All PIN structures were solved in the inward conformation, except two additional PIN8 structures which revealed the outward conformation. This allowed a mechanistic model to be deduced for auxin transport in the PIN family [66]. The PINs operate using an elevator mecha- nism, similar to the mechanism previously described in bacterial and human families of bile acid/sodium, bicar- bonate/sodium symporters, and sodium/proton anti- porters [67e75]. All these proteins share a common fold with a scaffold domain and a transporter domain, asym- metrically centered around a central proline crossover region, which pinpoints the binding site of the primary substrate (Figure 2). PINs feature 10 TM helices with a 5-TM inverted repeat between TM1-5 and TM6-10. The scaffold domain holds TM1-2 and TM6-7, while the remaining helices and the central crossover region are found in the transporter domain. Based on the data so far, PINs do not appear to make use of the chemical proton gradient for transport. Since auxin is negatively charged at cytosolic pH, it is possible for PINs to exploit the elec- trical membrane potential (DJ) without transporting protons. The negative charge has been shown to suffi- ciently drive transport under experimental conditions, allowing auxin to be concentrated against its chemical gradient by an active uniport mechanism [64,66].

Conclusion It is still early days for the research field of plant transporter structural biology. Regardless, we know PMF is fundamental to plant transmembrane transport and that the emerging examples discussed here show how elegantly secondary active transporters are employed. The plethora of human membrane protein structures has revolutionized human biophysics and provided the medical field with specific targets for drug development

[76]. A similar improvement in our structural under- standing of plant transporters is needed to lay the foundation for a new green revolution to support and maintain our ecosystems in light of the challenges associated with rapidly changing climates. We are at the frontier of a vast (green) unexplored structural space-we do live in interesting times.
