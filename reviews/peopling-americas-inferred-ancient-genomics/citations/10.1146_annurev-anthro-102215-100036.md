The last decade has witnessed a remarkable transformation in anthropological genetics. At the same time, anthropological geneticists have become increasingly sensitive to the concerns, interests, and perspectives of research participants and communities affected by ge- netic research. As the nature of interactions among researchers, participants, and communities change, more collaborative approaches to study design and data interpretation are being devel- oped, a greater diversity of perspectives are being incorporated into scientific research, and more communities are becoming interested in participating in genetic research. These developments have had a significant impact on how researchers approach long-standing questions in anthropology and human population genetics, and they are yielding important new insights about human genomic diversity and population history. In this review, we discuss the methodological advances and recent genetic studies that have contributed to our understanding of Native American genomics and population histories. Native Americans have been underrepre- sented in genomic research to date, but this research has nevertheless had important implications for how we comprehend population genetic structure, demographic histories, and even health and disease in the Americas. Native American populations have also been a focus of research because the Americas were settled more recently than other continents, and Native American diversity patterns may therefore help elucidate aspects of the global dispersal of modern humans (Hoffecker et al. 2016). We begin here with an overview of the latest technologies and computational approaches that have applications in anthropological genetics. Our goal here is not to duplicate the many excellent reviews of next-generation sequencing methods or recent computational advances (e.g., Mardis 2013, Vitti et al. 2013, Hofreiter et al. 2015, Orlando et al. 2015, Schraiber & Akey 2015, Malaspinas 2016), but to discuss how these methods can help researchers investigating Native American genomic diversity and population histories. We then synthesize what has been learned about Native American genomic variation to date, focusing on research published in the last decade, and develop a model of population history in the Americas based on the observed genetic patterns. Finally, we discuss the broader implications of this research for anthropology and related disciplines, and we highlight challenges, topics that remain understudied, and other issues that need to be addressed in future research. #### Advances in Laboratory Methods for Recovering Ancient DNA

Recent efforts to improve the recovery and analysis of aDNA have greatly enhanced our ability to characterize genetic variation in the past. In particular, paleogeneticists have made strides in iden- tifying the best sources of aDNA and in developing methods to recover more endogenous DNA while minimizing exogenous contamination and damage to human remains. Hair and petrous bone (a dense portion of the inner temporal bone) are excellent sources of aDNA (Rasmussen et al. 2010, Pinhasi et al. 2015) but may be less common in the archaeological record than other skeletal elements. Teeth are more common and provide more endogenous aDNA than do most other bones, especially when tooth cementum is sampled (Damgaard et al. 2015). Ancient DNA can also be extracted from teeth nondestructively, without deliberately breaking or drilling into a tooth (Bolnick et al. 2012, Hofreiter 2012). Dental calculus (calcified dental plaque that can be scraped off a tooth) and coprolites (paleofeces) can also be used as sources of human DNA (Poinar et al. 2001, Black 2011, Ozga et al. 2016). These approaches are especially important for the study of Native American remains, as many descendant and/or culturally affiliated communities object to destructive analysis of ancestral remains (Bolnick et al. 2012, Bardill 2014). However, how these approaches compare to the destructive extraction of DNA from bone or teeth, in terms of the amount of DNA recovered, still needs to be rigorously tested. #### New Technologies for Genomic Analysis

The last decade has also seen significant advances in technologies for collecting genomic data. The advent of high-throughput DNA sequencing, whereby millions of fragments throughout the genome are sequenced at once, has made it more feasible to “shotgun” sequence whole genomes (Mardis 2013). Because the cost of whole genome sequencing has been quite high, only a few dozen genomes from Native Americans have been sequenced thus far (1000 Genomes Proj. Consort. 2015, Raghavan et al. 2015). However, as the price of sequencing drops, whole genome sequencing will become more common. Other recently developed technologies allow researchers to collect genome-wide data at a lower cost per sample. Microarrays genotype millions of preselected single nucleotide polymorphisms (SNPs) at a relatively low cost, making it possible to study genome-wide variation in many sam- ples (LaFramboise 2009). This technology has been instrumental in characterizing genome-wide variation in contemporary Native Americans (Reich et al. 2012, Raghavan et al. 2015). In-solution target-capture methods have also been developed that capture millions of specific genomic regions for high-throughput sequencing (Rohland & Reich 2012, Carpenter et al. 2013). This approach has been particularly useful in aDNA studies (Cui et al. 2013, Haak et al. 2015), where whole genome sequencing and microarray analysis are not feasible for many samples because too little endogenous DNA is present.

#### Considerations for Research Design

Given the array of methodological choices available today, researchers must think carefully about which types of genetic data to collect. Several factors affect what inferences can be drawn and which kinds of questions can be addressed. In particular, locus selection, sample size, and depth of sequencing coverage should be considered.

**Locus selection.** Single or uniparentally inherited loci, genome-wide markers, and complete genomes can all be used to make inferences about evolutionary and demographic histories, but each approach has advantages and disadvantages. Single-locus studies can be less expensive, and because they often focus on the maternally inherited mitochondrial DNA (mtDNA) and paternally inherited Y-chromosome, they can clarify matrilineal and patrilineal relatedness as well as sex-specific patterns of migration and gene flow (Underhill & Kivisild 2007). These studies have helped investigators test archaeological hypotheses about the initial peopling of the Americas (Kemp & Schurr 2010), population replacements (Smith et al. 2009, Mata-M´ıguez et al. 2012, Reynolds et al. 2015), postmarital residence practices (Bolnick et al. 2006), and how kinship affected ancient burial practices (Baca et al. 2012, Tackney et al. 2015). However, studies of uniparentally inherited loci may yield faulty inferences because these loci are more subject to genetic drift, may be biased by the confounding effects of natural selection, and may exhibit patterns not representative of the genome as a whole (Ballard & Whitlock 2004). Inferences can be made more robust, though, using sophisticated statistical tools for modeling population histories (discussed below). Studies of preselected genome-wide SNPs from microarray or target-capture methods may cost more per sample but provide a picture of diversity patterns across the genome. Genome- wide SNPs can also be used to assess patterns of linkage disequilibrium (LD), which contain more information about the underlying genealogies and demographic history than do patterns of allelic variation at individual loci ( Jakobsson et al. 2008). Studies using these methods have yielded insights about Native American genomic variation and population history (Reich et al.

In addition to uncovering these broad patterns, studies of genome-wide variation in ancient and contemporary Native Americans are helping researchers elucidate the overarching popula- tion genetic structure of the Americas, as well as the timing for when that structure emerged. The current configuration has been shaped by both ancient and recent events, and these studies clearly show that the genetic structure of indigenous American populations reflects genetic con- tributions from non-Native sources following European contact (discussed in more detail below; Reich et al. 2012; Gravel et al. 2013; Moreno-Estrada et al. 2013, 2014; Verdu et al. 2014). Be- cause these contributions have altered the genetic structure of some populations, obscuring the patterns that existed previously (Hunley & Healy 2011), researchers have identified and masked chromosomal segments from non-Native sources in contemporary indigenous samples to help re- construct precontact population relationships in the Americas (Reich et al. 2012). Additional genetic substructure has been identified in North America, differentiating Eskimo-Aleut and Athapaskan (and related) populations, and in South America, dividing Andean and Amazonian populations (Reich et al. 2012; Raghavan et al. 2014a, 2015; Rasmussen et al. 2014, 2015; Skoglund et al.

#### Modeling Native American Population Histories

Because the genetic structure of Native Americans reflects their evolutionary and demographic histories, researchers have used the observed genetic patterns to develop a variety of models for human population history in the Americas. Native American genomic studies have thus far been concerned primarily with the early population history of the continent and have sought to identify which Eurasian populations share the most ancestry with Native Americans and which hypotheses about the initial peopling of the Americas have the most empirical support. We draw on these studies here, along with smaller-scale genetic studies (i.e., regional geographic studies and studies of one or a few loci), to reconstruct the population histories of this continent. We note that the model presented here is, by definition, less complex than these histories were in reality. We also note that this model is intended to provide an overview of current genetic inferences about Native American population histories and may not reflect some events recorded in ethnohistoric documents, archaeological remains, or indigenous histories. Notably, genetic models for the early population history of the Americas have changed sub- stantially in the last five years. Before the first analyses of Native American SNP variation, a consensus had emerged from mtDNA, Y-chromosome, and STR research proposing that all Native Americans were descended from a single ancestral population that lived in Beringia during the Last Glacial Maximum (∼28,000–18,000 years ago), perhaps moving there from south-central Siberia (where related mtDNA and Y lineages are found today) (Zegura et al. 2004; Schroeder et al. 2007; Tamm et al. 2007; Wang et al. 2007; Kemp & Schurr 2010; Hoffecker et al. 2014,

2016). What became known as the Beringian incubation or standstill model suggested that (a)the Beringian founding population was quite small (because reduced genetic diversity in the Americas is indicative of a significant founder effect); (b) it was isolated for 7,000–15,000 years while new genetic variants evolved through mutation and genetic drift; (c) it then spread rapidly through the Americas ∼15,000–20,000 years ago, such that these unique variants came to be present at high frequencies in all indigenous American populations; and (d) this dispersal southward likely fol- lowed the Pacific coastline, which became deglaciated more than 17,000 years ago, ∼4,000 years before an interior ice-free corridor opened between the Laurentide and Cordilleran ice sheets in North America (Zegura et al. 2004, Schroeder et al. 2007, Tamm et al. 2007, Fagundes et al. 2008, Kitchen et al. 2008, Kemp & Schurr 2010, Misarti et al. 2012). Although many aspects of this model still fit the available data, recent research shows that it was much too simplistic. Today, it appears that all Native Americans trace some or all of their ancestry to a founder population that was isolated in Beringia or northeastern Siberia for ≤8,000 years before spreading rapidly throughout the Americas (Reich et al. 2012; Chatters et al. 2014; Raff & Bolnick 2014; Rasmussen et al. 2014, 2015; Raghavan et al. 2015; Llamas et al. 2016). Their initial movements southward followed a coastal route, but they may have moved through the interior ice-free corridor as well once it opened ∼13,500 years ago, contributing to genetic substructure in North America (Perego et al. 2009, Achilli et al. 2013). Thus, the identification of multiple Asian source populations and multiple independent movements from Siberia demonstrates that the ancestry and early demo- graphic history of Native Americans was more complex than previously suggested. It is important to note, though, that current models exclude many of the more controversial hypotheses re- garding early Native American population history. The available data provide no evidence for ancient migrations directly from Europe (e.g., Solutreans), the Middle East (e.g., Israelites), or Australia/Melanesia, and they reject the hypothesis that early “Paleoamericans” with distinctive cranial morphology were descended from an entirely separate founding population (Chatters et al. 2014; Raff & Bolnick 2014, 2015; Raghavan et al. 2015; Rasmussen et al. 2014, 2015; Skoglund et al. 2015). After the initial dispersal of people through the Americas, populations became more genetically differentiated as they grew, evolved, and moved on more local or regional scales (Kemp & Schurr 2010, Raff et al. 2011, Raghavan et al. 2015). Thus, even though many studies have suggested that the initial peopling of the continent produced many of the genetic patterns we see, we must remember that subsequent population dynamics also played a critical role in shaping the genetic landscape of each geographic region. For example, in the North American Arctic, genome-wide diversity patterns stem more from successive migrations and population replacements in the last ∼4,500 years than from earlier population histories. The early inhabitants of Alaska exhibited mitochondrial haplogroups that are not found in the region today (Raff et al. 2010, Tackney et al. 2015), and coalescent times for the current haplogroups suggest that they appeared in the region only in the late Holocene (Volodko et al. 2008). Mitochondrial and genomic data indicate a Paleo-Eskimo expansion across the Arctic ≥4,000 years ago, which may have erased the genetic legacy of the earlier inhabitants, just as later (800–1000 AD) migrations of the Neo-Eskimo Thule (ancestors of contemporary Inuit/Inupiat peoples) replaced the Paleo-Eskimos and produced another genetic shift (Hayes et al. ˜ 2005; Rasmussen et al. 2010; Rubicz et al. 2010; Reich et al. 2012; Raghavan et al. 2014a,b; Raff et al. 2015). Genetic discontinuities are also seen over time in the Aleutian Islands, where the Paleo- Aleuts who first settled the islands were replaced by Neo-Aleuts (ancestors of contemporary Aleuts) who migrated there around 1000 AD (Rubicz et al. 2010). Holocene population dynamics left their mark in other regions of North America as well. In the midwestern United States, for example, mtDNA data from burial populations spanning the last 3,000 years provide evidence for genetic continuity across both time and space (Shook & Smith 2008, Raff et al. 2011, Marshall & Kaestle 2013, Reynolds et al. 2015). This pattern likely reflects demographic stability through time in the region, as well as a long history of gene flow among populations in association with the extensive trade networks that connected indigenous communities across eastern North America for thousands of years. Population expansions within the Southwest, including one triggered by the introduction and intensification of maize agriculture, have been important determinants of regional genetic structure, too (Kemp et al. 2010, Monroe et al. 2013). In Mexico and Central America, both ancient population structure and more recent demo- graphic expansions have impacted genetic landscapes (Wang et al. 2008, Gorostiza et al. 2012, Moreno-Estrada et al. 2014, Gonzalez-Mart ´ ´ın et al. 2015). The emergence and spread of Maya, Otomi, and Aztec culture contributed to regional patterns of homogeneity, as did an increase in trade-associated migration following the development of agriculture (Gorostiza et al. 2012, Gonzalez-Mart ´ ´ın et al. 2015). Fine-grained analyses of local populations have nevertheless added considerable nuance to our understandings of regional histories and their genetic consequences. For example, ethnogenesis in a Maya settlement (Sochtig et al. 2015) and demographic shifts ¨ and population movement associated with Aztec imperialism (Mata-M´ıguez et al. 2012) both had significant genetic effects. Similarly, whereas many genetic studies have suggested that South American diversity pat- terns reflect the early history of the continent [e.g., fast initial settlement and early differentiation into small groups (Wang et al. 2007; de Saint Pierre et al. 2012a,b; Roewer et al. 2013; Llamas et al. 2016)], others have identified more recent events that also contributed to genetic structure in South America. Expansions of the Wari, Tiwanaku, and Inca empires are thought to have contributed to the relative homogeneity of Andean populations compared with Amazonian pop- ulations (Gaya-Vidal et al. 2011; Fehren-Schmitz et al. 2011, 2014; Baca et al. 2012). Recent work has also documented multiple episodes of gene flow between the Andes and Amazonia, with genetic impacts in both regions (Barbieri et al.

2014). Finally, because the Caribbean was settled only in the last 5,000–8,000 years (Benn Torres et al. 2015), diversity patterns there reflect events primarily over that time frame. Migrations out of the Orinoco River basin in South America, the Lesser Antilles, and the Greater Antilles contributed to population genetic structure in the Caribbean, as did subsequent gene flow from Mesoamerica (Gravel et al. 2013, Moreno-Estrada et al. 2013, Mendisco et al. 2014, Vilar et al. 2014, Benn Torres et al. 2015). Additional population substructure developed as regional isolation and differentiation emerged within and between the Greater and Lesser Antilles (Moreno-Estrada et al. 2013, Mendisco et al. 2014, Benn Torres et al. 2015). #### BROADER IMPLICATIONS

This body of research has implications for researchers working across the four subfields of anthro- pology as well as in other disciplines. Studies of Native American genomic variation and population histories help us understand how evolutionary forces such as gene flow, genetic drift, and natural selection have shaped human genetic diversity over the course of human evolution, and these studies contribute to interdisciplinary efforts to elucidate the causes and consequences of human migration more broadly. Native American population genetic research has helped researchers test hypotheses that are drawn from many different disciplines (e.g., linguistics, archaeology, history, biodemography, ethnography) and are about topics as diverse as the history of animal/plant do- mestication, the impact of major events in human history, and the effects of language, climate change, social stratification, and other factors on human population structure. Understanding population genetic structure and the evolutionary histories of Native Americans is also critical for biomedical researchers who are seeking to identify the genetic and evolutionary factors that contribute to common diseases in indigenous American communities [such as the SLC16A11 hap- lotype, which significantly increases the risk of diabetes and occurs at a high frequency in Native American—but not Eurasian or African—populations, probably owing to the early population history of the Americas (SIGMA Type 2 Diabetes Consort. 2014)]. Finally, this genetic research has ethical, legal, and social implications for indigenous communities because it is increasingly seen as relevant to questions of legal sovereignty, tribal enrollment, repatriation claims, and land rights (TallBear 2013, Bardill 2014).

#### CHALLENGES AND CONSIDERATIONS FOR FUTURE RESEARCH

Many questions still remain about Native American genomic variation and population histo- ries, but researchers face two key challenges in trying to address them. First, to effectively apply recent technological and computational advances to significant anthropological and historical questions, researchers need to become ever more interdisciplinary to bridge the gaps between anthropology/archaeology, history, population genomics, biotechnology, computational biology, and indigenous knowledge. In this sense, anthropological geneticists are jacks-of-all-trades: in- terdisciplinarians who seek to integrate these disparate fields to further understandings of human history and biodiversity. Researchers therefore need to acquire some expertise in each of these Downloaded from www.annualreviews.org. Guest (guest) IP: 104.52.5.246 On: Fri, 26 Jun 2026 06:54:04 areas, but the amount of theoretical and technical knowledge in each discipline has grown so extensively that it is impossible for any individual to master it all. The time required to complete all stages of a large-scale study—from community collaboration/consultation to research design, sample collection, and data analysis—is also more than one person can do independently. In practice, the scale of this type of research means that most high-profile genetic studies in recent years have been carried out by only a few large, extremely well-funded laboratories with the personnel and resources to conduct large-scale projects (Pickrell & Reich 2014). Making it possible for a wider array of researchers to carry out such studies will be important in the

future. It might help for many researchers to expand their existing networks—to build more interdisciplinary, collaborative research groups, spanning multiple disciplines and institutions. Such groups are also needed to most effectively identify and address key questions about the evolutionary and demographic histories of Native Americans. Diversifying the kinds of questions being asked, to include both continent-wide evolutionary questions and smaller-scale, context- dependent anthropological or historical questions, will be critical to improving our understanding of Native American population histories. The second key challenge facing researchers interested in Native American genomics is the legacy of distrust that persists in many Native American communities, following decades of un- ethical and exploitative research on indigenous populations. Perhaps the best known case involves the Havasupai Tribe’s 2004 lawsuit against the Arizona Board of Regents after geneticists from Arizona State University (ASU) collected blood samples from tribal members with consent for research on diabetes, but used the samples instead for unconsented studies on migration, inbreed- ing, and schizophrenia (Mello & Wolf 2010). Other prominent examples include a similar case with Nuu-chah-nulth samples and the Barrow Alcohol Study, which released its findings about alcoholism in Barrow, Alaska before community review and portrayed residents in demeaning ways in sensationalist press coverage that led to negative consequences for the community (Balestrery

2010). Indigenous communities may also have concerns today about the potential negative ramifi- cations of genetic research for tribal enrollment issues, federal or state recognition, and territorial or repatriation claims (TallBear 2013). This context has made many Native communities wary of participating in genetic studies, and some, like the Navajo, have implemented formal moratoriums on genetic research (Navajo Nation Counc. 2002). The 2010 settlement of the Havasupai case, in which ASU admitted wrongdoing, also made many geneticists wary of conducting research with indigenous communities in North America, and some have responded by simply pursuing research opportunities elsewhere. How- ever, some communities do wish to participate in genetic research (Bardill 2014, Tsosie 2015), and the geneticists’ response may deny those communities the potential benefits of research. The solution, we would argue, is not to abandon research with indigenous communities, but instead to build collaborative research partnerships with the communities who want genetic research to be performed. In pursuing such partnerships, researchers must recognize that Native individuals and commu- nities hold diverse perspectives on genetic research and what questions should be studied. Some, such as the Aleuts and the Inupiat of the Alaskan North Slope, participate in research (and seek ˜ aDNA research on ancestral remains) that investigates their origins and population histories, but they refuse research related to health ( Jensen 2012, Raff et al. 2015). Others, such as the Alaskan Native Yup’ik, participate in health-related genetic research (Beaulieu-Jones et al. 2015). Still others participate in research that may help counter the loss of some cultural knowledge, such as one’s matrilineal clan affiliation (Miro-Herrans et al. 2016). ´ Communities also vary in how they approach issues of informed consent. Thus, no single approach—or set of research questions—is appropriate for all indigenous American communities, and it is in- cumbent upon researchers to learn about each community’s policies and research interests before beginning research. By approaching research with Native American communities as a collaboration or partnership, researchers can ensure that both they and community members are interested in the research questions, are invested in the project, and are poised to benefit from it. This approach typically

involves extensive community participation, with community members viewed as partners (rather than subjects) and involved at every stage of a project—from the selection of research questions and study design to data collection and interpretation of results for both academic and indigenous communities. Community members also participate in decisions about sample ownership and how data should be reported. Communities may wish to treat DNA samples as being “on loan” (Arbour & Cook 2006), with researchers as stewards rather than owners of samples, and they may place limitations on what is published or made publicly available to protect their privacy and to ensure that data are used only for consented purposes. If, for example, a community feels it would be detrimental to report the community name, anonymity should be respected, with perhaps only the general geographic region (ex. “southeastern United States”) reported instead. It can be beneficial to document such decisions, and the general plan of research, in a written memorandum of agreement (MOA) to ensure that the design, implementation, scope, and out- comes of research are transparent. MOAs should also specify how research will benefit the com- munity, where benefits may include coauthoring publications, receiving new information about the community’s history, building research infrastructure in the community, training students in genetic methods, etc. Training Native American geneticists and building research capacity are especially important for bridging the divide between researchers and communities and advancing genetic research, as training programs such as the Summer internship for INdigenous people in Genomics (SING) have shown (Claw & Garrison 2015, Malhi & Bader 2015). Ancient DNA studies should also be approached collaboratively when possible. When descen- dant communities are known, researchers should consult them about possible projects prior to seeking research funding, and they should develop research plans only with community input. When human remains are culturally unaffiliated, consultation may be more difficult, depending on the particular circumstances and history of the region. Researchers should nevertheless think carefully about when and with whom to consult before a project begins. Although collaborative partnerships take time—it may take years to build trust between re- searchers and communities, and relationships extend long beyond a project’s funding period, ending only after all results have been presented and discussed with the community—they can be extremely rewarding. Ongoing collaborations between several research groups and commu- nities in Alaska, the Pacific Northwest, California, the southeastern United States, Mexico, and elsewhere show that these partnerships yield important insights and more robust inferences about Native American population histories. As researchers partner with Native American communities in the future, we hope new projects will fill in critical gaps in existing knowledge about Native American genomics and population histories. In particular, we see two key areas in need of study. First, the continental United States has been severely understudied, largely because of the issues discussed above. It is unlikely that we can fully understand the early population history of the Americas, or more recent demographic histories of populations in the United States, without data from this region. Second, too little is known about the last ∼10,000 years of genetic history in most regions of the Americas. This lack of information is partly because recent history has obscured the genetic signatures of earlier Downloaded from www.annualreviews.org. Furthermore, because of this focus, sampling strategies have targeted individuals who are likely to have more ancestry from the first inhabitants of the Americas, and Native Americans with non-Native ancestry are underrepresented in genomic data sets as a result. Contemporary diversity patterns in Native American communities are there- fore surprisingly poorly characterized, and the genetic effects of European contact—other than admixture with non-Native peoples—are poorly understood. Another unintended consequence of these sampling practices is that genetic characterizations of Native Americans have reinforced

popular conceptions of Native Americans as historical relicts, living in the past and unaffected by the modern world (Reardon & TallBear 2012). Fortunately, recent shifts in research design and sampling practices are starting to rectify this situation (Gravel et al. 2013; Moreno-Estrada et al. 2013, 2014; Verdu et al. 2014). New methodological developments and insights from indigenous research partners will continue to improve our ability to model complex population dynamics over time in the Americas.
