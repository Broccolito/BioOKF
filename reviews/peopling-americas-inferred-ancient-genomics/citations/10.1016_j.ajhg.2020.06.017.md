Summary

Anticipating and addressing the social implications of scientific work is a fundamental responsibility of all scientists. However, expec- tations for ethically sound practices can evolve over time as the implications of science come to be better understood. Contemporary researchers who work with ancient human remains, including those who conduct ancient DNA research, face precisely this challenge as it becomes clear that practices such as community engagement are needed to address the important social implications of this work. To foster and promote ethical engagement between researchers and communities, we offer five practical recommendations for ancient DNA researchers: (1) formally consult with communities; (2) address cultural and ethical considerations; (3) engage communities and support capacity building; (4) develop plans to report results and manage data; and (5) develop plans for long-term responsibility and stewardship. Ultimately, every member of a research team has an important role in fostering ethical research on ancient DNA.

Ethical considerations are of particular importance because laws and regulations (such as the Native American Graves Protection and Repatriation Act, or NAGPRA, 31 in the United States) in various jurisdictions are generally silent on the issue of research (without specifically facilitating, precluding, or constraining it), and the policy debates leading to the adopted text of such laws predated the feasibility of many current paleogenomics- scale approaches. ## aDNA Research

Although the first aDNA studies were conducted in the mid-1980s, 35–38 aDNA research did not advance as quickly as other areas of genetic inquiry because low concentra- tions of aDNA, post-mortem changes to DNA (e.g., frag- mentation and deamination), and easy contamination by modern and environmental sources of DNA made aDNA difficult to analyze. 39,40 Only with new advances in DNA sequencing technologies and laboratory techniques over the last decade has it become feasible to sequence the com- plete genomes of ancient individuals and easier to study aDNA from larger numbers of samples. 1 These recent ad- vances have been dubbed a ‘‘revolution’’ as a result of the dramatic increase in aDNA data collected—in 2010 only five ancient human genomes had been sequenced; nine years later, nearly 2,000 have—as well as the plethora of ways in which these studies are changing and challenging narratives of humanity. 41,42 As the methods for analyzing aDNA have improved, making it less difficult and less expensive to obtain genome data, an increasing number of laboratories have been able to initiate aDNA and paleo- genomic research projects.

Anthropologists, archaeologists, and geneticists have used aDNA to help elucidate the evolutionary and demo- graphic histories of Indigenous populations in the Ameri- cas, including the timing and process of the initial settlement of these continents, the history of specific geographic regions, migration patterns associated with trade networks or the expansion of certain language fam- ilies, and the impacts of settler colonialism. 30,43–50 Other aDNA studies have sought to investigate the biological relationships between past and present-day communities; to identify living descendants and provide support for land claims and repatriation requests; and to shed light on ancestral diets, past experiences of health and dis- ease, and the interrelationships between Indigenous com- munities and other species and environments in the past. 24,44,51–57

Because the methods used to conduct aDNA research can have implications that are just as profound for com- munities as the findings generated by the research, it is important to consider that even technical factors such as DNA extraction techniques and laboratory technologies can carry implications for communities. Prior to con- ducting aDNA research on ancestral remains, researchers need to seek out understanding of relevant beliefs and pref- erences of communities in order to recognize the implica- tions of each step of the research process.

Indigenous Concerns Accompanying aDNA Research and Initial Responses

The recent success of aDNA studies has simultaneously highlighted important issues concerning access to sam- ples, permissions for research and analysis, ownership and distribution of data, genetic determinism (the belief that humans and other species are controlled by their genes), genetic exceptionalism (the belief that genetic ma- terials and data are different from other medical or biolog- ical materials and data), and biological exceptionalism (the idea that biological knowledge is superior to other forms of knowledge). These issues are important (1) because the biological and historical interpretations that emerge through aDNA research can have profound social, psychological, legal, and political consequences for indi- viduals, families, and communities and (2) because these studies affect how the public, as a whole, views and un- derstands genetic research. For aDNA research to be both successful and sustainable, it is critical for researchers to be aware of these and other issues faced by different communities and how they relate to genetics and geno- mics research. 32,58–64

Indigenous peoples and their allies have raised concerns about insufficient consultation and community engage- ment, appropriation, and the potential misuse or commodification of genetic material and the data ex- tracted, in addition to other concerns previously noted. Three examples of contentious aDNA research include

the two-decade legal and political battle over the 9,000- year-old Ancient One also known as Kennewick Man 65–68

and the perceived ethical violations when geneticists ex- tracted DNA from ancient Pueblo peoples in New Mexico69 and when geneticists studied the remains of an Indigenous child from Atacama, Chile. 70 Ethical and legal challenges can be particularly difficult when there are questions about existence or identity of culturally affiliated descendants, as in the case of the Beothuk of eastern Can- ada, where researchers have struggled to ethically curate human remains from an ‘‘extinct’’ tribe. 71

Fortunately, there are also noteworthy studies offering new models of co-production, collaboration, and decolo- nial ethics in which Indigenous peoples are working with, and often initiating, genetic studies. Examples of collaborations include the involvement of Aleut commu- nities in genetic research on ancient and modern individ- uals; 72–74 the Metlakatla First Nation of British Columbia partnering with paleogeneticists investigating the rela- tionship between a 5,500-year-old woman and living Indigenous peoples in the region; 24,75 and the Alaska Native communities collaborating in the analysis of a 10,300-year-old individual from On Your Knees Cave, Alaska. 55,76 In the reburial of Kwaday Da¨n Ts’inchi in British Columbia, the Champagne and Aishihik First Na- tions turned to DNA to identify living relatives to ascer- tain clan affiliations. 77

Indigenous peoples have been active in pushing for greater control of both the process of genetic research and data emanating from that research, 78 an example of which is the notion of ‘‘DNA on loan.’’ 79 Of special signif- icance are training opportunities for Native Americans, such as the annual Summer Internship for Indigenous Peo- ples in Genomics (SING) program, 80,81 the development of guidelines and resources (e.g., American Indian and Alaska Native Genetics Research Resource Guide82), and work- shops and conferences. 83,84 One key principle emerging from these efforts is that in successful research involving Indigenous DNA, ‘‘the decision to proceed [with an aDNA project] is always with the Indigenous group—and that ‘no’ is a perfectly acceptable answer.’’ 85

Anthropological geneticists, archaeologists, museolo- gists, ecologists, and other scholars have recently moved toward collaboration to engage Native American commu- nities. 80,86–89 Through methods such as participatory ac- tion research, which involves communities in the design and implementation of research, scientists have found that community knowledge not only fosters mutually beneficial relationships but can also contribute to method- ological and theoretical innovations. 90 This is especially true for archaeological research, where traditional knowl- edge can inform interpretations of broad patterns and spe- cific events or objects, such as migration patterns or a bat- tle. 91–93 In the Pacific Northwest, for example, oral histories and community knowledge have informed inter- pretations of human oral microbiome and genetic data to help reconstruct ancient diets.

Such an approach entails building collaborative relation- ships between researchers and descendant communities, in which both scientists and knoweldge keepers are both valued as authorities in their respective realms of knowl- 94 edge. Collaboration encourages a sense of humility about how we know and experience the past and fosters ‘‘multi- vocal’’ perspectives, where different interpretations can be critically evaluated to create a more dynamic and holistic 95 understanding of history. This kind of approach not only supports the notion of ‘‘epistemic justice’’ (a fair consideration of different ways of knowing) but also un- derpins the modern scientific method itself (which should 96–98 not decide a priori what knowledge to exclude).

## Existing Policies Relevant to Paleogenomics

The advent of archaeology and human biology in the late 1800s triggered widespread efforts to collect remains of recently deceased or ancestral Native Americans that were unearthed and deposited in museums across the United States. 99,100 Although Native Americans frequently tried to prevent the disturbance of their ancestors’ graves, 101 archaeological and museum practices did not begin to shift until the civil rights movement of the 1960s. 102 Conflicting perspectives on the matter per- sisted, 103–109 and in 1990 the U.S. Congress passed NAG- PRA, 31 which at its core acknowledges the rights of lineal descendants, Native Americans, and Native Hawaiians to claim certain kinds of cultural items and ancestral human remains from museums and federal agencies. 110 Under NAGPRA, culturally affiliated items and ancestral remains must be returned to the control of successful claimants, who may then choose their final disposition, including sci- entific study. 55,76,111 To date, the remains of more than 57,000 Native American ancestors and 1.7 million funerary items have been repatriated. 112 However, the re- mains of many more Native Americans are still held by mu- seums and other institutions, including the remains of more than 100,000 Native Americans who have been clas- sified as ‘‘culturally unaffiliated.’ NAGPRA does provide provisions for the return of culturally unaffiliated remains, and this categorization might change with new informa- tion or further consultation. 113 NAGPRA does not explic- itly state that no further research can be conducted on re- mains or items in museums placed in this category, but the law states that the documentation provisions ‘‘shall not be construed to be an authorization for the initiation of new scientific studies of such remains and associated funerary objects or other means of acquiring or preserving addi- tional scientific information from such remains and ob- jects’’. 114 In practice, many institutions have permitted research—including destructive aDNA analyses—on ‘‘culturally unaffiliated’ individuals without any consulta- tion with local or other stakeholder communities. This history of the removal of Indigenous human re- mains against the wishes of communities, protests by

living descendants, and the subsequent emergence of pol- icies that provide some measure of recourse is paralleled in many parts of the world. 115–118 No other country in the world has a law quite like NAGPRA in its specificity for a process for repatriation and protection of human remains and certain cultural items. However, other countries, such as Canada, have policies and guidelines that allow re- patriation and reburial policies to be developed at the pro- vincial level. Similarly, the Australian Government Policy on Indigenous Repatriation provides for both nationwide and international repatriations. 119 In New Zealand, the Protected Objects Act of 1975 and The Museum of New Zealand Te Papa Tongarewa Act of 1992 give museums the authority to reclaim ancestral remains from outside the country. 120–122 Many countries also have laws that dictate how human tissue must be handled for research. As an example, the UK’s Human Tissue Act of 2004 123 reg- ulates the use of human tissues from deceased persons, although, relevant to aDNA research, its consent require- ments do not apply to certain ‘‘excepted materials.’’ 124

The right of Indigenous peoples to have their ancestors’ re- mains repatriated has been the international standard since the United Nations’ adoption of the Declaration on the Rights of Indigenous Peoples in 2007. 125

Another category of policies are regulations addressing protections for living research participants. In most juris- dictions, these policies are not applicable to research con- ducted on humans who are no longer living. In the U.S., for example, the Common Rule applies only to living research participants. However, the principles of research ethics expressed in the Belmont Report, 126 the basis for the regulations in the Common Rule, provide useful guid- ance for aDNA researchers in addressing the potential im- plications of their research. For example, studies of aDNA could create harms for descendant communities that re- searchers are responsible for considering. 32

Practical Guidance to Promote Responsible aDNA Research

To foster and promote ethical research and operationalize collaboration and respectful engagement of communities in research practices, we offer five recommendations for paleogenomic researchers to follow. Many of these recom- mendations incorporate and build upon the fundamental principles and issues discussed in recent publications 32, 127 by Claw et al. (2018) and Bardill et al. (2018).

Before begin- ning the consultation process, the researcher should first learn about the community and its research regulations and speak with appropriate representatives to respect the community’s time and resources. Guiding questions that should be considered by researchers and communities are listed in Box 2. Appropriate bodies to engage in consultation about aDNA research with the community might include re- searchers, museum curators, laboratory directors, and parks and forests services. Appropriate community repre- sentatives might include governing bodies (e.g., tribal council, tribal Institutional Review Boards [IRBs], or coun- cil of elders) or representative(s) (e.g., tribal president, his- toric preservation officer, or tribal liaison). The appropriate community body or representative will vary from commu- nity to community and from country to country. It is important to also consider when and how consultations will take place as best practices continue to evolve in response to new insights. If consultations are done before a research team’s involvement in a project, then it is the ethical responsibility of the members of that team to ensure that the community consultation was sufficiently documented and substantiated so that those who were not present can reasonably evaluate whether it is appli- cable to specific projects. Although aDNA research often falls outside the purview of IRBs, it is the researcher’s responsibility to become aware of any local community research ethics boards, tribal IRBs, or other research oversight boards. Some tribal nations require formal tribal consultation when NAGPRA applies,

but it might be unclear whether these regulations apply when research, and not repatriation, is the focus. In addi- tion, although in the U.S. every museum that has received federal funding has an obligation to consult with tribes un- der NAGPRA, these consultations might have taken place many years ago, have little documentation, or have become outdated with the emergence of new tribal regula- tions regarding research and ethical standards. In the U.S., aDNA consultation requests often go through the local cultural resource offices, such as mu- seums or land offices, and might fall under the purview of other community oversight bodies. U.S. federal human subject regulations, which the IRB enforces, only apply to living individuals and not to deceased individuals. How- ever, some tribal communities might require that pro- posed research on deceased individuals be reviewed by local IRBs or other governing bodies because ancestors are an important part of the community’s history. In such cases, the expectations might be primarily ethical obligations, but they do raise regulatory compliance issues from the standpoint of community regulations, even if such compliance is not required by the federal govern- ment. Because of the sovereign status of tribal nations, tribal IRBs can supersede institutional IRBs and can imple- ment different requirements with respect to ancestors. The appropriate review board will determine whether the proposed project falls within their scope or that of other local governing bodies providing oversight of the research. Review boards might require researchers to obtain a historic preservation permit or community letters of support. Some U.S. institutions also require research

involving tribal groups to be reviewed by its tribal rela- tions office. In some cases, it is not immediately clear who the appro- priate descendant, culturally affiliated, or representative communities are. In such contexts, it is important to begin discussions with regional governing boards to identify appropriate representatives. As a starting point, study in- vestigators can reach out to other researchers who have worked with the communities. In the U.S., they might also contact the Tribal Historic Preservation Officer or the State Archaeologist. In addition, consultation with geographically proximate communities (NAGPRA 10.7, unaffiliated ancestors) might be an appropriate place to bring conversations and could help provide a sense of who that ancestor was. If the ancient ancestral remains are housed in a museum or other repository, the caretakers (e.g., curators, managers, etc.) could be one appropriate starting point for these conversations; however, note that caretaker-researcher interactions alone might be insuffi- cient if they do not lead to the identification of appropriate Indigenous representatives for discussions about the studies being considered. In the case of international research, researchers must abide by local or regional laws pertaining to research, and these can and do vary from place to place and case to case. Globally, researchers should be prepared to engage in aDNA requests in various communication forms and at various levels. Formal aDNA sampling requests often need to be made via written requests that are delivered through postal or electronic mail. Requests often need to be submit- ted to officials at federal institutions, such as at a Ministry of Culture (often present in Latin American contexts), at an equivalent to the Center for Indigenous Rights (e.g., CEDIAC; see Web Resources), or a Historic Preservation Of- fice. Local archaeologists, museum curators, and/or archae- ologists who originally excavated the site are also typically consulted. Researchers also need to be aware that these governing institutions and their representatives might or might not be in close contact with local Indigenous com- munities, and the onus is on the aDNA researchers to seek out Indigenous stakeholders to establish partnerships. For a recent example of a range of community consultation strategies related to aDNA requests, Posth et al. provided an ethics statement outlining several paths taken in various South American contexts toward obtaining permits. 128

2. Address Cultural and Ethical Considerations Paleogenomics research raises ethical and cultural consid- erations that need to be carefully addressed before, during, and after the research is conducted. Some communities will raise cultural concerns about disturbing ancestors through the destruction of remains or the desecration of sacred sites. Community members might become more comfortable with research if their concerns are addressed, for example by using the smallest amount of material necessary to extract sufficient aDNA for analysis or by ex- plaining how the results will link the community with
other groups worldwide. Results could have political rami- fications, especially if they could be misconstrued to sug- gest that certain groups are not Indigenous to the re- gion—an outcome that could threaten sovereignty rights. Researchers should inquire about, listen to, and work to understand the potential ramifications of the proposed research and should seek community input about research findings and interpretations before publishing or publiciz- ing the results. To help mitigate the ethical and cultural concerns, clear and explicit discussions are needed regarding research goals, how research is conducted (e.g., what samples will be collected and by whom; plans for sample destruction, return, or disposal; how samples or data will be archived and for how long; when and how will results be returned; who will control/own the data generated; how will results be published; and how the data will be de-identified or reused in the future), what we can and cannot learn from the genetic data, potential risks and benefits, and plans for research presentations and publications. Overstating the potential benefits of project goals can be problematic, and researchers should be careful to avoid doing so. Finally, researchers must engage the community in dis- cussions to achieve consensus about how the community might or might not benefit from the paleogenomic research. The goals of these discussions are to build trust, increase understanding of each others’ perspectives, and explore whether the research goals would be of mutual in- terest. These discussions should not be focused on convincing the community to agree to the research, as that would be not only potentially harmful to the relation- ship between researchers and the community but also harmful to the broader scientific goals. Nurturing a posi- tive and mutually respectful understanding of the research goals and methods is the most likely path to a commun- ity’s approval of research, whereas a community that feels browbeaten is unlikely to approve of research. If the community disapproves of the project and decides not to proceed, then ASHG believes the researchers should respect the community’s stance and the research should not be conducted. In some cases, it might not be the right time for the community to engage in such research, but, if relationships continue to be nurtured, there might be future opportunities for collaboration. In other cases, the community might remain firm in their stance, as is their prerogative. Additionally, it should be noted that changes in elected leaders and community contacts occur over time and that these can impact the level of community support for a project. Understanding and respecting these views is critical, and continued engagement will facilitate trust and respect over time.

4. Develop Plans to Manage Data and Report Results Like all DNA-based research, research involving aDNA will generate data that will need to be managed and stored. As part of the consultation process with appropriate commu- nity representatives, plans should be developed for the management of data and results. In particular, the partner- ship should agree on who (e.g., researcher, tribe, or other entity) should manage and store the data as well as identify the funds that will be allocated for this. A description of the management plan should detail which collaborating com- munities are involved and whether the data are co- managed by a lab, university, or community. The partner- ship should also consider how to handle requests for access to data from researchers who were not involved in the initial study. Preferences will vary from community to
community. Conversations should begin at the start and continue throughout the research process because the research questions and methods might change over time. In Box 3 we highlight examples of broad and specific data agreements that a community might prefer. Researchers should carefully consider the distribution of results for publications and presentations and proactively address recurring questions (e.g., whether the community wants to be consulted before results are published or pre- sented, how the community could provide feedback, who should be included as co-authors, etc.).

5. Develop Plans for Long-term Responsibility and Stewardship Although guiding principles for data sharing in biological anthropology have been emerging through professional workshops, 139
currently there are no guidelines specifically regarding the repatriation of ancestors from whom aDNA has been extracted nor any guidance on how the generated data (e.g., DNA, sequencing data, libraries, small excess materials) should be managed. Best ethical practices involve collaboratively developing a plan with the descen- dent, affiliated, or stakeholder community before research begins and detailing whether and how the ancestors’ re- mains and data will be repatriated, destroyed, or stored long-term. Sometimes the ancestors’ remains or generated data might stay in the care of the researcher, with the researcher assuming responsibility as caretaker or conser- vator to the community. If a principal investigator (PI) or researcher is the sole caretaker or conservator, it is impor- tant to plan for the future through succession planning. Biobanking might be an option for some communities, although an aDNA biobank currently does not exist. Some aDNA samples and the resulting data tend to stay in the laboratories where they were processed and gener- ated per a formal agreement between the PI and the com- munity. If the DNA and data are to go into long-term stor- age, a discussion about the public accessibility of data and future use by other researchers must be agreed upon. If the community wants the remaining DNA destroyed, then appropriate discussion should occur about the method of destruction (e.g., autoclave, biohazard, burned, buried) because this will vary on the basis of community values.

Conclusions

Technological advances and the increased availability of ancestral human remains together make it easier than ever to conduct aDNA analyses. These trends foreshadow a broader range of geneticists conducting this research in the future, and, as has been underscored by the recent stra-

tegic transformation of the ASHG’s Social Issues Commit- tee into a Professional Practice and Social Implications Committee, 141,142 it is incumbent upon all geneticists to anticipate the social implications of their research and embed ethics within their work. Here, we have offered five points of practical guidance to foster responsible aDNA research: (1) formally consult with communities;

(2) address cultural and ethical considerations; (3) engage the community and support capacity building; (4) develop plans to report results and manage data; and (5) develop plans for long-term responsibility and stewardship. The specific approaches that researchers take to satisfy these five recommendations will necessarily vary contextually, and—because communities are unique—no single, uni- form model for community engagement will do. Globally, researchers need to understand the politics and local laws (including Indigenous community regulations) of the country in which they are working. Every member on the research team is responsible for fostering ethical aDNA work.
