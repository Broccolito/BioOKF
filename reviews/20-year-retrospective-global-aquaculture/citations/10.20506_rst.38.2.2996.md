**Summary** The role of aquatic animals in global food and nutrition security is increasingly recognised. The global demand for fish is increasing, leading to a need to significantly increase its supply. Securing future fish supplies through sustainable production is a challenge as major resources such as fresh water and land are becoming limited worldwide. Aquaculture and capture fisheries face various threats from both human-mediated and natural environmental change, including climate change. Aquaculture systems and practices are vulnerable to such changes. Moreover, aquatic animal diseases are currently considered one of the most important constraints to sustainable global fish production. The need to increase global production, combined with climate change, may increase losses due to diseases. This paper examines key vulnerabilities, their impacts and management to achieve increased and sustainable global fish production. The authors focus on the need to apply international standards, and to develop biosecurity and capacity in aquatic animal health to improve global fish health. Research needed to underpin the development of improved detection and control of fish diseases is also discussed.

## Aquaculture diversity and productivity

Unlike livestock, aquaculture is a highly diverse production sector. Over 580 species are farmed in an array of environments, farming systems and practices (5). Aquaculture is practised in all aquatic environments: fresh water, brackish water and marine water. Farming systems range from small-scale, backyard-type, low-technology operations, to sophisticated, high-technology, industrially intensive production systems. Since both land and fresh water available for aquaculture are becoming scarcer, almost without exception, due to competition from other sectors for these primary resources, sustainable intensification has become a focus for aquacultural development.

Global aquaculture production (including aquatic plants) in 2016 was 110.2 MMt, with 54.1 MMt of finfish (US$ 138.5 billion), 17.1 MMt of molluscs (US$ 29.2 billion),

7.9 MMt of crustaceans (US$ 57.1 billion) and 938,500 tonnes of other aquatic animals (US$ 6.8 billion) (10). World aquatic animal production relies increasingly on inland aquaculture, which is typically practised using fresh water in most countries. Earthen ponds remain the most commonly used type of facility for inland aquaculture production, although raceway tanks, above-ground tanks, pens and cages are also widely used where local conditions allow (11, 12, 13). Rice–fish culture remains important in areas where it is traditional, but it is also expanding, especially in Asia (10). Freshwater fish contributed 57% to the global aquaculture production in 2015. This production was dominated by carp (20.4 MMt) and tilapias (5.7 MMt) (5). The bulk of global aquaculture production (approximately 70%) still originates from the small- and medium-scale farming enterprises (14), making aquaculture a major contributor to global food and nutrition security and the socio-economic well-being of millions of people worldwide (5).
## Climate change risks

The 2015 Paris Agreement recognises the need for effective and progressive responses to the urgent threat of climate change, through mitigation and adaptation measures, while considering the vulnerabilities of food production systems. Recent analyses indicate that climate change will lead to significant changes in the availability and trade of fish products, with potentially important geopolitical and economic consequences, especially for countries that are vulnerable to such changes and that are highly dependent on aquaculture (10, 16, 17).

Short-term climate-change-related events, such as floods, increased storm activity, drought and water stress, can have significant impacts on aquacultural systems leading to decreases in production and a loss of infrastructure, increased risks of harmful algal blooms, decreased dissolved oxygen, and increased disease outbreaks. Long-term impacts can include changes in fish migration patterns and habitats and changes in the availability of wild seed, as well as reduced precipitation, leading to increased competition for fresh water (10).

Climate-driven changes in temperature, precipitation, ocean acidification, the incidence and extent of hypoxia/ hypercapnia and sea-level rise, amongst others, are expected to have long-term impacts on the aquaculture sector at multiple scales (18, 19). At the same time, the aquaculture sector can reduce the vulnerability of food systems and farmers to climate change, for example aquaculture ponds can facilitate recovery after natural disasters, such as a drought or flood (17).

Climate-related disasters now account for more than 80% of all disaster events, with significant social and economic impacts (20). Not all extreme events necessarily result in a disaster, and the extent of their impacts on fisheries and aquaculture depends on how exposed and vulnerable the socio-ecological systems are, as well as their capacity to respond. ## Human-mediated risks

Environmentally unsustainable production systems and practices present human-mediated risks to aquacultural production. Although much aquaculture still takes place in small-scale, traditional, farming systems, there is a clear move towards expansion, intensification and diversification, driven by the need to improve economic efficiency.

Many technological advancements have been made towards the sustainable intensification of aquaculture, for example, recirculating aquaculture systems (RAS), indoor grow-out facilities and high-density, high carrying capacity, intensive production systems. With the intensification of aquaculture, the risks and consequences of disease outbreaks increase, which is driving many semi-intensive to intensive systems towards the use of full biosecurity throughout their systems and practices so as to reduce the risks of pathogen introduction, disease outbreaks and economic losses (24).

Alongside the expansion, intensification and diversification of aquaculture, patterns of trade in aquaculture species, products and services are changing. The global trade in live aquatic animals and their products is carried out for a variety of reasons: ornamental/aquarium trade, aquacultural development, food (live food market), baitfish, capture fisheries development/enhancement, biological control, angling/sport development and others (e.g. animal feed, medicine and health products, etc.). In the shrimp sector, efforts to improve health management so as to reduce the burden from diseases in farmed stocks have led to the introduction of exotic species into Asia, such as the white- legged shrimp (Penaeus vannamei), which has overtaken the production of tiger shrimp (P. monodon) in many countries, including the People’s Republic of China, Thailand, and Chinese Taipei (25).

#### Inherent production and management risks

For many reasons, fish are commonly farmed outside their natural environmental and physical ranges. Aquaculture often does not provide the species’ optimal conditions, thus, cultured fish may be physiologically stressed and more susceptible to infections. Lack of genetic improvement and poor husbandry practices can cause stress, weakening fish immune systems and thus increasing vulnerability to disease, both from infectious and non-infectious diseases. Sub-optimal water quality and poor feeding practices lead to reduced growth, poor health and elevated mortality in farming stocks (38, 39). Pathogens can evolve or change under selective pressures, such as the host immune system or changing environmental conditions; they can be exchanged between wild and culture populations. The risk of exposure to new pathogens from natural water sources will increase as aquaculture systems, particularly ‘open’ aquaculture systems (40), expand into new areas. For example, open shared-water polyculture farming systems in Bangladesh, with limited or no biosecurity protocols compared to closed land-based or intensive re-circulatory aquaculture systems, are particularly vulnerable to disease introduction, spread and production losses (41, 42).

Small-scale producers are largely resource-poor, often with limited farm management interventions (e.g. disease control) as compared to medium- to large-scale producers, and are thus particularly vulnerable. The ability and capacity of small-scale farmers to recognise and respond quickly to diseases to prevent their spread is a significant issue in the sustainable aquatic production process (43,

44). Improving awareness and knowledge on managing biosecurity, early disease reporting and disease control at the farm level, among small-scale producers, is necessary to reduce epizootic disease outbreaks and subsequent wide-scale production losses. Improved knowledge and consequentially management will facilitate more prudent use of antimicrobials and thus lower the rate of emergence of antimicrobial resistance (45).

#### Specific risks of disease in aquatic production

##### Carp culture

Carp are the most cultured fish species group in the world, mainly produced by small-scale farmers using simple technology. Carp suffer from many diseases of bacterial, viral and parasitic origin, causing mortalities and production losses, which are in many instances unrecognised and untreated. Apart from these ‘hidden mortalities’ which cause unaccounted, yet severe production losses, carp aquaculture has been challenged by two important viral diseases: spring viraemia of carp (SVC) and koi herpesvirus disease (KHVD) (46, 47).

The aetiological agent of SVC is spring viraemia of carp virus (SVCV), a species in the genus Vesiculovirus in the virus family Rhabdoviridae (48). Infection can result in an acute haemorrhagic and contagious viraemia in several carp species (e.g. common carp [Cyprinus carpio], silver carp [Hypophthalmichthys molitrix], grass carp [Ctenopharyngodon idella], crucian carp [Carassius carassius]) and other cyprinid and ictalurid fish species. SVCV usually occurs on farms experiencing low water temperature (47). The virus can be spread via water bodies and movement of infected animals and equipment from infected farms to uninfected locations. There are no licensed SVCV vaccines available as yet.

The cause of KHVD is koi herpesvirus (KHV), which was named by Waltzek et al. in 2005, as cyprinid herpesvirus-3 (CyHV-3) (46). Disease patterns are influenced by water temperature, virulence of the virus, age and condition of the fish, population density and stress factors (e.g. transportation, spawning, poor water quality). Control measures such as vaccination and chemotherapy to control secondary infections have limited success, and improved farm biosecurity remains the key preventive measure against KHVD. Biosecurity measures should ensure that new introductions of fish are from pathogen-free sources and that new fish are kept in quarantine (with sentinel fish held at permissive temperatures for KHVD) before they are released into the farm population. On-site hygiene measures should include disinfection footbaths and handwashing stations, disinfection of eggs, regular disinfection of ponds, chemical disinfection of farm equipment, safe disposal of dead fish, and tyre disinfection baths for vehicles before entering and exiting farms.

Over the years, epizootic-level outbreaks of KHVD and sporadic infections with SVCV have been reported from around the world. Significant economic losses were caused by KHVD in Indonesia during early 2000, resulting from massive mortalities of cage-cultured carp in natural and artificial lakes (49).

##### Tilapia culture

Tilapia, including all species, is the second most farmed fish globally. Tilapias are generally considered as hardy fish, with the reputation of adapting to extreme environmental conditions. Like carp, with the intensification of this species’ production, tilapia farming is becoming increasingly susceptible to pathogens that, if not controlled, can cause serious production losses. Some of these pathogens include *Streptococcus agalactiae (50), S. iniae (51), Aeromonas* *hydrophila (52), Trichodina (53), Columnaris (54), and tilapia* iridovirus (55). However, until the emergence of TiLV, no epizootic level disease outbreaks had been reported in tilapia aquaculture. A new virus in the family Orthomyxoviridae (56), TiLV, has been responsible for disease outbreaks in farmed and wild tilapia in Asia, Latin America and Africa, which have caused serious production losses (37). No effective vaccines or chemotherapy are as yet available to reduce tilapia farming’s vulnerability to this disease. The recommended control measures include restrictions on the movement of live tilapiines from farms and fisheries where the virus is known to occur. Generic biosecurity measures to minimise pathogen spread via equipment, vehicles or staff should also be implemented (57). Moreover, all transboundary movements of tilapia should be conducted with a certificate of freedom from TiLV.

##### Shrimp culture

Arguably, farmed shrimp have experienced more large-scale disease outbreaks over the last 30 years than any other group of cultured aquatic species (58). The main species of penaeid shrimps cultured in the tropics are the black tiger shrimp (P. monodon) and the Pacific white shrimp (P. vannamei). Some of the commonly recognised crustacean viral diseases listed by the OIE include infection with white spot syndrome virus, hypodermal and haematopoietic necrosis virus, yellow head virus genotype 1, Taura syndrome virus or infectious myonecrosis virus (59). Relevant bacterial diseases include luminous bacterial disease and non-luminous Vibrio infections (60). Fungal diseases include larval mycosis, while parasitic diseases include the current emerging threat to the shrimp industry, the hepatopancreatic microsporidiosis caused by *Enterocytozoon hepatopenaei (EHP) (58, 61).*

To date, most panzootic diseases reported have been of viral origin and caused lost revenue amounting to billions of dollars (29, 62). Recently, a new disease called acute hepatopancreatic necrosis disease (AHPND) emerged, caused by the bacterium Vibrio parahaemolyticus (36). AHPND is characterised by sudden, mass mortalities (up to 100%), usually within 30–35 days of stocking grow- out ponds with post-larvae (PL) or juveniles (63, 64, 65). Older juveniles may also be affected (66). Control measures currently available include maintaining stocks free of the pathogen through good hygiene and biosecurity practices,

such as the improvement of hatchery sanitation conditions, PL pathogen screening, good brood stock management and the use of high-quality PL. Good shrimp farm management practices may also be applied, including strict feeding-rate control, water-quality monitoring and appropriate stocking density, all of which are well-established practices that reduce the impact of diseases, including AHPND (65).

Since the bulk of aquaculture production still originates from family-owned small- and medium- sized enterprises (SMEs), any production losses can seriously affect these smallholders’ livelihoods and incomes. Although the economic impacts of diseases in aquaculture are not fully understood or estimated (26), direct and indirect socio-economic impacts, leading to food and nutrition insecurity among rural farming communities, are clear and evident (67). Such losses affect the livelihoods of people involved in aquaculture and the communities in which they occur through shortfalls in production, which can lead to food insecurity, and loss of income and employment, as well as other associated social consequences, especially for small-scale producers, and in some cases can trigger trade problems that may affect both fish and shrimp small-scale farmers.

## Mitigation strategies for reducing vulnerability to aquatic

## animal diseases

Producing sufficient fish to bridge the supply gap by 2030 will only be possible by maintaining a high aquatic animal health status in aquaculture around the world. This requires the establishment and implementation of aquatic animal health strategies at farm, regional, national and global levels (68). National strategies on aquatic animal health, notably biosecurity frameworks, need to be implemented within the broader national aquaculture development plans and designed to reach smallholder producers.

#### Biosecurity and aquatic animal health strategy

Aquatic animal biosecurity issues have recently gained increased attention on the global stage. International trade in animals, plants and their products has diversified and increased in volume and is considered as a key contributor to the spread of pathogens from one region to another. Many examples of this scenario exist in aquaculture. Changing agricultural practices, human ecology and behaviour have also contributed to the increased incidence and spread of hazards of importance to public, animal and plant health. Alongside trade liberalisation, the global community is increasingly demanding improved biosecurity to ensure

public health, environmental protection and biodiversity conservation. In the long term, effective biosecurity can be expected to improve human health, support agricultural development, improve food safety, maintain biodiversity, protect the environment, increase trade, improve genetics and enable greater access to markets.

The model for a national aquatic animal health strategy should be comprehensive enough to provide a good entry point for capacity-building for many countries, irrespective of their level of national economic development. The focus should be on prevention, responsible and better health management practices, and maintaining healthy aquatic production systems. A clear strategy is required, and any policy decisions should be based on the best available scientific information. The involvement of partners (stakeholders) at all levels will be necessary, including governments, public research institutions, industry and all interested parties, to protect and improve aquatic animal health. Generally, such strategies should comprise the following key elements:

-	 a reduction in risks of the introduction and establishment of pathogenic agents at all levels (from national to farm)

-	 engagement in early detection, characterisation and control of emerging diseases

-	 a reduction in the impact of diseases, if introduced

-	 an improvement of knowledge on aquaculture management practices, including health, nutrition, water and system management, through high-quality advice and guidance to farmers

-	 performance of research into maintaining and improving the capacity to detect, characterise and study disease at all levels.

In many countries, efforts are being made to improve biosecurity and reduce risks of aquatic animal diseases. However, the required expertise, human capacity, institutional capacity and organisation, allocation of resources and shared strategy between stakeholders is too often lacking. This shortfall needs to be clearly addressed and rectified to make any meaningful improvements in national aquatic biosecurity. For the national framework to be effective, it needs an action plan, and the authority (including a legal underpinning) and necessary resources to enable implementation. Therefore, strong national commitment from the concerned authority is necessary. Furthermore, effective contingency planning ensures that all the resources required for the control of a potential disease emergency have been defined and are available, and that these resources can be activated and deployed promptly. Advance planning and rapid action can significantly reduce the social and economic impacts of aquatic animal disease, as well as control or reduce its spread. With contingency planning, a country can provide a rapid and planned

response to the containment of a disease outbreak, which can greatly reduce the impact, scale and cost of the outbreak.

##### Compliance with international treaties, standards,

##### codes and practices

Within the fisheries sector, a major part of production is internationally traded, and good biosecurity can only be achieved through compliance with relevant binding or voluntary international treaties, standards, codes and practices. There are many international treaties, codes, regional guidelines and national strategies on aquatic animal health (68, 69, 70). The general objective of these guidelines is to assist countries in reducing the risk of the introduction and spread of serious pathogens via the international and domestic movement of live aquatic animals. These guidelines are constantly being revised to take account of the rapid development of aquaculture, changing trade patterns, and increasing knowledge of pathogens, and their epidemiology and hosts. Revisions and modifications of these guidelines should also reflect the diversity of political, social, industrial and economic environments of trading countries. The practical application of the guidelines in the field is needed. Improved compliance with international, regional and national guidelines will lead to the adoption of appropriate practices, assist in minimising the risk of disease transfer and other adverse effects on wild and cultured stocks, and promote responsible movement of live aquatic animals and healthy aquatic animal production.

#### Knowledge, skills and capacity development

Improving biosecurity knowledge and skills in the management of aquatic animal diseases, within the aquaculture sector, is a significant task. A well-resourced commercial sector is more resilient to disease outbreaks and generally finds remedial measures quickly. However, the small-scale farming sector, which produces the bulk of aquaculture products, is resource poor and therefore considerably more vulnerable, and less resilient. While improvements in biosecurity and trade at all levels (farm, national, regional and global) are important to reduce the overall impacts of diseases on aquatic animal production, there are few existing proven strategies and approaches to minimise socio-economic impacts on smallholders arising from aquatic animal diseases (44).

While commercial aquaculture continues to intensify, many small-scale producers, at global scale, are struggling to survive, not having adequate capital investment to improve their systems so they can compete in the global market. However, farmer cooperatives employing collective strategies to share and manage risks are helping farmers operating in geographically defined farming areas to implement better biosecurity and management practices (71), notably in fish and shrimp aquaculture in Asia (72).

In this regard, decentralised disease diagnosis, utilising farmer networks and community cluster management, is a promising avenue that may be beneficial, especially in remote farming areas. Key priorities and challenges are to:

*a) develop easy-to-use, data-driven mobile and Web-* based tools suitable for farmers’ needs (e.g. for syndromic surveillance)
*b) train farmers to identify and report early signs of disease* using those tools
*c) create incentives for farmers to use those tools*
*d) connect farmers with health professionals for disease* investigation and sample collection for diagnosis.
#### Research and development

In support of the sustainable growth of aquaculture production in the period to 2050, evidence-based, effective and stringent biosecurity plans for the management of aquatic animal health at international, national, and regional levels are needed. Despite years of dedicated research and development, adequate information on pathogen genetic diversity, routes of transmission, host susceptibility and immunity is lacking. Moreover, information on the complex microbial diversity dynamic in farming systems is also lacking.

Strong networking between farmers, aquatic animal health professionals, academics, industry and government authorities is needed to support cooperation on data- sharing, common platforms for research, and the development of innovative control strategies and capacity- building for rapid-track diagnostic/surveillance at all levels. These will strongly depend on investment in the training of aquatic animal health professionals (e.g. for diagnosis and epidemiology) and the education of farmers and industry on management practices to reduce vulnerabilities.

Conducting basic research on how pathogens are transmitted to new hosts by horizontal and vertical transmission is required. Research into pathogens of tilapia provide some useful examples: the transmission of tilapia larvae encephalitis virus (TLEV) (73), evidence of vertical transmission of streptococcosis in red tilapia (74), and population transmission dynamics of TiLV in farmed tilapia (75). More recently, an experimental infection study in Nile tilapia revealed the transmission of TiLV from broodstock to their reproductive organs and fertilised eggs (76). Those findings have serious implications for tilapia breeding and dissemination programmes, indicating that they should only use TiLV-tested negative broodstock for the production of TiLV-free tilapia seeds. Knowledge on the survival of a given pathogen in a particular environment and host will inform us on the most adequate preventive measure(s) to take for disease management.

The concept of pathogenicity has shifted from the traditional view of ‘one pathogen = disease’ to the concept of pathobiome, where many pathogens live and interact with other communities of microorganisms (9, 77). A production system is a living super-organism composed not only of the system itself (environment), but also of the host, and an invisible world of microorganisms (including bacteria, protists, fungi, viruses and phages). Those microbes are organised into community structures, communicating with one another in complex relationships. This has been previously referred to as ‘sociomicrobiology’ (78). An imbalance between those community structures (beneficial and detrimental microbes) may lead to disease processes (79). In some situations, under changing conditions, non-pathogenic commensal bacteria can become virulent following horizontal gene transfer from pathogenic bacteria or after exposure to antibiotics that can affect and shift the balance in those communities, possibly leading to diseases (80).

A better understanding of pond microbial structure using novel technologies, such as environmental DNA (eDNA), will help to decipher microbial dynamics during different scenarios (disease outbreak and normal farming situations) and provide early signs of a disease process (9, 81). Environmental DNA will also inform the way the microbiology of aquaculture systems can be manipulated to benefit the health of farmed animals (82, 83, 84, 85). High- throughput sequencing (HTS) applied to open aquatic systems will improve our understanding of the relationship between complex microbial communities and disease outbreaks (9, 77). It will support the development of rapid diagnostic tools (86, 87), and innovative therapeutic solutions and help to decipher host–pathogen interactions and the molecular mechanisms for host resistance to disease and environmental stress (88).

Fast-tracking pathogen identification using innovative portable technologies (e.g. Oxford Nanopore Technologies MinION miniature DNA/RNA sequencer, antibody strip- based diagnostic or loop-mediated isothermal amplification [LAMP]) should be a top priority for rapid disease diagnosis that will inform better interventions to contain and reduce the spread of diseases. The challenge is to define the research needs that will translate into tools (protocols, assays) that will be used on those innovative platforms. There are also societal barriers to improved disease detection in countries that do not have a history or culture of disease diagnostics, which will require training through community health cluster management programmes. The systematic reporting of early syndromic surveillance by farmers followed by the rapid intervention, isolation and characterisation of causative agents, hand in hand with academics, the private sector and government, will fast-track epidemiological surveillance, best management practices and interventions at local, regional, national and international levels.

In addition to the implementation of good biosecurity programmes, there is a need to pursue research to develop alternatives to common chemotherapy and antibiotics, which might include research on the use of probiotic bacteria (89), plant extracts (90), and vaccines (91). Vaccination will remain one of the major mitigation measures against known diseases in finfish (92), in particular, autogenous vaccines (vaccines produced on small to medium scales from pathogens isolated directly from the farm on which they are deployed). In aquaculture, autogenous vaccines are generally more effective against rapidly evolving pathogens such as Streptococcus species (93, 94, 95) and can be deployed and reformulated more rapidly due to the shortened development track and a regulatory framework that is focused on safety rather than efficacy.

In order to better understand host–pathogen interactions and pathogenicity, there is a need to create national repositories (biobanks) of aquatic animal pathogens, using a standardised and systematic approach. In a similar approach, the genomes of hosts, pathogens and environmental samples should be sequenced, assembled, and characterised and the data made available for public good. Pathogen genome data can be used to track the evolution, diversity and dissemination of globally important fish bacterial diseases (96, 97); it can aid real-time epidemiologic surveillance, improve outbreak response and provide information on vaccine development. Open access to host genome data will play a central role in supporting genetic selective breeding programmes to generate resilient and faster-growing animals (88, 98, 99). Realising this potential will require the support of emerging genetic technologies, such as single nucleotide polymorphism (SNP) analysis (100, 101) and genome- scale editing CRISPR [clustered regularly interspaced short palindromic repeats] knockout (GeCKO) (102, 103, 104).

## Conclusions

Sustainable aquaculture represents a significant opportunity to improve the supply of nutritious animal source foods and contribute to global food and nutrition security, with minimal negative impacts on ecological systems and environmental services. However, several factors are known to increase the vulnerability of aquaculture to the risk of aquatic animal diseases. With globalisation and an increasing volume of international trade in live aquatic animals and their products, new pathways have been created for the spread of transboundary aquatic animal pathogens (105). Intensive aquaculture operations often provide an ideal environment for the emergence of severe aquatic pathogens (80). Aquaculture and the global trade in aquatic products will continue to intensify and expand. Inappropriate trade restrictions can cause significant negative social and economic impacts.

Aquaculture, in view of its resilience and adaptability and diversity of species cultured, will be able to respond appropriately to different production vulnerabilities, including climate-change impacts and emerging diseases. There will be increasing pressure to change cultured species to deal with disease impacts and this will lead to increased industry demand for the transboundary translocation of established and new aquaculture species. Building capacity for science-based import risk analysis and the development of biosecurity governance mechanisms should be given high priority.

The intricate interactions between aquatic animal disease and climate change in aquaculture systems needs to be better understood. Precise impacts of climate-driven change on aquaculture and aquatic animal disease emergence are uncertain and difficult to predict due to the complex interactions involved. Considering the interpretations of De Silva and Soto and Leung and Bates (18, 39), it is almost certain that economic hardships of climate change will be felt mostly by communities living in lower latitudes (tropics and sub-tropics) where aquaculture is concentrated and disease incidence is higher (e.g. the deltaic regions of Mekong, Ganges and Irrawaddy). Notwithstanding the

uncertainty about the impact of climate change on aquatic animal health, it is still possible to implement generic mitigating biosecurity systems and strategies.

Future research and development should focus on interactions between climate-sensitive aquaculture landscapes and their aquatic animal species and pathogens, to map potential risks, and identify suitable adaptation/ mitigation intervention strategies. Meeting the future fish demand for 9 billion people by 2050 calls for major investment aimed at better understanding and addressing the vulnerabilities of aquaculture and fisheries globally.

### Les vulnérabilités de la production d’animaux aquatiques

#### R.P. Subasinghe, J. Delamare-Deboutteville, C.V. Mohan & M.J. Phillips

L’importance des animaux aquatiques pour la sécurité alimentaire et nutritionnelle mondiale est désormais reconnue. La demande mondiale en aliments issus d’animaux aquatiques ne cessant d’augmenter, il est devenu impératif de renforcer significativement l’offre du secteur. La sécurisation future de cette offre grâce à la mise en place d’une production durable constitue un défi de taille en raison de la raréfaction de certaines ressources cruciales, dont l’eau douce et les terres. L’aquaculture et la pêche de capture font face à plusieurs menaces induites par les transformations tant naturelles qu’anthropiques subies par l’environnement, y compris le changement climatique. Les systèmes et les pratiques de l’aquaculture présentent des vulnérabilités à ces changements. De plus, on considère aujourd’hui que les maladies des animaux aquatiques constituent l’une des principales entraves à une production aquacole durable au niveau mondial. Les pertes dues aux maladies vont probablement augmenter par l’effet conjoint du changement climatique et de la nécessité d’accroître les quantités produites dans le monde. Les auteurs examinent les principales vulnérabilités à l’œuvre ainsi que leur impact et les manières d’y faire face en vue d’une production aquacole mondiale accrue et durable. Ils mettent l’accent sur la nécessité de se conformer aux normes internationales et de renforcer la biosécurité et les capacités du secteur de la santé des animaux aquatiques afin d’améliorer le statut sanitaire de ces animaux à l’échelle mondiale. Ils font également le point sur les travaux de recherche qu’il convient de mener afin de mettre au point de meilleures méthodes de détection et de contrôle des maladies des animaux aquatiques.

**Mots-clés** Biosécurité – Carpe – Changement climatique – Crevette – Maladie émergente – Sécurité alimentaire – Tilapia.

### Factores de vulnerabilidad de la producción de animales acuáticos

#### R.P. Subasinghe, J. Delamare-Deboutteville, C.V. Mohan & M.J. Phillips

**Resumen** Cada vez está más clara la función de los animales acuáticos en relación con la seguridad alimentaria y nutricional del mundo. La demanda planetaria de alimentos derivados de animales acuáticos va en aumento, lo que obliga a incrementar sustancialmente el suministro. El objetivo de asegurar el abastecimiento de animales acuáticos en el futuro gracias a una producción sostenible plantea no pocas dificultades, en la medida en que recursos importantes, como el agua dulce o las tierras, empiezan a escasear en todo el mundo. La acuicultura y la pesca de captura afrontan una serie de amenazas derivadas de los cambios ambientales, ya sean de origen natural o antrópico, en particular el cambio climático. Las praxis y los sistemas acuícolas son vulnerables a esos cambios. Están además las enfermedades de los animales acuáticos, consideradas actualmente uno de los principales frenos a la producción sostenible del sector en el mundo. La necesidad de elevar la producción mundial, combinada con el cambio climático, puede acrecentar el volumen de pérdidas debidas a enfermedades. Los autores examinan las principales fragilidades del sistema, sus consecuencias y la forma de gestionarlas para lograr una producción acuícola mundial más cuantiosa y sostenible, centrándose en la necesidad de aplicar normas internacionales y de desarrollar tanto la seguridad biológica como la capacidad en materia de sanidad de los animales acuáticos para mejorar el estado de salud de estos animales a escala mundial. También se refieren a las investigaciones necesarias para fundamentar sistemas más eficaces de detección y control de las enfermedades de los animales acuáticos.
