The most extensively studied NKT cells, known as type I NKT cells, are defined by their expression of an invariant TCR-chain (V 24J 18 in humans and the orthologous V 14J 18 in mice) paired with a limited range of TCR chains (V 11 in humans and V 8, V 7, or V 2 in mice) (1). Type I NKT cells are also defined by their ability to recognize the synthetic glycolipid antigen-galacto- sylceramide (-GalCer) presented by CD1d (2). Type II NKT cells, by contrast, do not express the invariant TCR chain that characterizes type I NKT cells, nor do they recognize-GalCer, and they can adopt distinct CD1d-Ag docking modes when compared with type I NKT TCRs (3–5). This study will focus on type I NKT cells, which from here on will simply be referred to as NKT cells. Nevertheless, varia- tions on a theme were apparent in that there were differing roles of the complementarity-determining regions (CDR) of the NKT TCRs for some CD1d-Ag complexes (13, 14, 25). For example, NKT TCR-CD1d autoreactivity seems attributable to a greater role of the CDR3 loop, and altered juxtapositioning of the V 8/7/2 chains results in differing V-mediated foot- prints that can impact on V-J interactions with the CD1d-Ag (6, 16, 21). Moreover, alterations in the V and/or V usage within the NKT TCR can impact on the specificity toward CD1d-restricted Ags. For example, the V 10 NKT TCR pref- erentially recognizes-glucosylceramide (-GlcCer) over -GalCer (26), whereas V 7 NKT TCRs preferentially respond to iGb3 and-GlcCer (9, 27, 28). Moreover, V 24 and V 24 NKT TCRs can respond differently to-GalCer and the closely related-GlcCer (29). Although NKT cells are activated by a range of CD1d-re- stricted Ags, they also discriminate between closely related Ags (termed altered glycolipid ligands, AGLs) to generate distinct functional responses. For example, although some antigens can promote TH1-biased responses downstream of NKT cell acti- vation, others generate TH2-biased responses (30, 31). Recently, we provided insight into mouse NKT TCR fine spec- ificity against a panel of-GalCer AGLs (9). The V 8.2 NKT TCRs were more sensitive to alterations at the 4 -OH position in comparison with the 3 -OH position of the galactosyl head- group, and V 7 NKT TCRs preferentially proliferated to AGLs with modifications in the 4 -OH position. Studies using AGLs highlight the feasibility of manipulating the NKT cell response for therapeutic gain (reviewed in Ref. 32). However, to enable more rationally designed NKT-based therapeutics requires an understanding of the structural basis of antigenic modulation of the human NKT cell response. Although the J 18-encoded loop is identical in the mouse and human NKT TCRs, sequence differences reside in the corresponding V 14 and V 24 chains, which is notable given that the CDR1 loop contacts the lipid Ag (6, 18, 33). Accordingly, we asked whether these differences in the V chains manifest in differing fine specificity profiles between the human and mouse NKT TCRs. We demonstrate that in contrast to mouse NKT TCR reactivity, the human NKT TCRs are more tolerant toward modifications at the 4 -OH of-GalCer, yet are very sensitive to modifica- tions in the 3 -OH motif. Accordingly, our data demonstrate that notable fine specificity differences exist between human and mouse NKT cells despite the high level of identity between these respective NKT TCRs.

**RESULTS** *Human NKT Cells Show Differential Reactivity with Distinct* *-GalCer AGLs—We have previously observed that mouse* NKT cells show differential recognition of-GalCer AGLs with a high sensitivity to modifications in the 4 -OH moiety as detected using 4 ,4 -deoxy-GalCer and-GlcCer (9, 16). To determine whether human NKT cells exhibit a similar fine

specificity pattern, we used a CD1d tetramer dilution approach where CD1d tetramers were loaded with-GalCer AGLs that varied in the composition of the galactosyl headgroup (3 ,4 -deoxy--GalCer, 4 ,4 -deoxy--GalCer,-GlcCer (C20:2) and the acyl chain,-GalCer (C20:2) (Fig. 1). The 3 ,4 - deoxy--GalCer and 4 ,4 -deoxy--GalCer analogues lack their 3 -OH and 4 -OH on the sugar moiety, respectively, and both also lack the OH group at position 4 of the sphingoid base. The latter modification does not impact on NKT TCR recogni- tion (9, 16), and thus, the impact of the 3 ,4 -deoxy--GalCer and 4 ,4 -deoxy--GalCer AGLs can be attributed to the mod- ifications in the galactosyl headgroup. These tetramers were used to measure staining intensity of heterogeneous human NKT cells. Two approaches were used. Firstly, human NKT cells were enriched and expanded in vitro for 2–3 weeks prior to tetramer staining. The results demonstrated some unexpected differences between the fine specificity of mouse and human NKT cells. Although the mean fluorescence intensities were highest for-GalCer and-GalCer (C20:2), modifications to the 4 -OH sugar moiety associated with 4 ,4 -deoxy--GalCer and-GlcCer (C20:2) resulted in only a slight reduction in staining intensity. **DISCUSSION** A high level of sequence and structural identity exists between the V 24J 18 NKT TCR and the orthologous mouse V 14J 18 NKT TCR (44), and consequently, the structures of the corresponding NKT TCR-CD1d--GalCer complexes are very similar (6, 7). This conservation is underpinned by the J 18-encoded CDR3 loop, which contacts CD1d and the Ag, and the CDR2 loop from the V chain, which in the V 11 and V 8.2 NKT TCRs contains two tyrosine residues that sit above the F -pocket of CD1d (6, 7, 19). Despite this consensus NKT TCR-CD1d docking, variations in the V and CDR3 usage can modulate NKT TCR affinity and antigen specificity (6, 9, 13,

16). A greater role in the V-encoded region in contacting the lipid Ag is evident in some NKT TCR-CD1d-Ag complexes (13,
14), suggesting that sequence differences within the germline- encoded V regions of NKT TCRs can potentially impact on antigen specificity. For example, the mouse V 10 NKT TCRs preferentially recognizes-GlcCer-containing ligands, where- upon the CDR1 and CDR2 loops play a role in contacting the Ag (26). Further, sequence differences in the CDR1 loop of the V 14 and V 24 NKT TCRs suggested that mouse and human NKT TCRs may exhibit altered fine specificity profiles. Within the mouse NKT TCR-CD1d--GalCer complexes, the 3 -OH and 4 -OH moieties are sequestered by Asn-30, with the 4 -OH moiety of-GalCer being more critical for the interac- tion than the 3 -OH moiety (9). This was attributable to the modification of the 4 -OH position leading to a greater disrup- tion in the number of contacts between the galactosyl head- group and the NKT TCR in comparison with the 3 -OH modification. The human NKT TCR was more tolerant to modifications at the 4 -OH position and less tolerant to modifications in the 3 -OH position of-GalCer. Within the human NKT TCR-CD1d--GalCer complex, the 4 -OH group hydrogen- bonds to the main chain of Phe-29, whereas the 3 -OH hydro- gen-bonds to Ser-30. The human NKT TCR-CD1d-4 , 4 -deoxy--GalCer complex showed that there were limited perturbations arising from the loss of the interaction between the 4 -OH moiety and Phe-29. Although we were not able to crystallize the NKT TCR-CD1d-3 ,4 -deoxy--GalCer com- plex, we speculate that the 3 -OH modification results in a loss
of the hydrogen bond with Ser-30. Further, the 3 -OH modi- fication may result in a loss of interactions with the CDR3 loop and with Asp-151 of hCD1d, which may impact on the Asp- 151-mediated interactions with the CDR3 loop. The tetramer staining and affinity measurements show that the human NKT TCR is much less tolerant to modifications in the 3 -OH moiety than the 4 -OH moiety. Surprisingly, the three human NKT TCRs examined exhibited modest differences in the responses to some of the AGLs, indicating that CDR3 differences can subtly indirectly impact on CD1d-Ag-recognition, analogous to that observed in the mouse NKT TCR system (22, 24). Our findings show that the human V 24 NKT TCRs exhibit altered specificity profiles in comparison with the V 14 NKT TCRs and suggest that non-V 24 NKT TCRs may exhibit altered fine specificity profiles (40). Our data also provide a basis for understanding the conserved use of the J 18-encoded CDR3 loop in the non-V 24 TCRs (29) and suggest that these TCRs will adopt a similar docking mode to that of the V 24 NKT TCRs. Our results also suggest that glucose-containing glycolipids may be more antigenic toward human NKT cells. In line with this view, it was recently established that-GlcCer was a ubiquitous self-Ag for mouse and human NKT cells, with this Ag being more reactive toward human NKT cells (45). Of additional interest, some bacterial glycolipids seem to use glu- cose-based headgroups, suggesting that human NKT cells may be better at responding to such antigens (46, 47). Although the fundamental principles underscoring NKT TCR-CD1d-Ag rec- ognition are conserved across mice and humans, fine specificity differences between the mouse and human NKT TCRs are apparent. Such differences are important for NKT cell physiol- ogy and will be critically important to understand in the context of rationally developing human-based NKT cell therapeutics (48, 49).
