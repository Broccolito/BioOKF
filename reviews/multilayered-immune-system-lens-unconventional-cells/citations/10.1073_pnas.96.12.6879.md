# Broad tumor-associated expression and recognition by tumor-derived gd T cells of MICA and MICB

## VERONIKA GROH*†,REBECCA RHINEHART*, HEATHER SECRIST‡,STEFAN BAUER*§,KENNETH H. GRABSTEIN‡,

AND THOMAS SPIES*†

**ABSTRACT Human MHC class I-related molecules,** **MICA and MICB, are stress-induced antigens that are rec-** **ognized by a subset of gd T cells expressing the variable region** **V**d**1. This functional association has been found to be limited** **to intestinal epithelium, where these T cells are prevalent and** **where MICA and, presumably, MICB are mainly expressed.** **However, increased frequencies of V**d**1 gd T cells have been** **observed in various epithelial tumors; moreover, MICAyB are** **expressed on diverse cultured epithelial tumor cells. With** **freshly isolated tumor specimens, expression of MICAyB was** **documented in many, but not all, carcinomas of the lung,** **breast, kidney, ovary, prostate, and colon. In tumors that were** **positive for MICAyB, the frequencies of V**d**1 gd T cells were** **significantly higher than in those that were negative. V**d**1 gd** **T cell lines and clones derived from different tumors recog-** **nized MICAyB on autologous and heterologous tumor cells. In** **accord with previous evidence, no constraints were observed** **in these interactions, such as those imposed by specific peptide** **ligands. Thus, MICAyB are tumor-associated antigens that** **can be recognized, in an apparently unconditional manner, by** **a subset of tumor-infiltrating gd T cells. These results raise** **the possibility that an induced expression of MICAyB, by** **conditions that may be related to tumor homeostasis and** **growth, could play a role in immune responses against tumors.**

Cytolytic T cell responses against tumors require the recog- nition of specific peptides derived from tumor antigens in association with MHC class I molecules by CD8 1 T cells expressing ab T cell receptors (TCRs). Such responses are limited by antigenic peptides (1), which must be generated by intracellular antigen processing (2), by their highly selective binding to only some of the numerous polymorphic MHC class I molecules (3), and by the often-impaired expression of MHC class I on tumor cells (4). By contrast, T cells expressing gd TCRs (5, 6) recognize antigens directly, with no requirement for antigen processing or presentation (7–9). Recently identi- fied ligands for human gd T cells are MICA and MICB, which are distantly related to MHC class I but are functionally distinct. These molecules seem to have no role in the presen- tation of intracellular peptide antigens; instead, they may function as self-antigens that can be stress-induced (10). MICA and MICB are closely related and functionally indistinguish- able (10–12). Both are recognized by cytotoxic gd T cells expressing the TCR variable region Vd1. Although the recog- nition of MICAyB appears to be mediated by TCR engage- ment, these interactions are unusual because T cells expressing diverse g and d chain Vd1(D)J junctional sequences are capable of recognizing these molecules (10). This common antigen specificity may enable a subset of Vd1 gd T cells, which are relatively infrequent and endowed with a limited TCR

Expression of MICA and, presumably, of MICB in normal tissues is restricted mainly to intestinal epithelium (13). This limited distribution correlates with increased frequencies of Vd1 gd T cells in this site (14–16), which is opposed to their usually low numbers in lymphoid organs and peripheral blood (17). However, increased frequencies of these T cells also have been found in various epithelial tumors, including lung, colon, and renal cell carcinomas (18–21). Moreover, we have ob- served that many cell lines of diverse epithelial tumor origins express MICAyB (refs. 10 and 13; V.G. and T.S., unpublished data). Hence, this evidence led us to investigate a potential role of these molecules as tumor-associated antigens. As reported here, expression of MICAyB was documented in many freshly isolated specimens of carcinomas of the lung, breast, kidney, ovary, prostate, and colon. Significantly increased frequencies of Vd1 gd T cells were found in tumors that were positive for MICAyB, and Vd1 gd T cell lines and clones derived from such tumors recognized these molecules on diverse tumor cell lines and on freshly isolated heterologous and autologous tumor cells. These results provide a basis for further investigation of the biology underlying the tumor-associated expression of MICAyB and of their potential significance as antigens for Vd1 gd T cells in an immune surveillance of tumors.

**CONCLUSIONS**

The data presented show that MICAyB, which, under normal conditions, have a limited tissue distribution in intestinal epithe- lium, are expressed in many epithelial tumors independent of tumor type. This raises the question of whether a similar regu- lation may underlie this shared expression. Cultured epithelial tumor cells express intermediate to large amounts of MICAyB, which is associated with proliferation and is independent of cellular stress (10, 13). With nonproliferating cells grown at high confluency, however, the expression levels are low but can be increased strongly by heat shock and, presumably, other types of stress (10). Thus, tumor-associated expression of MICAyB could be induced by conditions related to tumor homeostasis and growth. It will be of interest to identify tumor-biology parameters and molecular markers that may be associated positively or negatively with expression of MICAyB. The results confirm and extend previous evidence that indicated a specific functional association between T cells in the Vd1 gd T cell subset and MICAyB. It is apparent that the Vd1 gd T cell responses share similarities with those of natural killer cells. However, although the latter usually are inhibited by binding of inhibitory receptors to MHC class I molecules on target cells, we have not observed similar effects with the Vd1 gd T cells. Further studies will be required to determine the in vivo activation phenotypes of these T cells and to define the signaling and costimulatory molecules that are necessary for triggering the cytolytic response. In addition, a link between TCR-g and -d chain sequences and the recogni- tion of MICAyB remains to be firmly established.

The physiological role and in vivo effector functions of Vd gd T cells are unknown, and no antigens except MICAyB have been identified. However, the tumor-associated expression of MICAyB and their apparently unconditional recognition by these T cells, independent of specific processing and ligand association requirements, suggest potential functions as com- ponents of an innate immune surveillance of tumors.
