## Abstract

Genomic analyses in cancer have been enormously impactful, leading to the identification of driver mutations and development of targeted therapies. But the functions of the vast majority of somatic mutations and copy number variants in tumours remain unknown, and the causes of resistance to targeted therapies and methods to overcome them are poorly defined. Recent improvements in mass spectrometry-based proteomics now enable direct examination of the consequences of genomic aberrations, providing deep and quantitative characterization of tumour tissues. Integration of proteins and their post-translational modifications with genomic, epigenomic and transcriptomic data constitutes the new field of proteogenomics, and is already leading to new biological and diagnostic knowledge with the potential to improve our understanding of malignant transformation and therapeutic outcomes. In this Review we describe recent developments in proteogenomics and key findings from the proteogenomic analysis of a wide range of cancers. Considerations relevant to the selection and use of samples for proteogenomics and the current technologies used to generate, analyse and integrate proteomic with genomic data are described. Applications of proteogenomics in translational studies and immuno-oncology are rapidly emerging, and the prospect for their full integration into therapeutic trials and clinical care seems bright.

* * *

Cancer proteogenomics encompasses methods that integrate mass spectrometry (MS)-based measurements of protein abundance and post-translational modifications (PTMs) with genomic, epigenomic and transcriptomic data from preclinical cancer models and tumour samples. Analysis of multi-omic data leads to new biological and diagnostic knowledge that improves our understanding of malignant transformation and therapeutic outcomes. Whereas genomics and epigenomics provide the cellular blueprint for what *may* happen, proteomics provides a determination of what *has* happened, as proteins and their modifications are the dominant final arbiters of biological phenotypes. Decades of research have identified various driver alterations such as *EGFR*, *ERBB2* (also known as *HER2*), *BCR-ABL*, *ALK* and *BRAF*, which has led to targeted therapies for metastatic breast cancer[1](#R1), chronic myelogenous leukaemia[2](#R2), non-small cell lung cancer[3](#R3),[4](#R4) and melanoma[5](#R5), among other cancers. However, the vast majority of somatic mutations are passengers without specific oncogenic function. Distinguishing driver from passenger mutations remains a difficult proposition and primarily relies on recurrence statistics[6](#R6). Typically, the number of recurrent point mutations, small insertions or deletions in any individual tumour is small, and sometimes none are identified or those identified are not readily druggable, limiting opportunities for targeted therapy[6](#R6). Chromosomal copy number amplifications or deletions affect multiple genes, but the functional consequences of such alterations, especially in combination, are poorly understood and difficult to model. The frequent development of resistance to targeted therapy is due to the cancer’s ability to enlist alternate pathways for cell survival and growth. As a consequence of these many issues, the impact of genomic sequencing on diagnosis, prognosis and therapeutic decision-making has been less than originally hoped. More recently, immuno-oncology approaches have required consideration of the interaction between the cancer genome and the immune system. Proteogenomics provides new opportunities here as well, as informatic approaches can deconvolute signals from the tumour microenvironment versus the tumour cell, in a setting where the mutational signatures at the DNA level have been assessed[7](#R7).

Proteomic technologies, largely based on liquid chromatography coupled to tandem mass spectrometry (LC-MS/MS), are increasingly used to study cancer. The objective is to identify and quantify proteins and PTMs modulated in cancer and relate them to copy number variations, epigenetic silencing and changes in microRNA (miRNA) expression. Thus, genomic and transcriptional profiling provide a read-out of the character and potential impact of genomic alterations, whereas proteomics provides direct information on protein modulation and signalling in response to those changes. Importantly, MS-based proteomics also provides deep, quantitative analyses of PTMs, such as phosphorylation, acetylation and ubiquitylation. These analyses can detect signalling network adaptations driven by genomic changes or more proximate influences, and can provide information on modifications that affect cellular signalling, location, molecular complex formation, translation and stability — dysregulation that cannot be revealed by DNA or mRNA analysis. Informatic approaches that examine outlier expression of kinases, ligases and other enzymes, and their PTMs, can focus attention on targets that are immediately druggable or focus efforts on developing new therapies. Thus, proteogenomics has the potential to provide critical insights into treatment response and to diagnose multiple sources of resistance in a clinical setting[8](#R8).

Prior reviews on cancer proteogenomics have largely focused on the computational approaches for building and applying customized protein sequence databases from genomic and/or transcriptomic data that are searchable with MS/MS data, on tools for proteogenomics data integration and analysis[9](#R9)-[12](#R12) or on the translation of proteomic markers into the clinic through the use of targeted MS[13](#R13). In the years since publication of these earlier reviews, proteogenomics has been extensively applied in the analysis of human tumours[7](#R7),[8](#R8),[14](#R14)-[28](#R28) ([FIGS 1](#F1) and [2](#F2)), much of which has been done under the auspices of the US National Cancer Institute (NCI) Clinical Proteomic Tumor Analysis Consortium (CPTAC) and the International Cancer Proteogenome Consortium (ICPC). Here, in addition to providing updates on methods ([BOXES 1](#BX1) and [2](#BX2)) and tools ([BOX 3](#BX3)), we focus on what proteogenomics has recently delivered with respect to new biological insights, actionable targets and directed therapies and comment on the future of this approach for cancer diagnostics and treatment choice.

## Box 1 ∣. Technologies for genomics.

Sequencing by synthesis technologies for whole-genome sequencing (WGS), whole-exome sequencing (WES) and RNA sequencing (RNA-seq) is exemplified by several platforms from Illumina, including MiSeq, NovaSeq and HiSeq[138](#R138),[139](#R139). For example, WES typically targets ~39 Mbp, yielding >30 Gbp of data and >250× mean coverage using a NovaSeq 6000 S4 flow cell designed for 2 × 150 bp reads, enabling readily and reliably detecting single-nucleotide variants (SNVs) down to 5% variant allele frequency (VAF), indels in the range of a few nucleotides up to ~40 bp and microsatellite instability (MSI).

Sequencing technologies providing long reads exemplified by platforms from 10x Genomics, PacBio and Oxford nanopore achieve read length of ~50 kbp (PacBio). These ‘second’ and ‘third’-generation technologies have been reviewed in detail elsewhere[140](#R140),[141](#R141).

Single-cell RNA-seq (scRNA-seq), highlighted by *Nature Methods* as the methodological breakthrough of 2013 (REF.[142](#R142)), is today held as a gold standard for discerning cells and their states via expression profiles. Protocols involve high-throughput confinement of single cells, for example by microfluidic oil drop encapsulation[143](#R143), where material can be coupled with barcodes and unique molecular identifiers (UMIs). The usual steps of reverse transcription, amplification and library preparation, and sequencing are executed through any of numerous established protocols, for example SMART-seq[144](#R144), CEL-seq[145](#R145) or Quartz-seq[146](#R146).

Single-nuclei RNA-seq profiles gene expression directly from nuclei. Similar to scRNA-seq, processing can exploit droplet microfluidics, but cells must be lysed and nuclei purified with flow cytometry or centrifugation. Many options are likewise available, including sNucDrop-seq, DroNc-seq and 10x Genomics, although there are evidently cost differences[147](#R147).

Originating only a few years ago, ATAC-seq is now routinely used to interrogate chromatin accessibility. With microfluidics to separate single nuclei, ATAC-seq reactions can be performed individually (single-cell/single-nuclei ATAC-seq). The cleaving and tagging of double-stranded DNA with adaptors can be conducted in a relatively high-throughput manner[148](#R148). This is also an active area of bioinformatics development, given the generally sparse and noisy nature of the data[149](#R149),[150](#R150).

Technologies currently employed for genomic analysis of tumour samples are reviewed by Goodwin et al.[141](#R141).

## Box 2 ∣. Analysis of post-translational modifications.

Deep-scale analysis of post-translationally modified peptides requires enrichment. Post-translational modifications (PTMs) are generally sub-stoichiometric, and peptides bearing these modifications are present in a virtual ocean of unmodified peptides that dominate the mass spectrometry (MS) signal and interfere with detection of the modified peptides. Analysis requires the use of robust methods to enrich them away from the highly abundant unmodified peptides in the sample (see [box table](#T2)). Immobilized metal affinity chromatography is an efficient and general method to enrich all phosphorylated peptides. Antibody-based methods are used to enrich acetylated peptides and to selectively enrich tyrosine-phosphorylated peptides without interference from phosphoserine or phosphothreonine-modified peptides. Ubiquitylated, neddylated and ISGylated proteins leave a residual glycine-glycine stub on the modified Lys residues following digestion with trypsin that is selectively enriched using an anti-KGG antibody[121](#R121),[151](#R151)-[153](#R153) (see [box figure](#P106)). Enrichment methods can be applied serially so as to obtain, for example, the proteome, phosphoproteome and acetylome[7](#R7),[17](#R17),[19](#R19),[154](#R154). Serial enrichment conserves sample material and provides multiple ‘-omes’ derived from exactly the same sample.

## The proteogenomic landscape of cancer

Following the release of the early proteogenomic studies of breast, colon and ovarian cancer[21](#R21),[27](#R27),[29](#R29), a series of proteogenomic ‘landscape’ studies in various cancer types have been published[15](#R15)-[20](#R20),[22](#R22)-[24](#R24),[28](#R28),[30](#R30). These and several additional studies[25](#R25),[31](#R31)-[33](#R33) systematically analysed and integrated genomic, transcriptomic, global proteomic and PTM data to further biological insights into disease pathogenesis and to identify therapeutic targets for each cancer type. Common statistical methodologies have been established to refine cancer subtypes, explore the immune microenvironment, infer neoantigen repertoires, examine the coordinated regulation of DNA, RNA, protein and PTM levels as well as proteome-specific or PTM-specific regulation, and determine cancer drivers and therapeutic targets. Highlights of insights into cancer enabled by these published proteogenomic studies follow below ([TABLE 1](#T1); see [Supplementary Table 1](#SD1)).

### Disease mechanisms and biology.

Proteomic characterization of 95 genomically annotated The Cancer Genome Atlas (TCGA) colorectal cancer (CRC) samples by the CPTAC enabled the functional interpretation of genomic abnormalities in human cancer[27](#R27). Integration of somatic copy number alteration (CNA) data with mRNA and protein expression data enabled effective prioritization of CNA drivers, highlighting a strong functional impact of the chromosome 20q amplicon, with 40/79 genes (51%) showing significant correlation at both the mRNA and protein levels, including transcription factor *HNF4A*, non-receptor tyrosine kinase SRC and TOMM34, each implicated in colon cancer pathogenesis. In addition, this analysis separated one TCGA-reported transcriptomic subtype into two subtypes associated with distinct clinical outcomes. Li et al.[34](#R34) performed proteogenomic profiling of tumour tissue from a cohort of Chinese patients with CRC including 70 patients with metastatic CRC and 76 patients with non-metastatic CRC. Mutational profiles showed a high level of concordance between primary and metastatic tumours, but the two groups could be clearly separated at the proteomic level. Kinase–substrate network analysis further predicted drug sensitivity of metastatic tumours.

Another early proteogenomic study involved the analysis of tumour tissue from 77 patients with breast cancer genomically characterized by TCGA[21](#R21). The study provided insights into the impact of recurrent mutations and CNAs at the protein and phosphoprotein levels. This initial study also reported CNAs and phosphoprotein-level associations across multiple driver genes, including *ERBB2* in HER2-enriched tumours as defined by subtype analysis based on mRNA expression of 50 genes — also known as prediction analysis microarray 50 (PAM50) — *PTK1* and *TLK2* in luminal subtype tumours, and *PRKDC* and *SPEG* in basal subtype tumours, among others. Proteogenomic data integration also provided an opportunity to examine downstream signalling effects of mutations in the most common driver genes, *TP53* and *PIK3CA*. Johansson et al.[31](#R31) performed proteogenomic and metabolomic characterization of tumour tissue from 45 patients with breast cancer (BRCA) from the Oslo2 study cohort with equal representation of all 5 PAM50 intrinsic subtypes[35](#R35). The authors observed protein co-expression of EGFR and MET in ductal carcinoma in situ (DCIS) regions of normal-like tumours, suggesting new histopathological markers and possible therapeutic approaches for this poorly defined subtype.

Another study performed proteomic profiling on tumour tissue from 174 patients with ovarian cancer previously analysed by TCGA, 169 of which were high-grade serous carcinomas (HGSCs)[29](#R29). The study showed that the proteins associated with CNAs in *trans* converged on biological functions of motility, invasion and immune regulation. A follow-up HGSC study profiling tumour tissue from 83 patients with cancer and 20 normal adjacent tissue (NAT) samples implicated activation of mitotic kinases and replicative stress as key components of HGSC[20](#R20). Two subsequent studies[36](#R36),[37](#R37) extended proteogenomic analysis to include glycoproteomic analysis of 119 TCGA HGSCs that were part of the original Zhang et al. study[29](#R29), and 83 prospectively collected HGSC samples reported in the confirmatory cohort[20](#R20). Pan et al.[37](#R37) explored relationships between glycans and glycosylation enzymes in TCGA HGSC cohort and proposed three glycoproteomic-based signatures: immunoreactive and proliferative; mesenchymal; and differentiated and stromal subtype. Hu et al.[36](#R36), in addition to the glycoproteomic landscape, explored differential glycoproteomic signatures between HGSC tissues and 23 relevant non-tumour tissues from fallopian tubes. The CPTAC uterine corpus endometrial carcinoma (UCEC) study analysed tumour and blood samples from 95 UCEC patients, 49 of which had matched NATs[17](#R17). Statistically significant associations were found between histone acetylation and somatic mutations in genes involved in the Wnt signalling pathway. Integrating proteomics and somatic CNA data suggested that protein products of genes encoded on chromosome 1q may inhibit p53 activity by repressing p53 pathway components and/or downstream target genes. Proteo-transcriptomic integration revealed a putative positive feedback loop between QKI, circRNAs and miRNAs to promote epithelial-to-mesenchymal transition (EMT) in UCEC.

In a proteogenomic study of tumour tissue from 103 patients with treatment-naive clear cell renal carcinoma (ccRCC) and 80 paired NAT samples[16](#R16), integration of mRNA, protein and phosphoprotein data highlighted numerous *cis* effects traceable through the transcription–translation–PTM cascade, notably an effect involving *YY1*, the protein product of which is a co-repressor of hypoxia-inducible factor 2α (HIF2α) activity and an indirect inhibitor of MYC function[38](#R38). This observation raises a mechanistic hypothesis whereby *YY1* deactivation effectively links HIF2α signalling with MYC function in ccRCC. Methylation of several genes near *VHL*, including *PLCL2*, *IQSEC1* and *VGLL4*, the last of these not previously reported to be methylated, were also observed to have *cis* effects including protein downregulation. Given its role in inhibiting cell proliferation, the investigators speculated that *VGLL4* might be a new-found tumour suppressor for ccRCC.

Genomic, proteomic and PTM analyses of tumour tissue from 110 patients with lung adenocarcinoma (LUAD) and 101 paired NATs with broad geographic distribution[19](#R19) detected consistent differential phosphorylation of anaplastic lymphoma kinase (ALK) Y1507 in samples with *ALK* fusion, in addition to multiple other proteins such as SND1, a transcriptional co-activator and component of the RNA-induced silencing complex, exclusively regulated at the level of phosphoproteome, underscoring their likely relevance to ALK-associated biology. Proteomic and PTM data identified a novel KEAP1–NRF2 regulatory mechanism. Paired tumour–NAT analysis illuminated elements of oncogenesis and nominated biomarker candidates and potential drug development targets. In parallel, a Taiwanese team working as part of the ICPC focused on female patients with early-stage LUAD, 83% of whom were never-smokers[15](#R15). This population showed predominantly cytosine-to-thymine transitions rather than the cytosine-to-adenine transversions of smokers. Assessment of tri-nucleotide sequence mutations revealed the presence of APOBEC and tobacco signatures, as well as dibenzanthracene signatures associated with combustion and consistent with established regional epidemiology of carcinogen exposure. An APOBEC-high signature aggregated to female patients ≤60 years of age and those without *EGFR* mutations conferred decreased overall survival within TCGA cohort[39](#R39), and defined a population that might be responsive to immunotherapy. DNA repair and replication proteins and phosphosites were upregulated in the APOBEC-high samples. In tumours and paired NATs characterized from a cohort of 103 treatment-naive Chinese patients with LUAD[40](#R40), a proteomic comparison of stage I patients with >3 years disease-free survival (DFS; *n* = 36) with those with <3 years DFS (*n* = 15) revealed upregulated fatty acid metabolism and oxidative phosphorylation in the former, and EMT, tumour necrosis factor (TNF), MYC and mTOR signalling in the latter.

In a proteogenomic study of metastatic thoracic tumours from four LUAD and one thymic primary lesions obtained by rapid autopsy from previously treated patients, Roper et al.[41](#R41) found that both APOBEC-driven mutagenesis (especially in *TP53* mutated tumours) and late-evolving CNA increased the heterogeneity within and between metastases. Intriguingly, heterogeneity was greater at the global protein level than at the CNA or transcriptional level, highlighting the importance of proteomic characterization to the understanding of tumour biology.

Mutations in key genes in the NRF2 (also known as NFE2L2) antioxidant response pathway can lead to constitutive activation of the NRF2 pathway and provide selective advantage to cancer cells in lung squamous cell carcinoma (LSCC). Trials are underway to target tumours with these mutations ([NCT02417701](https://clinicaltrials.gov/ct2/show/NCT02417701); NCI 10327). Satpathy et al.[24](#R24) showed that NRF2 phosphorylation, a read-out of pathway activity, was present in a subset of tumours lacking these mutations, providing additional insights into LSCC biology and potentially expanding the population of patients for whom NRF2-targeted therapy would be appropriate. Genomic alterations in *NRF2* and *KEAP1* also drove defining proteomic changes in the ‘Redox’ clusters in the LSCC analysis of 108 LSCC tumours by Stewart et al.[25](#R25). Integrative analyses of PTMs in LSCC found evidence for the interactive influence of phosphorylation, acetylation, ubiquitylation and attachment of the ubiquitin-like protein ISG15 to lysine residues of proteins (ISGylation) on key glycolytic and other metabolic enzymes, for instance in their clustering near enzymatic pockets and at multiple known regulatory sites closely positioned on the 3D structure, hinting at the complexity of regulation of glycolysis, a key cancer hallmark[24](#R24). Ubiquitylation also appeared to be important in regulation of redox systems in LSCC[24](#R24).

Mun et al.[22](#R22) analysed tumour tissue samples of diffuse gastric cancers (GCs) from 80 patients with early-onset gastric cancer (EOGC) (diagnosed before the age of 45 years). This study used iTRAQ 4-plex isobaric labelling reagents and compared tumour with NAT in each plex without the use of a common reference to link the plexes, presumably due to the limited plex size. *CDH1*, *BANP* and *MUC5B* were more frequently mutated in the EOGC samples than in late-onset cases from prior TCGA and Hong Kong cohorts[42](#R42),[43](#R43), but *TP53* was less frequently mutated, an observation suggesting onset-dependent mutational landscapes. Correlations between mutation and phosphorylation were observed in *CDH1*, *ARID1A* and *RHOA* for proteins enriched in migration-associated mechanisms and signalling pathways, including mTOR, WNT and MAPK. This study included N-glycoproteome data and reported increased N-glycosylation of cell migration and immune response-associated proteins in the *ARID1A* mutant cohort compared with those without ARID1A mutation These correlations furnish a handle for discerning drug-sensitive versus drug-resistant cases. Gao et al.[18](#R18) undertook a comprehensive proteogenomic characterization of HBV-related hepatocellular carcinoma (HCC) in a cohort of 159 patients in order to delve deeper into the mechanisms underlying disease onset and progression. Noting links between HCC and traditional Chinese medicines containing aristolochic acid (AA) and that roughly 80% of the study participants likely had received such interventions, the investigators observed a characteristic AA mutation signature dominated by A:T >T:A transversions[44](#R44) in more than a third of the cohort. Proteomic data suggested that mutations associated with this signature can lead to altered protein expression.

Sinha et al.[32](#R32) performed genomic, epigenomic, transcriptomic and proteomic analyses on tumour tissue samples from 76 patients with localized, intermediate-risk prostate cancers. Proteomic profiling identified five proteomic subtypes, which were largely independent of the previously reported genomic subtypes. Transcriptomic and proteomic data linked *ETS* fusions, the most frequent somatic aberration in prostate cancer, to cell migration and lipid metabolism.

In a proteogenomic study of childhood acute lymphoblastic leukaemia (ALL), Yang et al.[33](#R33) showed that CTCF and cohesin, which are master regulators of chromatin architecture, have very low expression in hyperdiploid ALL compared with *ETV-RUNX1*-positive ALL. They further showed that this leads to genome-wide dysregulation in relation to topologically associating domain (TAD) borders, with high hyperdiploid ALL having loss of insulation at TAD borders and aberrant chromosome metaphase morphology.

### Proteomics and phosphoproteomics identify new potential therapeutic targets.

The added value of profiling proteomic and phosphoproteomic changes in tumours for nomination of novel therapeutic targets has been demonstrated by several studies. Comparative proteomic and phosphoproteomic analyses of paired colorectal (CRC) tumours and NATs from 110 patients with CRC identified colon cancer-associated proteins, phosphosites and kinase activities, including known and putative biomarkers, drug targets and cancer/testis (CT) antigens[26](#R26). Proteomic data also associated increased glycolysis with decreased CD8+ T cell infiltration in tumours with high microsatellite instability (MSI-H), suggesting glycolysis inhibition may render MSI-H CRCs more sensitive to immune checkpoint blockade. Phosphoproteomic data revealed a dual role of RB1 hyperphosphorylation in promoting proliferation and repressing apoptosis in colon cancer, highlighting a unique opportunity to target RB1 hyperphosphorylation through CDK2 inhibition in this disease.

Comparison between serous and endometrioid tumours in the UCEC study[17](#R17) revealed elevated phosphorylation of TP53BP1-S1763 and CHEK2-S163 in serous tumours, which is associated with higher DNA damage response (DDR), an attractive target in cancer therapy. A comparison between DDR-high and DDR-low tumours further identified multiple hyperphosphorylated proteins, such as DNMT1, that can be targeted by US Food and Drug Administration (FDA)-approved drugs in DDR-high samples. Hotspot mutations in the *TP53* DNA-binding domain were associated with elevated protein abundance of AURKA, which can be targeted by AURKA inhibitors.

Multi-omic analysis of the immune landscape of 122 tumours in breast cancer[7](#R7) found evidence for an active immune tumour microenvironment including immune checkpoint protein expression, immune signalling and infiltration, and a stimulatory immune modulator protein signature, across tumours of all four major PAM50 subtypes, and nominated a subset of luminal tumours that would potentially benefit from immunotherapy. The study also demonstrated that a proteogenomic read-out of the amplification status of *ERBB2* provides a potentially better assessment than immunohistochemistry and FISH and that the protein status of RB may nominate a subset of triple-negative breast cancer (TNBC) tumours with intact RB for CDK4/6 treatment.

Outlier phosphorylation events in LUAD[19](#R19) point to therapeutic possibilities including SOS1 inhibition in *KRAS* mutant and PTPN11 (also known as SHP2) inhibition in both *ALK* fusion and *EGFR* mutant tumours, the latter amenable to inhibitors already in clinical trials. In a study of 103 patients comprising primarily non-smokers[15](#R15), a subset of females with high APOBEC scores showed upregulation of kinases including CDK1, CDK2 and AURB, suggesting potential therapeutic vulnerabilities in that population.

The clinical advances conferred by targeted therapies in LUAD have not been seen in LSCC despite a decade of genotype-driven clinical trials focused on potentially actionable oncogenic alterations. But here is an example of the ways in which proteomic data can help functionalize genomic data and, potentially, guide therapeutic investigations. FGFR1 has been repeatedly unsuccessfully targeted, but a proteogenomic characterization of tumour tissue samples from 108 patients with LSCC and 99 paired NATs[24](#R24) suggested that *NSD3*, not *FGFR1*, might be the critical oncogenic driver in the recurrent focal amplicon (8p11.23) — at once explaining the failure of anti-FGFR1 treatment and offering an alternative therapeutic target. Consistent with this, NSD3 has recently been shown to be a critical regulator of lung squamous cell tumorigenesis, with NSD3-dependent tumours sensitive to bromodomain inhibitors[45](#R45). The proteogenomic study also identified a subset of patients whose tumours exhibited low expression of p63 but high expression of survivin, a protein that regulates cell proliferation and cell death and is the target of clinical trials in other tumour types. SOX2 is widely overexpressed in LSCC, but considered undruggable; proteomic pathway analyses nominated chromatin modifiers including LSD1 and EZH2 as potential alternative targets[24](#R24). A separate LSCC study highlighted overexpression of *PSAT1*, *TP63* and *TFRC* genes and their protein products as deleterious in a subset of tumours characterized by redox biology, suggesting potentially targetable metabolic vulnerabilities in those tumours[25](#R25).

Proteogenomic characterization of tumour tissue samples from 99 patients with glioblastoma (GBM), the most common primary malignant brain tumour, identified several key phosphorylation events including phosphorylation of PTPN11 and PLCG1 as potential switches mediating oncogenic pathway activation[30](#R30). The authors also observed phosphorylation-specific changes suggesting potential novel targets in *EGFR*, *TP53* and *RB1* altered tumours.

### Biomarkers for patient selection and prognosis.

In the ovarian cancer study described above[29](#R29), supervised analysis of proteins associated with CNAs identified overall survival signatures enriched for targets of SRF, a proliferation-associated transcription factor, and identified PDGFRB and VEGF pathways as upregulated in association with poor survival. Proteomic data provided insight regarding the potential role of HDAC1 in modulating the choice of DSB repair pathways, as well as alternative biomarkers of homologous repair deficiency (HRD) and a rationale for the selection of patients in future clinical trials of HDAC inhibitors.

Several other studies have shown that proteomic data enable better prediction of survival than other data types. Proteomic data were more significantly associated with patient survival than RNA sequencing (RNA-seq) data in GBM[46](#R46). In a prostate cancer study[32](#R32), proteomic data better predicted patient relapse than any other individual data type, and their combination with genomic or epigenomic features further improved prediction performance.

A study on head and neck squamous cell carcinomas (HNSCCs)[28](#R28) focused on human papillomavirus (HPV)-negative HNSCCs, with proteogenomic profiling of tumour tissue samples from 108 patients with HPV-negative HNSCCs and 66 matched NATs suggesting candidate biomarkers to guide the selection of FDA-approved and investigational agents for HNSCC treatment. Specifically, characterization of the EGFR pathway revealed that *EGFR* amplification and over-expression drive ligand-independent, cell adhesion and motility-associated EGFR activation rather than the classical EGFR pathway activity, whereas the latter is determined primarily by the abundance of EGFR ligands. Because EGFR monoclonal antibodies (mAbs), including the FDA-approved cetuximab, function primarily by preventing ligand-induced EGFR pathway activity[47](#R47), the authors reasoned that EGFR ligand abundance, rather than EGFR amplification or overexpression, should be used as a biomarker to identify patients with HNSCC for EGFR mAb treatments. Proteogenomic characterization of the CDK4/6–RB pathway suggested that RB phosphorylation status should be considered as a biomarker for clinical trials of CDK4/6 inhibitors, whereas *CCND1* and/or *CDKN2A* genomic aberrations or transcriptomic markers of E2F activity alone may not be sufficient for accurate estimation of CDK4/6 responsiveness.

As in HNSCC, CDK4/6 inhibitors have shown minimal efficacy in LSCC. Upstream regulators of the CDK4/6 pathway did not correlate with phosphorylated RB protein levels, however, suggesting that the latter might identify a subset of patients with tumours sensitive to CDK4/6 inhibition[24](#R24).

### Proteogenomic assessment of the tumour immune landscape.

Proteogenomic investigation of the immune landscape in the UCEC study described above[17](#R17) identified eight recurrently overexpressed CT antigens and found putative neoantigens in 49% of tumours. UCEC cells with high tumour mutational burden (TMB) have developed several mechanisms to suppress antigen processing and presentation, which may lead to immune evasion. These mechanisms include *JAK1* and/or *STAT1* mutations and reduced protein levels of antigen peptide transporters TAP1, TAP2 and TAP2BP. These proteogenomic alterations should be considered when selecting patients with UCEC for treatment with immune checkpoint inhibitors.

Immuno-proteogenomic analysis revealed a wide range of immune cell infiltration levels in HNSCC tumours[28](#R28). Concordant upregulation of multiple immune checkpoint proteins in these tumours may explain the moderate response rate to anti-PD1 monotherapy[48](#R48) and provides a rationale to investigate combined checkpoint blockade in tumours with high levels of immune cell infiltration (immune-hot).

In the CPTAC LUAD study outlined above[19](#R19), integrated proteogenomics allowed extensive characterization of the immune landscape of LUADs and identification of numerous potential therapeutic vulnerabilities, including anti-CTLA4 therapy and IDO1 inhibition in immune-hot tumours. The paper high-lighted the particular association of *STK11* mutation with immune-cold behaviour, and implicated neutrophil degranulation as a potential immunosuppressive mechanism in *STK11* mutant LUAD that was evident only in the proteomic space. In the LUAD analysis of metastatic lesions by Roper et al., interferon pathways were associated with APOBEC mutagenesis and tumour heterogeneity, suggesting that the immune microenvironment might alter the mutational landscape of tumours[41](#R41).

Stewart et al. defined two ‘inflamed’ proteomic clusters in LSCC, both having protein expression consistent with neutrophil infiltration and activation, but one with stronger evidence for antigen processing and presentation[25](#R25). Multiple RNA-based deconvolution tools demonstrated increased immune scores in the ‘inflamed’ clusters. A prominent CD33+ signature in one of the inflamed clusters was validated by immunohistochemistry, suggesting that therapies targeting those populations might augment immune checkpoint therapy in a subset of tumours. Notably, the inflamed subtypes also had increased numbers of tertiary lymphoid structures (TLS) compared with other proteomic subtypes, and the presence of TLS in any subtype correlated with both recurrence-free and overall survival. LSCC proteogenomics also showed that CNAs of *IL-18* and the non-redundant immune checkpoint *NR2F6* each were reflected on the protein level, and showed pathway-level effects consistent with them playing important immune regulatory roles in NSCLC[24](#R24). In LUAD, interferon pathways were associated with APOBEC mutagenesis and tumour heterogeneity, suggesting that the immune microenvironment might alter the mutational landscape of tumours[41](#R41).

The GBM study used RNA-based immune cell type deconvolution and identified four distinct GBM immune subtypes that were further confirmed by protein deconvolution and by single-nuclei RNA-seq of a subset of the 99 GBM tumours[30](#R30).

### Redefining and characterizing cancer subtypes using multi-omics.

Cancer subtypes have primarily been defined using clinical, genomic or transcriptomic features. A multi-omic approach enables further refinement, or redefinition, of subtypes based on underlying biology and/or outcome. Such finer-scale subtyping in cancer is part of a growing trend that will likely be exploited at the clinical level to tailor treatment and assess response.

Transcriptomic and proteomic analyses of ccRCC[16](#R16) separated tumours into four immune-based ccRCC subtypes — CD8+ inflamed, CD8− inflamed, VEGF immune desert and metabolic immune desert — each having distinct mutation and tumour microenvironment signatures.

In a study[34](#R34) of 70 metastatic and 76 non-metastatic CRC cases, proteomic profiling identified 3 subtypes of primary CRCs with distinct clinical prognosis, and phosphoproteomic data further distinguished primary tumours with metastasis from those without metastasis.

Archer et al.[14](#R14) acquired global proteome, phosphoproteome and acetylproteome data on a subset of 45 medulloblastoma tumours representing all 4 major conventional subgroups — wingless (WNT), Sonic hedgehog (SHH), Group 3 (G3) and Group 4 (G4) — from a larger cohort previously genomically characterized by whole-genome sequencing (WGS), RNA-seq and DNA methylation[49](#R49). Unsupervised analyses of proteomic data identified stable subdivisions — SHHa and SHHb, G3a and G3b — of the respective SHH and G3 subgroups. SHHa and SHHb tumours were distinctly regulated on post-transcriptional and post-translational levels, despite showing similar RNA expression. CD47 protein was found to be enriched in the SHHb subdivision, suggesting efficacy of anti-CD47 therapeutics. The proteomic subdivisions in G3 were mainly driven by MYC activation. Although all tumours in the MYC activated subgroup showed increased phosphorylation and acetylation of the MYC protein, only two tumours showed gene copy amplification of *MYC*. The subdivided patients showed clear differences in progression-free survival, with G3b and G3a at roughly 80% and 20% after 80 months, suggesting that PTMs on MYC are predictive of patient outcome.

A more recent study[23](#R23) performed a proteogenomic analysis of tumour tissue samples from 218 patients with paediatric brain cancer spanning 7 histological types. Unsupervised clustering identified eight clusters with distinct survival outcomes. Some of the proteome clusters coincided with histological types, but other clusters contained a mixture of different types. The craniopharyngioma tumours were assigned to two clusters — one with low-grade glioma tumours harbouring *BRAF* V600E mutations (C4) and the other with *BRAF* WT low-grade gliomas (C8). This separation was detectable only in the proteome and phosphoproteome, and not in the transcriptome, and MEK–ERK–AKT kinases were upregulated in the C4 clusters compared with C8, as validated by targeted MS experiments.

Wang et al.[30](#R30) added protein and phosphoprotein abundance to gene expression data to cluster tumour tissue from 93 patients with *IDH*-WT GBM into 3 subtypes. Although their results were generally concordant with TCGA expression-based classification[50](#R50), 27 tumours (29%) were classified by multi-omics as a different subtype to the one assigned based on gene expression alone. The authors also identified six DNA methylation subtypes, including two distinct glioma CpG island methylator phenotype (G-CIMP) subtypes (dm2 and dm6). The dm6 subtype was specific to *IDH*-mutant tumours and showed upregulation of chromatin organization pathways relative to other subtypes, whereas dm2 consisted of *IDH*-WT tumours with upregulation of transcription and mRNA splicing pathways. Another GBM study by Yanovich-Arad et al.[46](#R46) performed MS-based proteomics and RNA-seq on tumour tissue samples from a cohort of 87 patients. Both RNA-seq and proteomic data classified tumours into three subtypes, but only 25% of the samples clustered similarly by both data types.

Unsupervised clustering of the Oslo2 BRCA cohort[31](#R31) revealed 6 stable proteomic clusters derived from the subset of 29 tumours with a sufficient level of similarity to at least 1 other tumour (Pearson *R* ≥ 0.5). The poorprognosis basal-like and luminal B tumours were further subdivided by immune component infiltration, suggesting the PAM50 classification is incomplete. A subdivision of luminal B tumours was also observed in the CPTAC BRCA study[7](#R7) and was associated with overall survival.

Unsupervised multi-omics clustering of LUAD patients[19](#R19) showed that previously described terminal respiratory unit and proximal-inflammatory clusters translated to the protein level, whereas proximal-proliferative samples showed substructure based on *TP53* status and place of origin. In the native Chinese LUAD population[40](#R40), unsupervised clustering at the protein level defined three proteomic subtypes: S-I, characterized by high environmental and metabolic signatures, early stage, lepidic phenotype (that is, where tumour cells proliferate along the surface of alveolar walls without stromal or vascular invasion) and the best prognosis; an intermediate S-II ‘mixed’ cluster; and S-III, with proliferation and proteasome signatures, less differentiated phenotype, more solid histopathology and the worst prognosis. Phosphoproteomic analysis suggested differential kinase activity between the proteomic subtypes, with elevated phosphorylation of AURKB, PRKCA and MAPK14 in S-III, PRKCE in S-II and AKT1 in S-I. Increased phosphorylation of β-catenin, spectrin and dystrophin was associated with improved overall survival across the cohort.

The LSCC study by Stewart et al.[25](#R25) identified five different proteomic clusters, two of which were dominated by redox biology and two by immune signatures. A multi-omics-based clustering approach to LSCC uncovered a new, EMT subtype[24](#R24). Although tumours of this subtype might have greater potential for metastasis, they featured molecular pathways driven by kinases PDGFR and ROR2 that could be targeted therapeutically[24](#R24). Multi-omics clustering of HNSCC grouped the tumours into three clusters characterized by high chromosomal instability (CIN), basal markers and immune markers, respectively[28](#R28). Pathway analysis associated the three groups with high potential for treatment with CDK inhibitors, EGFR mAbs and immunotherapy, respectively.

In Sinha et al.’s study[32](#R32), proteomic profiling identified five proteomic subtypes in prostate cancer, which were largely independent of the previously reported genomic subtypes.

### Proteomics adds a complementary dimension to cancer studies.

Many proteogenomic studies of cancer discussed in the previous sections have highlighted the crucial role of proteomics, especially PTMs, in delineating cancer biology and identifying potential therapeutic targets. Current cancer clinical practice is almost exclusively driven by genomics, and the additional information offered by profiling the proteome is made clear by the low to moderate RNA–protein correlation reported in many proteogenomic studies. The early studies[21](#R21),[27](#R27),[29](#R29) reported average RNA–protein correlations in the 0.3–0.45 range, with highly correlated gene–protein products derived from amino acid and fatty acid metabolism pathways whereas housekeeping ribosomal and spliceosomal pathways were enriched for gene–protein pairs with very low correlation. A medulloblastoma study[51](#R51) using a super-SILAC reference proteome derived from medulloblastoma cell lines also noted RNA–protein correlation values of around 0.31 in spite of the different MS technology used and the much smaller number of proteins measured. Using a similar super-SILAC spiked-in strategy, Forget et al.[52](#R52) profiled tumour tissue samples from 41 patients with medulloblastoma and found remarkable differences in the mRNA/protein expression ratio in G3 and G4 samples, leading to identification of aberrant ERBB4–SRC signalling in G4. A prostate cancer study[53](#R53) analysing benign prostatic hyperplasia (BPH), untreated primary prostate cancer (UPC) and castration-resistant prostate cancer (CPRC) also showed that somatic mutation, DNA methylation and RNA expression levels do not directly correlate with protein expression, while noting that BPH and UPC had, in general, higher mRNA–protein correlation than CPRC. By studying miRNA–target correlations at the protein level, the authors were able to identify potential regulatory events. Sinha et al.[32](#R32) found that only ~10% of variation in protein abundances was explained by mRNA changes in prostate cancer.

Several studies have explored the relation between RNA–protein correlation and stability, showing that genes with similar stability in both mRNA and protein tend to have higher RNA–protein correlations[33](#R33),[51](#R51). In addition, genes with low mRNA–protein correlations tend to be the target of the ubiquitin–proteasome pathway or regulated by miRNAs[33](#R33).

More recent studies[19](#R19),[28](#R28) have confirmed the moderate level of correlation between transcript and protein, but a study of paired tumour and NAT samples from patients with LUAD went on to show that NATs have significantly lower correlation compared with tumour samples[19](#R19). Furthermore, a proteogenomic study of uterine leiomyomas (ULMs)[54](#R54) showed that transcript–protein correlation was higher (*r* = 0.35) in more aggressive hereditary leiomyomatosis and renal cell cancer (HLRCC) compared with non-syndromic ULMs (*r* = 0.24). Presumably, the same constellation of factors (transcriptional dynamics, translational rate constants, protein degradation, tissue-specific post-transcriptional regulation, protein-level buffering and technical noise, among other factors) accounts for the variation in correlation observed between studies.

A more detailed discussion of the complex biological and technical underpinnings of variation in mRNA–protein correlation across genes within a study is beyond the scope of this Review; for an excellent recent review, see Buccitelli and Selbach[55](#R55).

## Sample quality in proteogenomic studies

Proteogenomics studies are resource-intensive, and careful attention to sample selection is essential to ensure that resulting data are suitable for the intended analyses. As with any biological study, if a specific question is being explored or hypothesis tested, it is critical to ensure that the sample population is appropriate to the query and is adequately powered. To date, cancer proteogenomics efforts have been chiefly exploratory, hypothesis-generating, ‘landscape’ studies, in which the objective is to have a sample set that is representative of the specific disease being studied (or an intentional subset thereof). Increasing recognition of the translational potential of proteogenomics[13](#R13) is motivating applications to model systems[56](#R56)-[58](#R58) or clinical trial samples[8](#R8), in which questions pertaining to mechanisms, drug response and resistance may be better addressed.

Cancer proteogenomics studies may also impose particular requirements for sample acquisition, handling and processing to achieve high data quality and maintain fidelity to the underlying biology. Whereas the global proteome is comparatively stable over the conditions and approximately 1-h handling times typically recommended for large-scale genomics projects[59](#R59), the phosphoproteome is much more dynamic, with biologically coherent stress responses (which overlap with cancer biology) seen after 5 min of cold ischaemia in some cancers[60](#R60). Less is known about the specific stability of other PTMs, but these are likely to be similarly dynamic. Although ischaemia and other insults to tissues cannot be readily eliminated in human studies, regimented prospective collection with rigorous annotation allows such pre-analytical variables to be minimized and downstream analyses to be controlled. The expense, complexity and protracted timelines involved with prospective collection may be prohibitive, however, and prospective samples will often lack the longer-term clinical follow-up and patient outcome data required for certain translational analyses. Retrospective sample collections allow timely access and often include follow-up data, but unless collected under a strict SOP with tightly controlled ischaemic time may not be suitable for PTM analysis. Unfortunately, robust and uniform sample quality control standards (analogous to the RNA Integrity Number for RNA analyses) have not yet been developed for proteomic and phosphoproteomic analysis. Whatever strategy is used for sample acquisition, processing and reagent incompatibilities will often require that separate tissue portions be allocated for genomic and proteomic analyses. The use of different tumour fragments, as was done in the earliest CPTAC studies, in which TCGA genomics data were being leveraged[21](#R21),[27](#R27),[29](#R29), is susceptible to variation due to intratumoural heterogeneity. Methods developed to address this include the use of aliquots of tissues cryo-pulverized for uniformity[61](#R61) or of alternating serial sections through a tissue core[8](#R8).

The inevitable inclusion in tumour specimens of stromal tissues, infiltrating immune cells and other cell types that are not malignant epithelium also requires consideration. This applies equally to the vast majority of cancer genomic and transcriptomic studies to date, and a case can be made that those other cell types are instrumental to tumour biology and are appropriately included in the analysis (although generally with the expedient of specifying a certain minimum tumour epithelial enrichment for inclusion in the study). Nevertheless, there are occasions in which a more specific focus on tumour epithelium is desired. Some studies have directly analysed the extracellular matrix (ECM) to better parse contributions of tumour epithelial and stromal cells[62](#R62), and efforts are underway to develop protein-based tissue deconvolution strategies (non-peer-reviewed data presented in Petralia et al.[63](#R63)) that parallel or refine those using RNA. More direct approaches using preparative techniques such as laser-capture microdissection have also been employed to isolate tumour epithelium and other compartments of interest[64](#R64)-[70](#R70).

## Computational methods and tools

Computational tools used to process raw genomics data to identify somatic and germline mutations, quantify copy number aberrations and perform transcriptomic profiling typically encompass various different components[71](#R71),[72](#R72) for read alignment[73](#R73)-[75](#R75), quality control[76](#R76),[77](#R77), variant identification[78](#R78)-[80](#R80) and RNA quantification[81](#R81),[82](#R82), and assemble them into comprehensive pipelines following established guidelines for best practices[83](#R83),[84](#R84) such as the Genome Analysis Toolkit (GATK)[85](#R85),[86](#R86) or the Genomic Data Commons (GDC) pipelines[87](#R87). Computational tools used to analyse raw LC-MS/MS proteomic data to produce identified and quantified proteins, phosphosites, acetyl sites and ubiquityl sites include MS-GF+[88](#R88), Spectrum Mill, MaxQuant[89](#R89), MSFragger[90](#R90), Philosopher[91](#R91), CDAP[92](#R92) and many others[93](#R93). A particular challenge in proteomics stems from the larger extent of missing values in data matrices due to the stochastic process of peptide sampling and limited dynamic range of measurements compared with, for example, RNA-seq, especially in PTM data sets where measurements are based on single peptides that are not readily observed across all samples. Various imputation strategies for proteomic data have been proposed[89](#R89) to address the problem computationally, including a multi-strategy approach (non-peer-reviewed preprint[94](#R94)). Alternative MS acquisition strategies such as DIA inherently help mitigate the problem of missing data.

Combining multi-omics data sets from different cohorts for pan-cancer analyses poses additional challenges. Workflows relying on multiplexed quantification strategies ([FIG. 3](#F3)) require a cohort-specific common reference sample (typically a mixture of all tumours) that is measured in each plex and used to provide a measure of abundance for each protein, peptide or PTM site relative to the cohort-specific common reference. Thus, careful attention will be required when combining these data sets computationally to correct for any cohort-specific signal in the data sets (using batch correction or similar computational approaches) as cancer type and data set are often overlapping confounding variables. Some CPTAC studies have exchanged and incorporated common reference samples from other analyses into their study design to provide experimentally determined ‘correction factors’ between the respective data sets. However, this approach is logistically challenging and cannot be applied to combine data sets retrospectively.

Integrated proteogenomic data analysis draws upon methods and algorithms from a range of disciplines spanning statistics, machine learning and big-data analysis. [BOX 3](#BX3) and [Supplementary Table 2](#SD2) summarize a selection of tools for computational analysis of proteogenomics data. In Ruggles et al.[12](#R12), proteogenomic data analysis methods are grouped into three major categories — sequence-centric methods, analysis of proteogenomic relationships and integrative modelling — as described in greater detail below.

Sequence-centric approaches to proteogenomics focus on linking genomic, transcriptomic and proteomic sequences for gene annotation and refinement. Increasingly, personalized protein sequence databases derived from the genome, exome and transcriptome are being used to identify proteins with single amino acid variants (SAAVs), insertions, deletions, alternative splicing and gene fusions unique to specific cancer samples. customProDB[95](#R95) and QUILTS[96](#R96) are commonly used and well-maintained tools for generation of personalized protein sequence databases. These tools start with standard output from tools used to process genomic and transcriptomic data, and output personalized databases that can be used with MS search software to identify variant peptides.

In previous studies, only a small fraction of genomic variants have been identified as variant peptides in cancer samples[21](#R21),[27](#R27). This is attributed to low sequence coverage of proteins, low abundance of mutated peptides and the artefacts of using trypsin as the predominant digestion enzyme.

Analysis of proteogenomic relationships primarily involves the application of correlation analysis to derive insight into cancer biology. Correlative analyses include quantitative trait locus (QTL) mapping such as eQTLs (mRNA versus DNA variants), rQTLs (ribosome occupancy versus DNA variants) and pQTLs (protein abundance versus DNA variants). The impact of CNA on mRNA and protein abundance can also be estimated using correlation analysis. Integrative correlation analysis can span different omics types and provide insights relating the genome, transcriptome and proteome to miRNA expression, DNA methylation and circular RNA. The critical work of relating mutations to PTMs and signalling in cancer is also a major application of correlative analysis.

Integrative modelling involves applying statistical and machine learning tools to one or more types of omics data, primarily for class discovery or class prediction. Clustering is one of the most commonly used methods for class discovery, and is applied in an unsupervised manner with the goal of discovering subgroups in cancer cohorts. Whereas earlier studies used *k*-means or hierarchical clustering-based consensus clustering applied primarily to proteome or PTM data[21](#R21), more recent studies have used multi-omic clustering based on non-negative matrix factorization (NMF)[7](#R7),[19](#R19),[24](#R24),[28](#R28),[30](#R30). Principal component analysis (PCA) is widely used to visualize sample groups, identify phenotypic features driving the separation of samples and detect batch effects.

Predictive modelling has been used to accomplish a wide range of analyses from data preprocessing to high-level analysis. Methods such as COMBAT[97](#R97) and linear models[98](#R98) can correct batch effects in data sets. Linear models — from simple linear regression and *t* tests to more complex and multi-omics approaches such as iProFun[99](#R99) — have been used for normalizing PTM data to account for protein levels, differential marker selection, *cis* regulations of genes across RNA, protein and PTMs, comparative analysis of tumours and normal tissues, and a host of other analyses.

Many analysis approaches, such as correlative analysis and differential marker selection, yield lists of genes, proteins and PTM sites. To make biological sense of these lists, functional enrichment analysis and network analysis are used. Common methods for enrichment analysis include the Fisher test and gene set enrichment analysis (GSEA)[100](#R100),[101](#R101). Historically, enrichment analysis has been performed at the level of gene symbols. With the availability of high-quality PTM data from proteogenomic studies, it is increasingly important to perform enrichment analysis at the PTM-site level, and tools such as PTM-SEA[102](#R102) enable this.

Although simple and well-established methods such as clustering, correlation and linear models applied to proteogenomics data can be used to make significant inroads towards biological understanding, more recent methods such as deep learning are enabling new applications that were previously infeasible. One such area is the use of deep learning for image analysis[103](#R103), exemplified by the highly accurate prediction of *STK11* mutation status in LUAD from conventional haematoxylin and eosin-stained slides of tumour sections[19](#R19). Combining these approaches with image archives such as The Cancer Imaging Archive enables the use of images as an added dimension of multi-omic analysis. Deep learning applications in proteomics, including retention time prediction, MS/MS spectrum prediction, de novo peptide sequencing, PTM prediction, MHC–peptide binding prediction and protein structure prediction, have been summarized in a recent review[104](#R104).

## Towards clinical application

To address each clinical hypothesis and to move towards clinical utility, rapidly frozen samples are required from cohorts of patients uniformly treated in a way that facilitates precise and focused discovery research. In the future, samples from phase III clinical trials will be necessary to generate level one evidence for changes in clinical practice based on proteogenomic principles. However, this will require a commitment to accrue the right specimens, including carefully curated and annotated clinical metadata to address relevant translational objectives. A major step forward is the introduction of a microscaled TMT-based discovery approach using modest amounts of protein (for example, 50 μg) extracted from OCT-embedded core needle biopsies that enables proteome, including PTMs, to be obtained from the same cores as RNA and DNA[8](#R8). This is a critical next step because in locally advanced or metastatic cancer, systemic treatment typically follows the diagnosis, not surgery, which means that a core needle sample is the only opportunity to obtain tumour tissue in the unperturbed state.

Once a discovery experiment has been completed, supervised and unsupervised proteogenomic analyses are conducted against the primary end point of the study (which is typically response to therapy) and biological and clinical hypotheses are developed. Verification of these hypotheses, the critical next step, will likely involve use of targeted, MS-based assays or other multiplexed immunoassays provided that suitable reagents are available.

There are numerous barriers to the execution of this plan. The accrual of OCT-embedded material is not routine, and specimens often do not contain adequate tumour content or yield sufficient material for multi-omic analysis, particularly for PTMs. Multiple efforts are underway to address these issues, such as accrual of frozen specimens during routine diagnostic procedures, further microscaling efforts that require less peptide and an examination of proteins from formalin-fixed tissues[64](#R64),[105](#R105) to assess which proteins and PTMs might be accessible in routinely collected material. Robust methods for RNA and DNA sequence analysis at the single-cell level with throughput sufficient to sequence hundreds to many thousands of single cells has arrived[106](#R106). These capabilities are anticipated to provide an entirely new level of detail to our understanding of how malignancy and resistance to therapy develop and the dynamics of the tumour environment. Proteogenomic methods will lag as methods able to provide approximately 1,000 proteins from single cells are just beginning to emerge, and are not yet robust or routine[107](#R107)-[109](#R109). Further improvements in sample processing, handling, chromatography and MS instrumentation will soon enable true single-cell proteomics at reasonable throughput, thereby restoring the benefits of integrating proteomic data with genomics data for understanding cancer.

## Conclusions

Although the proteogenomic analysis of hundreds of tumours for individual cancers and thousands of tumours in aggregate represents a substantial scientific achievement associated with a vast amount of molecular data, proteogenomics is still an emerging field. As with the early days of genomics, bulk analyses of hundreds of tumours are likely to yield important and actionable insights into cancer biology, but much larger numbers will be needed to identify oncogenic processes that are less common or have smaller effect size, and different approaches will be needed to explore proteogenomic intratumoural heterogeneity. The increasing international participation in technically sophisticated proteogenomics studies will be an important asset in this expansion, ensuring broader representation of ethnicity and environment, and thus better sampling the proteogenomic diversity of cancer. PTM analyses may hold the greatest promise for exposing the nuanced regulation of cancer-relevant pathways, but the dynamic nature of those modifications and their vulnerability to pre-analytical variables such as ischaemia[60](#R60) mean that those analyses are likely to be imperfect reflections of endogenous biology. Every biological observation and therapeutic hypothesis proffered in the current suite of proteogenomic landscape studies require further investigation and validation. Nevertheless, it is striking how many of these there have been, and how often the addition of proteomic and PTM characterization has focused genomic and transcriptomic findings into specific, testable hypotheses. This evolution is likely to continue for the foreseeable future, as more tumours and cancer types are proteogenomically analysed, more PTMs are examined, pan-cancer proteogenomic studies gain traction and proteogenomic techniques are increasingly applied to specific clinical questions in translational contexts.

Ultimately, as these approaches and technologies mature, we expect proteogenomics to become a more integral part of cancer diagnosis and treatment, providing precisely targeted treatment at an individual patient level, harnessing the combined potential of genomic and proteomic data to develop a holistic picture of that patient’s cancer[110](#R110).
