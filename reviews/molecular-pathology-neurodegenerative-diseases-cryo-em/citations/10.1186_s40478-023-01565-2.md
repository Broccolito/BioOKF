# Identical tau flaments in subacute sclerosing panencephalitis and chronic traumatic

# encephalopathy

1† 2† 3,4 Chao Qi , Masato Hasegawa , Masaki Takao , Motoko Sakai5, Mayasuki Sasaki6, Masashi Mizutani3, 1* 1* and Michel Goedert Akio Akagi7, Yasushi Iwasaki7, Hiroaki Miyahara7, Mari Yoshida7, Sjors H. W. Scheres

**Abstract** Subacute sclerosing panencephalitis (SSPE) occurs in some individuals after measles infection, following a symptom- free period of several years. It resembles chronic traumatic encephalopathy (CTE), which happens after repetitive head impacts or exposure to blast waves, following a symptom-free period. As in CTE, the neurofbrillary changes of SSPE are concentrated in superfcial cortical layers. Here we used electron cryo-microscopy (cryo-EM) of tau flaments from two cases of SSPE to show that the tau folds of SSPE and CTE are identical. Two types of flaments were each made of two identical protoflaments with an extra density in the β-helix region. Like in CTE, the vast majority of tau flaments were Type I, with a minority of Type II flaments. These fndings suggest that the CTE tau fold can be caused by difer- ent environmental insults, which may be linked by infammatory changes. **Keywords Tau, Subacute sclerosing panencephalitis, Chronic traumatic encephalopathy, Infammation, Electron** cryo-microscopy

**Introduction** Subacute sclerosing panencephalitis (SSPE) is a fatal dis- order of the central nervous system that occurs follow- ing infection with measles virus and manifests itself after a symptom-free period of several years [1]. It occurs in approximately 1 in 75,000 cases of measles [2]. Te neu- ropathology of SSPE is characterized by severe nerve cell loss, demyelination, perivascular lymphocytic infltra- tions and viral intranuclear inclusion bodies. By silver staining, abundant neurofbrillary tangles are present in cerebral cortex and other brain regions in a proportion of cases [3, 4]. Tey have been reported to be made of paired helical flaments like those from Alzheimer’s dis- ease brains [5, 6] and stain for abnormally phosphoryl- ated tau and ubiquitin [7]. Tangle-bearing cases of SSPE have mostly a long disease duration [8] and in a recent immunohistochemical study, tau pathology was found in all cases of SSPE [9].

Tauopathy has been inferred to result from difuse brain infammation triggered by infection with measles virus and not from a direct efect of the virus [9]. Neu- rofbrillary tangles of SSPE stain with antibodies specifc for 3R and 4R tau, like the tau inclusions of primary age- related tauopathy (PART), Alzheimer’s disease (AD) and chronic traumatic encephalopathy (CTE) [10]. Unlike PART and AD, but like CTE, the neurofbrillary tangles of SSPE are present in superfcial cortical layers [9, 11]. We previously used electron cryo-microscopy (cryo- EM) to determine the atomic structures of tau fla- ments from a number of neurodegenerative conditions, which has resulted in a structure-based classifcation of tauopathies [12]. We showed that the tau flament folds of 3R + 4R tauopathies separate into two groups, the frst of which is formed by PART, AD, and familial British and Danish dementias (FBD and FDD), and the second by CTE. Te neurofbrillary pathology associated with some cases of Gerstmann-Sträussler-Scheinker disease (GSS) also belongs to the frst group [13]. We now report that the structures of tau flaments from two cases of SSPE are identical to those of CTE. Tis suggests that the CTE tau fold can form in response to diferent environmental insults, which may be linked by infammatory changes.

#### Materials and methods

**Clinical history and neuropathology** We determined the cryo-EM structures of tau flaments from the frontal cortex of two individuals with SSPE. Tere was no history of head injury. Case 1 was a male who developed measles when 1.5  years old; at age 8, he developed a speech disturbance, as well as eating and walking difculties. He was diagnosed with SSPE based on clinical presentation, a history of measles infection and characteristic electro-encephalogram and cerebro- spinal fuid abnormalities. Despite intensive antiviral treatment, his condition worsened progressively and he died aged 42, after having been on mechanical ventila- tion for 11  years. Te brain was severely atrophic with a weight of 439  g. Neuronal rarefaction was extensive in cerebrum, brainstem and cerebellum and there was a severe loss of myelinated nerve fbres. Tau-immunoreac- tive NFTs showed a wide distribution and were present in layers II and III of the cerebral cortex. Te clinico- pathological characteristics of SSPE case 2 have been described [case 4 in [9]]. Briefy, this was a male who developed measles at the age of 0.8  year; at age 22, he developed convulsions, myoclonus and parkinsonism. He was diagnosed with SSPE based on the neurological fnd- ings, a history of measles infection, periodic synchronous discharge on electro-encephalogram and cerebrospinal fuid abnormalities. Intensive antiviral treatment failed to improve his symptoms, he became bedridden and died

aged 41. Te brain was severly atrophic with a weight of 735 g. Pathological examination revealed the presence of marked brain atrophy with nerve cell loss and gliosis in cerebral cortex, basal ganglia, thalamus and hippocam- pus, which were associated with severe white matter atrophy [9]. Tau immunoreactive NFTs showed a broad distribution, in particular in superfcial layers II and III of the cerebral cortex, in oculomotor nuclei and in the locus coeruleus.

**Extraction of tau flaments** Sarkosyl-insoluble material was extracted from the fron- tal cortex of cases 1 and 2 of SSPE, as described [14], with minor modifcations. Briefy, tissues were homogenized with a Polytron in 40 vol (w/v) extraction bufer consist- ing of 10 mM Tris–HCl, pH 7.4, 0.8 M NaCl, 10% sucrose and 1  mM EGTA. Homogenates were brought to 2% sarkosyl and incubated for 30  min at 37  °C. Following a 10 min centrifugation at 27,000 g, the supernatants were spun at 257,000 g for 30 min. Pellets were resuspended in 2 ml extraction bufer containing 1% sarkosyl and centri- fuged at 166,000 g for 20 min. Te resulting pellets were resuspended in 100  µl phosphate-bufered saline (PBS) and used for subsequent analyses.

For immuno- blotting, samples were run on 5–20% gradient gels (Fuji Film). Proteins were then transferred to a polyvinylidene difuoride membrane and incubated with the following primary antibodies overnight at room temperature: Tau N (1:1000, Cosmobio TIP-TAU-P-03), AT8 (1:500), RD3 (1:500, Millipore 05-803), RD4 (1:500, Millipore 05-804), anti-4R (1:1000, BioLegend MMS-5020), Tau354-369 (1:1000, Millipore ABN2178-100UL) and T46 (1:1000, Termo Fisher Scientifc 13-6400). Histology and immunohistochemistry were carried out as described [16]. Brain sections were 8  µm thick and were counterstained with haematoxylin. Image processing was performed using RELION-4.0 [19,

20], unless indicated otherwise. Raw movie frames were gain-corrected, aligned, dose-weighted and summed into a single micrograph. Contrast transfer function (CTF) parameters were estimated using CTFFIND-4.1 [21]. Filaments were picked manually and segments extracted initially with a box size of 1024 pixels. 2D classifcation was used to remove suboptimal segments and separate Type I from Type II flaments. **Results** For cryo-EM, we extracted tau flaments from the frontal cortex of two cases of SSPE. Both individuals had measles as children, with the clinical picture of SSPE manifesting itself following a number of symptom-free years. In SSPE case 1, immunohistochemistry with anti-tau antibodies showed abundant neurofbrillary tangles that were stained by anti-tau antibodies specifc for 3R tau, 4R tau and phospho-tau (AT8) (Fig. 1). Neurons and glial cells with intranuclear inclusion bodies were observed using an antibody against measles virus. Microglial cells were activated, as shown by Iba-1 staining; the same was true of cytotoxic and T helper lymphocytes, as evidenced by CD3 staining (Fig. 1). Similar abnormalities have been described in SSPE case 2 [9]. Filaments from the sarkosyl-insoluble fractions were decorated by anti-tau antibodies and gave the same bands on Western blots as those from AD brains (Fig. 2). It has previously been shown that the bands of sarkosyl- insoluble tau from AD are identical to those from CTE [34]. Te observed bands indicate that the flaments of SSPE cases 1 and 2 are made of all six tau isoforms in a hyperphosphorylated state, consistent with previous results [9]. By cryo-EM, we show that the CTE fold of assembled tau is characteristic of SSPE cases 1 and 2 (Fig.  3; Addi- tional fle 1: Figure S1). As in the CTE fold, two types of tau flaments were present, each with an unknown, inter- nal density in the β-helix of the structured core. Type I and Type II flaments are both made of two identical protoflaments, with diferent interprotoflament pack- ing, i.e. type I and type II CTE flaments are ultrastruc- tural polymorphs. Type I comprised more than 90% of the observed flaments and Type II less than 10%, similar to what has been observed in CTE [26].

We previously showed that the CTE fold dif- fers from the Alzheimer tau fold by adopting a more open conformation of the β-helix region, which contains an internal density of unknown identity [26]. In the pres- ence of NaCl, recombinant tau comprising amino acids 297–391 assembles into flaments with the CTE fold, but in its absence, the Alzheimer tau fold forms [35]. We now fnd that the tau fold of SSPE is identical to that of CTE. As in CTE, two types of flaments, each made of two identical protoflaments, were present in SSPE cases 1 and 2. Western blots of sarkosyl-insoluble tau fractions indicate that these flaments are made of all six (3R + 4R) brain tau isoforms [12, 13, 26, 36]. For 3R + 4R tauopathies, one protoflament fold (the Alzhei- mer fold) is found in PART, AD, GSS, FBD and FDD, and

the second protoflament fold (the CTE fold) is found in CTE. Te present fndings show that SSPE is a second example of a 3R + 4R tauopathy with the CTE fold. It remains to be seen if other conditions with tau inclusions in cortical layers II and III also share the CTE fold. Infammation may be what CTE and SSPE have in common. In SSPE case 2, extensive infammatory changes have been described, with perivascular lym- phocyte infltration, aggregates of hypertrophic astro- cytes and activated microglia [9]. Here we show that the frontal cortex from SSPE case 1 also exhibited micro- glial activation and lymphocyte infltration.
