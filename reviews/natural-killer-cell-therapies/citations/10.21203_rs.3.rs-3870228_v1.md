**Abstract**

68 Natural killer (NK) cells are innate lymphoid cells (ILCs) that serve as a first line of defense 69 against pathogens and tumors. Historically, their classification hinged on a limited array of 70 surface protein markers. However, recent advancements in single-cell technologies, such as 71 single-cell RNA sequencing (scRNA-seq) and cellular indexing of transcriptomes and epitopes 72 by sequencing (CITE-seq), have unveiled a more complex and nuanced understanding of NK 73 cells. This has led to variations in nomenclature and inconsistencies across scientific literature. 74 In our study, we utilized these technologies to dissect the heterogeneity of NK cells in healthy 75 human blood. We identified three prominent NK cell subsets: NK1, NK2 and NK3, further 76 differentiated into six distinct subgroups. All subsets were present in all donors, irrespective of 77 their human cytomegalovirus (HCMV) status. Our findings delineate the unique molecular 78 characteristics, key transcription factors, principal biological functions, significant metabolic 79 traits, and cytokine responses of each subgroup. These data also suggest two separate 80 ontogenetic origins for NK cells, leading to divergent transcriptional trajectories. We explore 81 these pathways in depth and propose a comprehensive, standardized nomenclature for NK cells. 82 This standardized terminology aims at fostering clarity and consistency in future research, 83 thereby improving cross-study comparisons.

**Main** NK cells are lymphocytes of the innate immune system, classified as part of the ILC family1. 86 Initially recognized for their capability to identify and eliminate virus-infected and tumor cells 87 independently of prior sensitization, NK cells have since been acknowledged for their 88 multifaceted roles. These include not only direct immune responses but also regulatory 89 functions that influence the adaptive immune system. 90 The heterogeneity of NK cells is central to their varied functions. Over time, researchers have 91 identified distinct NK cell subgroups, each characterized by unique functional potentials and 92 developmental pathways. These traditional classification methods mainly relied on surface 93 marker expression. Along this line, human NK cells are typically divided into two main 94 categories based on the cell surface density of CD56, the 140-kDa isoform of the neural cell 2 bright dim 95 adhesion molecule (NCAM) : CD56 and CD56 NK cells. Further distinctions within the dim 96 CD56 population are made based on the cell surface expression of the CD57 carbohydrate 4, 5, 6, 97 moiety3, and the absence of CD94/NKG2A and CD62L, indicating a more mature subset 7

98. Additionally, a distinct NK cell subset demonstrating characteristics akin to adaptive immune 99 cells, termed adaptive NK cells, emerges in certain immune contexts, such as HCMV
8, 9 100 encounters. The advent of advanced single-cell technologies, scRNA-seq and CITE-seq, has 101 precipitated a paradigm shift in our understanding of NK cells. These technologies reveal a 102 more intricate and nuanced NK cell landscape than previously understood, marked by subtle 103 distinctions. However, despite these advancements, a unified and standardized description of 104 NK cell heterogeneity remains elusive. Current definitions are often inconsistent, vary between 105 laboratories, and lead to discrepancies in scientific literature. This lack of standardized 106 terminology creates significant challenges, particularly in translating research across different 107 model systems or patient cohorts.

The increasing relevance of NK cells in therapeutic approaches, especially in NK cell-based immunotherapy against cancer, underscores the necessity for a comprehensive understanding 110 of their heterogeneity. Misinterpretation or neglect of specific NK cell subsets could have 111 significant implications, potentially affecting the effectiveness or safety of therapies. In this 112 study, we integrated scRNAseq and CITEseq data from 219 donors (~140,000 NK cells) to 113 establish a baseline classification of NK cells in the blood of healthy individuals. This 114 classification is intended to serve as a reference point for future studies, thereby facilitating a 115 more standardized approach to understanding and utilizing NK cells in both research and 116 clinical settings.

**Results** **Identification of three prominent subsets of circulating NK cells in humans** 119 To systematically and comprehensively categorize human blood NK cells, we utilized a high- 120 dimensional CITE-seq dataset, encompassing 228 antibody-derived tags (ADTs) and the 121 transcriptional profiles of 5,708 NK cells from eight healthy donors10. To effectively integrate 122 both RNA and protein expression data, we employed the Weighted Nearest Neighbors (WNN) 123 method10. Initially, we isolated non-proliferating NK cells at baseline and then re-clustered 124 them to elucidate the foundational heterogeneity among blood NK cells. Our analysis revealed 125 three primary NK cell subsets: NK1, NK2 and NK3 (Fig. 1a), which we subsequently analyzed 126 for their transcriptional (Fig. 1b), and proteomic signatures (Fig. 1c–d). 127 The NK1 cluster was marked by high protein expression of CD16, CX3CR1, CD161, β7- 128 integrin, and CD38 (Fig. 1c,d). Its transcriptional profile highlighted genes corresponding to 129 these proteins, along with elevated levels of cytotoxic molecules (GZMB, PRF1) and markers 130 of NK cell maturity, such as CD160, CD247, ADGRG1, NKG7, FCER1G, LAIR2, SPON2, 131 *CLIC3, and CHST2 (Fig. 1b). This cluster displayed lower surface levels of CD56 compared to* dim 132 NK2 and of CD57 compared to NK3, aligning with the traits of CD56 NK cells. 133 The NK2 cluster was defined by high expression of CD56, CD27, CD44, CD54, CD45RB, 134 CD314 (NKG2D), CD335 (NKp46), and low or absent CD16 and CD57 at the protein level 135 (Fig. 1c,d). At the transcriptome level, NK2 cells showed pronounced expression of ribosomal 136 genes (RPL and RPS gene families, Supplementary Table 3) and proteins involved in protein 137 synthesis and structural integrity (EEF1A1, TPT1), indicative of heightened protein synthesis 138 and proliferative capacity. This subset also expressed various cytokine receptors (IL2RB, IL7R), 139 membrane receptors (KLRC1 encoding NKG2A), transcription factors (TCF7), soluble factors 140 that modulate immune responses (XCL1, XCL2, AREG), and molecules implicated in cell migration and tissue homing (CD44, GPR183, SELL), along with granzyme K (GZMK) (Fig.

1b). The classic NK cell markers CD57 and CD16 were reduced or absent on NK2 compared bright to NK1 and NK3 clusters, indicative of a population comprised of CD56 and early-stage dim 144 CD56 NK cells. 145 For the NK3 cluster, the protein expression profile included CD16, CD57, CD271 (NGFR), 146 CD2, CD18, CD49d, and inhibitory Killer-cell immunoglobulin-like receptors (KIR) (CD158e, 147 CD158b), with a lower expression of CD56, NKp30, NKp46, CD161, and CD122 (Fig. 1c,d). 148 Transcriptionally, NK3 cells were characterized by the preferential expression of the 149 transcription factors PRDM1 (encoding BLIMP1) and ZBTB38, surface molecules and 150 receptors CD2 and KLRC2 (encoding NKG2C), CD3 chain transcripts (CD3E, CD3G, CD3D), 151 and secreted cytokines and chemokines (IL32, CCL5), alongside granzyme H (GZMH) (Fig. 152 1b). Using these transcriptional signatures11, we performed module score 159 analysis, which confirmed the robustness of the NK cell classification, as evidenced by the clear 160 delineation of three distinct regions on the Uniform Manifold Approximation and Projection 161 (UMAP) plot (Extended Data Fig. 1b). Subsequent analyses indicated that the 4 primary dim 162 CD56 subsets identified in the blood in Ref11 aligned closely with the NK1 profile, while the dim high 163 CD56 CD16 _c8_KLRC2 cluster corresponded with the NK3 signature. ##### Molecular features of NK cell subsets

Our investigation extended to delineating the principal molecular characteristics of each NK 268 cell subpopulation. Upon confirmation that the three NK cell populations’ defining markers 269 were accurately reflected within the six subpopulations (Extended data Fig. 2i-k), we undertook 270 a detailed examination of recognized NK cell markers to compile a precise molecular profile 271 for each subgroup (Fig. 3a). We computed the z-scores for the expression levels of various 272 pertinent markers within the NK cell subpopulations, considering only those genes expressed 273 above a threshold—detected in more than 5% of circulating NK cells —for inclusion in the 274 heatmap. This approach yielded a heatmap that, beyond previously identified markers, unveiled 275 additional distinctive characteristics for the subpopulations (Fig. 3a). 276 When examining cytokine and chemokine production, we found that NK1 subpopulations were 277 characterized by robust transcription of CCL4, CCL3, CCL4L2, and IL16, while NK2 and 278 NKint cells predominantly transcribed FLT3LG along with XCL1 and XCL2. Finally, NK3 cells 279 were marked by a high transcription of IFNG, IL32, and CCL5. Differential expression was 280 also apparent in chemotaxis receptors and cell-cell adhesion proteins. In particular, the subsets 281 were marked by different patterns of sphingosine-1-phosphate receptors (S1PR1 for NK2, 282 *S1PR4 for NK3, and S1PR5 for NK1 subpopulations). The CXC chemokine receptor family* 283 further discriminated among the subpopulations (CXCR2 and *CX3CR1* for NK1 284 subpopulations, CXCR3 for NK2, and CXCR4 for both NK1A and NKint). Classic NK cell 285 activating receptors also exhibited subset-specific expression patterns. High levels of NKp46 286 (NCR1), CD160, NKp30 (NCR3), and SLAM receptors were characteristic of the NK1 287 population. NK2 shared a pronounced expression of NKG2D (KLRK1) with NK1A and NK1B. 288 As expected, NKG2C (KLRC2) was predominantly expressed by the NK3 population. The 289 inhibitory receptors displayed divergent expression profiles from one subset to the other. NK1 cells expressed higher levels of inhibitory KIR, along with TIM3 (HAVCR2), CD161 (KLRB1),

and SIGLEC7, while NK2 and NKint cells had elevated NKG2A (KLRC1) expression and NK3 cells appeared to express more TIGIT. In terms of cytokine receptor expression, NK1 293 populations notably exhibited a heightened expression of TGFBR1, TGFBR2 and TGFBR3 as 294 well as IL12RB1, IL10RB, and IL2RG. In contrast, NK2 cells were characterized by a preference 295 for IL2RB (consistent with their strong expression of CD122 at the protein level, Fig. 1c), 296 *IL10RA, and a distinct expression of IL18R and its accessory protein IL18RAP. These findings* 297 suggest a varied sensitivity to cytokines and chemokines among the subsets. 298 The cytotoxic profiles were in line with prior observations: NK1 populations exhibited a 299 spectrum of cytotoxic molecules and associated proteins — GZMA, GZMB, PRF1, NKG7, 300 *GSDMD, FASLG — while NK2 and NKint seemed to display a strong expression of GZMK* 301 and TRAIL (TNFSF10). The NK3 subset expressed intermediate levels of cytotoxic molecules 302 and was distinguished by high GZMH expression. 303 Activation markers also served as differential markers, with CD69 being most prominent in 304 NK1B and NKint, while TNFRSF18 (encoding GITR) was more pronounced in NK2. 305 Moreover, classic markers of NK maturation aligned with prior descriptions: CD56 (NCAM1) 306 was more prevalent in NK2, and CD16 (FCGR3A) expression increased progressively from 307 NK2 to NK1C. In addition, CD11B (ITGAM) levels were higher in NK1B, whereas CD11C 308 (ITGAX) expression was more significant in NK2. 309 To further elucidate the functional attributes of the NK1, NK2, and NK3 subpopulations, we 310 engaged in a Gene Ontology (GO) term enrichment analysis. This analysis revealed distinct 311 functional specializations within these subpopulations. NK1 cells were primarily involved in 312 processes such as cell-cell adhesion, activation response, signaling, cytoskeletal activity, and 313 cell-mediated cytotoxicity (Fig. 3b). These findings underscore the NK1 cells' pivotal role in 314 cytotoxic effector functions of NK cells. In contrast, NK2 cells were linked to enhanced chemotaxis regulation and leukocyte differentiation, suggesting their ability to infiltrate tissues

and an ongoing maturation process. The NK3 cells, on the other hand, displayed an upregulation in leukocyte activation. 318 Building upon this analysis, we explored the functions of the six main NK cell subpopulations 319 we previously identified (Fig. 3c). In this context, NK1B cells were found to be highly 320 responsive to activation via surface receptors, indicating their potential as primary targets in 321 immunotherapeutic strategies. Both NK1A and NK1B populations were significantly enriched 322 for TNF and cytokine production. Notably, the NK1C subset was suggested to be the most 323 cytotoxic, as indicated by its pronounced cytoskeletal activity and cell-killing signature. An 324 intriguing discovery was the significant enrichment of tricarboxylic acid (TCA) cycle activities 325 in the NK1C subset, prompting further investigation into this aspect using single-cell Gene Set 326 Variation Analysis (scGSVA). 327 Clustering analysis based on metabolic pathway enrichment analysis separated the NK cell 328 subpopulations into two broad categories, with the NK1B and NK1C clustering more closely 329 together and separately from the NK1A, NK2, NK3, and NKint subsets (Fig. 3d). The NK1C 330 subset appears ‘hypermetabolic’ in nature with significant enrichment across central carbon 331 metabolism, including glycolysis and the TCA cycle, and mitochondrial oxidative 332 phosphorylation (OXPHOS), which may support enhanced cytotoxic activity. Similarly, the 333 NK1B subset also exhibits enrichment in the TCA cycle and OXPHOS, albeit to a lesser extent 334 than NK1C, which contrasts with the other NK cell subpopulations. In addition, NK1B cells 335 are more clearly defined by an enrichment in the mTOR signaling pathway. Finally, cysteine 336 and methionine metabolism appear to be enriched in the NK2 subset. **Discussion** Though scRNA-seq and CITE-seq have helped a lot in exploring the diversity of human NK 468 cells, the definition of their cell types and sub-types has varied across publications, for several 469 reasons, including differing experimental protocols, data acquisition, and analysis tools. This 470 has led to complexity in the literature and even disagreement as to whether or not certain cell 471 subsets are real or artifacts arising from one particular processing methodology. For example, 472 an apparent NK cell sub-type may arise as a result of a stress response being triggered by the 473 process of their isolation, or culture conditions. Thus, it is important to establish a consensus 474 framework for a basic set of NK cell types, pooling together datasets obtained from multiple 475 laboratories, but analyzed holistically. This is required to then be able to test for the expansion 476 or reduction of subsets, or indeed the presence of another subset, in blood or tissues, in states 477 of health and disease and at different life stages. 478 The integration of CITE-seq and scRNA-seq NK cell data in our meta-analysis, including a 479 total of more than 140,000 NK cells, discerned three major peripheral blood NK cell 480 populations annotated herein as NK1, NK2, and NK3. These populations are highly enriched dim bright 481 in canonical CD56, canonical CD56, and HCMV-driven adaptive NK cells, respectively. 482 The gene expression profile of the NK1 population described here aligns with hNK_Bl1 cells 483 described previously, with strong expression of FGFBP2, GZMB, SPON2, and FCGR3A16. The 484 gene expression profile of the NK2 population overlaps with hNK_Bl2, defined by high levels 485 of COTL1, CD44, XCL1, LTB, and GZMK. Importantly, the equivalent of NK1 and NK2 has 486 also been characterized in the mouse blood, mNK_Bl1 and mNK_Bl2 respectively16. **Methods** **scRNAseq data retrieving and preprocessing** For datasets 1 to 4, scRNAseq data were retrieved from the studies referenced in Supplementary Table 1. Newly generated single-cell sequencing data were aligned with the GRCh38 human reference genome and quantified using Cell Ranger (v6.1.2, 10x Genomics Inc). The preliminary filtered data generated from Cell Ranger were used for downstream filtering and analyses. First, each sample was examined one by one to remove low-quality cells and cell contaminations. The genes detected in more than 3 cells were retained and the cells expressing less than 200 distinct features were removed. Then, for each sample, the cells were normalized, scaled, and clustered following the standard Seurat protocol. The remaining contaminations were identified using the SingleR package (v1.4.1). The detailed metadata (including patient identifier and CMV status) were retrieved from the original studies. For dataset 4, since the original data were enriched at a ratio of 1:1 between NKG2C + and NKG2C-NK cells, the samples were down-sampled to match the initial biological ratio of each sample (individual). For dataset 6, the preprocessed Seurat object was used directly.

##### Batch effect correction and unsupervised clustering

The samples were then merged. To reduce the batch effect during the clustering process, the 11965 genes present in each of the samples were kept for the clustering step of the analysis. To account for the difference in depth of sequencing from one sample to the other, the count data were normalized using the Multibatchnorm function with the parameter “batch= *sample” of the batchelor (v1.6.3). The top 5000 highly variable genes (HVG) were identified* in each sample using the FindVariableFeatures function in Seurat. Then, to choose the 2000 best features to keep for integration, the SelectIntegrationFeatures function of Seurat was used with the parameter setting “nfeatures=2000”. The gene expression was then scaled and

centered using the ScaleData function of the Seurat library. Next, principal component analysis (PCA) was performed on the matrix of HVGs to reduce noise and reveal the main 616 axes of variation using the RunPCA function, and the top 30 components were retained for the 617 following analysis. The batch effects were corrected using harmony correction algorithm 618 across samples12. UMAP dimensional reduction and shared nearest neighbor graph were 619 calculated on harmony-corrected PCA embeddings. The resolution parameter of the 620 *FindClusters function of Seurat was chosen for maximizing the mean sc3 stability of the* 621 clustering for a granularity ranging from k=0.5 to k=1.4. The cluster of proliferating cells was 622 identified using the CellCycleScoring function of Seurat. Cells of these clusters were then 623 removed and a new UMAP visualization was calculated to better visualize the remaining 624 clusters and cells. The final object used for the analysis of datasets 1 to 4 is available for 625 easily exploration at the following address: [https://collections.cellatlas.io/meta-nk](https://collections.cellatlas.io/meta-nk) 626 The cluster-specific marker genes were identified using the FindAllMarkers function of 627 Seurat with the parameter “method= wilcox, only.pos=TRUE, min.pct=0.2, logfc.threshold= 628 *0.25”.* 629 630 **Scoring with signatures** 631 To score cells with some specific signatures, the top 20 cluster-specific markers (calculated as 632 defined above) were given to the AddModuleScore function.
