6 7 8 9 Bacteria and their viruses have co-evolved for billions of years. This ancient and still ongoing arms race 10 led bacteria to develop a vast anti-phage arsenal. The development of high-throughput screening 11 methods expanded our knowledge of defense systems from a handful to more than a hundred systems, 12 unveiling a high number of different molecular mechanisms. These findings force a change in our 13 perception of bacterial immunity to a much more complex one. Here, we review recently discovered anti- 14 phage defense systems, with a particular focus on their molecular diversity, and discuss the ecological and 15 evolutionary drivers and implications of the existing diversity of anti-phage mechanisms. 16 17

18 **Introduction:** 19 Phages are viruses that infect bacteria and hijack the cell machinery to replicate. Phages possess 20 two main types of reproduction cycles. During a lytic cycle, the phage nucleic acids are expressed, 1 21 producing both the phage genome and proteins that will be assembled into new virions . At the end of a 22 lytic cycle, the host is lysed, and progeny phages are released. During a lysogenic cycle, the phage genome 23 is integrated into the host genome as a prophage and is transmitted across bacterial generations. Phages 2 24 are extremely abundant and are thought to outnumber bacteria ten to one in the ocean 25 . Estimates suggest that phage infection could account for 20 to 40% of bacterial daily mortality. 2 26 As such, phage infection represents a major evolutionary driver for bacteria . 27 In response to this strong evolutionary pressure, bacteria have developed an arsenal of anti-phage 28 systems, which can be defined as single genes or groups of genes that provide to their host partial or full 29 resistance against phages. The anti-phage arsenal of bacteria has long been thought to be limited to 30 Restriction-modification (RM), CRISPR-Cas, and a few other systems with unknown mechanisms. 31 However, the development of high throughput bioinformatic and experimental approaches (Box 1) has 32 extended our knowledge of the anti-phage arsenal of bacteria to more than a hundred (Table 1), revealing 33 an unsuspected wealth of diverse systems and mechanisms. 34 Whereas existing knowledge of antiphage mechanisms was before almost exclusively limited to 35 two systems detecting and degrading invading nucleic acids, these discoveries uncovered unsuspectedly 3,4 36 complex immune strategies and notably systems that produce small anti-phage molecules , systems 5–7 37 that rely on intracellular signal transduction through production of signaling molecules , and systems 8 38 that recognize conserved structural patterns of viral proteins to trigger immune response . These recent 39 findings have made a compelling case to revisit existing paradigms on antiviral immunity in bacteria from 40 a simplistic picture to a much more detailed one. 41 9–11 42 Early discovered defense systems were previously reviewed (see ). Here, we will first describe the 43 diversity of anti-phage mechanisms, focusing on the most recently discovered ones, and discuss the 44 evolutionary drivers and the ecological consequences of this diversity. 45

46 **I. Antiphage defense systems are highly diverse**

47 1. Genomic diversity 48 49 The genomic features of anti-phage systems reflect their diversity. First, the repertoire of genes 50 associated with phage defense includes diverse functions and domains, comprising nucleases, helicases, 51 proteases, kinases, ATPases, reverse transcriptases, etc. The variety of this repertoire seems in itself 52 limited and comprises mostly nucleic acid interacting domains and mostly a few other domains (SIR2, TIR, 53 and transmembrane domains for instance) (Supp Table 1). However anti-phage genes can be combined 54 in unique ways to compose diverse defense systems. Certain defense systems are single-gene (AbiH, Lit, 55 NixI, BstA…), while other systems can encode 5 genes or more (CRISPR-Cas, BREX, DISARM, Dnd, Ssp…).

57 The abundances of different defense systems in bacteria vary greatly from one system to another. 58 Analysis of the RefSeq database with DefenseFinder, a tool developed to systematically detect defense 59 systems in prokaryotic genomes, reveals that RM and CRISPR-Cas are encoded in respectively 84% and 60 40% of bacterial genomes, while more than half of detected systems are found in less than 3% of bacterial 12,13 61 genomes12 (Fig 1B). The distribution of defense systems appears to be long-tailed, and contains a 62 wealth of rare systems that are the main source of diversity in antiviral arsenals. 63 64 Multiple defense systems can also be combined in unique ways into an antiviral arsenal that is specific to 65 a given strain. Out of 21,364 fully sequenced bacterial genomes, 78% of them encode more than two 12 66 defense systems (Fig 1A), with important variations between strains. For instance, certain intracellular 67 parasitic bacteria (e.g. members of the Chlamydiae phylum, Fig 1B) harbor few or no defense systems, 12,13 68 while other species encode more than 50 . 69 70 It is also notable that many of the defense systems encoded by bacteria can also be detected in archaea. 71 Among the systems detected by DefenseFinder more than a third can be detected in archaea, showing 72 strong conservation of antiviral immunity between prokaryotic kingdoms. 73 74

### II. Drivers of anti-phage defense diversity

The outstanding diversity of the defense strategies implemented by bacteria to resist phages has both ecological and evolutionary roots. Indeed, the coevolution between bacteria and phages, as well as between bacteria and other Mobile Genetic Elements (MGEs), has driven the emergence and diversification of anti-phage systems for billions of years. This phenomenon is facilitated by the genetic organization of anti-phage systems: the reservoir of anti-phage genetic elements (domains, sensors, effectors ...) is limited, but its elements can be combined in a multiplicity of novel associations to create defense systems.

*1. Coevolution between bacteria and phages drives the diversification of anti-phage systems* Ecological studies of bacteria-phages interactions in natural populations hint at a fast-paced coevolution. For instance, a matrix of interactions of 195 Vibrio crassostreae strains against 243 vibriophages isolated over a 5-month time period shows that only 2.2% of the host-phage interactions tested are positive and that a given phage is more likely to infect a host isolated in a close space/time area 70 . Behind the coevolution of phage and bacteria is a continuous arm-race process between phages and bacteria. Notably, the diversity of anti-phage defense systems is mirrored by a similar diversity of anti- defense systems encoded by phages (Box 3)
10,71,72 .

429 3. Modularity as a source of diversity 430 431 The evolution and diversification of defense systems is probably supported by their modularity. Both in 432 terms of domains, sensors, and/or effectors, the diversification of defense systems seems driven by an 433 evolutionary mix-and-match game played at different and imbricated scales. 434 435 First, at the gene level, certain domains appear to be co-opted by many defense systems. For 436 instance, HEPN domains are common RNase effectors in nucleic acid degrading systems like CRISPR-Cas 92 32 437 Type III, Prrc, RloC, RnlAB and ApeA and were also found in defensive hotspots . Similarly, SMC 13,54 28 438 domains are encoded by several systems, including Lamassu and Wadjet , and are also found in 32 439 defensive hotspots . 440 441 At the system level, several studies suggest that a given sensor module can activate multiple 442 effector modules. For instance, the DNA-modifying module of the Dnd system (DndACDE) is not only 443 found with the DNA-cleaving effector (DndFGH) but can also be associated in archaea with an effector 19,20 444 module (PbeABCD) that does not degrade or cleave viral DNA but halts phage replication . Similarly in 445 Ssp, DNA-modifying SspBCD can be associated with either SspE (DNA-nicking) or SspFGH (unknown 21,22 446 mechanism) , suggesting additional mechanisms. Other examples are the endonucleases NucC, which 46 447 is an effector of both CRISPR-Cas and CBASS systems , and Cap4, which can be an effector of both 93 448 Lamassu and CBASS systems . Conversely, an effector module can also be activated by multiple sensor units. For instance, in genomes encoding a Thoeris system, the thsA effector gene is often found accompanied by several thsB

451 sensors. It appears that different ThsB proteins recognize different phages and all activate the same 6 452 effector, widening the activity spectrum of the system . In the Avs defense systems, the NLR sensor 453 domain can be fused with diverse effector domains. Gao and colleagues created a chimeric system where 454 the natural Mrr-like nuclease effector domain of E. coli’s Avs4 was swapped with the transmembrane 455 domain effector domain from an Avs4 homolog encoded by Sulfurospirillum sp. The chimeric system 456 retained its antiviral activity, further demonstrating the modularity of the Avs systems8. 457 It has also been proposed that domains and modules associated with defense systems can either 458 evolve from or be domesticated into non-defensive cellular processes, as it has reported for certain RM 91,94,95 459 enzymes . Highlighting even more the modular nature of defense systems are the many newly 460 discovered multi-effector or multi-sensor families of systems, such as CBASS, Lamassu, retrons and pAgos 26,27,38,39,48 461 . 462 The co-optation of domains, sensors, and/or effectors, from other defense systems or other 463 cellular pathways could allow the evolution of novel anti-phage mechanisms not de novo but as a 464 combination of existing modules. 465

466 **III. Impact of the diversity of anti-phage systems on phages and bacteria** 467 468 Defense systems have strong ecological repercussions, both on phage-bacteria interactions and on 469 phage and bacteria separately.

470 1. Impact of defense systems diversity on host range 471 472 The impact of the diversity of anti-phage defense systems on host range is not fully understood yet. 473 However, several tendencies have already emerged. 474 First, the combination of different defense systems is often synergetic. On one hand, encoding several 475 defense systems active against the same phage can increase the host resistance compared to the 70,82,96 476 expression of a single system . On the other hand, most defense systems only defend against specific 477 phages. For instance, 15 Pseudomonas aeruginosa systems were cloned and 11 of them were specific to 97 478 one or two phage families when expressed in a P. aeruginosa host .The expression of several defense 479 systems active against different phages can result in an increase of the resistance range, which can be 81,97 480 equal or superior to the sum of the resistance profiles conferred by individual systems . 481 Secondly, encoding diverse defense systems could hinder the emergence of resistant phages, as reported 98 81 482 for instance for the combination of ToxIN and RexAB , BREX and RM systems , CRISPR and RM systems 99 483 . 484 Overall, the diversity of anti-phage sensors and effectors could allow defense systems to target a 485 wider range of phages while preventing the emergence of resistance.

486 2. Defense systems shape microbial genomics 487 488 Defense systems also shape microbial genomics. They can impact horizontal gene transfer and shape the 489 spread and fixation of mobile genetic elements in microbial populations in various ways. 490 First, the diversity of defense systems impacts MGEs. Indeed, anti-phage defense systems can restrict not 491 only phages but also other types of MGEs. As MGEs can bring beneficial elements to the host (antibiotic resistance genes for instance), this could prove detrimental to the host. For instance, in Vibrio cholerae the presence of genetic elements encoding defense systems was shown to limit the acquisition of two

80 494 beneficial MGEs , and the introduction of a CRISPR-Cas system in a Bacillus strain naturally lacking one 100 495 resulted in reduced growth, sporulation, biofilms, and adaptability to environmental stresses . 496 Depending on their molecular mechanisms, defense systems are more or less permissive toward invading 497 nucleic DNA, and therefore towards MGEs. For instance, systems that detect RNA can coexist with 498 unexpressed foreign DNA. 499 500 Second, the diversity of anti-phage mechanisms influences HGT in bacteria. For instance, “addictive” 501 systems such as TA systems can participate in stabilizing MGEs in the bacterial population by killing any 502 progeny cells that did not inherit the MGE encoding them. DNA degrading systems like RM could facilitate 101 503 homologous recombination by introducing breaks in dsDNA . CRISPR-Cas systems have also been 504 proposed to promote HGT, possibly by favoring homologous recombination between CRISPR spacers and 102,103 505 phage protospacers . 506 507 Although the impact of viral predation on microbial communities is probably still underestimated, several 508 studies highlight the importance of phage-bacteria interactions on microbiomes and ecosystems 2,104,105 509 .Phages not only impact the species they target, but also indirectly impact competing species of the 510 ecological niche. As an example, a studied showed that introduction of lytic phages in gut microbiome of 511 a mouse model led to stable coexistence of the phage and the targeted host, and had cascading indirect 104 512 effects on non-targeted species, and ultimately impacted the gut metabolome . As defense systems 513 shape phage-bacteria interactions and influence microbial genomics, they probably have major ecological 514 implications that remain to be fully comprehended.

515 **Conclusion** 516 The diversity of anti-phage mechanisms in bacteria is striking and comprises unexpectedly 517 complex immune strategies. Despite recent advances in our knowledge of anti-phage defense 518 mechanisms in bacteria, many questions remain to be explored. In particular, very few systems have been 519 characterized outside of classic model organisms, and future research on distant bacteria or archaea could 520 provide fascinating insights and allow the discovery of novel mechanisms. In the past, the mechanistic 521 richness of bacterial anti-phage defense has been the source of major biotechnological breakthroughs. In 522 fields as diverse as molecular biology, genome editing, and clinical diagnosis, systems like RM, CRISPR-Cas 523 and retrons have provided invaluable tools, and many more applications are probably yet to be uncovered 106–110 524 . 525 526 527 The giant leaps made in our understanding of anti-phage defense systems have major ecological 528 and evolutionary repercussions. These advancements compel us to shift our perspective towards an 529 integrated understanding of the role of defense systems within a complex network of interactions, moving 530 away from a simplistic view of how a single system impacts the interaction between a phage and its host. 531 8,26,43,62,69 532 First, the overwhelming presence of abortive infection systems , despite their important fitness 533 cost for the bacteria expressing them, demonstrates that defense systems should not be considered 534 exclusively at the single-cell level, but rather at the population level. Reports on the anti-phage activity of 535 certain molecules excreted by certain Actinobacteria also support the importance of considering anti- 536 phage defense at the level of microbial communities. Studies suggest that real-life factors such as inter- 111 537 species competition and spatial heterogeneity could affect the dynamics of anti-phage defense in 112 bacteria . Yet, the role of anti-phage systems has rarely been explored outside of simple and controlled laboratory settings. Notably, very few anti-phage systems have been studied in their original host, and

540 studying anti-phage mechanisms in their native conditions will probably be key in a better understanding 541 of anti-phage defense in nature. 542 543 Secondly, the interplay between defense systems and the genetic elements that encode them 544 appears far more complex than initially envisioned. Defense systems’ diversity not only stems from an 545 ongoing war between bacteria and MGEs, but also shapes the fate of all protagonists involved. From 546 bacteria to plasmids, phages, prophages, and even phage satellites, all kinds of MGEs can develop and/or 547 hijack for their own benefit existing defense systems, anti-defense systems, and even anti-anti-defense 548 systems. These findings challenge our perceptions of defense systems as weapons in a war between 549 bacteria and phages and suggest that anti-phage defense systems can be envisioned as a toolbox used by 550 both MGEs and bacteria to interfere with gene fluxes, alongside notably anti-plasmids and viral anti- 83 551 defense systems . Investigating a potential continuum between anti-phage defense and HGT and/or 552 MGE regulation will no doubt help refine current knowledge of microbial genomics. 553 554 Lastly, from an evolutionary perspective, defense systems appear to be an excellent example of the 555 concept of evolutionary tinkering developed by François Jacob in 1977, which argues that natural selection 556 is akin to a tinkerer assembling existing objects, i.e. in this case defensive modules, until they combine 113 557 into something useful . Interestingly, some defensive modules appear to be conserved across domains 558 of life. As mentioned earlier, many antiviral systems can be conserved between Bacteria and Archaea. 559 Maybe more surprisingly, both protein and domain conservation between prokaryotic and eukaryotic 64 65 560 immunity have also been reported, with for instance Viperins , Gasdermins , but also TIR and NLR- 6,114 561 domains . Several hypotheses could explain the conservation of antiviral immune systems between 562 prokaryotes and eukaryotes, one of them being that these systems were inherited from the last common 115 563 ancestor(s) of prokaryotes and eukaryotes . It is also possible that some defense systems were acquired 564 during later events of HGT between prokaryotes and early eukaryotes. Combining ideas of evolutionary 565 tinkering and immune building blocks opens two fascinating research avenues: i) Delimiting what can be 566 considered anti-phage building blocks, mapping them in chromosome genomes and studying their 567 possible combinations might allow systematic exploration of the diversity of anti-phage systems in 94 568 bacteria . ii) The conservation of building blocks across domain of life could allow the discovery and 50,116 569 understanding of eukaryotic immune mechanisms sharing homology with known anti-phage systems . 570 571
