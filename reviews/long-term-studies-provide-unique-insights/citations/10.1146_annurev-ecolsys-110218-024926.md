#### 1. INTRODUCTION

How, why, and how fast does adaptive evolution happen in the real world? Our work on the Trinidadian guppy is built around addressing these questions and, more precisely, testing pre- dictions of evolutionary theory with experiments performed in nature. We chose the path of ex- perimental studies of evolution in nature because, at the time (1978), it was a path rarely traveled and our goal was to see how evolution works in the complexity of the real world, rather than in a laboratory setting. We built the project around testing predictions from the theory of life history evolution because the topic was compelling and the theory provided clear, testable predictions. We chose guppies because they displayed the population variation in mortality risk necessary to test theory and they occurred in a setting that made those tests feasible. The early success of our experiments produced, as a by-product, the discovery that evolution in nature could be far more rapid, by several orders of magnitude, than had been inferred from the fossil record. Others were making the same discovery (Hendry & Kinnison 1999), which led to profound changes in multiple disciplines in how evolution was incorporated into theory and practice (Reznick et al.2019b).In our own research,this meant thinking of ecology and evolution as contemporary, interacting processes, which makes their joint dynamics capable of producing different results than one would predict under a more traditional, historical view of evolution. Our ongoing research is a continuing exploration of both the drivers and the consequences of contemporary evolution. We have expanded beyond the initial focus on life histories to con- sider how evolution shapes single-species population regulation, multi-species communities, and ecosystem processes. We have also become interested in other fundamental questions: How fast and how repeatable is evolution? Does the speed of evolution inform us about the underlying genetics, specifically the role of standing genetic variation? Are the adaptations we see in natu- ral populations driven by a single, dominant agent of selection, or multiple, potentially interact- ing agents? How much ecology do you need to understand adaptive evolution? Conversely, how much evolution is necessary to understand ecology? Finally, can we understand evolution simply by studying the footprints of adaptation in living organisms,or is there something to be gained by also considering the dynamics of the process? In this review, we show how our work is answering these questions. Our research program is built on the pioneering work of Caryl Haskins (Haskins et al. 1961), whooriginallydescribedthediversefishcommunitieswhereguppiesoccurintheNorthernRange Mountains of Trinidad.He documented how those communities were distributed throughout the many streams that drain the seasonal rain forest covering this mountain range, and he character- ized guppy predators. This article was the inspiration for all who came after him.

#### 2.2. Fish Communities

The Northern Range Mountains offer a natural laboratory for studying evolution in action. The rivers draining these mountains flow over steep gradients punctuated by waterfalls that define boundaries between different fish communities.Species diversity decreases upstream because wa- terfallsblocktheupstreamdispersaloflarger,predaceousfishspecies.Thesuccessionfromdiverse communities downstream to simpler communities upstream is repeated in multiple rivers,provid- ing natural replication. In addition, fish can be introduced into portions of streams bracketed by waterfalls, creating in situ experiments (Endler 1978, 1980). Guppies are found throughout most of these communities. In the downstream localities, they co-occur with a diversity of predators, which prey on adult guppies (high predation). Waterfalls often exclude predators but not guppies, so guppies found above waterfalls have greatly reduced risks of predation and increased life expectancy (low predation). Rivulus hartii, the only other fish found in these localities, rarely preys on guppies. When it does, it preys predominantly on small, immature size classes (Endler 1978, Haskins et al. 1961). In some headwater streams, R. hartii is the only fish species present because individuals are capable of overland travel on rainy nights. A second form of replication occurs among these rivers. During past glaciations, when sea levels were lower,Trinidad was joined to South America,and some rivers on the south slope were tributariestoothersinSouthAmerica.Thepredatorsonthesouthslopearecichlidsandcharacins, the same as those in South American freshwater streams. The rivers of the north slope never had mainland connections. The downstream predators are instead mostly gobies that invaded from the sea. Nevertheless, the contrast between high and low predation, downstream to upstream, is repeated on both sides of the Northern Range. The spatial scale of this variation is small. All of our sites in the Northern Range fit within a rectangle that is approximately 25 km from west to east and 15 km from south to north. The elevational range of guppies runs from sea level to a few hundred meters. The distance between high-andlow-predationcommunitieswithinariverisgenerallyintherangeofhundredsofmeters but can also be defined by a single barrier waterfall, with a high-predation community below the fall and a low-predation community above it. The small scale of variation means that differences in fish communities are not confounded with differences in the physical environment. The rivers are small, as well. Most of our study sites are small enough to step or jump over, and many are subdivided into discrete pools and riffles, which enables us to follow every fish in the population.

#### 2.4. Theoretical Framework

Early investigators (Haskins et al. 1961, Seghers 1973) built a circumstantial case for differences between high- and low-predation communities in size- and hence age-specific predation. Crenici- *chla alta, a dominant predator in high-predation streams, feeds preferentially on large, adult size* classes of guppies. R.hartii,the only predatory fish in low-predation streams,feeds predominantly on small, immature size classes of guppies. Endler (1978, 1980) argued that the absence of vi- sually oriented predators found in high-predation communities allowed males in low-predation communities to evolve more colorful patterns. In the 1970s, when this research program began, a few theoretical papers predicted how life histories would evolve in response to changes in age-specific mortality risk. Early, density- independent theories of the vintage of Gadgil & Bossert (1970), Schaffer (1974b), Law (1979), Michod (1979), and Charlesworth (1980) all predicted that high adult mortality risk would select for earlier maturity and a higher rate of investment in reproduction, with the opposite expected in environments with selective predation on juveniles. Theory that included density-dependent regulation made similar predictions, provided that there was heterogeneity among age classes in their susceptibility to the deleterious effects of high population density (Charlesworth 1980). The theory of r-andK-selection was more prominent but seemed less relevant because it excluded age-specific differences in mortality risk.Theoretical treatments also addressed the role of fluctuating environmental conditions on the evolution of life histories (Murphy 1968,Schaffer 1974a).This body of work was not central to our research at the time,partly because testing this theory would require long-term data and partly because we knew nothing about environmental variation in this system. Testing these three flavors of life history theory has one major challenge: Certain patterns of life history can be, on the surface, compatible with all of them. This means that merely quanti- fying life history and ecological differences would not be sufficient, and a variety of approaches and experiments would be necessary to falsify any one of them. In recent years, these theoretical Downloaded from www.annualreviews.org. Guest (guest) IP: 104.52.5.246 On: Fri, 26 Jun 2026 06:21:14 perspectives have been unified (Engen & Sæther 2016, Engen et al. 2013, Lande et al. 2009), and novel methods for bringing empirical data to bear on theory have been suggested. In addition, as we show, we have been collecting the long-term data necessary for rigorously testing this entire body of theory.

#### 3. METHODS

Our research is a combination of retrospective and prospective studies of evolution.Retrospective studies quantify the differences among guppies adapted to high- and low-predation communities;

they reveal the footprint of adaptive evolution. Prospective studies are experimental studies in which we look at evolution in action. Combining the two allows us to test a variety of hypotheses about how and why guppies have evolved. The elements of our retrospective research include the following:

1. Comparative studies of natural populations in which we characterize life histories and the comparative ecology of guppies from high- and low-predation environments.For examples, see Reznick (1989), Reznick & Endler (1982), and Reznick et al. (1996a).
2. Laboratory common garden experiments in which we characterize differences among pop- ulations in life histories of the laboratory-reared grandchildren of wild-caught adults.Rear- ingfishthroughtwogenerationsundercontrolledlaboratoryconditionseffectivelyremoves confounding environmental influences on the phenotypes of guppies collected from high- versus low-predation localities. Differences that persist are assumed to have a genetic basis (e.g., see Reznick 1982b, Reznick & Bryga 1996).
3. Mark-recapture studies that characterize mortality, growth rate, and population density in high- and low-predation environments. This work was coupled with a quantification of the comparative ecology of high- and low-predation environments (Reznick et al. 2001) (e.g., see Bryant & Reznick 2004, Reznick & Bryant 2007, Reznick et al. 1996a).
4. In situ manipulations that have enabled us to demonstrate density regulation in action in low-predation streams plus characterize ecological interactions between guppies and killi- fish. For examples, see Bassar et al. (2013) and Reznick et al. (2012).
5. Factorial experiments in artificial streams. Here we compared the performance of guppies derived from natural high- and low-predation environments. We characterized the perfor- mance (e.g.,response to density,impact on the ecosystem) of high- and low-predation gup- pies in a common environment that replicated a natural stream. Our comparative ecology study (Reznick et al. 2001) enabled us to parameterize the density and size distributions of guppies to match those typical of natural high- and low-predation populations.This earlier work also provided a frame of reference for evaluating whether the growth rates, mortality rates, and fecundity correspond to values seen in natural streams. They did. For examples, see Bassar et al. (2010, 2012, 2013, 2017a,b). Our prospective studies are the experimental transplantation of guppies derived from high-

#### 4. WHAT WE HAVE LEARNED FROM GUPPIES

#### 4.1. Retrospective Evolution

Pronouncedandconsistentdifferencesareapparentinthelifehistoriesoflow-predationandhigh- predation guppies.High-predation guppies are smaller and younger at sexual maturity.They pro- duce more offspring per litter, and individual offspring size is smaller. Combined, these factors make the reproductive biomass per litter (number of offspring × offspring size) larger. The time interval between successive broods is shorter. The combination of initiating reproduction earlier, reproducing more often, and putting more resources into each litter means that the rate of in- vestment in reproduction is much higher in guppies from high-predation environments. These differencesinthelifehistoryphenotypesofwild-caughtfishhavebeenreplicatedindifferentrivers from all three biogeographic regions in the Northern Range (Reznick 1982b, 1989; Reznick & Bryga1996; Reznick & Endler 1982; Reznick et al.1996b) (Figure 1b,c).These differences persist in the grandchildren of wild-caught fish when reared in the laboratory, one per aquarium, on the same amount of food. Multiple hypotheses are consistent with these results (Reznick & Bryga 1987, Reznick & Endler 1982). First, they could be a direct consequence of size-specific predation. Second, they could be an indirect consequence of the differences in predation. High or low predation rates produce low or high population densities of guppies,respectively.The life histories by themselves cannot distinguish between these alternative hypotheses. When we discovered these life history differences (in the early 1980s), little was known about either the real differences in mortality rate, the magnitude of differences in density, the extent to which the populations were regulated, oranythingelseaboutthecomparativeecologyofguppies.Theseunknownsdefinedthenextsteps in our research.

**4.2.1. Population biology.** We developed methods to individually mark guppies and then per- formed mark-recapture studies in several high- and low-predation populations. Mark-recapture studies showed that high-predation guppies experience substantially higher mortality rates than low-predation guppies (Reznick & Bryant 2007,Reznick et al.1996a),consistent with the hypoth- esis that direct effects of predation select for the high-predation phenotype and alleviating these effects selects for the low-predation phenotype. However, for this to be so, theory tells us that adults in high-predation environments must be at greater risk than juveniles and the opposite pat- tern must occur in low-predation populations. In fact, we found that the differences in mortality

rates were equally distributed across all size classes.This result suggested that the direct effects of predation could not be causing these differences in life history.

**4.2.2. Comparative ecology.** We quantified features of the comparative ecology of high- and low-predation communities. High-predation guppies have lower population densities; are domi- natedbysmaller,youngerfish;andhavehigherindividualsomaticgrowthratesthanlow-predation populations (Reznick & Bryant 2007, Reznick et al. 1996a). The numerical dominance of small, young fish in high-predation populations is, in part, attributable to demography (high-predation guppies have higher birth and death rates). But ecological differences also contribute to these patterns: Predators reduce guppy population densities and increase per capita food availability to the survivors.In the absence of predators,guppy population density increases,leading to resource limitation and slower somatic growth. The difference in resource limitation is accentuated by the fact that low-predation environments are smaller streams with lower light availability. No other differences between the respective habitats could offer alternative explanations for higher growth rates in high-predation environments (Reznick et al. 2001). Guppies in low-predation environments are resource limited and are adapted to life at high population densities. Low-predation guppies have smaller, asymptotic body sizes (Reznick & Bryant 2007) and produce larger offspring (Reznick 1982a,b), which is an adaptation to a food- limited environment (Bashey 2008,Jørgensen et al.2011).They produce fewer offspring per litter in both the laboratory and the field.The magnitude of this difference in fecundity is much smaller inthelaboratory,wherelow-andhigh-predationguppiesarerearedonthesamefoodrations,than it is in the field (Reznick & Travis 1996, Reznick et al. 1996b). Density regulation.** Iftheabsenceoflargepredatorsandtheassociatedincreaseinguppy population density cause low-predation populations to become density regulated,increasing their densities in the field experimentally should produce demographic changes that would lead to a decline in population density. Reducing their density should have the opposite effect. We exploited the pool–riffle structure of many natural streams to test this hypothesis by ma- nipulating guppy density within pools in low-predation environments. For each replicate of the experiment, we selected three pools similar in structure and close to one another in a stream. We captured, measured, and marked all fish from the three pools and then reintroduced them at three different densities—typical density, reduced density, and increased density. We recollected them approximately 20 days later; quantified survival,growth,and reproduction; and then applied integral projection models (IPMs) to estimate the instantaneous population growth rates of the three treatments. The reduced-density treatments had accelerated population growth rates, and the increased-density treatments had decelerated population growth rates; the ambient-density treatments tended to remain stable in population size (Bassar et al. 2013, Reznick et al. 2012).

**4.2.4. Density-dependent selection.** We compared the responses of high- and low-predation guppies to high and low population densities in artificial streams (see Section 3 for methods). The experiment was similar to the manipulations of density in natural streams, in that marked and measured guppies were added to each channel and then recollected after 28 days. We then applied IPMs to the demographic data to estimate population growth rate, which is also a point estimate of mean fitness for that cohort. High-predation guppies had higher mean fitness than low-predation guppies in the low-density treatment but equal mean fitness at high population densities (Bassar et al. 2013). Both high- and low-predation guppies had reduced mean fitness at

high population densities, but the high-predation guppies were much more sensitive to density and hence sustained a greater loss of fitness than low-predation guppies. The results for density regulation and density-dependent selection show that the indirect ef- fects of predators, mediated through their impact on population density, play a role in shaping how guppies adapt to low-predation environments.

#### 4.3. Senescence

Our interest in the evolution of senescence may seem a digression, but the results created a chal- lenge that helped shape subsequent research. The low mortality rates experienced by guppies in low-predationenvironmentsarepredictedtofavortheevolutionofdelayedsenescence,ontheba- sisofG.C.Williams’santagonisticpleiotropyhypothesisfortheevolutionofsenescence(Williams

1957).Williams hypothesized that the evolution of senescence is shaped by trade-offs between the contribution of reproduction to fitness early and late in life. When selection favors increased in- vestment in reproduction early in life, the reduced investment in somatic maintenance to ensure reproduction later in life should cause some form of systemic breakdown that is independent of extrinsically imposed mortality, such as that by predators. However, high-predation guppies are unlikely to live long enough in nature to pay that price.Absent predation,low-predation guppies’ life spans should be extended enough to realize the costs of high,early investment.Low-predation guppies’ lower investment in reproduction and greater prospects of surviving to advanced ages led us to predict that the rate of deterioration with age should be higher in high-predation guppies than in low-predation guppies. We quantified senescence as age-specific reproduction, mortality rate, and swimming perfor- mance in a common garden setting with second-generation, laboratory-born guppies from the low-predation environments of the Yarra River (north slope) and the Oropouche River (east Trinidad). Our results were qualitatively the same for both paired comparisons and were, for the most part, opposite to predictions. High-predation guppies were, as expected, younger at matu- rity,reproduced more often,and produced more babies per litter.However,in the laboratory,they had lower mortality rates, longer life spans, continued to reproduce at more advanced ages, and had a lower rate of decline in the rate of reproduction than their counterparts from low-predation environments (Reznick et al. 2004). These results defy the trade-offs assumed in virtually all theoretical treatments of life history evolution. They also beg the question—why does the low-predation life history evolve? These results by themselves imply that the high-predation phenotype is unconditionally superior to the low-predation phenotype, so the low-predation phenotype should never evolve. Yet it does. Our finding that low-predation guppies are less sensitive to high population densities represents a par- tial answer.The senescence study was performed on fish kept one per aquarium and fed controlled rations, as in all of our common garden studies. #### 4.4. Guppy–Rivulus Interactions

Killifish are also distributed across the full spectrum of fish communities, but they exceed the range of guppies by invading even the smallest and least accessible mountain streams, where they are the only fish species present. #### 4.6. Prospective Evolution: Eco-Evo Dynamics

The second set of experiments reflects our interest in the consequences of contemporary evolu- tion. We were attracted to the concept of interactions between ecology and contemporary evolu- tion, hereafter referred to as eco-evo dynamics, which was initially conceived by David Pimentel (1961) but was brought to our attention by the outstanding series of papers by Hairston, Ellner, and colleagues on predator–prey dynamics in a model ecosystem (Fussmann et al. 2003, Shertzer et al.2002,Yoshida et al.2004).Pimentel (1961,p.65) framed his hypothesis as applied to density- dependent evolution: “Density influences selection; selection influences genetic make-up; and in turn, genetic make-up influences density.” This hypothesis was a natural fit for the guppy sys- tem,and we had the foundation upon which we could test it: ecological differences between high- and low-predation environments,density regulation in low-predation environments,evidence for density-dependent selection, and demonstrably rapid evolution. To test whether this kind of feedback characterizes the guppy system,we addressed three ques- tions. First, do guppies affect their ecosystem in a manner that alters the selective pressures they encounter? Second, do guppies respond to those altered selective pressures? Do guppies affect their ecosystem in a manner that alters selection on guppies?** The introduced guppy populations grew to peak densities in two to three years and then fluctu- ated seasonally. The strikingly different effects of high-predation and low-predation guppies on their ecosystems, as revealed in the mesocosm study, indicate that guppy evolution affects the ecology of their communities. #### 5.1. How Fast and How Repeatable Is Evolution?

Descriptions of contemporary evolution in other systems (Hendry & Kinnison 1999, Reznick & Ghalambor 2001) demonstrate that observing it in guppies was no fluke.Of course,contemporary

evolution had been documented long before we began our work [e.g., Myxoma (Pimentel 1961), heavy metal tolerance in plants (McNeilly & Antonovics 1968), industrial melanism (Kettlewell

1973)].However,the earliest demonstrations of contemporary evolution were responses to strong anthropogenic selection pressures.The understanding that evolution in response to natural selec- tion could occur just as rapidly was a novel result. The patterns of evolution we and others have seen in guppies have proven highly repeatable across the three biogeographic regions of Trinidad and across two very different types of fish communities—one dominated by species typical of South American freshwater streams,the other by oceanic species. The rate of evolution has also proven repeatable in the two original and four new introduction experiments.
#### 5.2. Does the Speed of Evolution Inform Us About the Underlying Genetics?

The rapid and repeatable evolution of life histories in six introduced populations means that this evolution was fueled by standing genetic variation rather than by new mutations.The finding that natural populations harbor extensive standing genetic variation is not a novel discovery. What is strikingis therepeated demonstrationofrapid adaptation,in thesame manner,toa novel environ- ment.On the one hand,this result supports the well-known hypotheses that much of the standing variation in populations can be adaptive (Lewontin 1974). On the other hand, our results focus attention on the fundamental paradox of evolutionary biology—namely, how genetic variation is preserved (Mitchell-Olds et al. 2007). One potent mechanism for preserving variation, demonstrated in natural populations of gup- pies, is frequency-dependent selection. Experiments in natural high- and low-predation environ- ments proved that rare male color phenotypes have higher survival and higher mating success than more common phenotypes (Hughes et al.2013,Olendorf et al.2006).These articles provide only a partial answer to how heritable variation is preserved, as much more than male coloration predictably evolves as guppies adapt to life with and without predators.

**5.3. Are the Adaptations We See in Natural Populations Driven by a Single,** **Dominant Agent of Selection or by Multiple, Potentially Interacting** **Agents of Selection?** An ecologist’s perspective and prior research on other organisms (Jones et al. 1977) make it seem obviousthatadaptationinnatureislikelytobedrivenbymultiplefactors(Reznick&Travis1996). The more interesting questions are which agents of selection act on which traits, how do they combine to drive adaptive change, and how do the relationships among traits shape the responses to selection. Guppy adaptation to the different fish communities is clearly driven by multiple, interacting agents of selection. The interaction between predation and guppy population density embraces several ecological fac- tors that can act cumulatively on foraging, diet, assimilation efficiency, physiology, trophic mor- phology, and resource allocation through the cascading impacts of guppies on their ecosystem (Auer et al. 2018, Palkovacs et al. 2011, Reznick et al. 2004, Zandona et al. 2011): the depletion of high-quality invertebrate prey, the selective removal of the competitively dominant diatom (Bassar et al.2010,2017a),and the effects of crowding on pathogen transmission and social stress. #### 5.5. Conversely, Do We Need to Understand Contemporary Evolution to

#### Understand the Ecological Relationships Among Species in a Community?

Our work demonstrates the extensive reach of interactions between contemporary evolution and ecology in the form of the scope of guppy impacts on their ecosystem (Bassar et al. 2010) and the dramatic differences between the ecosystem impacts of guppies adapted to high- versus low- predation communities (Bassar et al. 2010, Simon et al. 2017). Even more remarkably, the dif- ferent phenotypes exert different indirect effects on their ecosystems (Bassar et al. 2012), in this

case on nutrient cycling, mediated through the differences in their diets and through nitrogen and phosphorus excretion (El-Sabaawi et al. 2015). Early empirical work on interactions between ecology and evolution was based on simple two-species interactions in model ecosystems, leading to an understandable skepticism about whether such feedbacks could play any role in a more com- plex community. Our work shows that these interactions do indeed have strong effects on natural ecosystems.

**5.6. Finally, Can We Understand Evolution by Simply Studying the Footprints** **of Adaptation in Living Organisms, or Is Something to Be Gained by Also** **Considering the Dynamics of the Process?** Our ability to observe evolutionary dynamics in this system, as opposed to earlier experiments that documented beginning and end points with comparisons among high- and low-predation guppies, has shown how the climb up the adaptive peak is, in fact, a struggle, not a simple hike. Our ongoing quantification of numerical dynamics and selection gradients has shown that these gradients vary seasonally and, for a given season, even among years. The trajectory of phenotypes experiences accelerations and decelerations as it ascends the peak, along with reversals and stasis. We have also found that males and females evolve at different rates (Reznick et al. 1997), setting the stage for an interesting sexual conflict as both approach their respective peaks. None of this could have been discovered merely by comparison. Looking forward, we are using our ability to follow the dynamics of evolution to answer new questionsinecologyandevolutionarybiology.Ontheecologicalside,weareaskinghowabidirec- tional intraguild predation interaction can be stable. In addition, our ability to collect long-term data on population dynamics of interacting species is allowing us to evaluate the contribution of storage effects to species coexistence.On the evolutionary side,our mark-recapture studies in four replicate introduction experiments will create four pedigrees and a DNA archive of almost every individual in these populations.We can combine this pedigree with measures of performance and fitness to study the genetics of adaptation as it unfolds. We want to add guppies to the roster of populations whose long-term study has informed so much of evolution and ecology: the medium ground finch, great tits, red deer, and Soay sheep. We hope to use the added power of replication and experimental manipulation to explore issues these other systems could not address.
