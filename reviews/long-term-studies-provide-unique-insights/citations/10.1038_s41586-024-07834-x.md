## Abstract

Temporally fluctuating environmental conditions are a ubiquitous feature of natural habitats. Yet, how finely natural populations adaptively track fluctuating selection pressures via shifts in standing genetic variation is unknown[1](#R1),[2](#R2). Here we generated genome-wide allele frequency data every 1–2 generations from a genetically diverse population of *Drosophila melanogaster* in extensively replicated field mesocosms from late June to mid-December (a period of approximately 12 total generations). Adaptation throughout the fundamental ecological phases of population expansion, peak density and collapse was underpinned by extremely rapid, parallel changes in genomic variation across replicates. Yet, the dominant direction of selection fluctuated repeatedly, even within each of these ecological phases. Comparing patterns of change in allele frequency to an independent dataset procured from the same experimental system demonstrated that the targets of selection are predictable across years. In concert, our results reveal a fitness relevance of standing variation that is likely to be masked by inference approaches based on static population sampling or insufficiently resolved time-series data. We propose that such fine-scaled, temporally fluctuating selection may be an important force contributing to the maintenance of functional genetic variation in natural populations and an important stochastic force impacting genome-wide patterns of diversity at linked neutral sites, akin to genetic draft.

* * *

A ubiquitous feature ofthe habitats in which populations persist is sharp temporal fluctuations in the selective environment. Life has evolved distinct mechanisms to cope with these fluctuations. At the individual level, organisms may alter their phenotype to track predictable changes in the environment via phenotypic plasticity, or else produce a fixed phenotype that minimizes fitness variance amid unpredictable environmental shifts[1](#R1),[2](#R2). At the population level, populations can adapt to different states of the environment via shifts in standing genetic variation, a process termed adaptive tracking[2](#R2). What remains unknown is the temporal scale over which populations have the capacity to adaptively track fluctuations in the selective environment.

Even in the face of strong, rapidly fluctuating selective pressures, adaptive tracking may be limited if a population lacks genetic variants with sufficiently large selection coefficients to drive differential survival and reproduction at the same pace as the shifting environmental conditions[3](#R3). Determining whether such conditions are met in natural populations hinges on well-resolved, time-series genomic data. Procuring such data may ultimately reveal a functional relevance of standing genetic variation that remains hidden from inference approaches using static snapshots of population genomic variation[4](#R4)–[6](#R6). Furthermore, such approaches would help to clarify whether temporally fluctuating selection could act to maintain adaptive variation in natural populations, an issue of longstanding theoretical and empirical debate[7](#R7)–[11](#R11).

Seasonal environments, which fluctuate cyclically and on a timescale that lends to repeated empirical observation, are well-suited to quantify how finely populations adaptively track fluctuating selection pressures. For example, populations of *Drosophila* spp. inhabiting temperate environments expand exponentially from spring through summer as resources become abundant, then crash during the onset of winter as abiotic conditions deteriorate and resources become scarce[12](#R12). These boom-and-bust dynamics are a dominant feature of the demographic trends in a range of species, occur on scales shorter and longer than the seasonal scales[13](#R13),[14](#R14), and have been shown to drive the evolution of life-history, reproductive and stress tolerance traits[15](#R15)–[17](#R17).

Investigation into the population genetics of adaptation in seasonally fluctuating environments has a long history[18](#R18),[19](#R19). Recent studies that have leveraged genome-wide sequencing of *D. melanogaster* populations have shown how adaptive tracking keeps pace with fluctuating selection on seasonal timescales[20](#R20)–[22](#R22). Of note, Rudman et al.[22](#R22) have established that parallel, genome-wide signatures of fluctuating selection can be observed on monthly intervals (approximately 1–3 generations) across 10 replicate populations, suggesting that changes in the direction of selection may occur due to more fine-scale shifts in selective pressures than the most conspicuous environmental fluctuations across seasons. Still, the underlying granularity of this process remains unknown, including whether alleles responding to fluctuating selection over monthly intervals exhibit parallel, deterministic changes on even finer scales in response to the exceptionally dynamic environment of the system, which shifts daily across multiple biotic and abiotic axes. Resolving the ultimate limit of adaptive tracking in this system would in turn lend broader insight into the power oftemporally fluctuating selection to preclude the fixation ofadaptive alleles, and thus help to maintain variation, in natural populations. Furthermore, repeated observation in this system may be leveraged to probe the predictability of the targets of selection across independent bouts of adaptive tracking, an outstanding issue ofwidespread practical importance[23](#R23).

Here we aimed to resolve the scale and pervasiveness of fluctuating selection and the predictability of adaptive tracking via high-frequency monitoring of a genetically diverse population of *D. melanogaster* in an extensively replicated field mesocosm system ([Fig. 1](#F1)). Specifically, we seeded 12 replicate outdoor cages with a genetically diverse population (hereafter, baseline population), generated via 4 generations of outbreeding a panel ofinbred reference lines. This inbred panel was derived from individuals collected in local Pennsylvania orchards, and the resulting outbred population was representative ofvariation segregating in natural populations ofthe species ([Methods](#S7); [Extended Data Fig. 1](#F5)). The experiment spanned from early summer through autumn, a period of 10–12 generations[16](#R16), during which time the replicate populations were subject to the same general shifts in abiotic and biotic selective pressures. A well-characterized demographic trend in the system was observed, in which population sizes expanded rapidly after cage founding, stabilized at a peak density by late summer and declined during autumn[12](#R12),[22](#R22) ([Fig. 1](#F1)). Amid this generic demographic trend, replicate-specific population trajectories exhibited distinguishable behaviour, probably representing unique environmental exposures that alter the particular selective environment within each replicate at any given point in time. We sampled and quantified patterns of genomic variation weekly between 13 July and 7 September 2021, after which samples were collected on 21 September, 20 October and 20 December (12 total timepoints; [Fig. 1](#F1)). Our sampling scheme accounted for shifting age structure throughout evolution by quantifying genome-wide allele frequencies in a manner that reflected shifts to the instantaneously reproductive genotypes (that is, those genotypes actively contributing offspring to the growing population)[24](#R24) ([Methods](#S7); [Fig. 1](#F1)). The resulting data from 12 cages and timepoints were used to quantify parallel, deterministic shifts in allele frequencies across replicates and infer instances of fluctuations in the selective environment over a range of temporal scales. Finally, patterns ofobserved allele frequency change were compared with an independent study using the same mesocosm system[22](#R22) to explore the predictability ofindependent bouts ofadaptive tracking.

## Parallel allele frequency shifts across cages

We aimed to detect whether amid the inherent complexity ofour outdoor study system ([Fig. 1](#F1)), there existed evidence of parallel shifts in genomic variation in response to the shared selective pressures experienced across replicates. We first computed genome-wide *F*ST between the replicate populations and the baseline to ensure that the evolution of allele frequencies quantified throughout the experiment exceeded sources ofbiological and technical noise ([Extended Data Fig. 2](#F6)). Next, we used principal component analysis (PCA) to assess the effect of two key experimental factors, replicate cage and collection time, on variance in allele frequencies throughout the experiment. Segregation ofsamples along the first principal component (5.54% variance explained) indicated that variation in allele frequencies across all samples was predominantly driven by collection timepoint ([Fig. 2a](#F2),[b](#F2)), a pattern maintained when the analysis was run separately across each chromosomal arm ([Supplementary Fig. 1](#SD4)). The segregation oftwo replicate cages along the second principal component ([Fig. 2a](#F2)) indicates that background allele frequencies across replicates were potentially perturbed during cage founding (for example, via bottlenecking or idiosyncratic selective events). Yet, these two cages maintained the temporal trend along the first principal component, further indicating parallel shifts in allele frequencies throughout the experiment ([Fig. 2b](#F2)). An unsupervised clustering (*k*-means) ofthe PCA results further validated, over a range of cluster sizes, that the predominant axes ofvariation in the data were most strongly associated with sample collection time ([Supplementary Fig. 2](#SD4)). In concert, these results suggest that the shared selective pressures experienced by the replicates induced concordant changes in genomic variation throughout the experiment.

We next sought to identify the number and genomic distribution of single-nucleotide polymorphisms (SNPs) underpinning parallel changes in genomic variation. Accordingly, we fit allele frequencies to a generalized linear model (GLM; with quasibinomial error variance) to identify SNPs moving systematically across replicates throughout the entire summer-to-autumn transition (*t*1 → *t*11; before the total crash of the adult population occurring between *t*11 and *t*12)[25](#R25). This yielded a pervasive, genome-wide signal, in which 28.5% of all SNPs exhibited a Benjamini–Hochberg false discovery rate (FDR)-corrected *P* < 0.05 ([Fig. 2c](#F2)). Using an algorithm to identifygenomic clusters ofunlinked loci enriched for significant SNPs (and potentially containing an underlying causal locus), we identified 264 loci underpinning our GLM signal ([Methods](#S7))[22](#R22). These loci were located across all chromosomal arms and inside and outside known cosmopolitan inversions ([Supplementary information 1](#SD4) and [2](#SD4)). The range of allele frequency shifts for the top SNP of each unlinked locus, defined as the lowest *P* value SNP, was 2–11% (median 5.5%; selection coefficients 10–50% throughout the experimental period), values on par with previous documentation of seasonal allele frequency change in the species[20](#R20),[21](#R21). We ensured that complex patterns oflinkage (such as the interaction of inversions with polymorphisms outside the breakpoints) were not inducing correlated patterns ofallele frequency movement (and thus non-independence) between these loci ([Extended Data Figs. 3](#F7) and [4](#F8))[26](#R26). However, we note that particular patterns ofcorrelated allele frequency movement between chromosomes (for example, 2L and 3R versus X) may indicate the impact of polymorphic inversions that segregate in our outbred population, although the direct effect of such inversions on evolutionary dynamics was not quantified here. In summary, the number of putatively unlinked loci that we identified, in combination with their underlying magnitudes of allele frequency changes, suggests that adaptation in the system is neither underpinned by an extremely large number of loci with relatively subtle effects (for example, a polygenic model of adaptation), nor a small handful of loci, but rather a moderate number (on the order ofhundreds) with relatively large effect sizes.

We quantified the amount of parallel, neutral and anti-parallel evolutionary change for individual replicate cages by re-conducting the GLM analysis using a leave-one-out cross-validation approach: sets of GLM significant SNPs (hereafter, ‘target SNPs’) were identified iteratively in *n* −1 ‘training’ cages, and the frequency dynamics of the favoured alleles (those increasing in frequency during the identification window; FDR < 0.2, allele frequency change > 0.5%) were then quantified in the left-out cage. Each target SNP was matched to a control SNP on the same chromosomal arm and with comparable baseline frequency, recombination rate and inversion status ([Methods](#S7)). The distributions of target and control shifts for each cage were then compared to: (1) determine whether the magnitude of target shifts exceeded background allele frequency shifts, therefore indicating that the target SNPs exhibited non-neutral dynamics, and (2) infer whether the direction of non-neutral shifts was in a predominantly concordant (that is, parallel) direction as that observed in the training cages. This revealed a striking degree of parallelism across all replicates, in which the median shift of target SNPs within left-out cages was significantly greater than allele frequency change at matched control sites ([Fig. 2d](#F2)). These patterns suggest that even amid variation in the genomic composition of replicates ([Fig. 2a](#F2)), changes in genome-wide allele frequencies within the system follow detectably parallel behaviour throughout the summer-to-autumn transition.

## Measurement frequency dictates inference

We explored whether the direction of selection was constant throughout the experiment by quantifying allele frequency dynamics of each set of *t*1 → *t*11 target SNPs (identified above; [Fig. 2c](#F2),[d](#F2)) in sequentially shorter intervals. In this way, we explored whether there existed a limit at which point the replicates no longer exhibited deterministic shifts in allele frequency (the magnitude of phased allele frequency shifts no longer differed from matched control shifts) or whether evidence of anti-parallel behaviour emerged on shorter intervals (significantly negative, median phased shifts at target SNPs). Anti-parallel behaviour would in turn indicate fluctuating selection for the variants that were inferred to move in a consistent direction from the beginning to the end of the experiment via coarser quantification of allele frequencies. We observed persistently deterministic and parallel behaviour of target SNPs from the longest test interval (*t*1 → *t*11; 15 weeks) to the 2-week interval (*t*1 → *t*3; [Fig. 2e](#F2)), with the magnitude of allele frequency changes declining monotonically as a function of test interval length. Our shortest interval length (1 week; *t*1 → *t*2) continues to indicate predominantly deterministic shifts at target SNPs, but also provides us with the first evidence of anti-parallel behaviour across replicates (both red and blue points observed within the measurement interval; [Fig. 2e](#F2)). [Figure 2f](#F2) expands the set of test intervals to include all pairs of single timepoints (*t*1 → *t*2, *t*2 → *t*3…*t*11 → *t*12), the first nine of which are weekly intervals. Concordant with the *t*1 → *t*2 comparison, we see largely deterministic shifts at target sites in left-out cages, yet both parallel and anti-parallel behaviour. This suggests that even those SNPs inferred to shift systematically and in parallel throughout the summer-to-autumn transition ([Fig. 2d](#F2)) could be subject to repeated bouts of fluctuating selection on much finer temporal scales.

## Pervasiveness of fluctuating selection

Most cages experienced at least one reversal in the direction of selection on alleles identified as shifting in a predominately systematic manner from *t*1 to *t*11 ([Fig. 2f](#F2)). We hypothesized that sets of alleles identified via systematic behaviour on shorter timescales, which may exhibit a negligible net frequency change throughout the experiment, may reveal even greater incidence of fluctuating selection in the system. To test this, we first defined the shortest interval length over which we had power to detect SNPs shifting systematically and in parallel across replicate cages using a GLM ([Supplementary Fig. 3](#SD4)). The models regressing allele frequencies across five timepoint intervals (for example, *t*1 → *t*6, *t*2 → >*t*7…*t*7 → *t*12) represented the shortest interval length in which all comparisons yielded pervasive, genome-wide significant and large effect size variants across all chromosomal arms ([Supplementary Figs. 4](#SD4)–[14](#SD4) and [Supplementary information 3](#SD4)). We thus focused on this interval length to quantify the complex selective environment suggested in [Fig. 2f](#F2), but we also included results from all interval lengths to ensure that observed trends were not simply a feature of SNPs identified on this timescale ([Supplementary Figs. 15](#SD4)–[23](#SD4)). Furthermore, this analysis discarded those SNPs identified as strongly parallel across the *t*1 → *t*11 interval (GLM FDR < 0.05; approximately 28% of all SNPs), as these exhibit predominantly directional change ([Fig. 2e](#F2)) and may mask the finer-scale fluctuations in the selective environment that we ultimately aimed to uncover.

We first used leave-one-out to quantify the behaviour of SNPs discovered for all five timepoint intervals across the timepoints over which they were identified (note that intervals containing *t*10–*t*12 exceed 4 weeks absolute time; [Fig. 1](#F1)). In accordance with SNPs identified from *t*1 to *t*11, those identified on five timepoint intervals exhibited largely parallel shifts across replicates; for the majority of left-out cages and intervals, the magnitude of frequency shifts at target SNPs were significantly greater than those at matched control SNPs ([Fig. 3a](#F3), red points). Of note, during the first two intervals (*t*1–*t*6 and *t*2 → *t*7), two cages (replicates 1 and 5) exhibited predominantly anti-parallel allele frequency change (blue dots). These are the same cages that are offset along PC2 in [Fig. 2a](#F2),[b](#F2), and this dynamic further illustrates that perturbations in genome-wide allele frequencies (via either selection or drift) can drive idiosyncratic behaviour across replicates in the early season, but these are ultimately overcome by parallel shifts in genomic variation once population sizes become sufficiently large ([Fig. 1](#F1)).

Next, we quantified the number of times fluctuating selection produced changes in the inferred direction of selection acting on replicate populations throughout the experimental period. Specifically, we measured the shifts of target and matched control SNPs identified on five timepoint intervals across all pairs of single timepoints (that is, *t*1 → *t*2, *t*2 → *t*3…*t*11 → *t*12; as in [Fig. 2f](#F2)). [Figure 3b](#F3),[c](#F3) depicts the results of this analysis for sets of SNPs identified from *t*4 to *t*9; points are coloured corresponding to replicate cage and represent the median shift of target SNPs ([Fig. 3b](#F3)) or matched control SNPs ([Fig. 3c](#F3)), with the grey lines connecting the shifts for each cage across measurement windows. We inferred a flip in the dominant direction of selection when the median allele frequency shift at target SNPs switched signs (that is, fluctuated from positive to negative, or vice versa) and maintained a magnitude that was significantly greater than matched controls. Thus, the number of flips computed represents the minimum number of changes in the direction of selection required to explain the observed patterns of median allele frequency behaviour throughout the experiment. Across replicates, the median number of flips in the dominant direction of selection was five, indicating that coefficients of selected alleles repeatedly flip in sign from positive to negative over weekly timescales, and that the replicate populations adaptively track fluctuations in the environment that occur over much finer timescales than the dominant ecological phases ofpopulation expansion, peak density and decline ([Fig. 1](#F1) and [Extended Data Fig. 5](#F9)). The underlying magnitude of median allele frequency change at target SNPs throughout these flips frequently exceeded 1% ([Fig. 3d](#F3)), corresponding to selection coefficients greater than 10% per generation and exceptionally strong fluctuating selection on this timescale. Our leave-one-out approach and comparison to matched controls ensures that these fluctuations are not a product of regression to the mean, and by using a set of biological replicate samples collected at *t*3 and *t*11 we further validated that observed patterns of fluctuating selection are not a function of inherently noisy behaviour of our target SNPs ([Methods](#S7); [Extended Data Fig. 6](#F10)).

To further validate the pervasive signals of fluctuating selection inferred by our leave-one-out cross-validation, we re-analysed our allele frequency data using a method that leverages temporal covariances in allele frequencies to estimate the amount of frequency change attributable to linked selection[4](#R4),[27](#R27). These covariances are directional, in which positive values indicate directional allele frequency change across intervals, and negative values implicate signals of fluctuating selection[27](#R27). Furthermore, covariances between replicates can be used to quantify the degree of parallel allele frequency change across independent populations. This method inferred a genome-wide signal of fluctuating selection (with the number of changes in the dominant direction of selection similar to those quantified via our GLM leave-one-out approach) and strongly parallel allele frequency change across replicate cages throughout the experiment ([Extended Data Fig. 7](#F11)). Thus, this independent analytical approach adds credence to our inference that fluctuating selection is pervasive throughout the experiment and can manifest on finer timescales than the dominant ecological phases.

## Predictable evolution across study years

Our results thus far indicate that even amid differences in starting allele frequencies ([Fig. 2a](#F2),[b](#F2)), adaptive tracking across the 12 replicate cages proceeded via detectably parallel shifts in genome-wide variation ([Fig. 2d](#F2)). Similarly, using the same mesocosm system, a largely overlapping set of founder lines, and monthly allele frequency estimation between July and November of 2014, Rudman et al.[22](#R22) have identified parallel allele frequency change across ten replicate populations. Across both experimental years, the replicate cages exhibited population expansion and collapse dynamics and were exposed to the same generic seasonal shifts in abiotic conditions. Thus, this independent dataset from the same system provides the unique opportunity to ask whether the genomic underpinnings of adaptive tracking across independent bouts of evolution in cyclically fluctuating environments are predictable, or whether differences in starting allele frequencies, in combination with year-specific environmental conditions, drive largely unpredictable patterns of genomic variation from year to year. Although there is substantial evidence for predictable phenotypic trajectories in response to repeated selective regimes in this and other systems[16](#R16),[28](#R28), evidence of predictability at the genetic level is largely confined to traits with simple genetic bases[29](#R29)–[31](#R31) (but see [ref. 32](#R32)).

We first explored genome-wide patterns of differentiation across all samples collected by both studies using PCA. Although the clustering of samples by year along the first principal component indicated strong differentiation of genome-wide allele frequencies between studies, the separation of early from late season samples along the second principal component suggested that changes in allele frequencies through time may have been similar across years ([Fig. 4a](#F4)). To test this systematically, we computed Pearson’s correlations between genome-wide allele frequency shifts from all possible intervals of the current study (all 2–11 timepoint intervals) and each monthly interval of the Rudman et al.[22](#R22) study. These correlations provided a directional metric of concordance, in which positive correlations indicated that genome-wide allele frequency changes were predominantly in the same direction between the tested 2014 and 2021 intervals, and negative correlations corresponding to predominantly anti-parallel behaviour across compared intervals. The significance of observed correlations was evaluated using SNP identity permutation ([Methods](#S7)). The distributions of empirical correlations exhibited an enrichment ofcomparisons that were both significantly correlated and anti-correlated between years ([Fig. 4b](#F4); *F*-test, *F* = 3,821, *P* < 0.001), a pattern that is maintained when running this analysis separately across each chromosomal arm ([Supplementary Fig. 23](#SD4)).

To explore whether those intervals with heightened allele frequency concordance across years aligned with conspicuous shifts in the selective environment, we identified the interval of the current study that was best matched (that is, highest genome-wide correlation of allele frequency shifts) to each of the four monthly intervals of the 2014 experiment. The first three monthly intervals of the 2014 experiment were best matched to the *t*1 → *t*7, *t*3 → *t*9 and *t*5 → *t*12 intervals of this study, generally overlapping with the periods of population expansion, peak density and decline ([Fig. 4c](#F4)). The final monthly interval was best matched to *t*7 → *t*9, another mid-late season period, but we note that the correlation coefficient for this matching was an order of magnitude less than those for the first three monthly intervals (although still significant based on our empirically derived null distribution). Ultimately, these matchings suggest that amid stochasticity in year-to-year abiotic conditions, cyclical boom-and-bust population dynamics could induce a repeatable ecological filter that shapes patterns of selection to recurrently affect the same set of loci. Although disentangling such forces from seasonally fluctuating abiotic conditions is outstanding, these observed patterns generally conform to long-standing ecological hypotheses regarding the distinct selective environments associated with population expansion and contraction, and corroborate mounting evidence of pervasive, genome-wide selection throughout the progression of seasonal adaptation[13](#R13),[18](#R18)–[22](#R22),[33](#R33).

## Discussion

The pervasive fluctuating selection that we report here reveals key insights into the dynamics of adaptive tracking, with implications for patterns of genome-wide diversity and the maintenance of genetic variation in natural populations. Specifically, we identified changes in the dominant direction of selection repeatedly over multiple weekly intervals throughout the course of the experiment ([Fig. 3](#F3)). This indicates that our outbred population maintains alleles at relatively high frequency that exhibit large selection coefficients that flip sign in accordance with fluctuations in environmental conditions over sub-generational timescales. More specifically, across the timepoints over which each instance of fluctuating selection was inferred, the environment must have changed in such a way to induce trade-offs in the combination of traits underpinned by these alleles. Thus, even within each of the dominant ecological phases (for example, population expansion), the adaptive direction of multi-variate phenotypic change probably shifts constantly as changing population densities interact with specific combinations of abiotic conditions. These changes to the selective landscape may represent complex interplays between the traits dictating fitness differences between genotypes over the sampled weekly intervals, such as the interaction of temperature with larval competition or genotype-specific egg-laying preferences that affect the effective fecundity of an individual. We note that the fine-scaled reversals in the frequency shifts could, in theory, result from linkage-induced interference among different directionally selected loci[34](#R34)–[36](#R36). This, however, is unlikely to be the dominant force driving the observed dynamics. Specifically, the signals of fluctuating selection were inferred via the median behaviour of a large number of SNPs located throughout the genome, and such systematic shifts are unlikely to arise from the random impact of interference across sites (as evidenced by the relatively muted allele frequency behaviour at our matched control sites; [Fig. 3b](#F3),[c](#F3)). It may also be possible that changes to the efficacy of selection across generations could induce a signal of fluctuating selection if marker SNPs are linked to multiple causal loci that are subject to varying degrees of directional selection across generations. Although such a process is unlikely to be the dominant driver producing the magnitude and pervasiveness of fluctuating selection observed here, ultimately determining whether it in fact occurs at all hinges upon resolving the underlying causal loci driving adaptation in our system, providing an exciting area of future research.

Repeated flips in the direction of selection throughout the experiment is a pattern suggestive of antagonistically pleiotropy, in which alleles that confer advantageous trait values during the intervals in which they are favoured produce disadvantageous trait values for the sets of traits subject to selection during intervals in which they are selected against[37](#R37). As these alleles exhibited undetectable systematic change throughout the experiment (our assessment into fine-scaled shifts in the selective environment presented in [Fig. 3](#F3) discarded those alleles shifting systematically throughout the experiment), they would probably be inferred to be neutral using inference approaches based on coarse temporal sampling or static snapshots of population genomic data. Thus, our high-frequency sampling revealed a functional relevance to standing variation that would have remained hidden, bolstering recent calls for the procurement of well-resolved, time-series genomic sampling[4](#R4)–[6](#R6),[20](#R20)–[22](#R22).

The patterns of fluctuating selection that we observed are likely to have appreciable effects on genome-wide patterns of diversity within the species. Specifically, selection in various forms (for example, background selection, selective sweeps and fluctuating selection) have been shown to broadly reduce diversity at linked neutral sites[38](#R38)–[45](#R45). In the case of temporally varying selection, it has been shown that even modest fluctuations can have substantial effects on genome-wide patterns of diversity[45](#R45). Thus, the strong, rapid and genome-wide signals of fluctuating selection that we quantified are likely to alter patterns of diversity in a pronounced manner. This holds implications for recent debate as to whether elevated selection in large population-size species can help to resolve ‘Lewontin’s Paradox’ — the puzzling observation that neutral genetic variation does not scale with census population size as expected[26](#R26),[45](#R45)–[48](#R48). Although some studies have inferred that selection alone is insufficient to explain the narrow range of polymorphism observed across species[46](#R46),[47](#R47), the exceptionally strong selection quantified here calls this assertion into question and warrants increased effort towards the procurement of well-resolved, time-series genomic data in other systems.

Our results may additionally lend insightto the mechanisms maintaining variation in natural populations. For example, a large body of theoretical models has suggested that temporally fluctuating selection pressures are not likely to maintain balanced polymorphisms for genotypes specialized to different states of the environment, but rather drive the fixation of optimally plastic genotypes that are able to alter their phenotypes to match the shifting conditions[1](#R1),[2](#R2),[49](#R49). Yet, more recent models have begun to incorporate various forms of multi-locus dominance, particularly environmentally induced reversals of dominance, and demonstrated that polymorphism at a large number of loci can be maintained in temporally fluctuating environments without imposing unsustainable genetic load on the population[8](#R8),[11](#R11). Our observations add credence to these models, demonstrating how rapidly fluctuating selection could limit the fixation of adaptive alleles in a population. However, whether it indeed is the key force maintaining variation across species ranges is an outstanding empirical issue that will necessitate identifying causal loci and their underlying patterns of dominance in fitness.

It is critical to note that our detection of rapidly fluctuating selection was contingent on our strategy for quantifying changes in genomic variation in the system. Specifically, changes in allele frequencies over our shortest sampling intervals probably represent subtle, yet evolutionarily relevant, shifts in the relative contribution of specific genotypes to the next cohort of flies. Our detection of such fine-scale shifts hinged on our highly replicated system and standardized sampling of the instantaneously reproductive population at each collection point. Furthermore, as demonstrated in [Fig. 2d](#F2),[e](#F2), the resolution of sampling throughout the season dictates our ability to infer instances of fluctuating selection, which are averaged out and remain hidden when evolutionary change is quantified over longer timescales[6](#R6),[50](#R50). We thus conclude that the relatively sparse empirical evidence of fluctuating selection across systems may be due to an ascertainment bias of quantifying evolutionary change on longer timescales[22](#R22),[51](#R51). Indeed, research into patterns of spatially varying selection has indicated that signals of balancing selection over exceptionally fine spatial scales (‘microgeographical’ adaptation) can be masked by coarse sampling[52](#R52). Thus, this pattern may be recapitulated under instances of temporally varying selection, although the ubiquity ofthis dynamic across study systems and species remains to be determined.

Our findings additionally add to a growing body of research into whether adaptation across independently evolving replicate populations proceeds via predictable shifts in genomic variation[28](#R28). We detected a large degree of within-year parallelism and across-year predictability, despite hundreds of putatively unlinked loci driving patterns of adaptive tracking in the system ([Fig. 2c](#F2)). This parallelism and predictability are evident despite substantial variance in starting allele frequencies across replicate cages within and between years, demonstrating that the genomic architecture of adaptive tracking in the system is resilient to perturbations of background allele frequencies ([Figs. 2a](#F2),[b](#F2) and [4](#F4)). The predictable patterns of genomic variation across study years further suggests that despite variation in specific environmental conditions, there exists a generality to the suite of selective pressures imposed on the populations. We hypothesize that this generality is in large part driven by the boom-and-bust ecological dynamics observed in our mesocosm populations, which is ubiquitous to natural populations of *Drosophila* spp. and a range of species with differing life-history characteristics[12](#R12)–[14](#R14). This hypothesis is bolstered by the fact that the most concordant periods of allele frequency change across years were those when the population sizes were in similar phases of the boom-and-bust cycle ([Fig. 4d](#F4)), as well as previous studies reporting concordant allele frequency shifts between the spring and autumn over multiple years[20](#R20) and across widely dispersed *D. melanogaster* populations[21](#R21). It is not feasible to disentangle the relative contribution of these biotic forces from covarying abiotic selective pressures (for example, temperature) with the data collected here, although it may be possible in the future through a systematic manipulation of these variables, such as a staggered initiation of the replicate cages and population trajectories. Finally, it is important to note that overwintering dynamics, which have not been studied in our mesocosm system, may affect the predictability of evolution across years. For example, the severity of winter conditions can influence the predictability of subsequent spring-to-autumn phenotypic evolution in natural populations of the species[16](#R16).

In conclusion, the allele frequency changes that we observed illustrate an instance of adaptive tracking underpinned by strong, rapidly fluctuating selection that induces parallel allele frequency change across 12 independent replicates. Such dynamic patterns of selection are likely to have pronounced effects on patterns of, and the maintenence of, genetic diversity within the species. Given that the observed adaptive tracking is probably driven by continual selection and reshuffling of standing variation, it is not implausible that we could detect evolutionary change over weekly intervals. Still, an important note on the observed timescales is that although the patterns of fluctuating selection were quantified across single weeks, comparing the tempo of adaptive tracking across species should be conducted using evolutionarily relevant units, such as generation time. Previous studies have indeed quantified selection in the wild occurring on sub-generational periods, such as those observed here, but these are largely confined to pulse-selective events, such as hurricanes, harvest and poaching[53](#R53),[54](#R54). Thus, although this may be the first instance of sustained evolutionary change at this tempo, the phenomenon may in fact be widespread in populations evolving with overlapping generations and ultimately revealed by sufficiently resolved time-series genomic data. Finally, the dynamic nature of the adaptive tracking observed lends further promise towards the capacity for species to adaptively track the changes in the environment induced by global change. An important consideration in this context, however, is how dependent such fine-grained adaptive tracking is on the total amount of genetic variation present in populations, which itself may be reduced as global change progresses[55](#R55). Ultimately, resolving the underlying causal genes underpinning adaptive tracking will lend insights into the total number of loci necessary for fine-grained, rapid adaptation in complex selective regimes.

## Methods

### Study population and mesocosm system

We quantified and tracked shifts in genomic variation of a genetically diverse *D. melanogaster* population over a 6-month period in a highly replicated, field mesocosm in Philadelphia, PA, USA. The study population was derived via outbreeding a panel of 76 inbred strains originally collected wild from Linvilla Orchards, Media, PA, USA[15](#R15). Each line had been previously sequenced to a minimum genome-wide coverage of 50×, following 20 generations of full-sib mating. An outbred, baseline population was initiated via combining 10 males and 10 females from every line into each of 4, 30-cm3 replicate indoor cages. Each resulting replicate, outbred population was subject to four generations of unmanipulated recombination and population expansion, a process aimed at purging deleterious alleles that probably accumulated throughout inbreeding and generating a total number of flies sufficient for mesocosm founding. Eggs from the fourth generation were collected and density controlled in vials. Upon eclosure, 500 randomly selected males and females from the same 24-h cohort across all four outbred population replicates were released into each of 12 2-m3 outdoor mesh cages.

Each of the mesocosm cage replicates had a single dwarf peach tree (non-fruiting) and was exposed to natural environmental conditions and insect and microbial communities[22](#R22),[56](#R56). Before seeding the replicates with our outbred population, a leaf blower was used to eliminate any *Drosophila* (or other macroscopic insects) present within the replicate cages. Four hundred millilitres of *Drosophila* media (‘spradling cornmeal recipe’), provided in 900-cm3 aluminium loafpans, was placed in a shaded shelving unit within each cage three times per week, providing the only source of food and egg-laying substrate. Egg laying on each loaf pan was carried out for 2 days, after which a new pan was added, and the original pan was covered with a mesh lid to prevent any further laying. Thus, the total amount of food (and therefore egg laying and larval development substrate) was kept constant throughout the experiment, even as the population sizes rapidly changed. This induced changes in adult and larval competition that mirror those caused by the fixed and finite food available to wild populations during spring and summer expansions. The sealed pans were monitored daily, and the lid was removed after eclosure was first observed. This process generated a near-continual input of new flies into each replicate cage, generating evolution with overlapping generations and a population age structure that varied throughout the duration of the experiment. The census size of the adult population was estimated five times throughout the progression of the experiment (19 July, 5 August, 20 August, 17 September and 20 October). To estimate population sizes, we photographed four, 0.3-m3 transects on the ceiling of each mesocosm within 30 min of sunsets (when adult flies were aggregated on the mesocosm walls). The number of flies within each transect were counted using semi-automated image processing in ImageJ, the average of which was used as the estimated number of adult flies per cubic metre for a given cage and timepoint. Finally, mesocosm-wide temperature was monitored using a weather station located adjacent (within 10 m) of the replicate cages ([Extended Data Fig. 8](#F12)).

### Sample collection, sequencing and allele frequency estimation

We conducted high-frequency sampling of the cage replicates to quantify patterns of genomic variation throughout the experiment. The first sampling was conducted on 13 July 2021, 3 weeks after the 21 June seeding of the mesocosms (a period aiming to further purge residual deleterious mutations and allow the laboratory-adapted population to experience 1–2 generations of field selection). Samples were collected weekly for the first 9 weeks of the experiment (13 July to 7 September), after which samples were collected on 21 September, 20 October and following the first freeze and population crash on 20 December (12 total timepoints; [Fig. 1](#F1)). Note that egg samples for two replicates (replicates 3 and 7) were lost during the *t*1 sampling point, and eggs for one replicate (replicate 5) were lost during the *t*3 sampling point. As a consequence, allele frequency data are missing for these samples.

Throughout the experiment, the replicate populations evolved with overlapping generations and thus exhibited shifting age structure as the experiment progressed[24](#R24). It was thus critical to account for this demographic structure in quantifying the ecological and genetic state of the system to ensure that our quantification of allele frequencies reflected shifts to the instantaneously reproductive genotypes, or ‘leading edge’, of the population[24](#R24). Accordingly, at each timepoint, eggs were collected overnight directly from the mesocosms, and larval development was carried out in laboratory-based conditions, where eggs developed and eclosed to F1 adults in 30-cm3 cages. Three-to-five days post-eclosure, a random set of 100 females were sampled from each cage and preserved in 99% ethanol at −20 °C for later DNA extraction. The individuals sequenced both survived larval competition (the intensity of which changes as a function of the size of the adult population in the replicate cages) and were derived from a parental generation that experienced fecundity and viability selection in the mesocosm at the time of sampling. As we expect allele frequencies between sexes of offspring from the same parents to be equivalent, we discarded males from sequencing to obtain higher overall coverage on chromosomes 2, 3 and X (due to the elimination ofthe Y chromosome within sequenced genomic DNA). This allowed us to focus analysis solely on alleles present in both sexes. While there exist many different possible ways to derive allele frequency estimates from our replicates through time (for example, sampling adults directly from cages), our specific goal was to identify changes in the relative fitness of the instantaneously fecund and viable portion of the population in a highly consistent and standardized manner that minimized sources ofnoise that could obscure underlying patterns of deterministic allele frequency changes. Our approach thus derives allele frequency estimates solely from the offspring of those individuals that were viable and reproductive at the time of sampling.

We collected an additional, independent sample of 100 females from each cage at *t*3 (26 July) and *t*10 (20 October) to quantify biological variation in our allele frequency estimates. Furthermore, an additional four replicate samples of the baseline population before field exposure were preserved for genomic analysis. Finally, it is important to note that the final sample point (22 December) occurred following the crash of the adult population, and as a result sampling methodology for this timepoint was altered such that larvae were collected directly from the remaining loaf pans within each cage (that is, no reproductively viable adults remained in the cage at this timepoint, but larvae persisting in cage loaf pans were taken into the laboratory and reared to adults for pooled sequencing). In addition, two replicate mesocosms were unable to be sampled at this final timepoint, reducing replication. We accommodate for these differences in sampling of *t*12 by focusing analysis on patterns of variation throughout the summer-to-autumn transition (*t*1 to *t*11; see below).

We generated high-accuracy, haplotype-informed allele frequency estimates at most of the common polymorphic sites within the species. Specifically, genomic DNA was extracted from each homogenized pool of 100 flies using the Monarch Genomic DNA Purification Kit (New England Biolabs). DNA quality was assessed using Nanodrop and quantified with Qubit, before whole-genome sequencing libraries were generated for each pooled sample using the Illumina DNA Prep Tagmentation Kit. All samples were sequenced on Illumina Novaseq 6000 flow cells using 150 bp, paired-end reads. A total of three separate sequencing rounds were required to generate sufficient coverage across all samples, and technical replication across rounds were used to quantify the negligible effect of batch effects on allele frequency estimation ([Extended Data Fig. 2](#F6)). Raw sequencing reads were trimmed of adapter sequences and bases with quality score of less than 20, and aligned to the *D. melanogaster* v5.39 reference genome using bwa and default parameters[57](#R57). Aligned reads were deduplicated using Picard tools ([http://broadinstitute.github.io/picard/](http://broadinstitute.github.io/picard/)), and the final set of reads for each sample was downsampled to obtain an equivalent, genome-wide coverage of 8× across all samples.

We computed haplotype-derived allele frequency estimates using a local inference method and pipeline developed by [ref. 58](#R58). In brief, using an expectation-maximization algorithm for estimating haplotype frequencies in a pooled sample from mapped sequence reads (originally developed by [ref. 59](#R59)), we obtained allele frequencies at sites previously identified in the genome sequences of each founding inbred strain. We conducted haplotype inference in window sizes that varied proportionally to the length of unrecombined haplotype blocks expected as a function of the estimated number of generations since the original outbreeding. Allele frequencies were compiled to only include those sites with an average minor allele frequency > 0.02 in the baseline population and present in at least one evolved sample at a minor allele frequency > 0.01. We validated that our haplotype-informed allele frequencies replicated across different sets of subsampled reads from the same sample, and produced an accuracy of allele frequency estimates that is comparable with deep sequencing and standard methods for pooled, allele frequency estimation[58](#R58),[60](#R60) ([Supplementary Figs. 24](#SD4) and [25](#SD4)).

### Quantifying genome-wide patterns of variation

All statistical analyses of allele frequency data were carried out in R v3.5.6. We first quantified the extent to which variation in the baseline populations was representative of variation in wild populations of *D. melanogaster*. Specifically, we used a population genetic summary statistic, *F*ST, to explore genome-wide patterns of differentiation between the four replicate baseline samples and a broad geographical, natural population sampling dataset previously published[21](#R21). This dataset includes 5 years of spring and autumn sampling from the original population from which our baseline was derived (Media, PA, USA), as well as samples collected in spring and autumn throughout eastern North America, California and three locales in Europe (Ukraine, Spain and Austria). We compared genome-wide *F*ST between our baseline populations, the wild Pennsylvania population from which it was derived, and populations throughout North America and Europe to quantitatively validate that the genomic variation in our replicate mesocosm populations was representative of that observed, and subject to selection, in natural populations.

Next, we used *F*ST to determine whether observed shifts in genomic variation exceeded sources of technical and biological noise throughout the sampling period. Specifically, we quantified genomic differentiation through time by computing *F*ST between each evolved sample and each of the four baseline samples (*F*ST was computed separately for each polymorphic site and averaged across all sites to obtain a genome-wide mean of differentiation). Next, we quantified sources of biological and technical noise by computing *F*ST pairwise between technical sequencing replicates (same 100 flies and pooled DNA extract, different library preparation and sequencing rounds; *n* = 20 pairwise comparisons) and biological replicates (same cage and timepoint, different sample of 100 flies; *n* = 17 pairwise comparisons). We tested whether evolutionary-driven changes in genomic variation exceeded sources of noise using a two-tailed Student’s *t*-test, and whether evolved samples became increasingly differentiated from the baseline population throughout the experiment using a GLM.

We tested whether variance in allele frequencies across all collected samples was dominated by replicate cage or collection timepoint using PCA (prcomp package). Allele frequencies were centred and scaled, and each sample was projected on the first two principal components. We ran this analysis both genome-wide, as well as separately for SNPs on each chromosomal arm. We conducted *k*-means clustering of the principal component scores for each sample (stats package) to, in an unsupervised manner, quantify the extent to which principal component clustering was predominantly driven by replicate cage or collection time. Specifically, if *k*-means clustering of sample points corresponded to sample collection timepoint (that is, segregated early from late season samples), this provided a quantitative validation of trends visualized along the first and second principal components.

### Target SNP identification and characterization of genetic architecture

We identified SNPs shifting systematically through time and across replicates by fitting allele frequencies to a GLM (formula: allele frequency–timepoint)[22](#R22),[25](#R25). Allele frequencies were weighted by the total number of chromosomes sequenced (*n* = 200) and depth per sample[58](#R58). GLMs for each site were run with a quasibinomial error model, and *P* values were adjusted using the Benjamini–Hochberg FDR correction (p.adjust package). We considered a SNP significant if it exhibited an FDR < 0.2 and effect size > 0.5%. We used this approach to identify SNPs shifting systematically throughout the entire summer-to-autumn transition (*t*1 to *t*11; before total population crash and reduction in mesocosm-wide replication of *t*12). We also used this approach to determine the interval length for which we had sufficient power to identify significant, relatively large effect size SNPs (FDR < 0.05 and effect size > 1%) across all chromosomal arms. Finally, we explored how SNP discovery changed when using intermediate timepoint data in the model. For example, for regressions of allele frequencies from *t*1 to *t*3, we compared the number of SNPs identified using data from *t*1, *t*2 and *t*3, as well only end timepoint data (that is, just data from *t*1 and *t*3). The increase in SNP discovery using intermediate timepoint data informed our decision to incorporate intermediate timepoints in all subsequent regressions.

We used our GLM results to explore the genetic architecture of adaptation in the system. Specifically, we implemented a method developed and described by Rudman et al.[22](#R22) to identify unlinked clusters of the genome enriched in significant SNPs. In brief, this method scores each SNP as follows: FDR > 0.2 (0); FDR < 0.2 (1); FDR < 0.05 and effect size > 2% (2); and FDR < 0.01 and effect size > 2% (3). The average SNP score is then calculated for sliding windows of 500 SNPs, using a 100 SNP step size. An empirical FDR for each window is generated by comparing observed scores to those generated for permuted windows, in which SNP site information is randomly shuffled and window scores are re-computed. Windows with an empirical FDR < 0.05 are defined as significantly enriched, and overlapping enriched windows are merged into a single, enriched window. To winnow the list of enriched windows to a set of putatively unlinked (independently segregating) loci for each comparison, the squared correlation coefficient of founder genotypes (SNP-pair linkage) is calculated between all pairs of significant SNPs within 1 Mb of each other. If the average SNP-pair linkage between two clusters is more than 0.03 (the value at which average SNP-pair linkage decays to within chromosomal arms; [Extended Data Fig. 3](#F7)), the clusters are merged. This process proceeds iteratively until all pairs of clusters exhibited average linkage of less than 0.03 (source code provided at [https://github.com/greensii/dros-adaptive-tracking](https://github.com/greensii/dros-adaptive-tracking)). We computed linkage between the top 50 SNPs of each locus (located within 1 Mb of each other) to further validate that the SNPs defining each independent locus were not simply driven by high levels of linkage to SNPs in adjacent clusters ([Extended Data Fig. 3](#F7)).

We further evaluated our unlinked locus identification procedure by examining the correlations of allele frequency dynamics for SNPs within and between loci. Specifically, we randomly sampled SNPs iteratively across all unlinked clusters and computed correlations in allele frequency movement of SNPs within the focal locus, and between SNPs in loci on the same and different chromosomal arms (compared SNPs exhibited starting frequencies and effect sizes in the same decile and with SNP-pair linkage that fell below our threshold for merging putatively linked clusters (*r*2 < 0.03); *n* = 1,000,0000 total comparisons). We evaluated differences in correlated allele frequency movement within and between putatively unlinked loci to infer whether long-distance patterns of linkage (for example, among inversions and polymorphisms located outside their breakpoints) could induce a degree of non-independence among loci that was not detected by our original clustering procedure.

### Quantifying fluctuating selection using leave-one-out cross-validation

We quantified the amount of parallel, neutral and anti-parallel evolutionary change for individual replicate cages by re-implementing our GLM analysis using a leave-one-out cross-validation approach; sets of GLM-significant SNPs (target SNPs) were identified iteratively in *n* −1 ‘training’ cages, and the frequency dynamics of the rising alleles (Benjamini–Hochberg FDR < 0.2, allele frequency change > 0.5%) were then quantified in the left-out, ‘test’, cage. A set of control SNPs were selected for every set of target SNPs, in which each control SNP was matched on the following: chromosomal arm, ±5% baseline population frequency, ±0.5 cM Mb−1 recombination rate and inversion status (that is, inside or outside an inversion). The distributions of shifts between sets of target and matched control SNPs were then compared using a paired, two-tailed Student’s *t*-test to: (1) determine whether the magnitude of target shifts significantly exceeded (FDR < 0.05) background allele frequency change, and (2) infer whether the direction of non-neutral shifts were in a predominantly concordant (that is, parallel) direction as that observed in the training cages (positive median allele frequency shift) or exhibited significantly anti-parallel behaviour (negative median allele frequency shift). For a given left-out cage and set of target SNPs, changes between parallel and anti-parallel behaviour across test intervals were indicative of a fluctuation in the dominant direction of selection. As target SNP behaviour was always measured in the left-out cage, inference of fluctuating selection was insensitive to statistical artefacts, such as regression to the mean, which can arise spuriously in autocorrelated time-series data.

We first implemented this GLM and leave-one-out approach using sets of target SNPs generated across the entire summer-to-autumn transition (*t*1 → *t*11). The frequency dynamics of target SNPs within left-out cages were measured from *t*1 to *t*11 (the interval over which they were identified in the training cages), separately for each chromosomal arm and genome-wide. Next, to resolve the pace of adaptive tracking in the system and explore the scale at which signals of fluctuating selection arise, we re-measured allele frequency shifts of *t*1 → *t*11 SNPs in increasingly shorter intervals. Specifically, we measured SNP behaviour in all intervals starting at *t*1 and of decreasing length from *t*1 to *t*10 (11 weeks) through *t*1 to *t*2 (1 week). The sustained deterministic behaviour of SNPs across replicates during the *t*1 → *t*2 interval motivated assessment of SNP behaviour in each pair of single timepoints (*t*1 → *t*2, *t*2 → *t*3…*t*11 → *t*12), the first nine of which are single-week intervals. This final pair of single timepoints (*t*11 → *t*12) includes the first observed freezing of the year ([Fig. 1](#F1)) and the total crash of the adult fly population within each replicate.

We next aimed to quantify the pervasiveness of fluctuating selection throughout the experimental period. Accordingly, we re-conducted our GLM and leave-one-out approach in all interval lengths greater than five timepoints (for example, *t*1–*t*6…*t*7–*t*12; a five-timepoint interval length represented the shortest producing substantial, genome-wide GLM signal across all chromosomal arms; [Supplementary Fig. 3](#SD4)). For each set of target SNPs, we measured their allele frequency shifts across the timepoints over which they were identified, as well as all single-timepoint comparisons (for example, for SNPs identified during *t*1 → *t*6, we quantified allele frequency shifts from *t*1 to *t*6, as well as *t*1 to *t*2, *t*2 to *t*3…*t*11 to *t*12). For each set of target SNPs, we excluded sites identified as strongly parallel (FDR < 0.05) throughout the summer-to-autumn transition (*t*1 → *t*11). This approach thus explicitly eliminates those sites subject to primarily directional selection throughout the experimental period to quantify whether finer modalities of environmental change drive systematic shifts in allele frequencies across cages. Finally, we quantified the changes in the dominant direction of selection throughout the experiment for each individual cage by enumerating the number of instances that sets of target SNPs switched from being predominantly favoured (significantly positive median phased allele frequency change) to predominantly selected against (significantly negative median phased allele frequency change), or vice versa, across all pairs of single timepoints.

We used a set of biological replicate samples collected at *t*3 and *t*11 to further validate that observed patterns of fluctuating selection are not simply a function of inherently noisy behaviour of our target SNPs (for example, starting frequencies, recombination rates and proximity to overlapping structural variants). Specifically, we calculated the variance of allele frequency estimates between biological replicate samples (same cage and timepoint, different sample of 100 flies) at sets of target SNPs (sets obtained via 12 iterations of leave-one-out GLM cross-validation) identified from summer through autumn (*t*1 → *t*11; [Fig. 2f](#F2)) and across all five timepoint intervals ([Fig. 3](#F3)). We compared these variances to those generated via sets of matched control SNPs (matched on chromosomal arm, starting frequency, recombination rate and overlapping structural variants) using a Wilcoxon rank-sum test.

### Inferring fluctuating selection via allele frequency covariances

We validated the pervasive signals of fluctuating selection identified using our leave-one-out cross-validation by reanalysing our allele frequency data using a previously developed method[4](#R4),[27](#R27). Specifically, this method computes covariances of allele frequency changes between sampling intervals to quantify the extent to which linked selection, as opposed to drift, dominates a genomic time series. We computed covariances between all consecutive timepoint intervals, excluding covariances between adjacent time intervals as their values are biased by shared technical noise. Covariances are scaled by the heterozygosity at the start of each interval, which probably varies as a function of population size across replicates and through time; however, direct estimates of population size (for example, via our census measurements) are not explicitly incorporated by the method. We further used the method to compute convergent correlations, which quantify the extent to which allele frequency dynamics are shared (that is, parallel) across independent replicate cages. Uncertainty of the covariance calculations was assessed using a block bootstrap procedure, with 5,000 bootstrap replicates and a block size of 500 kb.

### Quantifying the predictability of independent bouts of adaptive tracking

We leveraged results reported in Rudman et al.[22](#R22) to probe the extent to which independent bouts of adaptive tracking in the system were underpinned by predictable shifts in genomic variation. The Rudman et al. study took place during the 2014 summer-to-autumn transition, using the same outdoor mesocosm system (with a total of 10 replicate cages) and a total of 81 inbred strains, 51 of which were also used in this study (the 25 strains unique to this study were derived from flies collected at the same local Pennsylvania orchard population as overlapping strains). Rudman et al.[22](#R22) collected samples for analysis of genomic variation monthly (five total timepoints, spanning 25 July to 7 November) and used identical methods of haplotype-derived allele frequency estimation, GLM-based identification of sites moving systematically through time and clustering of enriched regions of genomic variation.

We first used PCA to explore the effect of experimental year and collection time on variance in allele frequencies across all samples from both studies. Although the experiments were initiated on different calendar days and extended different periods of time, we standardized collection time by days since initial founding of the mesocosm replicates. We projected each sample onto the first two principal components to ask whether variance in allele frequencies is predominantly driven by experimental year or collection time. Next, we tested whether, despite interannual differences in founder population allele frequencies and environmental conditions, a parallel set of SNPs underpinned seasonal adaptation across years. We compared the allele frequency shifts obtained across all interval lengths and comparisons of this study to each of the four monthly intervals of the Rudman et al.[22](#R22) study (270 total comparisons). For each comparison, we computed Pearson correlations between observed allele frequency shifts (*n* = ~1.6 million SNPs with overlapping data across studies). Positive correlations indicated that SNPs moved in a concordant manner between the tested 2014 and 2021 intervals, and negative correlations to SNPs moving in predominantly opposing directions between years. To infer whether observed correlations exceeded those expected by chance, we computed a distribution of null correlations by shuffling the allele frequency shift value for one of the SNPs to that of a separate matched SNP (same chromosomal arm, starting frequency, recombination rate and inversion status, as above) and re-computing the correlation. We compared the distribution of observed and shuffled correlations using an *F*-test to determine whether an excess of parallel (and anti-parallel) intervals were observed between years. We re-conducted this analysis separately for each chromosomal arm. Finally, to explore whether those intervals with heightened allele frequency concordance across years aligned with conspicuous shifts in the selective environment, we identified the interval of the current study that exhibited the highest genome-wide correlation to each of the four monthly intervals of the 2014 experiment.
