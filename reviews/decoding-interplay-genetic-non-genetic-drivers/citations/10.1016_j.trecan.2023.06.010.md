## Perivascular Niches: critical hubs in cancer evolution

Ada Nowosad 1,2 , Jean-Christophe Marine 1,2* and Panagiotis Karras 1,2* 1 Laboratory for Molecular Cancer Biology, VIB Center for Cancer Biology, KU Leuven, Leuven, Belgium 2 Department of Oncology, KU Leuven, Leuven, Belgium *Correspondence: jeanchristophe.marine@kuleuven.be, panagiotis.karras@kuleuven.be

**Abstract** Tumors are heterogeneous ecosystems in which cancer cells co-exist within a complex tumor immune microenvironment (TIME). The malignant, stromal and immune cell compartments establish a plethora of bi-directional cell-cell communication crosstalks, that drastically influence tumor growth and metastatic dissemination, which we are only beginning to understand. Cancer cells either co-opt or promote the formation of new blood and lymphatic vessels to cope with their need in nutrients and oxygen. Recent studies have highlighted additional key roles for the tumor vasculature and identified the perivascular niche as a cellular hub, where intricate and dynamic cellular interactions promote cancer stemness, immune evasion, dormancy and/or metastatic spreading. Here, we review these findings, and discuss how they may be exploited therapeutically.

**Keywords** Perivascular niche, cancer stemness, cell-cell communication, immune evasion, cell plasticity, metastasis.

## The perivascular niche: beyond nutrients and oxygen.

A constant supply of nutrients and oxygen is required for tumor growth. To this end, tumor cells induce de novo angiogenesis by secreting vascular endothelial growth factor (VEGF) to recruit endothelial cells (ECs, see glossary) and thus create new blood vessels through sprouting from pre-existing vessel(s) (Figure 1A) [1,2]. Cancer cells also line existing blood vessels and migrate among the vasculature to give rise to metastasis (a process of tumor vessel co-option) (Figure 1B) [3]. In some rare cases, tumors may recruit bone marrow-derived hemangioblasts and EC precursors to initiate de novo generation of new blood vessels through vasculogenesis (Figure **1C) [4]. Tumor cells may even adopt an EC-like phenotype to generate vascular** channels which deliver nutrients and oxygen to tumors thought a process known as vasculogenic mimicry (Figure 1D) [5,6]. Regardless of the mechanism(s) by which the tumor vasculature is formed, tumor- associated blood vessels often display abnormalities in many aspects of their structure and function [7]. Tumor vessels can be leaky, and thereby facilitate tumor cell dissemination and metastasis. Importantly, there is converging evidence that the tumor vasculature may function as a central hub to promote cancer stemness, and thereby stimulate tumor growth independently of its role as a supplier of oxygen and nutrients. Nikolova, Strilic and Lammert used the term “’vascular niche” when referring to a microenvironment generated by ECs and mural cells that affects the behaviour of adjacent tumor cells [8]. Recent evidence indicates that non-vascular cells present in this niche, such as fibroblasts may also directly influence cancer cell phenotype **(Figure 2) [9,10].** Herein, we review recent findings supporting an emerging model in which the complex TIME assembled around the tumor vasculature (that we hereafter refer to as the perivascular niche) functions as a dynamic, bidirectional and heterotypic cell-cell communication hub that influences tumor evolution and response to therapy.

## Endothelial cells, beyond the building blocks of the perivascular niche

ECs form the monolayer that lines blood and lymph vessels and allow the delivery of nutrients and oxygen to the surrounding tissue. As blood vessels allow immune cells access to tissues, they also have an immune-modulatory function. Under physiological conditions, ECs respond to changes in their environment by releasing a subset of signaling molecules that drive angiogenesis and tissue remodeling [11].

In tumors, chronic exposure to metabolic and mechanical stimuli may alter the cytogenetic, morphological and phenotypic characteristics of ECs [12–16]. The plasticity of tumor-associated ECs (TECs) is illustrated by their ability to acquire cancer-associated fibroblast (CAF)-like [17] or pericyte-like [18] phenotypes by engaging endothelial-to-mesenchymal transition programs (EndMT) [19]. Co-culturing ECs with breast cancer cells promotes a reversible acquisition of a mesenchymal phenotype by ECs in a Notch and TGFβ-dependent manner, which in turn favours tumor growth and stemness [20]. This highlights the ability of tumor cells to promote the formation of a transient niche that spatiotemporally coordinates their own expansion (and differentiation) by modulating TEC plasticity. TECs can indeed exhibit stem cell-like properties, which enable them to promote angiogenesis and tumor growth [21]. In glioma, CD133 + cancer stem-like cells stimulate angiogenesis via VEGF secretion, and thereby enhance tumor vascularization. [2]. Similarly, Ogha and colleagues showed that TECs of aggressive metastatic tumors stimulate the malignant compartment to express cancer stemness- related genes [22].

Immunomodulation and tumor-associated ECs The tumor vasculature acts as a major gatekeeper of immune cell trafficking and thereby inflammation [23]. In melanoma, interferon (IFN) type I secreted by TECs activates stimulator of IFN genes (STING), an adaptor molecule responsible for cytosolic DNA sensing, triggering a CD8 T-cell response that limits tumor growth [24]. Unexpectedly, however, accumulating evidence supports a TEC-mediated immunosuppressive role for the perivascular niche [23,25]. In lung cancer, TECs express high levels of death mediator Fas ligand, which selectively promotes the killing of CD8 + T cells and accumulation of T-regulatory cells (T-regs) to favour an immunosuppressive environment [26]. In keeping, Sharma et al. described the presence of a population of fetal-associated PLVAP + ECs in human hepatocarcinoma that, together with fetal-like (FOLR2) tumor-associated macrophages, promotes an immunosuppressive perivascular niche environment. This onco-foetal program is directly controlled by PLVAP + ECs via Notch and VEGFR-signaling. Notably, PLVAP + EC population has not been found in healthy normal liver tissues, suggesting that the de-differentiation of ECs may be induced by tumor cells themselves (Figure 3A) [27]. Similarly, in lung and breast cancer, TECs promote an immunosuppressive

environment by downregulating genes involved in antigen presentation (MHCI and II), chemotaxis and immune cell homing [15]. In melanoma, TECs upregulate IDO1 (Indoleamine 2, 3-Dioxygenase 1) upon CD40-stimulating immunotherapy, in response to secretion of IFNγ by T-cells, creating a forward feed-back loop that inhibits T-cell activation [28]. ECs may act as semi-professional antigen presenting cells (APCs) expressing gene signatures related to antigen processing and presentation [23]. APC gene signature is downregulated in TECs when compared to healthy lung and breast tissues [15,29], indicating that restoration of APC functions in TECs may constitute a promising anti- cancer treatment avenue. Accordingly, concomitant targeting of VEGF, Angiopoeitin- 2 and PD1 in glioblastoma induces reprogramming of TECs into APCs and triggers T- cell infiltration and anti-tumor response [30]. Finally, ECs may also modulate the innate immune response by driving angiocrine epigenetic-metabolic reprogramming of macrophages [31] or by acquiring a macrophage-like phenotype [33]. However, the clinical relevance of these findings and the molecular mechanisms underlying TEC-induced anti-tumor innate immunity remain poorly understood.

Vasculogenic mimicry and endothelial trans-differentiation of tumor cells Vasculogenic mimicry (VM) was first described in the context of melanoma more than 2 decades ago [6] and later observed in other types of cancer. VM is thought to be an adaptive response of tumors to hypoxic conditions [5,32]. VM refers to perfusable channels and network lined by malignant cells expressing EC-specific markers such as CD144 (VE-cadherin), Tie-1, and Eph2 [33]. Tumor cells are capable of VM exhibiting endothelial-like properties and high phenotypic plasticity [34]. Stem-like populations (CD133 + /ABCB5 + ) co-localize in perivascular niches express the EC marker VE-cadherin, suggesting that ECs promote a CSC phenotype. In line with this, depletion of CD133 in EC-like melanoma cells reduced tumor growth [35]. In glioblastoma, a fraction of ECs in tumor vasculature exhibits the same genetic alteration detected in malignant cells, indicating their neoplastic origin [36,37]. CSCs differentiate to functional endothelium in vitro and in vivo. Anti-angiogenic therapy blocking VEGF signaling prevented the terminal maturation of CSC-derived endothelial progenitors into endothelium but not differentiation of CSCs into endothelial progenitors. However, Notch1/γ-secretase inhibition prevented the

transition of CSCs into endothelial progenitors [36]. While molecular determinants that induce VM remains elusive, ETV2, a master regulator of endothelial cell development, is highly correlated with malignancy. Ectopic expression of ETV2 represses the neurogenic commitment of CSCs and their reprograming into ECs [38]. Finally, breast cancer cells acquire an EC-like phenotype through expression of Serpine2 and Slpi secreted proteins acting as anticoagulants at the vascular/extravascular interface, thus promoting perfusion of the tumor vasculature and dissemination through the bloodstream [39]. Cancer cells may also acquire EC-like phenotype via a process of trans-differentiation into ECs (EndT, Figure 3B) [38,40] whereby malignant cells gain the expression of EC genes and localize at the inner layer of blood vessels entering quiescence [5,41]. By mimicking surrounding ECs, those cells may escape immune surveillance and remain quiescent/dormant for long periods of time.

TECs-derived extrinsic factors as drivers of tumor growth TECs provide a vascular niche within the tumor microenvironment that supports the survival and self-renewal of CSCs. Tumor-derived VEGF engages the activation of TECs expressing high levels of VEGFR-2, promoting angiogenesis and supporting the maintenance of CSCs within the vascular niche [42]. However, TECs can also produce factors that favor tumor growth and maintain stemness at the perivascular niches. To dissect the molecular mechanisms underlying the ability of TECs to reprogram tumor cells, co-culture experiments remain the reductionist model of choice. Using such a model system, it was shown that TECs interact with glioblastoma cells via paracrine signalling to sustain their dedifferentiated phenotype and self-renewal properties. Accordingly, co-transplantation assays accelerated tumor growth, highlighting a critical role for TECs in promoting tumor initiation and stemness [43]. In melanoma, our group demonstrated that TECs also induce a dedifferentiation phenotype. Consistently, using lineage tracing combined with single-cell and spatial transcriptomics, we provided evidence that dedifferentiated melanoma cells, which harbour an embryonic pre-migratory neural crest stem cell transcriptional program, preferentially localize to the perivascular niche [44]. In B cell lymphoma, tumor-derived FGF4 upregulated the Notch ligand Jagged1 (Jag1) adjacent to ECs. This led to the activation of Notch2-Hey1 pathway in tumor cells and conversion into lymphoma- initiating cells (CD44/IGF1R/CSF1R + ), characterized by high invasive potential and

chemoresistance **(Figure 3C)** [45]. In hepatocellular carcinoma and lung cancer models, TECs secrete insulin growth factor (IGF1), which activates an IGF1-FGF4 signalling cascade in malignant cells and promotes the expansion of stem-like IGF1R + cancer cells (Figure 3D) [46]. The cumulative evidence indicates that TECs have functions that extend beyond their conventional role as structural components of tumor-associated blood vessels. Their identity and function may be hijacked by tumor cells to promote growth and metastatic dissemination. This underscores the complexity of the TIME and highlights the need disentangle the interplay between TECs and tumor shaping the landscape of tumor ecosystem.

## Mural cells, protecting the vessels and more.

Mural cells include smooth muscle cells and pericytes, which cover the abluminal surface of blood vessels. They participate to vessel development and homeostasis through their interactions with ECs and blood vessel stabilization . EC-derived PDGF- B binds to PDGFRb on pericytes and recruits them to newly formed blood vessels [47]. Cancer cells also release PDGFb to increase the recruitment of pericytes to tumor blood vessels and stimulate tumor growth. This was observed both in melanoma [48] and glioma [49]. Additionally, tumor-secreted PDGFb attracts pericytes towards cancer cells, limiting their interaction with ECs on the vasculature. This results in defective and leaky blood vessel structure, thus favouring metastatic dissemination [50]. In keeping, mural cells coverage negatively correlated with proliferation rates of ECs, limiting their angiogenic behaviour [51]. In addition to the crosstalk with endothelium, mural cells also directly communicate with tumor cells. Wong et al. described a positive correlation between the percentage of mural-β3-integrin-negative tumor blood vessels and tumor size. Mechanistically, mural-cell β3-integrin loss promoted FAK/HGFR/Akt/p65 signaling, driving CCL2 production, which in turn activated MEK1/ROCK2 pathways and enhanced tumor cell survival and growth [52]. Pericytes act independently and beyond of their interactions with endothelium [59]. In response to pro-inflammatory stimuli, pericytes control the trafficking of cells of the innate and adaptative immune systems. For instance, pericyte-derived CXCL8 and CXCL1 mediate neutrophil recruitment to the site of inflammation whereas CCL2 attract monocytes [53]. Hence, pericytes play an active and pleiotropic role in modulating the microenvironment of blood vessels, beyond their traditional function of providing structural support to ECs. However, the mechanisms underlying the dualistic function of pericytes, acting as both promoters and suppressors of tumor progression, remain incompletely understood and require further investigation.

## CAFs, architects of ECM remodelling and modulators of immune response

Perivascular fibroblasts emerge early during development and serve as pericyte precursors. Fibroblasts were also shown to stabilize blood vessels by establishing the extracellular matrix (ECM) around nascent vessels. In line with this, genetic ablation of perivascular fibroblasts in zebrafish disrupt newly formed blood vessels, suggesting their essential role during development [73]. Similarly, in tumors, CAFs secrete chemical signals, produce components of the ECM and fuel tumor cell metabolism by exchanging nutrients, proteins, lipids and even mitochondria with other cellular compartments [9,10,74,75]. Finally, they support stemness by engaging WNT and NOTCH signalling (Figure 3C) [76].

CAFs constitute a mixture of phenotypically distinct populations with contrasting roles in tumor dynamics. Consistently, not all CAF populations have the same potential to induce CSC phenotype [9]. Rudnick et al. showed that only fibroblasts expressing high levels of prostaglandin (PGE2) can increase the number of cancer stem-like cells in breast tissue via IL-6 secretion (Figure 3C) [77]. CAFs expressing CD10 and GPR77 can be the source of IL-6 via p65-NF-κB signalling, providing a niche for CSCs [81]. Thus, CAFs are often classified as myofibroblast-like CAFs (myCAFs) and inflammatory CAFs (iCAFs) [78]. When myCAFs remodel ECM, iCAFs interact with immune cells and promote an immunosuppressive environment to support tumor growth. In myCAFs, activated YAP promotes matrix stiffening, cell invasion and angiogenesis in breast cancer models [79]. Rho/ROCK signalling enhances the mechanical activity of CAFs, promoting the growth of the tumor vasculature [80]. The advent of single-cell RNAseq approaches allowed a deep dissection of the magnitude of the diversity of CAF subpopulations at the perivascular niche and their contribution to tumor growth [81–83]. Recently, Verginadis et al. showed that ATF4, a key regulator of integrated stress response, is responsible for the conversion of vascular fibroblasts into pro-tumorigenic, collagen-producing myCAFs [84]. Furthermore, depletion of fibroblast activation protein (FAP), expressed in CAFs, through adoptive transfer of FAP-targeted chimeric antigen receptor T cells, disrupts the integrity of intratumoral ECM and vasculature, reducing tumor growth in pancreatic and lung mouse cancer models [85]. Many reports highlighted the effect of iCAFs on immune cell recruitment and differentiation. They promote an immunosuppressive environment by conferring TAMs with a pro-tumorigenic phenotype [86] and by favoring the infiltration of myeloid- derived suppressor cells within the tumor bed [87]. In esophageal squamous cell carcinoma, iCAF-derived Wnt2 suppressed the differentiation and activation of dendritic cells and CD8 T-cells [88]. The immunomodulatory role of CAFs was further demonstrated in pancreatic adenocarcinoma, where a CAF subtype that expresses MHC class II and CD74, referred now to as antigen presenting CAFs (apCAFs), has the capacity to present antigens to T cells to boost anti-tumor immunity [81]. Thus, CAFs constitute a diverse and adaptable group of cells whose interaction with other cellular constituents of the perivascular microenvironment can elicit divergent effects. Therefore, a comprehensive analysis of the distinct phenotypic subpopulations

of CAFs can shed light on their precise contributions to perivascular niche maintenance.

## The perivascular niche as a hub for metastatic dissemination.

In addition to control (primary) tumor growth, the perivascular niche may represent a direct access to the blood circulation, and thereby a door to metastatic dissemination. Metastasis is indeed a multistep process that begins with the breakout of cancer cells from the primary tumor [89]. While most tumors use the hematogenous circulation to spread, the dissemination of tumor cells may also occur via lymphatics, the perineural system or through direct infiltration of adjacent tissue. During hematogenous dissemination, cancer cells enter neoangiogenic capillaries and eventually venous circulation allowing them to spread throughout the body. Pericytes were shown to limit hematogenous metastasis through stabilization of blood vessels [90,91]. Mechanistically, pericyte depletion causes hypoxia and MET receptor activation, which leads cancer cells to undergo epithelial-to-mesenchymal transition (EMT). Thus, pericytes prevent the acquisition of an invasive phenotype by cancer cells, by ensuring the functionality of blood vessels and thereby proper oxygenation of the tumor ecosystem [92]. However, cancer cells may still find their way to intravasate and disseminate. In breast cancer models, Tie2-expressing TAMs directly interact with ECs, increase vascular permeability and, thereby, facilitate cancer cell intravasation [93]. The metabolic interplay between ECs and tumor cells promotes reprogramming and dissemination [94]. ECs suppress the expression of phosphoglycerate dehydrogenase (PHGDH) in breast cancer cells, thereby boosting their metastatic potential [94]. Disseminated tumor cells (DTCs) are not always closely associated with the perivascular niche [50]. Instead, they can spread through the lymph nodes, which may facilitate immune tolerance [100] and protect against ferroptosis [95]. The lymphatic environment, characterized by higher levels of glutathione and oleic acid, along with reduced levels of free iron, protect DTCs from ferroptosis and enhance their survival-into the bloodstream. Interestingly, it was recently shown that non-CSCs (LGR5) are at the origin of dissemination in colorectal cancer, while once they colonize distant organs, they switch to CSCs (LGR5+) fueling metastatic outgrowth [96]. These findings suggest that the escape of DTCs from the primary site via blood vessels is not always linked to stemness and can be context-dependent.

During dissemination only a small fraction of DTCs survive the dynamic metabolic, mechanical and immune challenges giving rise to secondary tumors at distant sites [89]. Frequently, DTCs induce programmed necrosis (necroptosis) in ECs to facilitate exit from the circulation (extravasation, Figure 4A). Necroptosis is mediated by the RIPK1/RIPK3/MLKL pathway and triggered by the interaction between amyloid precursor protein (APP) on the tumor surface and death receptor 6 protein (DR6) on ECs. Extravasation occurs through direct EC necroptosis or the release of damage- associated molecular pattern molecules (DAMPs) from dying ECs, which disrupt the endothelial barrier and enhance tumor migration properties [97]. At the secondary/distant sites, DTCs remain at the perivascular niche in proximity of blood capillaries where they enter quiescence, allowing them to escape from the innate and adaptative immune response (Figure 4B) [98]. In contrast to primary tumors where presence of pericytes limits cancer dissemination [90], brain pericytes secrete factors that promote the adhesion and proliferation of breast metastatic cancer cells [99]. DTCs in the brain adhere to the abluminal surface of the perfused vessels in a manner similar to pericytes to further spread and form micro- and macro-metastasis (perivascular migration) [100]. This process may be mediated by the cell adhesion molecule L1CAM mimicking a pericyte-like spreading. L1CAM activates β-integrin and integrin-linked kinase (ILK) signalling, leading to actin skeleton remodelling and competition with pericytes for attachment at the microcapillary outer surface [101]. Al-Mehdi et al challenged the classical model of hematogenous metastasis, suggesting that DTCs do not often extravasate, but instead attach to the endothelium of blood vessels forming colonies entirely within the blood vessels. These colonies continue to grow within the vessels until they reach a certain size, at which point they destroy the vascular walls and continue to spread into the tissue parenchyma (Figure **4C) [102]. This is partially explained by induced inflammatory responses, primarily** caused by neutrophils, promoting spreading of tumor cells along the abluminal side of blood vessels through a process known as angiotropism [103]. Within metastatic vascular niches, the communication extends beyond cancer cells and ECs. In the context of breast cancer, tumor cells exhibit an increased expression of the extracellular matrix glycoprotein Tenascin-C [104–106], which in turn, stimulate macrophages and ECs, leading to the formation of a pre-metastatic vascular niche **(Figure 4D) [107].**

Successful colonization can also be dependent on signals released by primary tumors which remodel the environment at pre-metastatic site to ensure optimal conditions for subsequent tumor growth. Tumor-secreted factors can induce phenotypic switch of perivascular stromal cells leading to their de-differentiation and favouring metastasis [65].

## Perivascular niche as therapeutic target

The perivascular niche is thus emerging as a critical hub that promotes cancer stemness [43,44], therapy resistance [108] and may even, at least in some specific contexts, facilitates cancer cell dissemination [65,109]. Moreover, when DTCs colonize the perivascular niche at distant organs they establish organ-specific (symbiotic) interactions with the host [110,111]. As such, targeting may limit primary tumor growth and more importantly, halt metastatic outgrowth in vital organs. Targeting CSCs within the perivascular niche has been an appealing strategy to disrupt tumor growth and/or metastasis. In the perivascular niche of squamous cell carcinomas, myeloid cells are the major source of TGF-β secretion, leading to enhanced heterogeneity among CSCs. Interestingly, the subpopulation of TGF-β- responsive CSCs exhibits a greater resistance to chemotherapy, highlighting the importance of understanding the interplay between niche-derived signals and cancer cell phenotypes in therapeutic interventions [112]. However, it appears to have limited effectiveness. Another approach is to directly target the endothelium, making use of anti-angiogenic therapy [43]. Anti-angiogenic therapy targeting VEGF and its receptors has demonstrated efficacy in non-melanoma skin tumors. In this context, VEGF produced by ECs supports angiogenesis and, consequently, contributes to the maintenance of the CSC pool. Additionally, epithelial tumor cells produce VEGF, thereby autonomously contributing to stemness. As a result, inhibiting VEGF2 in ECs or VEGF (or its co-receptor NRP1) in CSCs disrupts the niche environment and leads to tumor regression. [42]. Targeting the cell-cell communication between cancer cells and the TIME offers an alternative approach to disrupt the perivascular niche. The NOTCH signaling pathway, which plays a role in maintaining cancer stemness near the endothelium in various cancer types, can be therapeutically targeted. Blocking NOTCH ligands secreted by ECs has shown promise in disrupting the perivascular niche and inducing tumor regression in preclinical settings. In particular, neutralizing antibodies against DLL4

have demonstrated efficacy in abnormal vascularization, sprouting, and tumor regression, even in cases of resistance to anti-VEGF therapy [44,45,113–117]. Despite their effectiveness in preclinical models, anti-angiogenic agents and Notch- targeting agents (γ-secretase inhibitors, monoclonal antibodies, and antibody-drug conjugates), often fail to provide clinical benefits. Alternative angiogenic pathways, the promotion of immunosuppressive environments, and significant drug-related toxicities are commonly encountered factors that contribute to the low safety and efficacy observed in both completed and ongoing clinical trials (reviewed here [115,118,119]). Furthermore, given the many cell types involved in the assembly of the perivascular niche, it is perhaps not that surprising that monotherapy regimens are poorly effective or produce only transient anti-tumor responses. Combination strategies that target cellular interactions should therefore be envisaged. In Acute Lymphoblastic Leukemia (ALL), leukemic cells use stromal derived ostepontin (OPN, secreted by osteoblasts) to maintain a status of dormancy and get protection against chemotherapy. Neutralization of OPN synergized with the chemotherapy agent Ara-C to reduce the minimal residual pool of cells in the bone marrow [120]. Drug Tolerant Persister Cells (DTPCs) can also remained in a state of dormancy and escaped adjuvant chemotherapy. Chemotherapy-resistant DTPCs are localized in the perivascular niche, in which the vascular endothelium protects them from chemotherapeutic agents. Targeting an integrin-mediated interaction pathway established between DTPCs and ECs, sensitized them to chemotherapy and prevented bone metastasis [65]. Thus, blocking cell-cell communication networks at the perivascular niche in combination with agents that target intrinsic vulnerabilities of cancer cells is an approach that holds great promise.

## Concluding remarks

Despite the recent breakthroughs in cancer therapies, a significant number of patients still succumb to their disease. The effectiveness of anti-angiogenic therapy in achieving primary endpoints is limited, and tumors often evade this treatment. Our current understanding of the perivascular niche provides insights into why targeting the endothelium alone is insufficient for tumor eradication and prevention of spread. Perivascular niches exhibit dynamic plasticity and cellular heterogeneity, creating a microenvironment promoting stem cell-like properties, dissemination, and immunomodulatory interactions. Our knowledge for the perivascular niche has expanded, shedding light on new aspects and potential vulnerabilities that can be targeted alternatively (Box 1). However, there are still unanswered questions that require a deeper comprehension of the dynamics and intricate interplay within this crucial cellular hub (see Outstanding questions). Dissecting the niche at cellular and molecular levels represents a fascinating area of research with the potential to deliver new innovative therapies against cancer progression and metastatic dissemination.

## Box: Future perspectives and technological advancements

Our comprehension of the significance of cellular niches paves the way for the exploration of novel therapeutic strategies. A crucial focus should be the development of approaches that specifically target the interplay between the TIME and the tumor. These efforts should acknowledge and address the paramount importance of cancer cell plasticity, heterotypic communication between stromal and cancer cells, niche- dependent cellular reprogramming events, and physical constraints that arise during the formation of cellular niches. The advent of single cell and spatial multi- omics technologies in combination with the fast-developing tools in lineage tracing approaches undoubtedly can transform our knowledge on the cellular dynamics and structural organization of the perivascular niches. Leveraging spatial technologies, along with multi-omics integration methods, open a new era of research to exhaustively characterize perivascular niches. Fate mapping and cell perturbation strategies applied to perivascular components, hold promise for elucidating the molecular mechanisms underlying niche formation and identifying potential targets for effective disruption. Genetic tracing techniques can

serve as powerful tools for investigating the dynamics within perivascular niches. These approaches provide invaluable insights into the clonal expansion of both cancer and non-cancer cells, allowing for a better understanding of whether and how specific clones exhibit preferential expansion within perivascular niches. Extrinsic mechanisms that regulate perivascular niches extend beyond cell-cell interactions. Paracrine signaling, mediated by soluble factors and molecular cargos packaged in extracellular vesicles, plays a crucial role in promoting stemness, dormancy and metastatic dissemination. Besides, secreted metabolites and lipids further unravel the metabolic dependencies of cancer cells within the perivascular niches. Advances in mass spectrometry technologies enable the capture of spatial and temporal metabolic activities, facilitating a better understanding of metabolic dependencies within the perivascular niches. The development and utilization of ex vivo engineered models and microfluidic devices provide another framework to functionally validate the impact of cellular interactions in perivascular niches on growth and/or invasion. By employing dimension reduction approaches and establishing stable vascular networks, these models offer a robust platform to investigate the dynamics and plasticity of individual cell types (e.g., pericytes and astrocytes) during niche formation. Moreover, these models enable efficient drug screenings aimed at disrupting cellular interactions within the perivascular niches, thereby driving therapeutic advancements [124,125]. Finally, the emergence of single-cell proteomics has introduced a potent tool for identifying pathways and the molecular interactome that are critical for the formation and maintenance of perivascular niches, empowering the need to identify targets for therapeutic intervention.

## Figure legends

**Figure 1: Tumor vasculature**

(A) Cancer cells recruit endothelial cells (ECs) which initiate generation of new blood vessels through sprouting from pre-existing vessels via angiogenesis [1,2]; (B) cancer cells may co-opt existing blood vessels and migrate among the vasculature to fuel tumor growth [3]; (C) vasculogenesis consists of de novo formation of blood vessels through the recruitment of bone marrow-derived hemangioblasts and endothelial precursors [4]; (D) cancer cells may adopt an EC-like phenotype (vasculogenic mimicry) and generate vascular channels [5,6].

**Figure 2: Perivascular niche composition and major functions**
 Tumor perivascular niche consists of (A) tumor-associated endothelial cells (TECs) which are structural units of blood vessels releasing angiocrine factors and extracellular vesicles which affect tumor growth and plasticity; (B) mural cells modulate blood vessel functions and EC-tumor cells crosstalk, controlling tumor growth and cell migration along vessels; (C) cancer associated fibroblasts (CAFs) are mostly responsible for extracellular matrix (ECM) remodeling (myCAFs), immunomodulation (iCAFs) and antigen presentation (apCAFs). They also fuel tumor metabolism. All cellular components of the niche may communicate with immune cells and affect anti- tumor immunity response.
**Figure 3: Perivascular niche as a dynamic ecosystem driving cell plasticity**
 Cell-cell interactions and signaling networks underlying phenotypic plasticity at the perivascular niche has been highlighted. Arrows represent phenotypic switch and cell- cell communication. (A) Cancer cells (CCs) induce de-differentiation of endothelial cells (ECs) to foetal-like PLVAP
+ ECs, which together with embryonic-like FOLR2 + tumor-associated macrophages (TAMs) suppress T cells via Notch-VEGF signaling, creating immunosuppressive environment [27]. (B) CCs promote endothelial to mesenchymal transition via Notch and TGFb signalling, giving rise to population of hybrid mesenchymal-endothelial cells, which may subsequently acquire features of mural cells [59] or fibroblasts [17-20]. Mural cells may also trans-differentiate into fibroblasts [63]. (C) Cancer associated fibroblast (CAF)-derived IL6 [77] and EC- derived FGF2 [45] promote CCs de-differentiation into cancer stem cells (CSCs) in Notch-dependent manner [44-45] and subsequent trans-differentiation to endothelial [36, 41] or pericyte-like cells [71-72]. Notch pathway was shown to drive the transition of CCs towards EC progenitors, whereas VEGF is required for progenitor maturation [36]. (D) ECs release IGF1 and promote CSC expansion [46].

**Figure 4: Perivascular niche as a driver of metastasis**

Cancer cells disseminate from the primary tumors and enter the circulation (intravasation). Disseminated tumor cells (DTCs) may migrate as a single cell or cluster of cells [89]. (A) At the metastatic site, tumor cells extravasate by inducing the necroptotic death in ECs, facilitating the migration across endothelium [97]. (B)

Alternatively, DTCs may transdifferentiate into EC-like phenotype and remain quiescent in the vessel lumen before switching back to cancer cell phenotype and re- enter cell cycle [41, 98]. (C) Proliferating tumor cells form bigger colonies within the lumen which eventually leads to the disruption of endothelial monolayer and subsequent growth at the abluminal side of vessels (angiotropism) in response to neutrophil-mediated inflammatory stimuli [102-103]. In some cases, cancer cells may also acquire pericyte-like phenotype (pericyte mimicry) and migrate along abluminal side of blood vessels to reach the metastatic organ, without entering circulation [100].

(D) The outgrowth of tumor cells at the metastatic site is supported by ECM remodelling which triggers sequential activation of macrophages and endothelial cells to from a pre-metastatic niche around blood vessels [107]. Other components of a perivascular niche may contribute to pre-metastatic niche formation via secretion of signaling molecules and extracellular vesicles, creating the optimal environment for metastatic tumor formation [65]. Arrows represent cell movement, dashed arrows represent transdifferentiation.
