# ‘From metabolism to metabolism’: holistic considerations on B-vitamin interactions, biofortification,

# and deficiencies

## Lisa De Zanetti and Dominique Van Der Straeten *

In the post-Green Revolution era, disparities in dietary access, rising obesity rates, demographic shifts, adoption of plant- based diets, and the impact of climate change collectively contribute to a progressive decline in dietary nutritional value, exacerbating B vitamin deficiencies across both low- and high- income countries. While the prevailing focus of biofortification has been on three micronutrients — provitamin A, iron, and zinc — utilizing conventional breeding, it is imperative to diversify biofortification strategies to combat micronutrient malnutrition. Metabolic engineering, facilitated by biotechnological tools, presents a promising avenue, contingent upon advances in fundamental knowledge, technological innovation, regulatory updates, and sustained public funding. Recognizing the intricate metabolic interplay of B vitamins in plants and humans, a comprehensive ‘from metabolism to metabolism’ approach is crucial for designing effective biofortification strategies that target multiple vitamins. This holistic perspective also extends beyond individual crops to encompass the entire food chain, a complex socioeconomic ecosystem that necessitates a paradigm shift, prioritizing quality over quantity.

### From hunger to the triple burden of

### malnutrition: why we are going backward

As of the transition from nomad existence to life in settlements, humankind and agriculture are in a con tinuously evolving mutual relationship that has shaped societies, economies, and cultures throughout the ages. Since the 18th-century Industrial Revolution and through ceaseless technological advancements and geo political shifts, our food system has never been more globalized than it is today. The interconnected nature of global supply chains makes local food prices susceptible to external shocks like conflicts, famines, pandemics, or economic crises on the other side of the globe. The unequal distribution of resources and purchasing power hits the most vulnerable social groups the hardest. Price rises primarily affect the poorest consumers, whose af fordability of and accessibility to food is reduced to a limited range of products, these being the less nutritious [2]. In the mid-20th century, when food insecurity was a synonymous of calorie shortage, the agricultural sector responded with the Green Revolution to increase the production of cheap, energy-dense staple foods. With farmers having adopted genetically uniform high- yielding varieties, 60% of dietary energy is nowadays derived from three cereal crops only: rice, maize, and wheat [3]. While undernourishment has been halved in the last two decades on the one hand [4], the dietary nutritional quality has been impaired on the other. ### It is not solely a matter of purchasing power

While HICs have access to a varied diet, MNDs do not spare the richest consumers [11]. Developed countries face a heightened obesity burden compared with de veloping counterparts [9]. Excessive consumption of processed foods high in carbohydrates and fats and low in vitamins and minerals amplifies the demand for mi cronutrients, such as vitamin B1, which is involved in carbohydrate and fat metabolism, generating a self-re inforcing vicious cycle [12,13]. The demographic shift toward a higher proportion of elderly worsens the mi cronutrient status, given their increased vulnerability to MNDs, such as vitamin D deficiency [14]. The rise in popularity of novel food consumption trends, like plant- based diets, even though imperative in the context of planetary health [15], poses certain risks of nutrient deficits in Western societies. For instance, plants lack the ability to synthesize vitamin B12, increasing the onset of anemia and neurological impairment, particu larly in aged individuals due to reduced absorption of the nutrient [16]. In addition, climate change and rising at mospheric carbon dioxide concentration threaten not only crop yields but also their micronutrient composi tion. Multiple studies indicate a decline in iron, zinc, provitamin A, and B vitamin contents in crops, alongside a reduction in diversity and populations of pollinators, hence also in nutrient-rich food supplies, as well as a rise in waterborne diseases by climate change-associated rainfall, flooding, and heat, provoking a higher need for micronutrients in affected individuals [17–19]. Decades of yield-centered conventional breeding (CB) has ne gatively impacted micronutrient content [20]. Acknowl edging both past and present threats, it is essential to reintroduce the lost quality traits into our crops. Meat and dairy products boast the highest B vitamin content [21], yet their consumption in LICs and among popu lations in HICs adhering to plant-based diets falls short of delivering the recommended dietary allowance (RDA) within their dietary context [15]. Staple foods like rice, maize, and wheat, which account for approxi mately 60% of dietary energy globally, and even around 80% in LICs, typically provide only 0–33% of the RDA

for most B vitamins per serving [22,23]. While fruits and vegetables such as oranges, bananas, grapes, apples, to matoes, cabbage, and onions are rich in B vitamins [22], their consumption remains insufficient to meet the RDA [15]. According to the EAT-Lancet Commission’s pla netary health diet, alongside doubling fruit and vege table intake, reducing consumption of B vitamin–rich animal products (especially in wealthier populations), and increasing consumption of whole grains, which still provide the majority of daily calories, are crucial [15]. This emphasizes the need to enhance the quality of staple crops, not only to align with current dietary habits but also to meet future requirements for human health and environmental sustainability.

Leaps forward have been achieved in the last quarter century in the reduction of MNDs-related diseases, by releasing bio fortified varieties through the HarvestPlus program of the Consultative Group for International Agricultural Research (CGIAR) [24]. ### What we need to unlock the full potential of

### metabolic engineering

Biofortification via metabolic engineering heavily de pends on an in-depth understanding of micronutrient metabolism. Because of cross-talk with essential meta bolic pathways, fundamental knowledge of both micro nutrient and macronutrient pathways is paramount to design efficient tailor-made strategies that consider major consequences of the metabolic intervention. This ensures the maintenance of a necessary physiological balance to redirect the flux toward the accumulation of specific metabolites without causing major impairments to the plant’s physiology [38,39]. However, there is a huge knowledge gap in this area [40]. The advent of spatial transcriptomics, such as single-cell RNA se quencing, can assist in mapping metabolic pathways within organs and visualizing cell type–specific differ ences in micronutrient metabolism [41]. At the meta bolome level, advances in detection and quantification methodologies that enable micronutrient multiplexing and minimize the amount of required tissue, remain unexplored. Moreover, while biotechnological techni ques are already powerful, advancements in cloning systems are essential to unleash their full potential [42]. Impressive results have been achieved in creating DNA fragments up to 31 kb [43]. However, constraints on genetic construct size impact cloning efficiency, limiting endeavors targeting complex pathways (e.g. vitamin B12 biosynthesis requiring 30 genes) [44] or multi-bioforti fication strategies [28]. Furthermore, deregulating legal frameworks that hamper the release of transgenic crops is vital for research to move beyond academic confines and reach the market. Reducing the burden of time- consuming and expensive approval processes would encourage developers to invest in such technologies, benefiting consumers, farmers, and the environment. The European Commission’s proposed new regulation on NBT-produced plants, serving as an update to Di rective 2001/18/EC, signals a promising change. How ever, the document excludes material from non- crossable species. Genome editing tools such as CRISPR-Cas9, while powerful, have limitations com pared with transgenic tools, including reduced construct size, lack of consensus on the insertion location, and the requirement for PAM sequences [45]. As such, genome editing and transgenesis should be complementary ra ther than in a mutually exclusive dichotomy [28,46]. The scientific community supports transgenesis [47], but persisting public skepticism requires more efficacy and safety trials [48], along with unbiased science-based

education [37]. In such context, philanthropic and public investments are crucial to keep intellectual property rights of public domain and prevent private sector dominance for the benefit of society.

### No one stands alone: B vitamins interplay in

### plant and human metabolism

B vitamins function as enzymatic cofactors in hundreds of metabolic reactions, ranging from catabolic processes for energy production to anabolic pathways for the construction of amino acids, fatty acids, and nucleic acids, in both plants and humans [49,50]. Given their pleiotropic functions, defi ciencies in B vitamins result in multiple symptoms, including neurological impairments, cardiovascular disorders, and an emia [51]. Children and pregnant women face the highest risk due to increased micronutrient need for growth and development [52]. Operating collectively, B vitamins form an intricate metabolic network, acting synergistically in common pathways [51]. While extensive reviews have covered the biosynthesis of thiamine (vitamin B1) [30], riboflavin (B2) [53], niacin (B3) [54], pantothenic acid (B5) [55], pyridoxine (B6) [49], biotin (B7) [56], and folate (B9) [30] in plants, as well as cobalamin (B12) [44] biosynthesis in microorganisms, less is known about their reciprocal interactions [57,58]. A careful examination of their biosynthesis routes in plants reveals their deeply interconnected nature (Figure 2). For instance, in B1 biosynthesis, 4-amino-2-methyl-5-hydro xymethylpyrimidine phosphate (HMP-P) synthase (THIC) requires B9 for assembling the iron/sulfur cluster and the B9- derived cofactor S-adenosylmethionine (SAM) (Figure 2) [30]. Furthermore, 5,10-methylene tetrahydrofolate is also a cofactor of ketopantoate hydroxymethyltransferase (KPHMT) in vitamin B5 biosynthesis [55]. In biotin bio synthesis, pyridoxine participates as a cofactor for two en zymes: 7-keto-8-amino-pelargonic acid synthase (KAPAS) and dethiobiotin synthetase (DTBS) [49]. Building upon established connections, delving into the metabolic circle to explore both upstream (the origin of vitamin precursors) and downstream (metabolic reactions involving vitamin-derived coenzymes), the mere biosynthesis pathways of B vitamins reveal an extensive cross-influence. ‘Progenitor’ glucose is a cornerstone in metabolism, which, through glycolysis, the pentose phosphate pathway (PPP), and the Calvin cycle, yields sugars, and provides the building blocks for amino acid synthesis, both comprising the precursors for the biosynthesis of B vitamins, which in turn are essential for the functioning of primary metabolic pathways. B vitamin precursors pri marily consist of amino acids, sugar-derived compounds, or a combination of both [59]. From the PPP intermediate ribose- 5-phosphate, 5-aminoimidazole ribonucleotide (AIR) is pro duced via the purine pathway. AIR and the amino acid glycine serve as precursors for B1. From AIR, guanosine triphosphate (GTP) is produced, which is the precursor of both B2 and B9, along with ribulose-5-phosphate (R-5-P) and chorismate, derived from the PPP and its intermediate erythrose-4-phosphate (E-4-P), respectively (Figure 2).

### From the micro to the macro scale

One problem may have multiple solutions, but the winning strategy is adopting many if not all solutions

together. The approach that increases food availability despite population growth can and should also aim at enhancing food quality [70]. Targeting individual nu trients is not the most efficient strategy. A concerted application of multiple approaches, acting simulta neously in a holistic perspective rather than isolated propositions, is the most sensible solution to tackle mi cronutrient malnutrition within a reasonable timeframe [28]. Metabolic engineering goes beyond the mere overexpression of biosynthetic genes. It involves en hancing stability, improving bioavailability, increasing salvage, reducing breakdown, and redistributing trans port flux, by inducing up- and downregulation, acting on epigenetic modifications, involving cis- and trans-genetic elements, transcriptional and post-translational levels of positive as well as negative regulators (Figure 3). Com binations of such strategies can both simplify and ac celerate metabolic engineering [71]. Transgenic modifications are just one means of genetically im proving crops. Depending on the case, other tools, in cluding CB, NBTs, and agronomic practices, work toward the same goal [71,72]. Simultaneously, in tegrating biofortification with parallel interventions, such as fortification, supplementation, education programs,

and equitable food distribution, is required [37] (Figure

3). Every method has advantages and disadvantages, and a holistic paradigm must consider the complex inter connectedness of context-specific biological, cultural, and economic factors [37]. In this perspective, not only staple foods but also orphan crops should be considered.
