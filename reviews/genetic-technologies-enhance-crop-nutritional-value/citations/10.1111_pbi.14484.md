Introduction

MicroRNAs (miRNAs) are a class of non-coding endogenous small RNAs in eukaryotes. MiRNAs regulate the expression level of their target genes by RNA cleavage or translational repression. Extensive research has shown the important roles of miRNA in regulating various aspects of plant life, including plant morpho- genesis, signal transduction, metabolism, nutrient absorption, hormone regulation, as well as biotic and abiotic stress responses (Chen and Rechavi, 2021; Chi et al., 2012; Fabian and Sonenberg, 2012; Lytle et al., 2007; Voinnet, 2009, 2022). The research on miRNAs, especially plant miRNAs, has primarily focused on the identification of miRNAs and their target genes, expression of miRNAs and functional validation of miRNAs via overexpression (Tang and Chu, 2017; Voinnet, 2009). Due to the small size of miRNA genes, it is often challenging to generate knockout mutants through T-DNA-based insertional mutagenesis.

Target mimicry (TM) and short tandem target mimic (STTM) knockdown approaches can inhibit the activity of endogenous miRNAs and are commonly used to investigate the function of miRNA in plants (Franco-Zorrilla et al., 2007; Yan et al., 2012; Zhang et al., 2017). In recent years, CRISPR-Cas9 has emerged as a powerful genetic knockout tool for investigating the roles of miRNA genes in plants (Bi et al., 2020; Cheng et al., 2021; Deng et al., 2022; Fan et al., 2024;Heet al., 2024; Miao et al., 2020; Tang and Zhang, 2023; Wang et al., 2021; Zhou et al., 2017, 2021, 2022). However, Cas9 produces largely blunt-ended double-strand breaks (DSBs), leading to the small insertions or deletions (Indels) of 1–3 base pair in most cases. In some cases (He et al., 2024; Tang et al., 2018; Zheng et al., 2016; Zhou et al., 2017), these small indels can lead to changes in the pre-miRNA structure, impacting the function of the miRNA. Discussion

Plant miRNAs play important roles in plant growth, development and stress responses. To elucidate the function of miRNAs, the methods of overexpressing pri-miRNA genes, inhibiting miRNA expression or knock-out miRNA genes are commonly used. As an effective gene-knockout tool, CRISPR-Cas9 have been widely used to knockout the miRNA genes in plants (Bi et al., 2020; Cheng et al., 2021;Heet al., 2024; Miao et al., 2020; Wang et al., 2021; Zhou et al., 2017, 2021, 2022). In order to completely knockout the miRNA genes, it was necessary to screen the mutants with Indels of more than 3 bp, and this kind of mutations are not predominantly produced by CRISPR-Cas9, unless two sgRNAs were simultaneously (He et al., 2024; Tang and Zhang, 2023; Zhou et al., 2017). Since CRISPR-Cas12a can efficiently introduce a larger deletion of more than 5 bp by a single crRNA, it seems to be a better editing system for targeted mutagenesis of miRNA genes to generate loss-of-function mutants. To assess CRISPR-Cas9 and CRISPR-Cas12a for knocking out miRNA genes, we targeted OsMIR390 as loss of function of the miR390 gene in rice can severely affect plant development (Bi et al., 2020). To ensure fair comparison, the sgRNA of CRISPR- Cas9 and the crRNA of CRISPR-Cas12a were designed to target the mature sequence of OsMIR390 with the DSBs being overlapping. We found that 1) Cas12a produced fewer regener- ated plants with much lower transformation efficiency than Cas9;

2) Significant difficulty in recovering genome-edited plants by CRISPR-Cas12a (4 out of T0 lines) as compared to
CRISPR-Cas9 (30 out of 50 T0 lines); 3) Lack of biallelic editing by Cas12a (0 out of 4 edited T0 lines) as compared to Cas9 (5 out of 30 edited T0 lines); and 4) All biallelically edited T0 lines by Cas9 contained at least one 1-bp indel mutation, while all the recovered heterozygous lines by Cas12a contained larger deletions. Previously, it was shown that 1-bp indels generated by Cas9 could not necessarily destroy the function of miRNAs (Bi et al., 2020; Tang and Zhang, 2023; Zhou et al., 2017). Analysis of the pri-miRNA structure in the edited lines further supported this (Figure 1g). Hence, our data clearly suggest that Cas12a, due to its high tendency to generated larger deletions, is a more robust tool for generating knockout mutants of miRNAs. To date, nearly all the targeted mutagenesis studies have used Cas9 with one sgRNA per target gene (He et al., 2024; Tang et al., 2018, 2019; Tang and Zhang, 2023). Hence, our work suggest Cas12a would be a better tool to efficiently generate null alleles of miRNA genes. To further demonstrate Cas12a as a superior nuclease for generating miRNA knockout mutants, we selected nine additional miRNAs genes in rice for targeted mutagenesis that have different expression patterns (Figure 2a). Based on our phenotypic analysis of the homozygous mutants of these miRNA genes, the Cas12a induced large-deletion alleles are indeed null alleles (e.g. Figures 1d and 3), which again support Cas12a as potent tool for generating miRNA knockout. These miRNA null mutants generated by Cas12a form an important resource for investigat- ing their functions in plant growth, development and response to biotic and abiotic stresses. In the reminder of the study, we explored the phenotypes of the knockout mutants of five miRNAs (OsMIR394, OsMIR827, OsMIR3979, OsMIR5789 and OsMIR5794) by focusing on seeds such seed size and weight, starch content and structure, and seed germination and seedling growth. Interestingly, we were able to reveal different pheno- types in all mutants, suggesting these five miRNAs are involved in seed development and quality. For OsMIR394, the mutants showed larger seed size by different measures (Figure 4). OsMIR394 is a highly conserved miRNA gene in plants. Previously, an F-box gene was indicated as a target gene of OsMIR394 (Qu et al., 2019). Studies have shown that miR394 plays an important role in regulating leaf morphology, fruit and seed development, iron-nutrient response, abiotic stresses such as drought and low temperature, as well as fungal and other biotic stresses (Bernardi et al., 2022; Lu et al., 2023;Quet al., 2019; Song et al., 2013; Tian et al., 2018; Zhang et al., 2021b). OsMIR827 has also been detected in different species, such as Arabidopsis, rice, potato and barley. OsMIR827 is strongly induced by drought stress and Pi starvation in both shoots and roots (Ferdous et al., 2017; Kant et al., 2011; Lin et al., 2013, 2018; Singh et al., 2023; Yadav and Ramakrishna, 2023). We found the mutants of OsMIR827 had significantly reduced grain width, grain thickness and 1000-seed weight (Figure 4). Furthermore, the OsMIR827 mutant had a slower germination rate and smaller seedling size (Figure 6). Together, our research here mapped the roles of OsMIR394 and OsMIR827 in controlling seed development in rice. OsMIR3979 was identified to be specifically and significantly expressed in the shoot and root tissues, and might respond to salt stress treatment (Kumar et al., 2023; Nguyen et al., 2023). OsMIR5794 was verified as a monoterpene biosynthesis-related miRNA by degradome sequencing (Xu et al., 2023). We found the knockout mutants of OsMIR3979 and OsMIR5794 had higher

grain chalkiness (Figure 5a–c). Furthermore, we found slight decrease of amylose and increase of amylopectin in the OsMIR5794 mutant, but not in the OsMIR3979 mutant (Figure 5e, f). This observation could be consistent with the different levels of grain chalkiness between these two mutants (Figure 5c). Interestingly, the OsMIR5789 mutant showed increased amylose and decreased amylose pectin in the grains (Figure 5e, f). Together, these data suggest the involvement of these three miRNAs in fine-tuning the amount and type of starch in the rice grain. Although the transcriptome analysis provided some insight (Figure 5h), the detailed mechanisms warrant further investigations. Cross-regulation between miRNAs had been previously reported (Guo et al., 2017;Heet al., 2024; Zhou et al., 2017,

2022). In our previous study, knocking out OsMIR408 and OsMIR528 results in expression profile changes of multiple other miRNAs, and these miRNAs with altered expression were either up-regulated or down-regulated in the same direction in mutants of OsMIR408 and OsMIR528 (Zhou et al., 2017). Through sRNA- seq analysis of the OsMIR394, OsMIR3979 and OsMIR5794 knockout mutants based on Cas12a nuclease, we found that the knockout of these miRNAs had very little effect on the expression levels of pri-miRNA and mature miRNA in the whole genome (Figure 3c). Interestingly, nearly all the few affected miRNAs showed down-regulation in the mutants (Figure 3c). However, mRNA transcriptomes were significantly perturbed in the mutants of OsMIR394, OsMIR3979 and OsMIR5794 (Figure 3). We postulate that the abundance, tissue-specific expression and regulation mechanism may all play a role in the levels of miRNA cross-regulation. The significant number of differentially expressed genes and the observed phenotypes in the miRNA mutants both suggest these three miRNAs play important roles in rice growth and development. Interestingly, we observed the OsMIR394 and OsMIR5794 mutants have more than half common differentially expressed genes. However, the pheno- types of these miRNA mutants were different. Although differential expression of a few genes was sufficient to cause phenotypic changes, this phenomenon warrants further investi- gation. Further investigation may focus on the identification and validation of the target genes of these miRNAs, as well as untangling the complex regulation network controlled by these miRNAs. In conclusion, we demonstrated that CRISPR-Cas12a as a more efficient tool than CRISPR-Cas9 for generating null alleles of miRNA genes in plants. With CRISPR-Cas12a, we generated knockout mutants of nine miRNA genes in rice that have not been well studies genetically. The fact that we could detect phenotypes in the mutants of five miRNAs suggest prevalent roles of miRNAs in plants. Hence, the methodology and resource generated in this study have the potential to expedite our understanding of miRNA functions and facilitate their application in crop engineering efforts. We also envision that CRISPR-Cas12a could be used to efficiently generate genome-wide miRNA mutant libraries in rice and other plant species.
## Materials and methods

Plant materials and growth conditions

The rice cultivar Nipponbare (Oryza sativa L. japonica) was used as the WT control and transformation host. All mutants were created based on Nipponbare. The T2 and T3 generation of homozygote mutants without the transgene were used for

molecular and phenotypic characterization. The plants were grown in climate chambers under long-day conditions (16 h light at 30 °C and 8 h dark at 24 °C) and in greenhouse for experiment.

Vector construction

For vector pCas9-MIR390, we used a Cas9 expression backbone vector pGEL026 (pZmUBi::SpCas9::HspT-pOsU6::ccdB::sgRNA scaffold::pT) (Tang et al., 2024; Zhou et al., 2019). The DNA fragment was obtained from annealing products using pZST01F (5’-GTGTGTGGAACAATCCTTGAAGCTC-3 0 ) and pZST01R (5 0 - AAACGAGCTTCAAGGATTGTTCCAC-3 0 ). The anneal fragment was digested by Bsa I and then cloned into Bsa I-digested pGEL026. We used Cas12a expression backbone vector pGEL632 (pZmUBi1::LbCas12a::HspT-pOsUbi::HH::ccdB::HDV::NosT) (Zheng et al., 2023; Zhou et al., 2023) for construction all the miRNA-knockout vectors, from pMIR390 to pMIR5801 (Table S3). The different recombinant fragments of miRNA target sites were obtained by annealing using the primers listed in Table S4 and cloned into pGEL632 backbone by Bsa I-based ligation. The sequences of gRNA targets were shown in Table S1.

Rice transformation

The T-DNA constructs were introduced into Agrobacterium EHA105 by the freeze–thaw method. Rice stable transformation was carried out according to a previously published protocol (Ren et al., 2021a, 2021b).

Targeted mutation detection

Total genomic DNA was extracted from transgenic plants using the CTAB method. Genomic regions of targeted sites were amplified with specific primers for the detection of chromosomal deletions (Table S4). The amplified products were analysed by PCR-SSCP (Zheng et al., 2016). All the T0 and T1 mutant lines were further genotyped by Sanger sequencing. The genotypes of the mutant lines were analysed using the CRISPR-GE DSDecodeM software (Liu et al., 2015).

sRNA-seq and mRNA-seq

The OsMIR394, OsMIR3979 and OsMIR5794 T2 stable mutants without T-DNA and their corresponding WT plants were chosen for small RNA sequencing and mRNA transcriptome sequencing. Whole plants of 40-day old grown in the growth chamber were collected. Two independent plants for each mutant were chosen for library construction, sequencing and analysis. Small RNA and mRNA transcriptome sequencing were done using Illumina HiSeq 2500 planform at Biomarker Technologies Co. Ltd. Data processing and analysis were carried out with the BMKCloud service ([http://www.biocloud.net/](http://www.biocloud.net/)).

Investigation on agronomic characters of rice

The T3 generations originated from T0 mutation lines were used to test agronomic traits. The rice yield-related traits were measured according to previous method (Zheng et al., 2023; Zhou et al., 2023). At least three individual plants were used for data collection of each genotype. Each sample was tested for three times.

Grain starch content measurements and microscope observation

All the mutants and WT seeds were harvested, dried and stored at room temperature. To determine the total starch content,

amylose content and amylopectin content, all tests were entrusted to Shanghai SANSHU Biotechnology Company (Zheng et al., 2023; Zhong et al., 2023; Zhou et al., 2023).

Statistical analysis

All statistical analyses were performed with GraphPad Prism 8.0. Error bars represent standard deviations of at least three biological replicates. Data are means SD. Two-way ANOVA, Student’s t-test were used. Asterisks indicated significant differences (*P < 0.05, **P < 0.01, *** P < 0.001) between two treatments.
