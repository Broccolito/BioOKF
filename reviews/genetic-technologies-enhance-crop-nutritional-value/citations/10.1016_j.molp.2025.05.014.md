## INTRODUCTION

Cassava (Manihot esculenta) is an important staple food crop belonging to the spurge family (Euphorbiaceae) and known for its production of numerous large storage roots (SRs) enriched in starch. Characterized as tolerant to high light irradiation, heat, drought, and barren soils, it is an ideal future crop thriving despite global climate change (Ruiz-Vera et al., 2021). Compared to other major food crops, cassava is attractive due to its tremendous production potential, yielding an average of 45–60 tons of fresh roots per hectare or 75–90 tons per hectare under favorable conditions. Paleontological records of starch grains and palynology indicated that the origin and domestication of cassava took place 7500–9000 years ago in the Southwest Amazon (Allem, 1994; Schaal et al., 2006; Christian, 2011; Watling et al.,

2018), a region regarded as a center of early Amazonian civilization and agriculture fostering domestication of squash, beans, and guava. Cassava fossils excavated from Mayan ruins in Mesoamerica during the Late Holocene demonstrate that cassava is one of the earliest crops domesticated in South America, playing an essential role in the development of American civilization (Bartlett et al., 1969; Gibbons, 1990). Ninety-eight wild species of cassava have been identified as pre dominantly distributed within Brazil and the surrounding regions. Among these, Manihot esculenta ssp. flabellifolia (FLA) is a small climbing shrub found at the boundary between the tropical rain forest and savanna in the Amazon Basin. It is considered the most recent ancestor of cultivated cassava (Allem, 1999; Luiz et al., 2018). Evidence based on the structural variations (SVs) in the single-copy gene G3pdh suggests that cassava was origi nally domesticated in the Acre, Rondoˆ nia, and Matogrosso states in Southern Brazil, where an abundant number of ancestral spe cies and landraces are presently also growing (Olsen and Schaal,
1999). However, the process of cassava domestication most likely occurred in dryland areas, which are pivotal for SR formation and the promotion of C4photosynthesis. Recently, ev idence of cassava-producing SRs in regions near the Andes high lands as early as 10,350 years ago has been provided based on 14 C isotope measurements (Umberto et al., 2020). Cultivated cassava is capable of producing a large quantity of biomass even under harsh conditions of drought, high- intensity solar radiation, and hyperthermia (Luiz et al., 2018). These favorable agronomic traits of cultivated cassava are derived from their wild ancestors, resulting from adaptation to environmental challenges and artificial selection (Olsen and Molecular Plant 18, 1047–1071, June 2 2025
Wendel, 2013; Alonge et al., 2020), as evidenced by the expansion of genes and gene families that contribute to these traits. The genomic expansion and shrinkage processes that happened in the course of evolution can now be assessed using genomic technologies, e.g., by sequencing the genomes and constructing a pan-genome of the wild cassava species and cultivars and by re-sequencing and mining alleles associated with domesticated traits (Liu et al., 2020; Zhang et al., 2020; Varshney et al., 2021).

We previously reported the genome sequences of cassava cultivar KU50 and the wild ancestor W14 (Wang et al., 2014). Subsequently, the genome sequences of cultivar AM560 (Bredeson et al., 2016), TME7 (Mansfeld et al., 2021), SC205 (Hu et al., 2021), and TME204 (Qi et al., 2022) were obtained using PacBio sequencing and improved Hi-C sequencing. These genomic resources have enabled the discovery of insights into the evolution of favorable traits. For example, by comparing the genomes of the wild ancestor W14 and the cultivar KU50, we de tected variations in genes and gene families involved in starch accumulation, cell wall biosynthesis, and the production of specialized metabolites such as cyanogenic glucosides, demon strating the rigor of genomic technologies (Wang et al., 2014). A total of 203 selective sweeping regions, including 427 genes, many of which are involved in sugar and starch metabolism and stress tolerance, have also been reported (Ramu et al., 2017). However, no species-level analysis has been performed so far to compare wild ancestors and modern varieties of cassava.

Domesticated cassava possesses C3–C4intermediate photosyn thesis with a significantly higher photosynthetic carbon fixation rate and more efficient responses to light intensity compared with its wild relatives (EI-Sharkawy and Cock, 1987; Cock et al., 1987; James et al., 1987; Calatayud et al., 2002). For example, key enzyme representatives of C4carbon assimilation, such as py ruvate orthophosphate dikinase (PPDK), phosphoenolpyruvate carboxykinase (PEPC), and NADP-dependent malic enzyme (NADP-ME) (Mercado and Stude, 2022), are all abundantly expressed in mature leaves of cultivated cassava (EI-Sharkawy,

2003). Based on
13 C isotope experiments and other physiological indexes, cassava has been reported as a C3plant (Edwards et al., 1990). However, the cassava plants that formed the basis for that conclusion were grown in pots in a greenhouse at a low net photosynthetic rate (Pn) range of 16.7–23.6 μmol/m/s (Edwards et al., 1990), conditions at which the C system is not induced (Bra¨ utigam and Gowik, 2016). Increased knowledge in this area is crucial for understanding the evolution of photosynthetic mechanisms in cassava and other plants (Urte and Weber, 2020).

The coevolution of flowering and SR formation is another key event in the speciation of modern cassava. Ancestral species blossom early and produce a large number of fruits and seeds, but carry tiny SRs. Cultivars typically have delayed flowering and produce few fruits, but develop large SRs and are propa gated in a vegetative manner using nutrient-rich stem cuttings. Such drastic and essential changes transferred the nutrition res ervoirs from fruits and seeds to stems and SRs, and as a result, promoted starch accumulation (Luiz et al., 2018). The florigen (FT) gene family in plants plays an essential role in plant flower ing and vegetative organ formation. The FT1 and FT2 members of the family are expressed in specific companion cells in leaves in response to environmental signals. They are transported long distances to the apical meristem or the tips of adventitious roots in the form of mRNA or protein to initiate the development of flo ral primordia (FP) or tubers (Corbesier et al., 2007; Navarro, 2011; Hoang et al., 2020). A third member, TFL2, is a flowering suppressant and essential for the enlargement of vegetative organs. In cassava, there are 10 members of the FT family (Adeyemo et al., 2011, 2019), but, except for MeTFL2, no clear SVs between wild ancestors and cultivars have been identified. Interestingly, a sequence deletion has been found in a CONSTANS gene (MeCOL5), which is considered a co- regulator of FTs (Freytes et al., 2021; Wolfgang et al., 2021). The identity of the switch controlling the balance between flowering and SR development thus remains elusive.

Cyanogenic glucosides are a class of natural products that initially evolved in Pteridophytes 320 MYA ago and are present in many higher plants (Koleva et al., 1992; Zagrobelny et al., 2008; Gleadow and Møller, 2014; Thodberg et al., 2020). Cyanogenic glucosides serve as remobilizable storage forms of reduced nitrogen (Picmanova et al., 2015; Bjarnholt et al., 2018), but are mostly known for their function as defense compounds against herbivores and pests by their ability to liberate hydrogen cyanide upon cell disruption (Tattersall et al., 2001; Larsen and Møller,

2011). Cassava contains two cyanogenic glucosides, linamarin and lotaustralin (Andersen et al., 2000). When used as a food source, cassava leaves and SRs need to be processed to avoid exposure to toxic hydrogen cyanide upon ingestion. In cassava, linamarin and lotaustralin are synthesized mainly in the leaves and transported to the SRs through the phloem as demonstrated in girdling experiments in which the phloem cells were removed (Andersen et al., 2000; Jørgensen et al., 2005; Kannangara et al., 2011). The content of cyanogenic glucosides in SRs has decreased during the domestication process. ## RESULTS

### Haplotype-resolved genome assemblies for the cultivar AM560 and the wild ancestors FLA4047 and W14

Genome sequencing was carried out using a selfing S3 clone of AM560 with low heterozygosity as a representative of cultivated cassava (Manihot esculenta), an accession of the wild ancestor FLA4047 (Manihot esculenta ssp. flabellifolia), and another wild relative W14 (Supplemental Figure 1). Their estimated genome sizes were 720, 720, and 830 Mb, respectively (Supplemental

Figure 3; Supplemental Table 1). The basic frameworks of the

genomes were constructed by integrating multiple next- and third-generation sequencing technologies and de novo assembly (Supplemental Figure 4; Supplemental Table 2). Two cells of nanopore ultralong reads at ∼170× depth per genome were used for assembly with NextDenovo, yielding three nearly complete genomes. The AM560 genome was assembled into a 645 Mb reference with no gaps in 16 of 18 chromosomes and a few gaps on Chr3 and Chr18. The FLA4047 genome was 672 Mb with 1–9 gaps in 15 chromosomes. The W14 genome was 709 Mb, gapless in 10 chromosomes, and had 33 gaps across the remaining eight chromosomes. The scaffold N50 of the three genomes ranged from 35 to 40 Mb. The completeness and accuracy of the genome assemblies of AM560, FLA4047, and W14 were assessed using BUSCOs, achieving scores of 97.7%,

96.9%, and 97.5%, respectively (Table 1; Supplemental Table 3). The sequencing and assembly analyses demonstrated the high integrity and accuracy of the genome assemblies. A substantial proportion of repeats was observed in the cassava genomes, ranging from 63.7% to 67.9%. The most prevalent type of repeat identified was Gypsy, a form of long terminal repeat, constituting over 55% of all repeats. Following the masking and exclusion of repetitive sequences, the coding genes within the three genomes were annotated through homologous protein comparison and gene prediction methodologies. At the same time, long non- coding RNAs (lncRNAs) were identified utilizing RNA sequencing (RNA-seq) data. The AM560, FLA4047, and W14 genomes were annotated with 30,571, 33,913, and 32,406 protein-coding genes and 12,253, 12,683, and 8,687 lncRNA genes, respectively (Table 1). All predicted non-coding RNAs are detailed in Supplemental Table 4.

### Evolution and phylogeny of cassava ancestral and cultivated subspecies

The synteny of chromosome pairs in the three cassava genomes was analyzed using single-copy homologous genes (Figure 1A). There are 8,903 single-copy homologous genes between AM560 and FLA4047, 10,314 between AM560 and W14, and

10,173 between W14 and FLA4047. High synteny is noted between the wild and cultivated species, with minor disruptions due to a few inter-chromosome rearrangements, translocations (TRAs), and inversions (INVs) (Figure 1A and Supplemental Figures 7 and 8). The analysis of the three cassava genomes revealed 16,796 conserved genes and 646 unique genes for W14, 157 for FLA4047, and 819 for AM560 (Supplemental

Figure 9). The distribution map of the annotated genes across

the 18 chromosomes in the three genomes serves as a valuable resource for guiding cassava breeding efforts (Supplemental

Figure 10). Gene Ontology (GO) enrichment analysis revealed

that the unique genes in AM560 are significantly associated with DNA recombination, DNA polymerase activities, and transportation (Supplemental Table 5), as well as with defense responses, oxidoreductase activity, flower development, and lipid catabolic processes (Supplemental Tables 6 and 7). The evolutionary relationships among the three cassava varieties and other Euphorbiaceae species were assessed using synonymous substitution rates (K values) of single-copy ortholog genes. The speciation events for the three Euphorbiaceae spe cies—Hevea, Jatropha, and castor bean—occurred approxi mately 37.6, 62.6, and 79.8 million years ago (MYA), respectively.

### Pan SV genome of 30 representative cassava genotypes

Twenty-seven representative accessions and the three obtained cassava reference genomes were chosen for pan-genome con struction (Figure 2). The selection included 22 cultivars from different continents and eight accessions of wild ancestors. The additional accessions were subjected to ONT (Oxford Nanopore Technologies) long-read sequencing to generate a minimum of 50× genome coverage. This enabled high-quality genome assembly with a contig N50 of 5–10 Mb and total genome sizes ranging from 605 to 843 Mb (Supplemental

Table 9), as well as the prediction of 29,547–41,950 protein-

coding genes (Supplemental Table 10). We developed a comprehensive graphic pan-genome of cassava by incorpo rating 359,527 insertions (INSs) and 42,310 deletions (DELs) across eight wild accessions and 22 cultivated clones into the AM560 reference genome. This process resulted in a cassava pan-genome with a total size of 1.15 Gb, comprising 551,617 contigs. A representation of chromosome 1 is illustrated in

Figure 2H. The integrated orthologous genes can be classified

into 33,434 families, comprising approximately 33,470 gene models (Supplemental Table 10). The number of gene families increased with the number of genomes and approached saturation at 30 genomes (Figure 2A), suggesting that the pan- genome built provides a proper representation of the genomes of the cassava species. Overall, 5,352 gene families (∼16%) ap peared in all genomes and were assigned as core genes. Simi larly, 4,648 families (14%) were softcore gene families, and 22, 562 families (∼67%) were dispensable gene families. A total of 908 gene families were found exclusively in a single genome and were assigned as private genes (∼3%) (Figure 2B; Supplemental Table 11). A KEGG pathway analysis revealed that the core genes are enriched primarily in metabolic pathways, spliceosome, and proteasome (Supplemental

Figure 11a). In contrast, the softcore genes are enriched in

mismatch repair, non-homologous end-joining, and RNA degra dation (Supplemental Figure 11b). The dispensable genes are enriched in RNA degradation, plant–pathogen interaction, and ribosome biogenesis in eukaryotes (Supplemental Figure 11c). The private genes are mainly ascribed to pentose and gluconate conversions, valine, leucine, and isoleucine metabolism, and to butanoate metabolism (Supplemental

Figure 11d).

Using the AM560 genome as a reference, SVs were identified in the genomes of 22 cultivars and eight wild accessions with Sniffles software. A total of 346,322 SV events were detected (Figure 2F and Supplemental Figure 12; Supplemental

Table 12), with DELs and INSs comprising 51.01% and 23.32%

of the total SVs, respectively, and exhibiting a scattered distribution across the 18 chromosomes (Figure 2G). These were followed by TRAs (18.33%) and DUPs (5.5%). The DELs, DUPs, and INSs were relatively short, typically ranging from 50

to 200 bp, although some were up to 40 Kb (Figure 2C). A large proportion (78.7%) of the SVs appeared in repeat regions (Figure 2E), and the transposable element (TEs) like Gypsy and Copia account for more than half of the SVs among the INSs as well as DELs (Figure 2D). The number of INSs ranged from 3915 to 7878, with a minimum sum of INS size being 95.30 Mb and the maximum sum of INS size being 161.42 Mb. The average insertion size was 18.7 Kb (Supplemental Figure 13). This suggests that selection also drives structural variation within repetitive sequences in the evolution of cassava genomes. GO enrichment analyses revealed that genes associated with carbon metabolism, including pathways such as β-alanine metabolism, propanoate metabolism, DNA replication, and carbon fixation, are specifically enriched in the insertion regions of cultivars (Supplemental Figure 14A). Conversely, genes related to lipid and protein metabolism, such as those involved in glyoxylate and dicarboxylate metabolism and amino acid biosynthesis, are found in the deletion regions of the cultivated genome, and are absent in modern varieties (Supplemental Figure 14B).

### Gene expansion and contraction from wild ancestors to cultivars

A systematic comparison of gene models of the eight wild cas sava ancestors (FLA) and 22 cultivars (ME) revealed that the wild ancestral genomes harbored 141 homologous genes, which were lost to varying degrees in the modern cultivated varieties. These genes were primarily related to environmental adaptations, including the biosynthesis of specialized metabolites, metabolo mic pathways, and oxidative phosphorylation (Supplemental

Copy-number variations (CNVs) of genes and gene families were compared between the eight wild ancestors and the 22 cultivars. CNVs were frequent in photosynthesis, starch and sugar meta bolism, hormone metabolism, and signaling, and within some families of transcription factors. In photosynthesis, 13 gene fam ilies across the 30 KEGG codes in the light reactions are expanded in the cultivars. On the contrary, 12 contracted gene families are found among the ATP synthase subunits (atpD, *atpH, and atpE) and in photosystem I P700 apoprotein A1* (psaA and *psaB)* (Høj and Møller, 1986) (Supplemental

### Phylogeny and genetic variation from wild ancestors to cultivars

Using 30X Illumina short reads resequencing data for 291 acces sions and published data for 195 accessions (Ramu et al., 2017), we compiled a comprehensive dataset on genetic variation across the 486 cassava genotypes, including 35 wild relatives and 451 cultivated varieties. These domesticated varieties were

sourced from Central and South America, Africa, Southeast Asia, and China, comprising both landraces and improved hybrids (Figure 3A and Supplemental Figure 2; Supplemental

Table 19). We identified 96,032,008 reliable SNP and indel

variations, averaging 131.4 base mutations per Kb sequence. A total of 6,021,758 filtered SNPs were used to construct a cassava phylogeny. Using ADMIXTURE software, the population structure is divided into five groups (Figure 3C). Group A comprises 19 M. glaziovii accessions (GLAs), including Tree Cassava, GLAyn, GLAb, W14, and hybrids with cultivated cassava. The W14 is classified as originating from the GLA background, although principal component analysis (PCA) indicates that W14 is a hybrid with a cultivated species. This latter conclusion is further supported by the production of SRs with a low starch content (Figure 3B). Thus, GLA is among the few wild cassava species that have undergone gene exchange with cultivated cassava during natural evolution (Bredeson et al., 2016). Group B consists of 22 accessions of FLA, collected from various Brazilian states situated on the southern edge of the Amazon. Group C, is composed of domesticated type species most closely related to FLA, and includes 43 landraces from Ecuador, as well as their hybrid progeny with cultivars. Historically, Ecuador is considered the earliest region of human activity and species domestication in South America (Christian, 2011). Group D includes two subgroups: D1, with 68 varieties of landraces and hybrids from central and western Brazil near the cassava domestication center, and D2, with 85 genotypes, including sugary cassava and a series of hybrids, originating near the Amazon domestication center (Olsen and Schaal, 1999).

Group E consists of 248 genotypes categorized into seven sub groups (E1–E7) based on genetic distance. Subgroups E1 and E2 encompass 56 cultivated clones originating from Southern Brazil, including the cold-tolerant clones SC124, F201, and CH16. Subgroup E3 includes specific varieties from the state of Parana´ in Brazil, as well as edible clones from Asia and Africa, which are classified under the geographic subtype of Southern Brazil. Subgroup E4 comprises landraces and hybrids from Uganda, along with additional African collections that were introduced from Colombia. Accessions E5 through E7, totaling 99 samples, represent an extensive collection of landraces and their hybrid progenies collected from regions extending from Western to Eastern Africa (Figure 3B; Supplemental

Table 20). Evidence indicates that the genetic domestication

of cassava has been ongoing since its introduction to the African continent approximately 450 years ago. ### Selective sweeps during the domestication of cassava

We analyzed the selective sweeps represented in genomes of 198 cultivars (ME) and 28 accessions of the wild ancestor (FLA) using ROD (reduction of diversity), Fst (population genetic differ entiation index), and a combination of the two indices (ROD-Fst) with all diversity SNPs and indels (Supplemental Figure 27; Supplemental Table 13). For all 18 chromosomes, a sequence sliding window of 100 Kb with 10 Kb increments was applied, followed by calculation of the ROD and Fst values in each window. We identified the selective sweep regions using the significance criteria of 5%, 2%, and 1%. The statistical results reveal a total of 18,006 windows, where the diversity decreased in cultivars compared with the FLA. Within the top 5% ROD value, we identified 162 loci, covering 49.3 Mb over 2,150 genes. Only 28 loci are found within the top 2% of ROD, covering 16.4 Mb over 390 genes. Within the top 1% of ROD, 27 regions are identified, covering a total of 9.3 Mb and spanning 174 genes (Supplemental Table 23). Analyses based on the Fst parameter and the top 5%, 2%, and 1% significance criteria identified 271, 150, and 67 loci, accounting for 60.7, 27. 9, and 13.2 Mb of the genome and including 2,931, 1,412, and 652 genes, respectively (Supplemental Table 24). The cultivars have larger π values of gene sequence diversity compared with the wild relatives. These differences may relate to the preferred region of cassava domestication. Integrated index of ROD and Fst helped identify 122 selective sweeping regions with 1,519 genes within the top 5% (Supplemental Table 25). KEGG and GO annotations showed their involvement in metabolic pathways, including biosynthesis of cofactors (32 genes), plant hormone signal transduction (26 genes), starch and sucrose metabolism (24 genes), and ribosome composition (22 genes) (Supplemental Figure 28). These genes identified through sweep analysis are distinct from those that undergo expansion and contraction, and represent genes that have coevolved in both wild and cultivated varieties but undergone preferential selection in cultivars. For instance, the MeTFL2 (13G011900) and MeCSK (13G022900) genes are simultaneously identified on chromosome 13 (Supplemental Figure 29). For most of the cultivars (185/198), a 1,053 bp hAT-MITE TE appears 7 Kb up

stream of the coding region of MeTFL2. In contrast, no trans poson was found in the promoters of MeTFL2 in 26/28 wild an cestors. The MeTFL2 is associated with flowering inhibition and SR development in cassava. An 83 bp SINE retrotransposon element is inserted in the promoter regions of chloroplast sensor kinase MeCSK in many cultivars (167/198), but not in any wild rel atives except one. MeCSK regulates the levels of the D1 and D2 proteins in photosystem II, and is functionally linked to the effi cient adaptation of cassava to high light conditions. We also found the auxin regulator MeTIR (02G147400) in a sweeping re gion, as confirmed by population association analysis (Hu et al.,

2021). We further identified a total of 2469 genes that exhibit the insertion of 1 of 9 types of pivot transposons, including hAT-MITE, mutator-MITE, stowaway-MITE, and SINEs, within their promoter region (2 Kb upstream of the ATG codon). This analysis was conducted across all genes annotated in 198 culti vars and 28 wild relatives, utilizing ITIS software for comprehen sive examination (Supplemental Table 26).
### Evolution of photosynthesis

Conversely, *MeFT2 is predominantly expressed in the mature leaves of* cultivars. It is transported to the cambium cells of roots, where it binds with MeTFL2 and other functional proteins to initiate SR development (Figure 5I). Further experimental work is needed to elucidate the detailed regulatory mechanisms essential for practical applications in cassava breeding.

### Evolution of cyanogenic glucoside metabolism

Two cyanogenic glucosides, linamarin and lotaustralin, are known to be biosynthesized and accumulate in leaves, SRs, and other or gans of cassava and to act as protectants against herbivores and pests (Tattersall et al., 2001; Gleadow and Møller, 2014; Ogbonna et al., 2021). Sweet cassava cultivars with a low content of cyanogenic glucosides are available for human and livestock consumption, but the molecular basis explaining how this domestication was achieved is not understood. We utilized the genome sequences of wild and cultivated cassava to reconstruct the selection history embedded in the promoters of critical genes involved in cyanogenic glucoside biosynthesis, transport, and remobilization (Jørgensen et al., 2005; Kannangara et al., 2011; Andersen et al., 2000). A comparative analysis of the hydrogen cyanide potentials of the wild progenitors W14 and FLA4047 alongside six cultivated varieties shows that, in the SRs, the hydrogen cyanide potential varied from 71 to 398 μg/g, representing an increase of 1.4- to 5.6-fold in the wild species compared with the cultivars. Similarly, in the leaves, the hydrogen cyanide potential ranged from 379 to 1326 μg/g, which is 1.5- to 3.5-fold higher in the wild species than in the cultivars (Figure 6A; Supplemental Table 15). We further assessed the hydrogen cyanide potential in the SRs of 30 cassava varieties at DAP120. The results indicate that the mean hydrogen cyanide potential for 15 sweet cassava varieties is 57 μg/g. In contrast, the mean potential for the remaining 15 bitter varieties, including two wild accessions, is 250 μg/g (Supplemental Table 16). The development of sweet cassava varieties represents a significant achievement in the domestication of cassava by humans.

### Massive genomic information reshapes the adaptive domestication of cassava

A comprehensive analysis of resequencing datasets from 486 cassava accessions, sourced from multiple continents, was con ducted to construct a phylogenetic tree in comparison with a pre cise reference genome. This evolutionary tree distinctly catego rizes the outer groups (FLA), closely related wild subspecies (FLA), and cultivated subspecies. Furthermore, the cultivated subspecies are subdivided into four distinct subcategories: C, D1, D2, and E. Group C, which is most closely related to the wild subspecies and comprises 52 members, harbors landraces and hybrid offspring from Ecuador and Colombia, as well as several cultivated clones of uncertain origin. These findings corroborate that the cultivated subspecies of cassava originates from a single M. esculenta ssp. flabellifolia subspecies. This finding deviates from previous assertions inferred from single- base substitutions in the G3pdh gene, implying that the origin of cassava was in the regions of Acre, Rondoˆ nia, and Mato Grosso in Brazil (Olsen and Schaal, 1999). However, this

conclusion was based on an analysis of wild and cultivated genotypes from this particular area and excluded previous fossil evidence. Those proposed sites of origin are flat and humid, and do not provide the basic domestication conditions of drought and barrenness that are essential for the evolution of the SR of cultivated cassava. Paleoclimate research has proved that the climate in the Acre area was stable over 10,000 years (Pa¨ rssinen et al., 2020).

South America is recognized as the origin of several significant economic crops, including tubers, kidney beans, pumpkins, and peppers. Through the examination of plant fossils, interpre tive analysis, and the chronology of human activities, it has been concluded that the earliest instances of domestication occurred in the southwestern region of Ecuador, South America, between 10,200 and 8,500 years before present (BP). Notable among these crops are Calathea allouia (leren), Maranta arundi *nacea (arrowroot), Cucurbita moschata (squash), and cassava.* Evidence from macrofossils and starch grain analysis indicates that manioc (cassava) was present 8500 BP in the Zan˜ a Valley of northern Peru. These cultivated crops subsequently expanded to other regions. Carbonized starch from cassava roots was later identified in southwestern Ecuador (4470 BP) and Panama (5600 BP). The domestication processes of these crops predominantly took place in areas between tropical rainforests and savannas, aligning with patterns of human activity (Piperno and Smith, 2006, 2011). Phytolith analysis, including extensive forest island samples from the Bolivian highlands adjacent to northern Peru, suggests that cassava with SRs emerged approximately 10,350 years ago (Umberto et al., 2020). Summarized, we propose that the domestication and origin of cultivated cassava occurred in the highlands of Northern Peru and Ecuador.

The graphic pan-genome of cassava species is provided by ana lyses of 30 representatives selected from the 486 accessions. Important expansion (1965) and contraction of genes (143) in the domestication of cultivated cassava have been unearthed. The interspecific evolutionary age of cassava was estimated us ing the protein sequences of 300 common single-copy genes. Cultivated cassava diverged from its progenitor subspecies FLA around 5.6 MYA. The FLAs diverged from W14 around 6.9 MYA, and W14 diverged from GLAs after approximately 8.4 MYA. This result deviates significantly from the known rise of local agriculture (Ugent et al., 1986). The high heterozygosity of the cassava genome may be the main reason. Resolving these problems requires further training and adjustment of molecular evolutionary parameters.

### Adaptation to high light for C4 photosynthesis domestication in cassava

The parallel evolution of more efficient light reactions in photo synthesis and C4enzymes enabled cultivated cassava acces sions to grow at high light intensities combined with increased photosynthetic carbon fixation capacity. Previous studies based on the use of 14 C isotope experiments have shown that cultivated cassava can carry out C photosynthesis (Cock et al., 1987; Edwards et al., 1990). In our current study, this conclusion is verified by electron microscopy and *in situ* hybridization studies, showing that leaves from cultivated cassava accessions exhibit the Kranz anatomy functioning as a CO

concentration pump in C4photosynthesis with MeRubisco pre dominantly being localized in vascular sheath cells and MePEPC preferentially expressed in the peripheral mesophyll cells as in maize, which is a typical C4plant (Reyna-Llorens and Hibberd,

2017). In contrast to the situation in the wild cassava ancestors, *MePPDK, MePEPC,* and *MeNADP-ME* show enhanced expression in leaves of the cultivated cassava accessions, and with the identified cis element mutation specifically present in the promoter region of MeNADP-ME. We also find systemic SVs in the promoter region of MeCSK and MeFNR2 in the light reaction pathway of photosynthesis, with their level of expression being significantly higher in cultivars than in the wild subspecies. The chloroplast receptor kinase (CSK) might be a key signal switch. It has a ferrithionein structure that balances plastiquinone electron transfer and coordinates the expression of multiple chloroplast-encoded photosystem II genes, regulating adaptation to high light intensities (Puthiyaveetil et al., 2008, 2012, 2013; Ibrahim et al., 2020). The SV analysis reveals that copy number expansion has occurred in 13 photosynthesis- related gene families, with a preference for MeCSK and genes related to glucose metabolism. A recent review of C4photosyn thesis suggests new ideas for C4-based plant improvement, including increasing the proportion and size of BS cells and the number of chloroplasts in these cells (Cui, 2021). Cultivated cassava accessions possess the C4enzyme system and Kranz anatomy and could be used as an experimental system for C4 improvement. It will be important to obtain a theoretical as well as experimental understanding of the high-light adaptation mechanisms and the basis for heavy biomass accumulation uti lizing cassava with C3–C4intermediate photosynthesis.
### Flowering or tuberization, which is the driving factor in cassava?

SVs in MeCOL5 and MeTFL2 are essential in driving SR formation in cassava. SR formation is the most profound change that occurred in the transition from the wild ancestor species to the cultivated cassava. In cultivated cassava, the delayed flowering related to fruit and seed production affords more carbon allo cated to starch accumulation in SRs. By comparing the coding and promoter sequences of 54 genes involved in the develop ment of flower primordia and SRs in FLA and cultivars, only those of MeCOL5 and MeTFL2 show a systematic structural evolution. *MeCOL5 exhibits induced expression in the leaves of plants* subjected to appropriate light regimes, temperature signals, and endogenous sugar levels. Yeast one-hybrid studies demon strate that MeCOL5 selectively interacts with the florigen MeFT1 and MeFT2 (MeSP6A) genes. A similar system is observed in po tato (Navarro et al., 2011). MeCOL5 preferentially initiates the expression of MeFT2 in leaves of cassava cultivars. Subsequent transport into the roots as a signal molecule in combination with MeTFL2 initiates SR development. In the wild ancestral species, MeCOL5 promotes the expression of MeFT1 and regulates floral primordium development (Collani et al.,

2019). Interestingly, the hormone signals after SR or flower primordium initiation are remarkably similar (Hoang et al., 2020). *CONSTANS (CO) and its homologous genes regulate flowering* in Arabidopsis, rice, and potato (Rodrı´guez-Falco´ n et al., 2006; Gonza´ lez-Schain et al., 2012). FT1 and FT2 are synthesized in leaves and transported to FP over long distances (Corbesier et al., 2007; Goretti et al., 2020) and to the tubers in potato
(Navarro et al., 2011). Both FT1 and FT2 are regulated by several upstream binding proteins and transcription factors, such as photoreceptors, CO, and FD (Zhu et al., 2020), and linked to sugar leakage transporter (Sweet11), activation of auxin and cytokinin (Navarro et al., 2011; Hoang et al., 2020). The mechanisms by which the CO-FT system selectively regulates flowering or tuberization remain unclear. The generation of overexpression and gene-editing mutants for MeCOL5 and *MeTFL2 is crucial for elucidating the integration of exogenous* light and temperature signals with endogenous sugar and hor mone signals (Freytes et al., 2021). It is well documented that high temperatures inhibit root tuber development in potatoes (Gu¨ nter et al., 2019), whereas cassava is unaffected. The role of *MeSP6A* and its associated binding proteins in the development of SRs in cassava is yet to be determined.

### Understanding the evolution of cyanogenic glucoside biosynthesis and remobilization in cassava

Although the biosynthesis, transport and remobilization pathways of cyanogenic glucosides and many of the involved genes have been identified in cassava (Andersen et al., 2000; Jørgensen et al., 2005; Kannangara et al., 2011), the regulation of these processes by environmental factors and endogenous sugar and hormone signals has remained unknown. A structural comparison of the 22 known genes involved in cyanogenic glucoside metabolism in the wild ancestors with high hydrogen cyanide potential with the cultured varieties with low hydrogen cyanide potential revealed predominant major differences in the promoter region of MeGTR, resulting in specific downregulation of its expression in leaves of the sweet cassava. In the *MeMATE1* transporter, an SNP mutation in cultivated cassava accessions alters a single amino acid and causes downregulation of its cyanogenic glucoside-related transport ca pacity. This turns wild bitter cassava into sweet cassava and most likely represents the main key event in the evolutionary pro cesses resulting in cassava domestication. A single-nucleotide variation in MATE has previously been reported to alter cyanogenic glucoside content in cassava (Ogbonna et al., 2021). In addition, the expression of the biosynthetic gene MeCYP79D1 and the *MeHNL10-like gene involved in remobilization of cyanogenic* glucosides is significantly downregulated in both leaves and SRs of the cultivars with low hydrogen cyanide potential. Still, no correlated systematic variations are observed in their gene sequences (Møller et al., 2024). Yeast one-hybrid experiments confirm that *MeAHL19* interacts with *MeCYP79D2* and *MeMATE1,* demonstrating that this transcription factor participates in the metabolic regulation of cyanogenic glucoside synthesis, transport, and remobilization. This would represent a parallel domestication event to that reported in almonds, where a single amino acid substitution in a key bHLH transcription factor completely abolishes its functionality and results in the formation of sweet almond lines (Sanchez-Perez et al., 2019). The findings presented provide an important basis for gene editing to breed sweet varieties of other cyanogenic crops using the non-GM FIND-IT technology or CRISPR-Cas (Knudsen et al., 2022; Cardi et al., 2023; Dockter et al., 2024).

In conclusion, our current study presents a comprehensive collection of cassava genome data, including both wild ancestors and cultivated cassava accessions. Detailed comparative

genomic analyses were carried out to elucidate the molecular mechanisms involved in the domestication of cassava. The find ings provide a unique foundation for future targeted breeding of new desired traits in cassava and offer valuable insights into the adaptive responses of crops to global climate change.

## METHODS

### Plant materials

A total of 290 different accessions of M. esculenta Crantz, its wild ancestor subspecies, M. esculenta ssp. flabellifolia and related species Manihot *glaziovii were used as the experimental material for the study. All cassava* accessions were grown on fields in Chengmai County, Hainan Island, Southern China, where sunlight and rainfall conditions are available for optimal growth of cassava. Leaves and SRs were sampled for DNA sequencing and RNA-seq.

### Genome size measurement by flow cytometry

The genome size is 1C = 0.72 ± 0.02 Gb for the cultivated cassava,

*M. esculenta accession KU50, the same size as its nearest ancestor,* FLA, M. esculenta ssp. flabellifolia accession FLA4047. The genome size of the related species GLA, M. glaziovii, with accessions GLAyn and GLAb, is 1C = 0.73 ± 0.01 Gb. The accession W14, originally ascribed to M. esculenta ssp. flabellifolia has a genome size of 1C = 0.83 ± 0.01 Gb. This estimated result is slightly larger than the previously reported refer ence, which is cited properly. Genome size estimations by flow cytometry were performed according to Dolezel et al. (2003). Roughly 0.5 cm
2 of young leaf tissue was chopped with a sharp razor blade in a petri dish together with an appropriate amount of leaf tissue of the internal reference standard rice with accession Nipponbare (1C = 0.43 Gb) and maize with accession B73 (1C = 2.30 Gb). Using a ‘‘CyStain PI Absolute P’’ nuclei extraction and staining kit (Sysmex-Partec), the resulting nuclei suspension was filtered through a 50-mm filter (CellTrics) and measured on a CyFlow Space flow cytometer (FACScalibur). The absolute DNA con tent (pg/2C) was calculated based on the values of the G1 peak means and the corresponding genome size (Mb/1C).

### Library preparations and sequencing

#### DNA isolation

High-molecular-weight DNA was isolated from 1.5 g of material with a QIAGEN Genomic DNA Extraction Kit (cat. no. 13323 QIAGEN). Quality was assessed with a NanoDrop One UV-Vis spectrophotometer (Thermo Fisher Scientific), and quantity was measured accurately with a Qubit 3.0 Fluorometer (Invitrogen).

#### Whole-genome shotgun sequencing

Genomic DNA from W14, FLA4047, and AM560 was deep sequenced with an Illumina HiSeq 2500 in 150-bp paired-end mode. DNA samples from 290 different cassava accessions were sequenced with an Illumina HiSeq 2500 to reach 30× coverage for each sample.

#### PacBio library construction and sequencing

PacBio libraries for W14 with the insert size of >10 Kb were prepared and then sequenced with P6-C4 reagent kits on PacBio RSII (Pacific Biosci ences, Menlo Park, CA). A total of 18G clean reads were obtained with a sequencing depth of 25×.

#### ONT library construction and sequencing

Long libraries of AM560 and FLA4047 with gDNA size-selected (>20 Kb) using a LSK108 ligation Kit (cat# SQK-LSK108, Oxford) were constructed and accurately detected using a Qubit 3.0 Fluorometer. The DNA library was added to a flow cell, which was transferred to Nanopore GridION X5 (Oxford Nanopore Technologies, UK) for real-time single-molecule sequencing. The ultralong DNA libraries for AM560 (two cells), FLA4047 (two cells), and W14 (two cells) genomes were constructed and used for complete genome sequence assembly. For each ultralong Nanopore li brary, approximately 8–10 μg of gDNA was size-selected (>50 Kb) with a SageHLS HMW library system (Sage Science), and processed using a Ligation sequencing 1D kit (SQK-LSK109, Oxford Nanopore Technolo

gies) according to the manufacturer’s instructions. Meanwhile, long DNA libraries were constructed with lengths exceeding 50 Kb for 21 ac cessions and were assembled independently for pan-genome analysis. About 800 ng DNA libraries were constructed and sequenced on the Promethion (Oxford Nanopore Technologies) at the Genome Center of Grandomics (Wuhan, China).

#### Hi-C library construction and sequencing

To enhance the continuity of the genome assembly, additional Hi-C sequencing reads were generated. The Hi-C library was prepared using a Dovetail Hi-C Library Kit (Dovetail Genomics, Scotts Valley, CA) and sequenced on an Illumina NovaSeq 6000 platform. This process yielded 108 Gb (180× coverage), 96 Gb (160× coverage), and 101 Gb (168× coverage) of Hi-C data for samples AM560, W14, and FLA4047, respectively.

#### BioNano

The ultrahigh-molecular-weight (uHMW) DNA was isolated from young leaves of M. esculenta genotype W14 and AM560 by the Amplicon Ex press (Pullman, WA). The nicked DNA molecules were labeled and stained according to the instructions of the IrysPrep Reagent Kit (Bionano Geno mics, San Diego, CA) for the NLRS (nick, label, repair, and stain) method as described in detail in Luo et al. (2017). The DNA sample was transferred to the nanochannel array of an IrysChip (Bionano Genomics) and was automatically imaged using the Irys system (Bionano Genomics). For the DLS method, the uHMW DNA molecules were labeled with the DLE-1 enzyme (Bionano Genomics). They were stained according to the instruc tions of the Bionano Prep Direct Label and Stain (DLS) Kit (Bionano Genomics).

#### Optical map construction

Two optical maps using NLRS and DLS (direct label and stain) technolo gies were constructed for W14, and one optical map using the DLS tech nology was constructed for AM560.

Raw DNA molecules >20 Kb were collected and converted into BNX files using the AutoDetect software to obtain basic labeling and DNA length in formation. Those molecules >180 Kb, which passed quality control, were aligned, clustered, and assembled into an optical map using the Bionano Genomics assembly pipeline (Bionano Genomics). The thresholds used for pairwise assembly, extension/refinement, and final refinement stages were 1 × 10 8 , 1 × 10 9 , and 1 × 10 15 , respectively. A consensus optical map was de novo assembled using 8 the Bionano Solve v3.3 pack age with significance cutoffs 9 of p < 1 × 10 to generate draft consensus contigs, 15 *p < 1 × 10* for draft consensus contig extension, and p < 1 × 10 for final merging of the draft consensus contigs. A recipe of ‘‘haplotype,’’ ‘‘noES,’’ and ‘‘noCut’’ was chosen. The initial optical map, constructed using either method, was then checked for potential chimeric contigs and further refined.

The run-ASM- pipeline.sh script from 3D-DNA was employed to scaffold a draft refer ence genome, and Juicebox software was subsequently used for manual ordering and orientation.

#### Repeat identification and gene annotation

Repeat elements in the three cassava genomes were screened using RepeatMasker with the Repbase library, and TE-encoded proteins were identified using RepeatProteinMask. The identification of de novo TEs was performed using RepeatModeler2 (Flynn et al., 2020). Three approaches were combined to predict genes. AUGUSTUS (Stanke et al., 2004), Glimmer-HMM (Majoros et al., 2004), and SNAP (Korf, 2004) were used for ab initio prediction. We also assembled RNA-seq reads into transcripts using Trinity (Grabherr et al., 2011) for gene structure annotation. Protein sequences from Arabidopsis *thaliana, Populus* *trichocarpa, and Ricinus communis were aligned to the cassava* genome using Exonerate (Slater and Birney, 2005) for homology-based gene prediction. The gene sets predicted by the three approaches were integrated with EVM (Haas et al., 2008) to produce consensus gene models, which were then updated by PASA (Haas et al., 2003). The cassava protein sequences were aligned to the NCBI non-redundant (nr) protein database using BLAST (E value ≤1E 5) (v2.2.6), and the best hit of the alignment was used to infer their biological function. GO annotation was performed using Blast 2GO (Flynn et al., 2020), which assigned homologous sequences aligned by BLAST with the NCBI nr database to GO terms. The cassava protein sequences were also compared with the KEGG GENES database by BLAST (E value ≤1E 5) for KO (KEGG orthology) assignments and pathway mapping.

#### Pan-SV genome construction

Using the AM560-2 v7.0 genome as a reference, we analyzed the gene density and repetitive sequences in 21 cultivated cassava varieties and eight wild relatives. We used Sniffles to detect SVs and found that INSs and DELs were most common, followed by TRAs. Among five mutation types, the longest missing SV exceeded the longest INS, with inversions (INVs) and DUPs following.

The construction of the graphical pan-genome sequence was accom plished utilizing Minigraph (Li et al., 2020), while visualization was performed with VAP software (Li et al., 2023). Minigraph iteratively constructs a directed, bidirected, and acyclic variation graph by mapping new haplotypes through the integration of the minimap2 tool and the graph wavefront alignment algorithm. The VAP software was used for visualizing read alignments and navigating paths within the graphical pan-genome.

#### Identification of SVs and CNVs

MUM&Co is a single bash script that detects SVs using whole-genome alignment. Using MUMmer’s nucmer alignment, MUM&Co can detect INSs, DELs, tandem DUPs, INVs, and TRAs greater than 50 bp. MUM& Co’s initial inputs are two genomes for comparison. The AM560 genome was set as the reference genome for comparison.

#### Phylogenetic tree reconstruction

A global collection of 482 cassava accessions was re-sequenced. Genomic DNA from fresh leaves was used to prepare 350 bp Illumina li braries. After filtering raw data with fastp, paired-end reads were aligned to the AM560 genome using BWAi (v0.7.8). Duplicate reads were removed with SAMtools (v0.1.19), and the data were converted into a GATK- compatible sorted file. Genomic variants were identified using GATK’s HaplotypeCaller, producing a gvcf file per individual. These files were merged to find high-quality SNPs and indels, filtered by depth ≥4, geno type quality ≥5, MAF ≥0.05, and missingness ratio ≤0.1. The SNPs and indels were then annotated with effSNP and categorized as intergenic, up stream, or downstream. The population genetic structure was assessed using ADMIXTURE (v1.23) with k values ranging from 2 to 12, with k = 5 selected due to the lowest cross-validation error. VCF2diffmatrix calcu lated genetic distances for cassava samples, and the resulting matrices were converted to tree files with neighbor software and visualized on ITOL. PCA analysis was performed using GCTA and visualized in R. Nucle otide diversity (π) and fixation index (FST) were calculated with vcftools in 10 Kb steps within 50 Kb windows. Results from 20 replicates were plotted as boxplots in R. To assess and compare LD patterns among groups, the squared correlation coefficient (R2) between SNP pairs was computed using PopLDdecay (v3.40) with parameters MaxDist 500, MAF 0.05, and Miss 0.1. Average R 2 values were determined for marker pairs in 500 Kb windows and averaged across the genome.

### Selective sweeping analysis

#### Detection of genetic variation

Based on the assembled genome of cultivated AM560, SAMtools Faidx was used to construct indexes, and BWA software was used to map all the re-sequencing reads of 238 cassava accessions onto the reference genome. After removing redundancy from the database, the Haplotype Caller program in the GATK software was used to detect variations in each sample and generate a GVCF file for each genotype. Then, GenomicsDBImport and GenotypeGVCFs programs can be used to extract SNPs and indels. Finally, VariantFiltration was used for cleaning of SNPs and indels. For Filter criteria, the SNP selection is based on the following parameters: "QD < 2.0 || MQ < 40.0 || FS > 60.0 || SOR >3.0 || MQRankSum <12.5 || ReadPosRankSUM < - 8.0". For filtering indels, the parameters were: "QD < 2.0 || FS > 200.0 || SOR >10.0 || MQRankSum < 12.5 || ReadPosRankSUM < 8.0".

#### Selective sweeping analysis

We focused on the genomic evolutionary selection signals between 198 accessions of cultivated species (M. esculenta Crantz) and 28 accessions of its wild subspecies (M. esculenta ssp. flabellifolia). ROD, Fst, and ROD- Fst were used for analysis. In brief, windows were set at intervals of 100 Kb on the 18 chromosomes of cassava, and the corresponding index of each window was calculated using a 10 Kb forward sliding distance. The ROD and Fst values of the diversity reduction index on each window of the culti vated population and the wild population were calculated. These two values were selected according to the criteria of the top 5%, the top 2%, and the top 1%, respectively. In addition, ITIS software was used to screen the distribution of Hat-mite, Mutator-mite, Stowaway, and SINE transposons in the promoter region (within 2 Kp upstream of the cod ing region) of cassava genes in cultivated and wild populations. The aim is to determine whether the promoter regions of the selected genes in the two populations have different transposon distributions.

#### RNA-seq

Total RNA was isolated from more than 160 different tissues, including leaves, SRs, and flower buds of cassava and its wild relatives, FLAs and GLAs. Poly(A) RNA was enriched from 1 mg of total RNA using the NEBNext Poly(A) mRNA Magnetic Isolation Module. The quality and quan tity were further assessed using a NanoDrop 2000C and an Agilent 2100 platform. For Illumina sequencing, 3 mg of RNA from each sample was used to construct a cDNA library using a NEBNext Ultra RNA library Prep Kit. The PCR products obtained were purified (AMPure XP system), and library quality was assessed on the Agilent Bioanalyzer 2100 system.

Library preparations were sequenced on Illumina HiSeq 2500 or HiSeq X Ten systems according to experimental designs.

### Measure of photosynthesis parameters

#### Net Pn

Photosynthetic parameters, including the Pn per leaf area, stomatal conductance, and transpiration rate of cassava cultivars and their wild an cestors, were measured under field conditions at 09:00 to 12:00 using an LI-6400XT portable photosynthesis system (LI-COR, Lincoln, NE) accord ing to the manufacturer’s instructions. The measurement adopted artificial light with intensity (PARi value) from 500 to 2,500 μmol/m2/s.

#### ATP content

According to the instruction manual for the ATP content detection Kit of SolarBio, reagents 1–6 were prepared. The ATP standard solution was diluted with distilled water to a concentration of 0.625 μmol/mL. 0.1 g of fresh cassava leaves was added to 1 mL of extract for homogenization in an ice bath, and then centrifuged at 8,000 × g for 10 minutes at 4◦C. The supernatant was transferred to another EP tube, and 500 μL of chlo rine was added. The samples were thoroughly shaken and mixed, then centrifuged at 4◦C for 3 minutes at 10,000 × g. The supernatant was collected and placed on ice. The absorbance value A1 of the ATP stan dard for 10 s at 340 nm was measured immediately. Then, the reaction solution with the cuvette was placed in a water bath at 25◦C for 3 minutes and measured immediately at 3 minutes and 10 seconds to obtain the absorbance value A2. The δAi (determination tube) = A2 determination tube was measured and calculated – A1 measurement tube; δAs (stan dard tube) = A2 standard tube – A1 standard tube. The ATP content was calculated according to the sample quality (W): ATP content (μmol/g mass) = 0.625× δAi ÷ δAs ÷ W. The plant leaves in the top third to fourth position were sampled at 09:00 to 11:00 and used for ATP detection under normal field conditions.

#### Measurement of phytohormones

Fresh plant material was harvested, weighed, frozen in liquid nitrogen, and stored at 80◦C. A 50 mg sample was ground into powder in liquid nitro gen and extracted with methanol/water/formic acid (15:4:1, v/v) at 4◦C. The extract was vortexed for 10 minutes, centrifuged at 12,000 rpm for 5 minutes at 4◦C, and the supernatant was collected. This process was repeated, and the combined extracts were evaporated to dryness under nitrogen gas, reconstituted in 80% methanol, and ultrasonicated for 1 min ute and filtered through a PTFE membrane (0.22 mm; Anpel) before LC- MS/MS analysis. The sample extracts were analyzed using an LC-ESI- MS/MS system using a UPLC system from Thermo and a Q Exactive

MS. The effluent was connected to an ESI-triple quadrupole-linear ion trap MS. Analytical conditions: Waters HSS T3 column (50 × 2.1 mm,

1.8 μm), 40◦C, 0.3 mL/min flow rate, 2 μL injection volume, solvent system of water (0.1% acetic acid) and acetonitrile (0.1% acetic acid), with a gradient program of 90:10 (v/v) throughout. #### Proteome sequencing and annotation

Protein extraction and digestion: Fresh leaves from wild-type cassava (W14), wild relatives (A4047), and cultivated species (KU50) were har vested and frozen in liquid nitrogen. Three biological replicates were collected for each accession. Samples were homogenized using P-PER Plant Protein Extraction Reagent to obtain organic phases. Protein concentration was measured using the BCA protein assay (Thermo Fisher). Proteins were purified by acetone precipitation, and the pellet was resuspended in 50 mM TEAB. Trypsin was added, and the solution was incubated at 37◦C overnight.

TMT labeling and LC-MS/MS analysis: equal amounts of protein samples from each biological replicate were labeled using a TMT-10Plex kit (Thermo Scientific) as per the manufacturer’s instructions. The labeled peptides were pooled, lyophilized, and then processed using LC-MS/ MS with a C18 column (Thermo Fisher). Gradient elution was conducted with acetonitrile and 0.1% formic acid as mobile phases, and spectra were acquired in data-dependent acquisition mode.

Data processing and bioinformatics: Raw data were processed with Pro teome Discoverer 2.4 and searched against the cassava protein database using trypsin, allowing two missed cleavages. The precursor mass toler ance was set to 10 ppm, and the peptide false discovery rate was kept at ≤ 1%. Bioinformatics analysis included GO annotation, KEGG pathway analysis, and STRING-based PPI network construction for proteins with a fold change > 1.5 and p < 0.05.

#### Quantification of cyanogenic glucosides in cassava

Fresh SRs and leaves were sampled, ground into a fine powder, immedi ately frozen in liquid nitrogen, and stored at 80◦C until needed. Two gram samples were weighed, placed into a triangle bottle, 100 mL of water added, and sealed for incubation at room temperature for 4 hours. The sample was transferred into a distillation bottle, 100 mL of water, 20 mL of zinc acetate solution (100 g/L) added, and distilled with 1–2 g of tartaric acid. Around 45 mL was collected in a small beaker containing 5 mL NaOH (5%) and transferred to a 50 mL volumetric flask and adjusted to 50 mL (V1) with distilled water. V2 (1 mL) was transferred into a 25 mL colori metric tube, distilled water added to 10 mL, and 0.5 mL 2% NaOH and 1 drop phenolphthalein added, and adjusted with acetic acid solution (1:24) until the red color disappeared, then 5 mL phosphate buffer (pH =

7) was added, and the solution was heated to 37◦C for 10 minutes, and then 0.25 mL chloramine T was added. A stopper was inserted, and the solution was mixed and shaken for 5 minutes, and 5 mL of pyrazolone iso nicotinate solution was added, and mixed with water to a total volume of 25 mL, then placed at 20◦C–40◦C for 40 minutes, until a colorimetric absorbance value of 638 nm was measured. A reference sample was pre pared using distilled water. The total amount of hydrogen cyanide was quantified by reference to the hydrogen cyanide standard curve.
#### Real-time qPCR validation

The expression of important genes in photosynthesis, differentiation of flower primordia and SRs, and cyanogenic glucoside metabolism has been validated by real-time qPCR. The total RNA of samples was extracted using a TRIzol Kit (Invitrogen, cat#15596018) for three biological repeats ac cording to the user’s manual. About 1.5 mg of each RNA sample was used for cDNA synthesis (Maxima H Minus cDNA Synthesis Master Mix, with dsDNase, Thermo Scientific, cat# M1682). Real-time qPCR was performed using primer pairs (Supplemental Table 13) in SsoFast EvaGreen Supermix (Bio-Rad, cat# 1725201) with the real-time PCR detection system (Bio-Rad, CFX96). PCR reactions were performed in quadruplicate for each sample, and expression levels were normalized to OaUBI.

#### Fluorescence in situ PCR

Freshly unfolded leaves and SRs from cassava plants at 120–150 DAP were collected for in situ PCR to study tissue-specific expression of Me *COL5, MeTFL2, MeGTR, MeMATE1, and MeHNL, which are involved in* the regulation of flowering and SR development and cyanogenic glucoside transport. The protocol follows the guidelines of Jørgensen et al. (2005). Before sectioning, all tissues were fixed in FAA (2% formaldehyde, 5% acetic acid, and 63% ethanol in PBS) for 5 hours at 4◦C and washed three times with a buffer of 60% ethanol and 5% acetic acid in PBS. The tissue was embedded in 5% agarose in PBS for sectioning into 80 μm slices using a Leica freezing microtome. Sections were incubated in 20 μL enzyme-free water with 4 U RNase inhibitor and 40 U DNase at 37◦C for 4 hours, then washed twice with enzyme- free water, soaked in pepsin (2 ng/μL in 10 mM HCl) for 15 minutes, and washed again. mRNA was converted to cDNA using specific reverse primers, 1× RT Buffer, and 2 mM dNTP at 65◦C for 5 minutes, with RNase inhibitor and Sensicript added at 4◦C. The mixture was returned to 45◦C for 60 minutes, then heated to 97◦C for 1 minute, and subsequently stored at 4◦C. Before cDNA amplification, the reverse transcriptase solution was removed, and the PCR reagents were added. DIG-labeled dUTP was incorporated as the basis for visualization. After PCR amplification, 175 μL of anti-DIG-POD working solution was added at 37◦C and gently shaken for 30 minutes. The sample was then washed four times with washing buffer. The solution was discarded, and 175 μL of ABTS solution was added, then gently shaken for 30 minutes at 37◦C in a dark environ ment. The sections were cleaned and examined using a laser scanning confocal microscope (Leica/TCS SP8).

#### Yeast one-hybrid assay

Yeast one-hybrid assay was performed according to the method described by Li et al. (2023). The yeast one-hybrid bait vector pAbAi- target genes (MeCYP79D2 and MeCOL5) were constructed, and the cas sava leaf total RNA was isolated. Then, the double-stranded cDNA was synthesized following the protocol as in a previous study. The double- stranded cDNAs were linked with Sma I linearized prey vector pGADT7- Rec, transformed into the yeast-positive strain Y1HGold/pAbAi target gene, and then cultivated on SD/-Ura-Leu medium with the appropriate aureobasidin A (AbA) concentration at 30◦C for 3 days. Plasmid PCR and sequencing identified the positive colonies. To confirm the interaction between TF (MeCOL5, MeFT1, MeFT2, and MeTFL2) and the target gene, the full-length TF coding sequence (CDS) was cloned by PCR. The CDS was ligated into the pGADT7 vector using Nde I and Sal I, and the resulting construct was named pGADT7-TF. pGADT7-TF and the pAbAi target gene were co-transformed into the Y1HGold yeast strains. pGADT7- Rec53 + p53-AbAi, pAbAi-target gene, pGADT7-TF, and pGADT7-TF + pAbAi were used as controls. The transformed cells were grown on SD/-Leu selective medium with the appropriate AbA concentration at 30 ◦ C for 3 days.
