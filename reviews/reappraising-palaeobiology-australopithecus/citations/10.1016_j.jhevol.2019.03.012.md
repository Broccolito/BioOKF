### Manuscript Details

**Manuscript number** HUMEV_2018_131_R3 **Title** Relevance of the eastern African coastal forest for early hominin biogeography **Article type** Full Length Article **Abstract** The influence of climate change on hominin evolution is much debated. Two issues hamper our understanding of this process: the limited hominin fossil record, and incomplete knowledge about hominin spatial occupation of Africa. Here, we analyze the presently known hominin fossil distribution pattern and explore the potential geographic distribution of hominins between ~4.5 and ~2.5 Ma. We focus on assessing the relevance of the Coastal Forest of Eastern Africa (CFEA) along the Indian Ocean as a core area for early hominin evolution. Based on biogeographic-phylogeographic data we propose the coastal refuge hypothesis: the CFEA provided a refugium for early hominins in periods of variable climate and strong seasonality during eccentricity maxima. From this refuge, evolved species could disperse inland (e.g. to rift basins) via vegetated humid corridors, whenever onset of stable climate periods with low seasonality during eccentricity minima allowed expansion out of the coastal enclave. We develop a conceptual model in time and space, comparing predictions with climatic and hominin fossil records. The results imply that: 1) between ~4.5-3 Ma, ongoing (mostly anagenetic) hominin evolution occurred in the CFEA, punctuated by inland dispersal events at ~4.4, 4.2, 3.8,

3.5, and 3.2 Ma; 2) before ~3 Ma, the Afar Basin was a (sub)core area similar to the CFEA, while other inland areas were more or less marginal for early hominin habitation; 3) after ~3 Ma, Northern Hemisphere Glaciation exerted strong influence by causing latitudinal contraction of the CFEA, leading to habitat fragmentation, isolation of hominin populations and possible cladogenetic evolution. A major challenge for the coastal refuge model is the fact that at present, no (hominin) fossils are known from the CFEA. We consider how this can be explained, and possibly overcome with targeted search efforts. Furthermore we discuss how the model can be tested, e.g. with molecular phylogeography approaches, and used to predict new hominin fossil locations. With this study, we hope to contribute a fresh perspective to the climate-evolution debate, emphasizing the role of climatic stability, length of dry season and vegetation cover to facilitate connectivity between hominin core and marginal habitats. **Keywords** hominin evolution; refugium; climate stability; eccentricity; dispersal corridors **Corresponding Author** Josephine Joordens **Order of Authors** Josephine Joordens, Craig Feibel, Hubert Vonhof, Anne Schulp, Dick Kroon

451. Introduction 46For decades, paleoanthropologists study the influence of climatic and environmental change on 47early hominin evolution in Africa (e.g. Vrba, 1988, 2007; Vrba et al., 1995; Potts, 1998a,b, 482007, 2013; Bobe et al., 2002; Bonnefille et al., 2004; Wynn, 2004; deMenocal, 2004, 2011; 49Behrensmeyer, 2006; Maslin and Christensen, 2007; Maslin and Trauth, 2009; NRC, 2010; 50Joordens et al., 2011; Grove, 2012; Potts and Faith, 2015; Levin, 2015; Marean et al., 2015;

51Cohen et al., 2016; Campisano et al., 2017). Yet, there are two major issues that still obstruct 52our understanding of factors influencing early hominin evolution (Spoor, 2016). Firstly, we 53have to face the ugly fact that the fossil record is intrinsically limited, constituting an imperfect 54representation (on the order of a few percent only) of actual species diversity that existed over 55time (Martin, 1993). Secondly, the part of this limited hominin fossil record that has been 56found so far (Fig. 1a) was recovered from areas that cover only a fraction of the African 57continent (Fig. 1b). The fossils mainly derive from the eastern branch of the East African Rift 58System (EARS) and from southern African caves, where conditions for fossilization and 59subsequent exposure are favorable and search efforts by paleoanthropologists have been 60greatest (Wood, 2002; Spoor, 2016). Maxwell et al. (2018) investigated whether the presently 61known early hominin fossil record is of sufficient quality to test climate-forcing hypotheses. 62They found that apparent relationships between early hominin taxon diversity and climatic 63indicators could be largely explained by variation in exposure of suitable sediments and fossil 64collection effort, instead of climate. 65 66Based on temporal and spatial hominin distribution patterns resulting from this evidently 67biased fossil recovery, the EARS and southern Africa have often been considered as not only 68fossil-rich locations, but also “birthplaces” of hominin species and thus the main theater of 69hominin evolution (e.g. Coppens, 1994; Maslin et al, 2014). However, the discovery of early 70hominin fossils in the Chad Basin in western-central Africa (Brunet et al., 1995, 2002; 71Lebatard et al., 2008), 2500 km west of the EARS, served as a reminder that the EARS and 72southern African sites must represent only a small and perhaps even marginal part of the total 73possible hominin occupation area in Africa (Joordens, 2011; Spoor, 2016; Joordens et al., 742012, 2017). The recent study on earliest Homo sapiens fossils from northwestern Africa also 75highlights the fact that hominin evolution was not restricted to eastern and southern regions,

76but must have been a pan-African process (Hublin et al., 2017). These observations on an 77imperfect fossil record and incomplete knowledge about hominin occupation of Africa 78demonstrate that paleoanthropologists have only few pieces of the puzzle, and that it is not 79even clear how big the puzzle is. Maxwell et al. (2018) concluded that a sustained and major 80increase in fossil sampling intensity is required before an accurate understanding of links 81between climate and early hominin diversification can be determined. Unfortunately even such 82an effort, though helpful, would not be enough to solve the issues of intrinsically limited fossil 83record as well as absence of fossiliferous Plio-Pleistocene deposits in large parts of Africa. For 84instance, fossils may never be found in the Congo Basin while it is entirely possible that such a 85forest area, an ideal primate habitat, played a role in hominin evolution (Spoor, 2016). 86 87The challenge now is to find a way out of this apparent impasse, and to assess what other areas 88in Africa besides the EARS, southern Africa and Chad Basin may have been relevant for 89hominin evolution. A new and complementary approach is needed to circumvent the issues 90outlined above. We maintain that despite shortcomings of the presently known hominin fossil 91record, the pattern it reflects can provide a wealth of information when placed in a large-scale 92biogeographical framework encompassing a range of organisms (from insects and birds to 93large mammals and primates). As Kingdon (2003) pointed out, hominins must have been 94embedded in the same ecological matrices as other animals in Africa, so insights from recent 95faunal distribution and phylogeography patterns can be applied to extinct hominins. Also Vrba 96(2007) observed that with respect to environmental change, evidence from a range of 97organisms has shown that evolution of taxa does not consist of independent pieces of history 98but is rule bound, with common rules giving qualitative and temporal coherence to the 99evolutionary responses across many lineages. In this review, we therefore seek to create fresh 100perspectives on early hominin evolution by emphasizing the role of African geography,

101drawing on knowledge of faunal and floral evolution in Africa acquired by different disciplines 102-notably biogeography and molecular phylogeography. The aim of this study is to explore 103spatial distributions of early hominins across Africa over time, and ultimately to understand 104influence of climatic and environmental change on evolution and dispersal of early hominin 105species between ~4.5 and ~2.5 Ma. This is a key period covering the early appearance of 106bipedal hominins and earliest appearance of the genus Homo (Spoor, 2015; Villmoare et al., 1072015; Fig. 1a). 108We first present and analyze the geographic distribution pattern of known early hominin fossil 109occurrences over time. Next, we attempt to explain the pattern by 1) comparing it to patterns in 110non-hominin fossil records, and 2) placing it in a biogeographic context using insights of 111Kingdon (2003) on the role of coastal forest along the Indian Ocean, concepts of refugia 112(Stewart et al., 2010), and results of molecular phylogeography studies. Based on these 113findings we formulate a working hypothesis and start developing a testable climate-driven 114biogeographic framework -a conceptual model- for early hominin evolution in Africa. Here we 115focus on assessing relevance of the coastal forest in eastern Africa (Fig. 2) as a primary 116refugium for early hominins, while acknowledging that it will be a simplification of the 117complex actual history of early hominin evolution. Likely, hominins used not one but multiple 118temporary small and large, secondary and cryptic refugia elsewhere (e.g. eastern montane 119forests, western-central African forests, northern coastal Africa?) over time. We hope that our 120study provides an impetus for future studies to focus on the coastal forest, and on evolutionary 121relevance of additional unexplored parts of Africa. 122

1232. The geographic distribution of early hominin fossils 124To investigate hominin biogeography, two basic tasks need to be accomplished (Roebroeks, 1252006): identifying the spatial and temporal pattern of hominin presence and absence (in other

126words ‘getting the pattern right’), and then explaining such a pattern. In figure 3 we present an 127overview of the currently known early hominin fossil record between ~4.5 and 2.5 Ma based 128on available literature, indicating numbers of fossils found per species, time period, and 129location. Detecting a meaningful pattern in fossil presence, absence and abundance is not a 130straightforward exercise (Roebroeks, 2006; Bobe and Leakey, 2009; Patterson et al., 2014; 131Sauron, 2015). Biased search efforts, absence or paucity of deposits from certain time periods 132and places, and differential preservation of bones in different environments prevent discovery 133of species that may have been present, and/or cause failure to capture actual first and last 134appearance dates of species (Hare, 1980; Signor and Lipps, 1982; Roebroeks et al., 2006; 135Vrba, 2007; Bobe and Leakey, 2009; Maxwell et al., 2018). Moreover, it is difficult to make 136taxonomic identifications and infer evolutionary relationships based on few and often partial 137and/or damaged fossil remains (Grine, 2013). Likewise, occurrences of homoplasy (Wood and 138Schroer, 2017) and hybridization (Ackermann, 2011; Popadin et al, 2017; Ackermann et al., 1392018) can result in additional confounding factors for interpretation. Dating fossils can also be 140a major challenge, especially in complex depositional settings such as caves in southern Africa 141(Pickering and Herries, 2017). These difficulties are illustrated here below in a brief review of 142the hominin fossil record. 143

1442.1. The presently-known hominin fossil record 145Recently, Spoor (2015), Kimbel (2015), Wood and Boyle (2016) and Haile-Selassie et al. 146(2016) reviewed what is known and what is still debated about early hominin diversity in 147Africa (Fig. 3). The early hominin species Australopithecus anamensis (from 4.2 to 3.9 Ma) is 148possibly descended from Ardipithecus ramidus (4.5-4.4 Ma) or a close relative (Leakey et al., 1491995; Ward et al., 2001; White et al., 2006, 2009a, 2015; Kimbel et al., 2014). At Galili in the 150Afar Basin, hominin fossils have been found at several levels in deposits aged between 5.4–3.9

151Ma but so far, their taxonomic attributions and precise ages remain unresolved (Kullmer et al., 1522008; Hammond and Ward, 2013; Hujer et al., 2015; Simpson et al., 2017). Next, 153Australopithecus afarensis (~3.8-3.0 Ma; Kimbel, 2015; Haile-Selassie and Su, 2016; Mbua et 154al, 2016) appeared, based on current fossil evidence, to be a descendent of A. anamensis; this 155relationship could be considered as an example of phyletic gradualism in hominin evolution 156(Kimbel et al., 2006, Kimbel, 2015). Around 3.5 Ma, A. afarensis overlapped in time with no 157less than four possible other hominin taxa: A. bahrelghazali in Chad (Brunet et al., 1995; Guy 158et al., 2008), Kenyanthropus platyops in Kenya (Leakey et al., 2001; Spoor et al., 2010), and A. 159deyiremeda (Haile-Selassie et al., 2015) in the Afar Basin in Ethiopia. In addition, the 160Ardipithecus-like “Burtele foot” fossil (BRT-VP-2/73; Haile-Selassie et al., 2012) from ~3.4 161Ma old deposits in the Afar indicates that another lineage --with adaptations to tree climbing-- 162was present in Africa at the same time as A. afarensis. However, due to problems associated 163with small sample size and lack of anatomical overlap of available fossils, it is not certain that 164these Pliocene fossils indeed represent four different species; they could represent three or two 165species only (Spoor, 2015; Haile-Selassie et al., 2016; Spoor et al., 2016). For instance, it has 166been suggested that K. platyops is a regional variant of A. afarensis (White, 2003). In contrast, 167Spoor et al. (2010) concluded that K. platyops and A. afarensis are different species, but 168observed that it cannot be ruled out that A. afarensis and A. bahrelghazali are conspecific. After 169~3 Ma, A. afarensis disappeared completely from the fossil record and A. africanus first 170appeared in South Africa (Lockwood, 2013; Grine, 2013; Kimbel, 2015; Herries et al, 2013; 171Pickering and Herries, 2017). Around ~2.8 Ma two new taxa first appeared in the fossil record, 172Homo sp. in the Afar Basin and the robust Paranthropus aethiopicus in eastern Africa 173(Villmoare et al., 2015; Reed et al., 2015; Spoor, 2015; Kimbel and Villmoare, 2016). 174

175Unresolved taxonomy and dating issues can hamper establishment of a consistent hominin 176fossil distribution pattern. A case in point is the age and taxonomy of the Australopithecus 177skeleton StW573 from Sterkfontein (South Africa), which has been controversial since its 178discovery. StW573 has been diagnosed as a possible second (in addition to A. africanus) 179species of Australopithecus in southern Africa, named A. prometheus (Clarke, 1998, 2008, 1802013, 2019; but see Grine, 2013). Recently StW573 was dated to 3.67 ± 0.16 Ma by Granger et 181al. (2015), which would make it the earliest hominin in southern Africa and coeval with A. 182afarensis in eastern Africa. However, this date has been challenged by Kramers and Dirks 183(2017): they argue that the fossil may have a maximum age of only 2.8 Ma, or ~2.6 Ma as 184suggested by Herries et al. (2013). In figure 3 we use question marks to keep the taxonomy and 185age of this particular fossil unassigned until more data become available (Clarke, 2019), and for 186now exclude it from our pattern analysis. It is clear that even though new hominin fossil finds 187continue to be made, the fossil record will remain an incomplete and imperfect reflection of 188original species distribution. Nonetheless, the overview presented in figure 3 compiles results 189of more than 50 years of fossil hunting in Africa and for now gives the best possible picture of 190the hominin distribution pattern. 191

1922.2. Analyzing patterns in the hominin fossil record 193While being aware of difficulties and limitations, we are confident that by considering all 194identifiable early hominin fossils found so far in deposits aged ~4.5 to 2.5 Ma (> 1400), we can 195discern a first approximate pattern that allows us to start the process of analysis. Ideally, fossil 196records of non-hominin taxa should be established and analyzed in the same way, since these 197taxa are usually represented by larger numbers of fossils. However, a major difficulty is that 198such fossils are often not sufficiently well constrained in time. While every hominin fossil is 199carefully dated, most non-hominin faunal fossils are listed in databases with only a large time-

200bin or formation member as age indication (e.g. Rannikko et al., 2017), which makes it difficult 201to obtain a dataset with sufficient time resolution for testing (see also Vrba, 2007). We 202therefore focus on the hominin record and compare occurrence patterns with other taxa (here 203below) as far as is possible.

204Species distribution. Before ~3 Ma, the same (or similar) hominin species occurred in several 205places in eastern Africa (Fig. 3). For instance, at 4.4 Ma Ardipithecus ramidus was present in 206the Afar Basin and likely also in the Baringo Basin (Kissel and Hawks, 2015). Australopithecus 207anamensis was present in both Afar and Turkana Basins. Later, A. afarensis occurred in the 208Afar Basin, at Kantis east of the EARS, at Laetoli and possibly also in the northern Turkana 209Basin (Wood and Leakey, 2011; Mbua et al., 2016). Notably around ~3.5 Ma, A. afarensis and 210other (closely-related or similar) taxa appear to have been remarkably widespread in eastern 211and western Africa, and perhaps even southern Africa (Granger et al., 2015). In contrast, just 212after ~3 Ma at ~2.7 Ma, Paranthropus aethiopicus was present in the Turkana Basin and 213Laetoli but absent from the Afar Basin and South Africa. The earliest fossils of the genus Homo 214were found solely in the Afar Basin at ~2.8 Ma, and only later (from ~2.3-2.4 Ma onwards) 215also in the Turkana Basin, and possibly Baringo Basin and Malawi. The species A. africanus 216has been found only in South Africa after ~ 3 Ma.

217This distribution pattern suggests that the period before ~3 Ma is characterized by hominin 218species similarity across different fossil localities in Africa, while the period just after ~3 Ma is 219characterized by regional endemism in hominin species: different species occur in the three 220separate regions of the Afar Basin, Turkana-Laetoli-Malawi, and South Africa. This is similar 221to patterns found in fossil hippopotamids (Boisserie et al., 2011; Boisserie, 2017), wherein 222there is wide distribution of hippopotamid species during the early Pliocene, and from ~2.9 Ma 223onwards, the occurrence of pronounced endemism even at an intrabasinal scale (Boisserie et al.,

2242003). Boisserie (2017) pointed out that the paleobiogeographic signal in hippopotamids 225conflicts with observations performed on other mammalian taxa. The patterns in hippopotamids 226and hominins are opposite to those identified for fossil bovids: Rowan and Reed (2013) found 227that bovid communities in eastern Africa were characterized by high degrees of isolation and 228possible endemism since the late Miocene, and that large geographic ranges evolved only 229recently from ~2.8 Ma onwards (see also Bibi et al., 2017). This was confirmed in a later study 230analyzing presence/absence data of ten families of large-bodied fossil mammals in eastern 231Africa (Afar, Turkana, Laetoli). Rowan et al. (2015) found endemism in the Pliocene (between

2324.2-2.95 Ma), and increasing species similarity across eastern Africa in the Pleistocene. 233Boisserie (2017) concluded that these contrasting distribution patterns indicate that different 234African mammal taxa had different biogeographical histories, linked to differences in 235ecological preferences. The distribution of hippopotamids was above all constrained by their 236dependence on perennial water bodies (Boisserie, 2017) and this may have been also the case, 237to some extent, for early hominins. The distribution of most bovids was more likely constrained 238by their dependence on open grasslands for grazing. Hence, we infer that the Pliocene humid 239and warm climate before onset of Northern Hemisphere Glaciation (NHG) at ~3 Ma (Raymo, 2401994; Liddy et al., 2016) produced wooded environments, perennial water bodies, and river and 241gallery forest connections in Africa, favoring wide distribution of forest/woodland-adapted and 242water-dependent taxa but causing isolation of more dry-adapted species. After ~3 Ma, the drier 243cooler climate produced large areas of more open grasslands, favoring opposite distribution 244patterns: isolation of woodland-adapted permanent water-dependent taxa and wide distribution 245of dry-adapted taxa such as grazing bovids (Lorenzen et al., 2012). 246Fossil abundance. In the Afar Basin, deposits older than ~3 Ma yield relatively large numbers 247(tens to hundreds) of hominin fossils. The Turkana Basin and Laetoli sediments of this time

248period yield fewer hominin fossils than those of the Afar, and the other EARS basins and the 249Chad Basin yield few or even no hominin fossils at all (Fig. 3). After ~3 Ma, the number of 250hominin fossils from the Afar Basin drops sharply, while hominin fossil abundance in the 251Turkana Basin remains at the same level. To assess the significance of these numbers and of 252observed fossil absences at certain times and places, we need to take into account the influence 253of sedimentology and taphonomy such as absence or rarity of fossiliferous deposits, and poor 254fossil preservation. Here we focus on the Afar Basin, Turkana Basin and Laetoli, which have a 255comparably intensive fossil prospecting history. In the Turkana Basin, absence of hominin 256fossils between 4.5 and 4.2 Ma is not due to absence of fossiliferous deposits, but rather may be 257due to the fact that available deposits at Lothagam sample open, not wooded, habitats (see 258section 3.3). The absence of hominin fossils around 3.8 Ma in the Turkana Basin can be 259explained by an absence of lacustrine deposition (which is favorable for fossilization and 260preservation) at that time, with the river system leaving only scattered Moiti floodplain 261sediments that contain few and poorly preserved fossils (Feibel, 2011). At Laetoli, absence of 262hominin fossils before 3.8 Ma is due to a combination of absence of deposits before 4.3 Ma, 263and the fact that Lower Laetolil beds aged 4.3-3.8 Ma contain only rare, scattered and 264fragmentary fossil vertebrate fossils (Kingston and Harrison, 2007).

265Both the Afar Basin and Laetoli have well-preserved fossiliferous deposits aged 3.8-3.5 Ma, so 266numbers of hominin fossils deriving from these sediments can be reliably compared. Su and 267Harrison (2008) devoted a full paper to discussing rarity of Australopithecus afarensis at 268Laetoli compared to its abundance at Hadar in the Afar Basin. They showed that although 269taphonomic factors may explain part of the discrepancy, the rarity of hominin fossils at Laetoli 270compared to Hadar is real. Su and Harrison (2008) concluded that Laetoli probably represented 271a less optimal habitat for A. afarensis than the more wooded and mesic Afar Basin. Villaseñor 272and Bobe (2015) compared hominin fossil abundance in Hadar (Afar Basin) and in East and

273West Turkana around 3.4 Ma, statistically controlling for taphonomic differences among the 274sites. Analogously to Su and Harrison (2008), they found that hominins were most abundant at 275Hadar in the Afar Basin (38%), less common at West Turkana (27%), and East Turkana had the 276smallest proportion of all samples (11%). We infer that relative difference in hominin fossil 277abundances between Afar Basin, Turkana Basin and Laetoli before ~3 Ma is a robust 278characteristic of the hominin fossil distribution pattern, reflecting the exceptional status of the 279Afar Basin as more optimal early hominin habitat.

280The relatively low number of hominin fossils from the Afar Basin after ~3 Ma (Fig. 3) is most 281likely due to differences in depositional character between the Hadar Formation (3.8-2.9 Ma) 282and the overlying Busidima Formation (2.7-0.16 Ma). The Hadar Formation is fine-grained, 283laterally extensive, relatively continuously exposed, and yields abundant vertebrate fossils 284including Australopithecus afarensis. In contrast, the Busidima Formation is coarser-grained, 285poorly preserved, discontinuous, and the density of faunal remains is low (Campisano, 2012). 286Moreover, because of an erosional unconformity at Hadar, sediments aged 2.9-2.7 Ma are 287generally lacking (diMaggio et al., 2015). Exceptions are sedimentary packages aged 2.84-2.58 288Ma in Ledi-Geraru that yielded the so far earliest Homo fossils (Dimaggio et al., 2015; Reed et 289al., 2015 and pers.comm.; Villmoare et al, 2015), and ~2.5 My old sediments in Bouri Hata 290where A. garhi fossils were found (Asfaw et al., 1999; De Heinzelin et al., 1999). We conclude 291that for the period after ~3 Ma, differences in numbers of fossils between the Afar Basin and 292other regions may not reflect real differences in hominin fossil abundance.

293With caveats taken into account we can distill the following pattern from the early hominin 294fossil record (Fig. 3): before ~3 Ma, there was hominin species similarity across different 295regions in Africa reflecting dispersal and wide distribution, with highest hominin abundance in 296the Afar Basin, somewhat lower abundance in the Turkana Basin and lowest abundance at

297Laetoli. After ~3 Ma, A. afarensis went extinct followed by the appearance of new regionally 298endemic hominin taxa reflecting regional isolation and evolution, and diminished dispersal 299options. The discontinuity after ~3 Ma likely resulted from major climatic and environmental 300change starting between ~3.15 and 2.95 Ma, associated with onset of NHG and global cooling 301and drying (deMenocal, 2004). Importantly, the pattern of regional hominin species similarity 302before ~3 Ma followed by endemism after ~3 Ma, testifies to adaptation of early hominins to 303forest/woodland conditions and permanent water availability. This confirms the interpretation 304of Rowan and Reed (2015) who posit that there is now accumulating evidence that early 305hominins preferred a to live in a mesic wooded habitat, rather than a dry open habitat (see also 306Forrest et al., 2018). 307Next, our objective is to understand and explain the hominin distribution pattern by developing 308a testable biogeographic framework for early hominin dispersal and evolution. We are aware 309that geographic ranges of hominins are influenced by a multitude of factors: abiotic and biotic 310factors (e.g. physical barriers, water and other resource availability, presence or absence of 311predators), population dynamics of a species, and genetic characteristics (e.g. Gaston, 2003). 312Moreover, these interconnected factors and their relative impact change over time (Roebroeks, 3132006). While acknowledging complexity, we here take a first simple step and focus on climatic 314and environmental factors influencing hominin dispersal and evolution. 315

3163. Biogeographic framework
3173.1. A biogeographic perspective 318Our premise is that in order to evaluate climatic and environmental drivers of early hominin 319evolution, much can be learned from African faunal and floral biogeography. Hominins are 320African mammals that must have been embedded in the same ecological matrices that other 321African animals are, and have been (Kingdon, 1971-1982, 1990, 2003). Jonathan Kingdon,

322zoologist and artist specialized in African natural history, used biogeographic patterns of 323present-day eastern African mammalian sibling species to derive an evolutionary mechanism 324of climate-induced isolation of ancient ape populations in Indian Ocean coastal forests, with 325evolution of bipedality followed by inland dispersal and subsequent evolution (formation of 326new species) in different inland river basins (Kingdon, 2003). In his book, he provided a 327detailed conceptual model for human evolution with many novel ideas but also some 328inaccuracies, which resulted in ambiguous reception by the paleoanthropological community. 329While Kingdon’s thoughtful biogeographic perspective, intimate knowledge of African 330mammals and landscapes, and out-of-the-box thinking was appreciated, not all of his ideas 331were fully embraced (Kimbel, 2004; Wood, 2004; Kingston, 2007). We feel that it is timely 332now to reconsider his contribution, using it as a source of inspiration for a fresh look at 333hominin evolution. Firstly, Kingdon draws humans firmly back to their place in nature, as “a 334very peculiar and special one, true, but in essence just one more African mammal”. Secondly, 335he directs our attention to the ecological and evolutionary significance of an often overlooked 336but important vegetation zone in eastern Africa, the coastal forest zone along the Indian Ocean 337(Fig. 2; White, 1983; Mayaux et al., 2004; Groves, 2015). Kingdon proposed that for 338mammalian -including hominin- evolution, this coastal forest zone has played a major role as 339an isolated refugium during adverse climatic-environmental conditions, and as a source area 340for new species (Kingdon, 2003). In the next subsections, we review what is now known about 341the eastern African coastal forest, and assess if Kingdon’s postulation of the coastal forest as 342refugium and source area is valid and applicable to hominin evolution studies. 343

3443.2. The coastal forest as refugium 345The concept of refugia stems from recognizing (e.g. by Darwin, 1859), that geographical 346ranges of species have expanded and contracted according to cyclic climate change during the

347Quaternary (Birks, 2015). The value of this concept has been challenged, because it can be 348argued that species respond to climatic and environmental changes in different and complex 349ways (Bennett and Provan, 2008). However, Stewart et al. (2010) pointed out that the refugium 350concept, if well-defined and delimited, remains relevant for species-level evolution. We follow 351definitions by Stewart et al. (2010): a refugium is the geographic location that a species 352inhabits during the part of the climate cycle that represents the species’ maximum contraction 353in geographic range. This implies that a refugium is at a certain moment the relatively best 354place to be for certain taxa. It does not mean that it is absolutely the perfect place to be. We use 355‘core area’ (or source area) for locations where a species’ habitat preferences are largely met 356and population size will be relatively large, as is the case in a refugium. We use ‘marginal 357area’ for locations where a species preferred habitat is only temporarily present, degrades over 358time, or no longer exists, as is the case in expansion areas when the climatic conditions swing 359from stable to strongly variable again. In marginal areas, population size will be relatively 360small and/or decreasing (Stewart et al., 2010). During favorable climate conditions, core and 361marginal areas are connected while during adverse climate conditions they are isolated from 362each other (Vrba, 2007; Stewart et al., 2010). 363Traditionally, refugia relate to temperate zone glacial-interglacial warm-cold climate cycles 364(e.g. Hublin and Roebroeks, 2009) but in this study, we focus on tropical climate (wet-dry) 365cycles that are orbitally forced by changes in insolation. In general, “latitude rules” in 366biogeography whereas the often longitudinal dimension of the coastal-continental axis is 367mostly –and unjustly- ignored (but see Cohen et al., 2012). Stewart et al. (2010) is one of the 368first studies (after e.g. Fortelius et al., 2003 and Kingdon, 2003) to recognize the importance of 369this axis. They distinguish a ‘continental’ adaptation to a drier climate with greater difference 370(in temperature, precipitation) between the seasons in inland areas, and an ‘oceanic’ adaptation 371to a more humid and stable, less seasonally different climate along the coast. In this paper we

372focus mainly on the oceanic-continental axis (in this case West-East), while also taking the 373longitudinal axis (North-South) into account. 374 375The present-day eastern African coastal forest zone is one of six well-defined forest floristic 376clusters associated with particular environmental conditions: Moist West Africa, Dry West 377Africa, Wet Central Africa, Moist Central Africa, Upland East Africa, and Coastal East Africa 378(Fayolle et al., 2014). These different forest types, now isolated from each other, resulted from 379complex climatically and tectonically driven fragmentation processes operating on the original 380single continuous equatorial forest that covered Africa during the Early and Middle Miocene 381(Burgess and Clarke, 2000; Plana, 2004; Bryja et al., 2017). The Late Miocene timing of this 382major environmental restructuring in Africa is supported by climate data from Herbert et al. 383(2016) who identified, based on sea surface temperature records, an increasing aridification of 384Africa starting between ~7 and 5 Ma. The central and eastern African forests are partitioned by 385a dry region that -analogous to Wallace’s Line (Mayr, 1944)- is called “Kingdon’s Line”: this 386division crossing eastern Africa represents the optimal separation of overlapping central and 387eastern forest faunal elements (Grubb et al., 1999; Kingdon, 2003; Fig. 4). The eastern African 388coastal forest zone comprises the narrow (width ~50-100 km) longitudinally aligned vegetation 389zone presently stretching along the Indian Ocean from southern Somalia to southern South 390Africa (White, 1983; Grubb et al., 1999; Burgess and Clarke, 2000; Fig. 4). More inland at 391higher elevation are eastern African montane forests such as the Eastern Arc Forests in Kenya 392and Tanzania, that were in the initial phase of Miocene forest fragmentation still linked to the 393coastal forest (Bryja et al., 2017). 394 395Clarke (2000) formally defined the Indian Ocean coastal forest zone as “Coastal Forests of 396Eastern Africa” (henceforth referred to as CFEA or coastal forest; see also Barratt, 2017;

397Barratt et al., 2018) and described the composition as a mosaic of halophytic mangrove forest, 398lowland mixed rain forest, Brachystegia woodland, swamp and scrub forest, and riverine 399gallery forest. The coastal forest zone is further characterized by the presence of estuarine 400wetland areas, shallow-water tidal creeks with sea-grass fields, and seasonal freshwater ponds 401with water lilies and other aquatic vegetation (Fig. 5). Jurassic, Cretaceous and Eocene 402limestone formations occur widely along the Indian Ocean coast, forming karstic hills and 403caves within the coastal forest (Schlüter, 2006; Helm et al., 2012; Carvalho et al., 2017; 404Habermann et al., 2018). In terms of present-day terrestrial ecoregions in Africa, the northern 405part from southern Somalia to northern Mozambique is classified as Zanzibar-Inhambane 406coastal forest mosaic. This part grades southward into Maputoland and KwaZulu-Cape coastal 407forests that continue until Cape St. Francis in South Africa (Burgess et al., 2004). Coastal 408gallery forests extending along rivers can reach far inland, up to 200-400 km (Burgess and 409Clarke, 2000; Kingston et al., 2007). The coastal region has, since the Late Miocene, benefited 410from climatic stability (Kingdon, 1990, 2003; Burgess et al., 1998; Burgess and Clarke, 2000; 411Azeria et al., 2007). Even during cyclic occurrences of severely fluctuating and dry global 412climate conditions, the buffering influence of the Indian Ocean maintained a relatively stable 413warm and humid climate in this zone, providing a vegetated and water-rich refugium for 414permanent-water adapted forest-woodland organisms (Kingdon, 1990, 2003; Lovett and 415Wasser, 1993; Burgess and Clarke, 2000). 416Recent molecular phylogeography studies on a range of extant forest-dwelling organisms 417(trees, birds, mammals, and insects) confirm a Late Miocene (~7-5 Ma) separation of West, 418Central, Upland and Coastal East African forests (e.g. Couvreur et al., 2008; Johansson et al., 4192007; Johnston and Anthony, 2012; Mikula et al., 2016; Bryja et al., 2017). Notably the 420phylogenetic positions of typical CFEA species like Ader’s duiker (Johnston and Anthony, 4212012) and Sombre greenbul (Johansson et al., 2007), as Late Miocene sister species of both

422western and other eastern branches of their respective lineages, demonstrate antiquity and 423relative stability of the coastal forest zone as a separate forest habitat. 424

4253.3. Hominin evolution along the Indian Ocean coast? 426The main question now is: was the CFEA indeed relevant for hominin evolution, as Kingdon 427proposed? Fossil finds indicate that between ~11-8 Ma, Africa was populated by hominoids 428(apes) such as Samburupithecus, Nakalipithecus and Chororapithecus (Harrison, 2010; Turner 429and O’Regan, 2015, Kunimatsu et al., 2016; Katoh et al., 2016). Kingdon (2003) suggested 430that before ~8 Ma, apes were spread over practically the whole of Africa from the Atlantic to 431the Indian Ocean. The climatic drying in Africa that led to forest fragmentation after ~7-5 Ma 432must have fragmented these ape populations as well, restricting some to west and central 433Africa and others to eastern Africa, east of Kingdon’s Line. Coppens (1994) remarked that 434geographic distributions of African great apes, together with molecular phylogeny and 435tectonic-climatic data, indicate that ancestors of present-day clades of Gorilla, Pan and Homo 436split in the Miocene: gorilla and chimpanzee lineages remained tied to western and central 437Africa, while the hominin lineage was present in eastern Africa. Based on this, Coppens (1994) 438-and many others before and since (see reviews by Potts, 1998; Bender et al, 2012; 439Dominguez-Rodrigo, 2014)- concluded that the birth of the bipedal hominin lineage must have 440resulted from evolution in relatively dry open savannas of the EARS, where today hominin 441fossils are mostly found. 442 443Recent molecular phylogeny studies have indeed corroborated that Pan and Homo last shared a 444common ancestor around ~7-5 Ma (e.g. Moeller et al, 2017 and references therein), and that 445the lineages then split. However as explained in section 2.2, in contrast to Coppens (1994) and 446decades of associating the origin of bipedal hominins with the expansion of grasslands during

447the late Miocene, current understanding is that early hominins inhabited forests and woodlands 448rather than open grassland savanna environments (e.g. Rowan and Reed, 2015). This is 449exemplified by the site of Lothagam (SW of present-day Lake Turkana) with deposits aged 450between 7.4 and 4.2 Ma, It is considered to be one of the oldest known examples of an open 451savanna biome in Africa (Leakey and Harris 2003). The site samples a rich and diverse Late 452Miocene fauna with over 9,000 fossil specimens collected. However, hominin/hominoid fossils 453are extremely rare: two hominoid teeth aged ~6.5-5 Ma, and one hominoid/hominin mandible 454(KNM-LT 329, constrained between ~4.9-4.2 Ma) have been found so far (Leakey and Harris, 4552003; McDougall and Feibel, 1999; Wood and Leakey, 2011; Kissel and Hawks, 2015). The 456rarity of hominoids and hominins in this savanna biome supports the current interpretation that 457early hominins preferred forest and woodland environments. We therefore infer that after Late 458Miocene forest fragmentation, a population of “eastern apes” ended up isolated in eastern 459African montane and coastal forests east of Kingdon’s Line, instead of in savanna areas 460between western-central and eastern forest blocks. 461 462Kingdon (2003) reasoned that, following the isolation of ape populations in their coastal forest 463enclave, they adapted to the specifics of their finite and confining environment. The CFEA 464habitat differs from west-central African forests in terms of size, shape, rainfall, seasonality, 465biodiversity, availability of food resources and intense resource competition from, for example, 466monkeys. Due to the relatively small ape gene pool in an isolated habitat experiencing different 467selection pressures, genetic drift and selection would have accelerated evolution in the coastal 468forest zone, leading to genetic separation from ape populations in the more extensive 469rainforests and woodlands west of the arid barrier separating west-central and eastern forests 470(Kingdon, 2003). Kingdon (2003) further suggested that during this isolation, adaptation to 471foraging for small plant and animal food items on the coastal forest floor in squatting position

472(the apes being largely forced out of the trees by monkey competition) led to pelvic 473reorganization and ultimately to bipedality. This is one of the many more or less speculative 474hypotheses on the origin of hominin bipedality, which so far remains elusive. We therefore 475merely suggest that, given the documented locomotor changes in Ardipithecus ramidus at 4.4 476Ma and A. kaddaba at 5.5 Ma, the evolution of changes in locomotion may have been favored 477in this coastal forest habitat. Kingdon (2003) predicted the existence of a Late Miocene to 478Early Pliocene bipedal, omnivorous ape living in the CFEA: the “coastal ground ape”. Based 479on present-day typical distributions of CFEA species, such as certain birds, monkeys, elephant- 480shrews, squirrels, duikers (Kingdon, 1971-1982), we expect that this coastal ground ape would 481have colonized the entire CFEA along the Indian Ocean from north to south. So far, there are 482no reports of Pliocene hominin fossil finds from the coastal forest zone. This can be explained 483by the fact that, generally, conditions in forests are less suitable for fossilization and 484subsequent fossil discovery than conditions in arid regions (Hare, 1980; Vrba, 2007); also, 485there has been only limited search effort in this eastern African coastal area (Szymanski, 2018). 486Recent exploratory research at Gorongosa Park in the coastal zone of Mozambique yielded, for 487the first time, Miocene animal and plant fossils from riverine forest/woodland and estuarine 488environments, beginning to fill in an important gap in the paleobiogeography of Africa 489(Carvalho et al., 2017; Habermann et al, 2018). Therefore, absence of evidence cannot be seen 490as evidence of absence in this case. In summary, we conclude that there is sufficient ground for 491assuming that the CFEA could have been a relevant area for hominin evolution. 492

4933.4. A coastal refuge hypothesis 494We hypothesize that the CFEA provided an isolated refugium for early hominins, conducive to 495evolution during orbitally forced climatically variable episodes (at 400-100 ky timescales) 496when extremely dry conditions alternated (at 20 ky timescales) with extremely wet conditions.

497From this coastal refuge, newly evolved hominin species could disperse inland to montane 498forests and rift basins via humid (riverine/vegetated/groundwater-fed) corridors, whenever 499onset of climatically stable episodes allowed expansion from the coastal enclave (Fig. 6). Three 500premises underlie this ‘coastal refuge hypothesis’:

5011) since its origin in the Late Miocene, the CFEA has always been climatologically buffered by 502the warm ocean and was an evolutionary source (core) area of many woodland/forest-adapted 503organisms needing permanent water (see examples below) – likely including early hominins 504(Kingdon, 2003; Leakey et al., 2008; Carvalho et al., 2017; Habermann et al., 2018);
5052) the ~100 and ~400 ky Earth’ eccentricity maxima episodes are characterized by climate 506variability with alternating extremely wet and dry conditions at precession (~20 ky) 507timescales, strong seasonality, and high dust production. In contrast, eccentricity minima 508episodes are climatically stable, characterized by relatively constant moderate rainfall 509intensity, moderate seasonality and low dust production (Vallé et al., 2016), promoting and 510sustaining vegetated/riverine/spring-fed connecting corridors between CFEA, eastern 511montane forests and other inland areas (e.g. EARS basins); and
5123) the existence of recurrent humid connecting corridors during climatically stable episodes 513facilitated hominin dispersal from refugial core area(s) such as the CFEA to marginal areas 514(e.g. inland rift basins), while climatically variable episodes lead to contraction, isolation, 515endemism and evolution in core area(s). 516 517Support for validity of the premises underlying the coastal refuge hypothesis is provided by 518the following examples. Hemp et al. (2015) studied biogeographical patterns of the forest- 519dwelling flightless grasshopper Parepistaurus and specifically addressed the questions: a) are 520the old coastal forests of Kenya and Tanzania the source for inland taxa in Parepistaurus? and 521b) did river systems serve as corridors for the spread of Parepistaurus into the hinterland?

522Indeed, based on molecular phylogeography they found that coastal Parepistaurus taxa 523belonged to ancestral lineages from which evolved the high diversity of species found in 524montane forests of the Eastern Arc Mountains of Kenya and Tanzania. They concluded that 525riparian vegetation along rivers draining into the Indian Ocean probably served as corridors 526for dispersal of coastal taxa to the hinterland. These findings are corroborated by a molecular 527phylogeography study demonstrating that in arboreal rats of the genus Grammomys, upland 528taxa (e.g. in Usambara Mountains, Tanzania and Mulanje Mountains, Malawi) are closely 529related to eastern African coastal taxa, which points to an inland spread of coastal taxa via 530riverine gallery forests (Bryja et al., 2017). Recently, Cuthbert et al. (2017) modeled networks 531of groundwater “hydro-refugia” in eastern Africa through a wet-dry precession cycle, further 532substantiating the occurrence of climate-modulated coastal-inland humid dispersal corridors 533(perpendicular on the N-S axis of the EARS) for permanent-water adapted fauna. 534 535The next step in our study is to develop the coastal refuge hypothesis into a biogeographic 536conceptual model for hominin evolution and dispersal in Africa between ~4.5-2.5 Ma. First, 537we set up tectonic-climatic (section 4) and geographic frameworks (section 5) by reviewing 538available literature on these subjects. Next, we establish “initial conditions” of the model by 539choosing, based on biogeographic and fossil information, the stem hominin in this conceptual 540model (section 6). We define the “algorithm” (a sequence of instructions) that makes the 541model “run”, producing predictions on hominin evolution and dispersal (section 7). In 542sections 8 and 9, based on combination of available climate and hominin fossil records, we 543test predictions following from the coastal refuge model and explore impacts of climatic and 544environmental change. Finally, we discuss examples of how to further test the conceptual 545model and start using it as a predictive tool, hopefully inspiring novel research directions 546(section 10).

5484. The tectonic-climatic framework
5494.1. Rifts and east-west connectivity 550Tectonic processes such as volcanism, uplift and rifting played a key role in shaping 551topography, local climate and hominin environments in Africa (e.g. Veldkamp et al., 2004; 552Sepulchre et al., 2006, Wichura et al., 2010, Veldkamp et al., 2012; Claessens et al., 2016; 553Ring et al., 2018). Two large rift systems can be distinguished: 1) the branches of the EARS 554both situated along a largely north-south axis, and 2) the interconnected Anza, Sudan and 555Central and West African Rifts running along an east-west axis between the Indian Ocean 556and western Africa. The two rift systems cross each other at the location of present-day Lake 557Turkana (Turkana Basin) in Kenya (Fig. 7; Winn et al., 1993; Bosworth and Morley, 1994; 558Bruhn et al., 2011; Macgregor, 2015). Development of the E-W oriented rift systems started 559in the Cretaceous (Winn et al., 1993) and while these rifts were progressively filled up over 560time, they still constitute relatively low-lying areas. 561Aquatic faunal evidence (reflected e.g. in ichtyological provinces) indicates that during the 562Mio-Pliocene, western and eastern Africa were hydrographically connected at least up to ~7 563Ma (Otero et al., 2009). After ~7 Ma this connection was lost due to aridification in Africa 564(Zhang et al, 2014; Herbert et al., 2016) but intermittently re-established during recurring 565orbitally forced humid climate cycles (Larrasoaña et al., 2013). Indeed, malacological 566evidence suggests that Chad and Turkana Basins were from time to time connected via the 567Central African Shear zone and Sudan Rift (Van Bocxlaer et al., 2008; Van Damme and Van 568Bocxlaer, 2009; Van Bocxlaer, 2017; Fig. 7). Bruhn et al. (2011) also drew attention to 569evidence reflecting ancient west-east connections and faunal dispersal along these 570interconnected rift systems, such as an isolated eastern African population of Cercocebus 571that is distributed principally in forests of west and central Africa-- the extant Tana River

572mangabey (Cercocebus galeritus) that inhabits gallery forests near the Indian Ocean coast. 573Leakey et al. (2008) concluded, based on sudden appearances and disappearances of monkey 574species in the fossil record of the Turkana Basin, that many species of fossil cercopithecoid 575monkeys that first appeared in the Turkana Basin without obvious local predecessors are 576likely to have migrated into the basin while having evolved elsewhere. The authors inferred 577that waterways linking the Turkana Basin with habitats elsewhere would have been critical 578in facilitating dispersals into and out of the basin, and recognized that the central African 579forests and particularly the Coastal Forest of Eastern Africa, as proposed by Kingdon 580(2003), would have provided ideal habitats for evolution of primates. For instance, the 581ancient Turkana River, which flowed from the southern Turkana Basin via Lamu 582Embayment to the Indian Ocean (Feibel, 1993; Bruhn et al., 2011), may have been a crucial 583link to coastal forest habitats and thus to a possible source of some of the primate species 584that appeared in the Turkana Basin (Leakey et al., 2008; Joordens et al., 2013). 585

5864.2. Global climate change 587Global climate evolution over the past 5 million years is characterized by several major climate 588transitions, together with ongoing regular orbitally driven climate cyclicity. Overall, the 589Pliocene (5.33-2.58 Ma; Gradstein et al., 2012) was a warm period with higher sea levels than 590today (Rovere et al., 2014), punctuated by several short-lived glaciations at ~4.9, 4.0, 3.6 and
5913.3 Ma (De Schepper et al., 2014) with concomitant sea level low stands (Toomey et al., 2016) 592and minima in sea surface temperatures (Herbert et al., 2010) at those times (Fig. 8). A major 593climatic transition occurred at the end of the Pliocene warm period, caused by geological 594events such as changing ocean circulation, changing atmospheric carbon dioxide levels, and 595intensification of NHG between ~ 3 and 2.6 Ma (Liddy et al., 2016; Ravelo et al., 2004; Bartoli 596et al., 2005). The transition from global warmth up to ~3 Ma to markedly cooler and more

597variable climate conditions is demonstrated by sea surface temperature records (Martinez- 598Garcia et al., 2010; Liddy et al., 2016; Herbert et al., 2010, 2016) and benthic foraminifera 599d18O records reflecting ice volume and bottom sea water temperatures (Lisiecki and Raymo, 6002005; Zachos et al., 2011; Bell et al., 2015; Fig. 8a,b). 601Superimposed on such “unique” climatic transitions are regular Milankovitch climate cycles 602due to orbital forcing, causing cyclicity in insolation and thus in monsoonal rainfall and 603seasonality in Africa, at different timescales ranging from ~10 ky to 1.2 Ma (Kingston et al. 6042007 and references therein). Of particular importance are Earth eccentricity cycles (with a 605frequency of ~100 and ~400 ky; Fig. 8c) as they modulate the precession cycle, determining 606the amount of insolation and thereby the amplitude of monsoonal intensity in Africa 607(Rossignol-Strick, 1983, 1985; Kutzbach and Liu, 1997). During eccentricity maxima the 608amplitude of insolation values is greatest (Fig. 8d), resulting in extremes in high rainfall and 609low rainfall periods at precession (~20 ky) timescales in Africa, and also extremes in 610seasonality (largest difference between wet and dry seasons) at these points in time (Berger et 611al., 2006; Wang, 2009). 612 613In some studies, eccentricity maxima (characterized by peaks in monsoonal rainfall) are 614correlated to major lake phases in Africa and considered to be “wet periods” (e.g. Trauth et al., 6152005, 2007; Maslin and Christensen, 2007, Maslin and Trauth, 2009). Here, however, we 616emphasize that eccentricity maxima result not so much in overall wet periods but rather in 617periods of high climate variability with high-amplitude extremes in wet and dry conditions 618(example indicated with v in Fig. 8d). In contrast, 400 ky and 100 ky eccentricity minima are 619periods of climate stability, characterized by relatively constant (low-amplitude variation) 620moderate rainfall intensity and moderate seasonality over a time span of ~20 to 150 ky 621(example indicated with s in Fig. 8d). We consider such stable moderate rainfall conditions to

622be beneficial for promoting and sustaining vegetation cover, and decreasing erosion and 623continental dust production. Indeed, eccentricity minima correlate with minima in continental 624dust percentages in marine cores drilled off the West African coast (ODP 659, Fig. 8e; 625Tiedemann et al., 1994) and in the Gulf of Aden (ODP 721/722, Fig. 8f; deMenocal, 1995, 626Wang, 2009; Vallé et al., 2016). Dust reduction strengthens vegetation-albedo feedbacks of 627precipitation, further stabilizing vegetation cover (Pausata et al., 2016). Stable moderate 628amounts of rainfall may be sufficient and even optimal to nourish vegetation and forest cover, 629while extreme amounts of rainfall -- as seasonally occurring during eccentricity maxima-- are 630not necessarily advantageous for vegetation. Moreover, it is notably the absence of extreme 631and prolonged dry spells (and long dry seasons) during eccentricity minima that may 632contribute to ensuring a critical amount of vegetation to maintain a humid self-propagating 633microclimate, thus enhancing forest ecosystem resilience to cope with climatic and other 634perturbations (Hirota et al., 2011). 635 636In figure 8, green dashed lines represent stable periods of moderate rainfall and seasonality, 637coinciding with low dust percentages in marine cores drilled off the African coast. They 638generally occur every ~400 ky (or 100 ky) when eccentricity is lower than ~0.01. These stable 639periods indeed coincide with “forest phases” as characterized by peaks in tree pollen and lows 640in “desert pollen” in marine core DSPD Site 231 (Gulf of Aden) at ~4.4 Ma, 3.8 Ma, 3.5 Ma,

6413.15 Ma, 2.8 and 2.45 Ma (Bonnefille, 2010; NB no data are available for the time period 3.9-
6424.4 Ma). The stable periods also coincide with onset of lake phases in the Turkana Basin at 643~4.2 Ma, ~3.5 Ma, ~3.15 Ma and ~2.4 Ma (Feibel, 2011, Joordens et al., 2013; Fig. 8). 644However, lakes are not always unequivocal indicators of climatic conditions, since the 645formation of lakes is contingent also on local tectonics and on basin thresholds. In case 646accommodation space (e.g. through basin subsidence) is not available, a lake phase will not

647occur even when climatic conditions for lake formation are favorable. This was the case in the 648Turkana Basin at ~3.8 Ma and 2.8 Ma (Feibel, 2011). Formation and characteristics of lakes 649also depend on sensitivity of basins, determined by e.g. basin shape and geographic location. 650For instance, the Baringo Basin has a different threshold for lake formation than the Turkana 651Basin: the Baringo Basin needs relatively high rainfall amounts, as caused by peak insolation 652values during eccentricity maxima (but not during eccentricity minima), to form relatively 653short-lived deep lakes at 20 ky timescales (Deino et al., 2006; Kingston et al., 2007; Wilson et 654al., 2014); during eccentricity minima, lakes also formed in the Baringo Basin but they were 655not as deep and well developed (John Kingston, pers. comm.). 656Notably the period around ~3.5 Ma appears to have been wet and wooded in Africa. For 657instance, Feibel (1988) found, based on paleosol types, that at ~3.5 Ma the Turkana Basin was 658characterized by enhanced local rainfall conditions, possibly related to high sea level and 659marine transgression into the low-lying Lamu Embayment providing more moisture at that 660time. This observed increased humidity signal is supported by Hernández-Fernández and Vrba 661(2006) who inferred a paleoprecipitation of 1100 mm/yr in the Turkana Basin at 3.5 Ma, based 662on taxonomic analysis of macromammalian faunas. In conclusion, we deduce that during 663eccentricity minima, low dust values coincided with (and are causally connected with) 664increased vegetation cover, forest expansion, onset of lake phases in the Turkana Basin and 665occurrence of vegetated/riverine corridors. Conversely, during eccentricity maxima high dust 666values coincided with (and resulted from) vegetation contraction, causing disruption of 667connective vegetated/riverine corridors. 668 669An interesting point of discussion is the question: why were mesic corridors suitable for inland 670dispersal only established -as we infer- during 100 and 400 ky eccentricity minima, and not 671every 20 ky during insolation maxima as for instance suggested by Larrasoaña et al. (2013) and

672Grant et al. (2017)? Larrasoaña et al. proposed the occurrence of over 230 “Green Sahara 673Periods” within the last 8 million years, with savanna and gallery forest expansion throughout 674most of the desert in northern Africa. While we cannot exclude the possibility that connectivity 675and to some extent also hominin dispersal may have occurred at 20 ky timescales, we argue 676that conditions were not always sufficiently suitable for successful establishment of viable 677long-lasting populations in marginal areas. We base this on the observation that before ~3 Ma, 678habitat requirements of hominins were more strictly limited to woodland and forest conditions, 679rather than to savanna/floodplain conditions. Establishment of woodland/forest conditions in 680marginal areas would have been adversely affected by extreme variability and seasonality 681associated with high insolation maxima. Especially the length of the dry season must have been 682a crucial threshold factor, not only for hominin dispersal opportunities but also for 683establishment of suitable habitats and viable populations in marginal areas (see e.g. Eronen et 684al., 2017). Consequently, we assume that 100 ky and 400 ky eccentricity minima, clearly 685coinciding with forest and lake phases, provided the most optimal conditions for mesic 686corridors, attractiveness of inland areas, and dispersal and successful inland settlement of 687hominin populations. 688

6895. Towards a conceptual model: the geographic framework
6905.1. The Coastal Forest of Eastern Africa: core area 691When developing the coastal refuge hypothesis into a conceptual model for African hominin 692evolution (“coastal refuge model”), it is vital to acknowledge the particular climatic and 693environmental characteristics associated with geographic position and elevation of presently 694proposed key areas for early hominin evolution: the CFEA, several basins in the EARS, and 695the Chad Basin (Fig. 9). Nowadays, the coastal forest zone stretches along the Indian Ocean 696coast from southern Somalia in the north until southern South Africa (Figs. 2, 4). In the warm

697Mio-Pliocene before ~3 Ma, sea levels were higher (Rovere et al., 2014) and more humid 698conditions prevailed in eastern Africa (Bonnefille et al., 2004; Neumann and Bamford, 2015), 699thus it can be expected that the coastal forest extended further northward than its present-day 700limits. Marine pollen records from the offshore Niger Delta and Gulf of Aden corroborate 701presence of forest and woodland conditions in NE Africa between ~5-3 Ma, including the 702typical Brachystegia woodland/forest in the Afar Basin (Bonnefille, 2010); Brachystegia 703woodland forms an important vegetation zone in the coastal forest of Kenya (Burgess and 704Clarke, 2000; Fig. 5). Hence we assume that during the early part of our study interval up to ~3 705Ma, the CFEA reached along the coast northward all the way around the Horn of Africa into 706what is now Djibouti, Eritrea and Ethiopia, and southward into South Africa. 707

7085.2. The Afar Basin: climatologically buffered until at least ~ 3 Ma 709The Afar Basin, the northernmost EARS basin situated in Ethiopia, differs from other EARS 710basins with respect to its unique tectonic setting, local volcanic activity and relative coastal 711proximity (Fig. 9). The Afar marks the subaerial triple junction between Nubian, Somali and 712Arabian plates, which are diverging from each other due to extension in the Red Sea, East 713African and Gulf of Aden Rifts (Keir et al., 2013). The rifts in the Afar area contain numerous 714smaller graben structures that host sedimentary basins, often filled with sediments (runoff from 715the adjacent uplifted Ethiopian Highlands; Kalb, 1995) and volcanics (numerous thin tuff 716layers and thick basalt packages; e.g. Alene et al., 2017; Campisano et al., 2017). At present, 717distances between Afar Basin hominin sites and Red Sea coast are only ~200 km; in contrast, 718other early hominin sites in the EARS are situated much further from the Indian Ocean coast, 719at least ~500-700 km. During the Pliocene, up to at least ~3 Ma when sea levels were higher 720than today, the coast may regularly even have been as close as 100 km to Afar Basin sites. 721Assuming that the coastal forest extended all around the Horn of Africa, this would bring sites

722in the Afar Basin (e.g. Middle Awash Valley, Gona, Hadar, Ledi-Geraru, Woranso-Mille) 723relatively easily within the typical inland extent of the CFEA. Hence, we propose that up to ~3 724Ma the Afar Basin was regularly, more often than other EARS basins, part of or connected to 725the CFEA. Importantly, we consider fossiliferous sediments deposited in the Afar Basin during 726the latter conditions as a “proxy” of, or a “window” into, the coastal forest ecosystem at that 727time. 728 729During highly variable climate episodes in eccentricity maxima, rivers draining the Ethiopian 730Highlands and running into the Afar Basin would not always supply enough water to sustain 731permanent lacustrine conditions in the basin, as evidenced by the temporary nature of its lake 732deposits (Campisano and Feibel, 2007). However, due to the relative proximity of the coast we 733expect that the Afar Basin would –just like the coastal forest along the Indian Ocean-- suffer 734less from extremely dry conditions through the buffering, humidifying influence of the warm 735Red Sea/Gulf of Aden water body. Indeed, it has been found that from ~3.3 to 3.23 Ma a lake 736was present at Hadar (Campisano and Feibel, 2007) while lakes were then absent in other lake 737basins in the EARS (Trauth et al., 2005, 2007; Feibel, 2011). Furthermore, in the Middle 738Awash Valley relatively wet and wooded conditions prevailed between at least ~5.8 and 3.2 739Ma (WoldeGabriel et al., 2001; Haile-Selassie et al., 2004; White et al., 2009a,b; Louchart et 740al., 2009; WoldeGabriel et al., 2009; Haile-Selassie et al., 2010a,b; Bonnefille, 2010). For 741instance, the habitat of Ardipithecus ramidus at ~4.4 Ma was characterized as woodland to 742grassy woodland (White et al., 2009a,b). While this interpretation was challenged by Cerling et 743al. (2010, 2014), White et al. (2010, 2014) maintained that A. ramidus fossils are associated 744with woodland to grassy woodland habitat indicators and riparian forests. This reconstruction 745is confirmed by a recent review of available independent lines of evidence (Rowan and Reed, 7462015).

747Palynological evidence from Hadar also indicates that conditions there were wetter in the past: 748Pliocene rainfall (~800-1200 mm/year) was about twice that of today (Bonnefille et al., 2004). 749Throughout the Hadar sequence between ~3.4 and 2.95 Ma, humidity coefficients oscillated 750around 60%, which is only slightly below the 65% threshold for forest biome worldwide today 751(Bonnefille et al., 2004). Reed (2008) provided a multi-proxy habitat reconstruction for the 752Hadar Formation: between 3.42 and 3.25 Ma, closed wooded environments prevailed, followed 753by wet edaphic grasslands between 3.25 and 3.2 Ma. After that, a transition occurred to 754increasingly more open and arid grasslands at 2.95 Ma (Dupont-Nivet et al., 2008). Woodland 755habitats also prevailed at Woranso-Mille, Dikika and Maka (Rowan and Reed, 2015). Based on 756the documented permanent high humidity and presence of wooded conditions even during 757highly variable climate episodes, we propose that before ~3 Ma the Afar Basin was almost 758continuously habitable for woodland-forest-adapted and permanent-water-dependent fauna. 759

7605.3. EARS rift basins and Chad Basin: marginal areas? 761Among inland EARS basins, the Turkana Basin (Fig. 9) occupies a unique position. This is due 762to its low elevation (now ~360 m above sea level) in the Turkana Depression, its permanent 763riverine connection to the vast Omo River drainage area in the Ethiopian Highlands (Feibel, 7641999, 2011), and its location at the intersection of two rift systems (Bosworth and Morley, 7651994). The Turkana Basin is connected via the Anza Rift to the near-coastal Lamu Embayment 766(Fig. 7) that flooded during periods of high sea level. Such marine incursions periodically 767brought the Indian Ocean coastline much closer to the Turkana Basin: ~300-400 km instead of 768700 km (Oosterom, 1988), increasing the amount of moisture carried inland from the Indian 769Ocean to the Turkana Basin by the monsoon (e.g. at ~3.5 Ma; Feibel, 1988). The Turkana 770Basin was effectively a “stepping-stone” on the recurrently available dispersal route between 771CFEA, EARS and central-western Africa. Turkana Basin paleohydrology over time was

772characterized by alternating lacustrine and riverine conditions (Feibel 2011; Fig. 8), ensuring 773permanent water availability throughout climate cycles. As such, the Turkana Basin can be 774considered as a potential regional refugium for hominins (Hernández-Fernández and Vrba, 7752006; Vrba, 2007; Joordens et al., 2011; Fortelius et al., 2016). 776In contrast, other early hominin find areas such as the Baringo Basin, Laetoli and Kantis are 777located in upland eastern Africa at much higher elevations of ~1000 m, ~1600 m and ~1750 m 778respectively. These areas did not have a permanent major riverine input and were therefore, 779despite the occurrence of seasonal orographic rainfall, less buffered against recurrent variable 780and arid climate conditions. The Karongo Basin in the Malawi rift is different again, situated 781further South at ~500 m elevation. It is located at the southernmost seasonal reach of the 782Intertropical Convergence Zone (ITCZ; Ivory et al., 2018) and currently falls in a different 783vegetation zone than the more northerly EARS basins (White, 1983). Lüdecke et al. (2016) 784found, based on carbon isotope ratios of soil carbonates and suid teeth, a consistently closed 785wooded (C3 vegetation) environment over the last 4.3 Ma, with regional patches of more dry- 786adapted C4 vegetation. These differences highlight the spatial heterogeneity of inland EARS 787basins, which influences how different environments responded to climate change over time. 788 789In summary, we infer that before ~3 Ma: 790-the CFEA can be considered as a possible refugium for hominins and core (source) area for 791hominin evolution; 792-the Afar Basin likely fulfilled an almost similar role as refugium and core area, due to its 793buffered climate and its regularly being part of or connected to the CFEA; 794-inland EARS basins (Turkana, Baringo, Kantis, Laetoli, Malawi) and Chad Basin can be 795considered as marginal areas for hominin evolution, while the Turkana Basin due to its

796permanent water availability was less marginal and more refugial (providing more favorable 797living conditions) than other inland EARS basins. 798

7996. Initial conditions of the conceptual model: who, when, where? 800Next, we need to establish the “initial conditions” of the conceptual coastal refuge model: 801choosing the stem hominin, starting time, and source area. So, which known species or genus 802could possibly be considered as (similar to) Kingdon’s “coastal ground ape”? Between ~7 and 8035 Ma, at least three alleged hominins were present in Africa: Sahelanthropus tchadensis at ~7 804Ma in the Chad Basin (Brunet et al., 2002), Orrorin tugenensis at ~ 5.7-6 Ma in Kenya (Senut 805et al., 2001) and Ardipithecus kadabba at ~5.8 Ma in the Afar Basin, Ethiopia (Haile-Selassie 806et al., 2004). The skull characteristics of Sahelanthropus can be interpreted as indicating 807bipedality (Zollikofer et al., 2005). Anatomical analysis indicated that O. tugenensis was a 808habitual biped (Richmond and Jungers, 2008), and also A. kaddaba was bipedal (Haile-Selassie 809et al., 2004, 2009); Haile-Selassie et al., 2016). Kingdon (2003) proposed that possibly one (or 810more) of the taxa above, and/or the younger ~4.4 My old Ardipithecus ramidus from the Afar 811Basin, could be closely related to his hypothetical coastal ground ape. The recent detailed 812description of A. ramidus (White et al., 2009; 2015) suggests that it indeed resembles 813Kingdon’s predicted ape: A. ramidus was a bipedal, omnivorous denizen of woodland with 814small patches of forest, and likely foraged both in trees and on the ground. Kingdon (2003) 815remarked that the find area of Ardipithecus in the Middle Awash Valley (Afar Basin) was too 816far from the coast for this genus to represent a “coastal ground ape”. However, the assumed 817Mio-Pliocene (up to ~3 Ma) far northern extent of the coastal forest around the Horn of Africa 818places find localities of Ardipithecus in a wooded habitat connected to (or being part of) the 819coastal forest zone, supporting Kingdon’s prediction of a bipedal coastal ground ape being 820similar to A. ramidus.

822Based on the hominin fossil record (and notably the discoveries of Sahelanthropus and 823Orrorin) we conclude that the geographic origin (or multiple origins?) of hominin bipedality 824remains an open question (see also Strait, 2013). It is possible that bipedality first arose around 825paleolake Chad, or even more northward along the ancient Sahabi river and delta system in 826Libya, instead of in the CFEA as proposed by Kingdon (2003). However, in view of the 827continuous forest cover and East-West connectivity prior to ~7 Ma (Otero et al., 2009), it does 828not matter where the origin was, since it can be expected that the earliest bipedal hominins 829could rapidly spread to colonize accessible habitats. Based on abundant fossil presence of 830Ardipithecus ramidus in the wooded Afar Basin (White et al., 2009b; 2015) and observed 831distribution of typical CFEA species (Kingdon, 1971-1982), we propose that this bipedal 832hominin inhabited the whole CFEA. Hence, as initial conditions for the conceptual model we 833suggest that A. ramidus serves as a good candidate for a “stem hominin”. The start time then is 834~4.4 Ma, and start location is the coastal forest extending along the Red Sea/Indian Ocean 835coast from Eritrea and the Horn of Africa down to southern Africa (Fig. 9). 836

8377. Defining the model “algorithm” 838During variable climate episodes lasting several hundred thousand years, isolation of 839populations in the geographically confined refugium along the coast would be particularly 840conducive for ongoing evolution (Lister, 2004; Stewart et al., 2010; Martínez Freiría et al., 8412015). During climatically stable episodes that follow, evolved populations could expand and 842disperse via riverine/vegetated corridors to inland areas (such as Chad and EARS basins). 843Lakes in some of these rift basins then provided well-watered habitats with abundant terrestrial 844and aquatic food resources (Braun et al., 2010; Joordens et al., 2011; Archer et al., 2014) and 845must have formed attractive destinations for permanent-water dependent fauna dispersing from

846the coast. What is generally the fate of populations trapped outside their refugium in marginal 847(expansion) areas, when the climate changes again from stable to variable conditions? The 848results from genetic studies (e.g. Dalén et al., 2007 and references therein) suggest that 849expanded populations outside refugia often become extinct instead of tracking their retreating 850habitats by physically moving back into the refugium (Roebroeks et al., 2011). According to 851Stewart et al. (2010) this implies that populations in long-term refugia are descended from 852those individuals that remained in these refugia during dispersal phases, and consequently that 853expanded populations outside refugia make little or no contribution to long-term evolution of 854the species. In this case, the refugium can be termed a ‘source area’ and the expansion areas 855‘sink areas’ for populations. However, it is also possible that populations in marginal areas 856manage to persist and even adapt to new environmental conditions (e.g. river floodplains in the 857Turkana Basin) when climate changes from stable to highly variable again. Rapidly fluctuating 858environments are often associated with increased phenotypic and behavioral plasticity that can 859be targeted by selection, resulting in new adaptive traits (Grove, 2012, 2014). In that case these 860populations find themselves on a new evolutionary trajectory, and as a consequence the 861previously marginal area is no longer a marginal area for them, but a relatively optimal area 862(and a possible future source area for a new lineage). An additional possibility that should be 863taken into account is interbreeding and hybridization between populations (Ackermann, 2011; 864Abbott et al., 2016; Popadin et al., 2017; Ackermann et al., 2018). These processes could have 865taken place for instance if newly immigrated evolved hominin populations from the coastal 866forest met with local hominin populations that persisted in an inland area, after dispersal from 867the coast during the previous stable period. 868 869In summary, we posit that hominin populations arriving from the CFEA in inland areas either 870go locally extinct at the end of climatically stable episodes when climatic-environmental

871conditions change again, or hybridize with local hominins that were there already, or persist 872and do not evolve, or persist and evolve into a new species. Thus, we define as “algorithm” of 873the conceptual model: 874•During a climatically variable episode: isolation and ongoing evolution of hominin 875populations in the CFEA (Step 1); 876•At the onset of and during a stable episode: expansion and dispersal of evolved hominin 877species to inland areas; and in case of establishment of a viable population: Step 2a) 878continuation of species as is, or 2b) meet local hominin population and interbreed, leading to 879hybridization. During stable episodes, the coastal forest remains populated with hominins; 880•At the beginning of and during a variable climate episode in inland areas: Step 3a) local 881extinction, or 3b) persistence of species as is, or 3c) adaptation and evolution, or 3d) 882persistence of hybrid population, or 3e) adaptative evolution of hybrid population; and Step

8831) again: isolation and evolution of hominin populations in the CFEA. 884 885In figure 10 we present one possible example (starting with a fictive hominin species during a 886variable climate phase in the coastal forest) of how this algorithm could play out. It is clear that 887even with simple climate forcing and this algorithm, the many degrees of freedom associated 888with multiple combinations of steps can produce bushy complex speciation and distribution 889patterns in a multitude of ways, approximating the levels of actual complexity and hominin 890species richness that must have existed but that we may never truly know (Martin, 1993; Spoor, 8912016). However, we can derive predictions from the coastal refuge model, and evaluate how 892they compare with the observed pattern in hominin and climate records (Fig. 3, 8) that we have 893combined into one figure (Fig. 11). 894

The impact of climate and environmental change between 3-2.5 Ma 949The hominin fossil record between ~ 3 Ma and 2.5 Ma (Fig. 3, 11) is characterized by: 950•the observed last appearance of the widespread and long-lasting species Australopithecus 951afarensis at ~2.95 Ma (Kimbel & Delezene, 2009; Kimbel, 2015); 952•the observed first appearance of Homo sp., in the Afar Basin at ~2.8 Ma (Villmoare et al., 9532015; DiMaggio et al., 2015; Reed et al., 2015); 954•the observed first appearance of Paranthropus aethiopicus in the Turkana Basin, Laetoli 955and Malawi Basin by ~2.7 Ma (Wood and Constantino, 2007; Harrison, 2011); 956•the observed first appearance of Australopithecus africanus at ~3 Ma in southern Africa 957(Herries et al., 2013). 958 959Together, these last and first appearances suggest occurrence of a major regional change in 960hominin taxa between ~ 3-2.8 Ma, resulting in at least three new lineages (Kimbel, 1995; Reed, 9612008; Rowan and Reed, 2015; Robinson et al., 2017). Where, why and how were these changes 962in hominin evolution brought about? Between ~3 and 2.6 Ma, onset and intensification of NHG 963resulted in increasing climatic cooling and drying in Africa (Fig. 8) and globally increased 964seasonality (Hennissen et al., 2015), which influenced vegetation cover in Africa. This is 965demonstrated by a pronounced decline in tree pollen density at ~2.7 Ma indicating an abrupt 966retreat of rain forests to an area of the same extension as today (Bonnefille, 2010). This is also 967shown in pollen records from ODP Site 659 off the western African coast (Leroy and Dupont, 9681994). A pronounced shift in d13C of soil carbonates in the Turkana Basin at ~2.7 Ma 969(Bonnefille, 2010; Levin et al., 2015) and in dietary d13C in herbivores in the Omo Valley at

970~2.8 Ma (Bibi et al., 2013) indicates a change from woodland to more open conditions, and/or a 971shift from C3 to more C4 grasses at that time. Robinson et al. (2017) provided a detailed multi- 972proxy analysis of the environmental shift at ~2.8 Ma the Lower Awash Valley of the Afar 973Basin, showing consistent changes from wooded towards more open and arid conditions. 974Interestingly, they found that after ~2.8 Ma the Turkana Basin was more mesic than the Lower 975Awash Valley, confirming its status as a continuously well-watered environment thanks to the 976Omo River. 977 978Between ~3 and 2.5 Ma, progressive climatic cooling and drying in Africa adversely influenced 979vegetation of the coastal forest. Moreover, from ~3 Ma onwards, weakening of the rain-bearing 980summer monsoon and strengthening of the cold and dry winter monsoon (Zhisheng et al., 2001; 981Xu et al., 2015; Vallé et al., 2015) may have contributed to drying out the Horn of Africa. 982Hence, for the CFEA this would mean a latitudinal retreat of the forest area, with its northern 983and southern borders moving in the direction of the equator, approximately to their present-day 984locations in southern Somalia and southern Mozambique respectively. Extensive dry coastal 985areas would occur to the north and south of the CFEA borders: respectively the Horn of Africa, 986and part of the flat river-less coastal lowland between the mouths of the Zambezi and Limpopo 987Rivers (Kingdon, 2003). However, we assume that some areas in the northeast and southeast of 988Africa remained relatively humid due to orographic rainfall: part of the Afar Basin still received 989water from the Awash River draining the Ethiopian Highlands, and the southernmost part of 990eastern Africa still received water via rivers flowing to the coast from the Drakensberg 991mountain chain. We infer that global climate change around ~3 Ma caused fragmentation of the 992coastal forest, resulting in the emergence of three geographically isolated relatively humid 993hominin occupation areas along the north-south axis of eastern Africa: the Afar Basin, the 994contracted CFEA along the coast of Southern Somalia, Kenya, Tanzania and northern

995Mozambique, and the coastal forest in southeastern Africa (Fig. 13). Environmental conditions 996in these isolated areas, arguably each containing populations of Australopithecus afarensis at 997that moment, would differ due to different rainfall, river water input and air temperatures 998associated with their particular geographical position. Especially in the Afar Basin where local 999rainfall decreased dramatically between ~3-2.7 Ma (Zhisheng et al., 2001; Bonnefille, 2004), 1000woodlands would disappear to be replaced by more arid open conditions (Campisano and 1001Feibel, 2007, 2008; Reed, 2008; Robinson et al., 2017) with gallery forests forming a narrow 1002verdant strip along the Awash River. The contracted CFEA likely became dryer compared to 1003conditions before ~3 Ma, but coastal woodlands and rivers with gallery forests and floodplains 1004continued to exist throughout orbital wet-dry climate cycles (Kingdon, 2003; Dupont and 1005Kuhlman, 2017). The humid area in southern Africa, consisting of a narrow coastal strip (~ 10- 100620 km) with mountains rising abruptly out of the plain, differs physically and ecologically from 1007equivalent coastal habitats further up the coast (Kingdon, 2003). Due to its southern position, it 1008experiences a strong seasonality with short cold winters and long hot summers, possibly driving 1009seasonal faunal migration between lowland and highland (inland) environments. The arid areas 1010north and south of the contracted coastal forest would inhibit spread of forest and water- 1011dependent species along the coast and thus act as a barrier to gene flow between populations on 1012either side of it (Kingdon, 2003), ensuring genetic isolation of separated hominin populations. 1013 1014In summary, environmental change in eastern Africa has likely led to a tripartite habitat 1015fragmentation slightly after ~3 Ma. We propose that this resulted in a tripartitite cladogenetic 1016split into three different taxa (X, Y and Z; Fig. 14). The northern population of A. afarensis that 1017became isolated in the Afar Basin gave rise to taxon X (and/or possibly Homo sp. at 2.8 in the 1018Afar Basin; Villmoare et al., 2015), and the southern population that became isolated in 1019southeastern Africa evolved into taxon Z (possibly A. africanus at ~3 Ma in Makapansgat;

1020Herries et al., 2013). The “central” population isolated in the contracted CFEA gave rise to 1021taxon Y. No fossil remains are known from possible descendants of this isolated CFEA 1022population, which is not surprising since the coastal forest has a poor vertebrate fossilization 1023potential. However, the presence of P. aethiopicus fossils at ~2.7 Ma in the Turkana Basin and 1024Laetoli (Kimbel and Delezene, 2009; Harrison, 2011) could suggest the identity of hominin 1025inhabitants of the contracted coastal forest at that time. Within the constraints of the coastal 1026refuge model outlined above, regular connections between contracted CFEA and inland EARS 1027basins indicate that P. aethiopicus may have evolved from A. afarensis after ~3 Ma in the 1028contracted CFEA, and then during a stable episode migrated inland via humid corridors. This 1029mechanism would explain the presence of P. aethiopicus fossils in Turkana Basin and Laetoli 1030(and the later presence of its descendant P. boisei in the Turkana, Baringo, and Malawi Basins; 1031Wood and Constantino, 2007), and its absence in the Afar Basin and southern Africa. However, 1032we have to bear in mind that an alternative explanation for presence of P. aethiopicus could be 1033that it locally descended from an as yet unknown robust hominin in an inland area (c.f. the dark 1034blue hominin evolving into the purple hominin in an EARS basin, Fig. 10). 1035 1036We conclude that the proposed climate-forced habitat fragmentation at ~3 Ma provides an 1037ecological mechanism that could explain patterns of observed taxon change in one of the 1038lineages of the presently known hominin record (Fig. 11, 14). Other, presently less well- 1039understood taxa such as Kenyanthropus platyops, “Burtele foot”, Australopithecus deyiremeda 1040and A. prometheus (and future discoveries of new taxa) are tantalizing indicators of the possible 1041existence of additional lineages in the hominin evolutionary record, reminding us of its 1042daunting complexity and species richness (Martin, 1993). 1043 104410. Discussion

1045We present a new view on geographic patterns in early hominin presence over time, placing 1046the core area of early hominin evolution not in the EARS or southern Africa, but in the Coastal 1047Forest of Eastern Africa (with the Afar Basin, notably before 3 Ma, being regularly part of this 1048core). We concede that this would not necessarily be the one and only core area: possibly the 1049forests in the central and western parts of Africa (and perhaps even the Atlantic and 1050Mediterranean coasts) played an as yet unknown role as well. It can be expected that during 1051stable climate periods when east-west connectivity existed, hominins were present in (some of) 1052these areas. For instance, occasional interfingering of separated western-central and eastern 1053biomes led to occasional spatial overlap and secondary contact between early hominin and 1054chimpanzee lineages at the fringes of ranges (Grubb et al., 1999; McBrearty and Jablonski, 10552005), as demonstrated by interbreeding and hybridization between these lineages after their 1056initial split (Patterson et al., 2006). Similarly, research on ape and human body lice genetics 1057has shown that there must have been a secondary meeting (probably predator-prey contact) 1058between early hominin and gorilla lineages at some time in the Pliocene (Light and Reed, 10592009). 1060We have presented supporting evidence for the assumptions underlying the coastal refuge 1061model, and concluded that several predictions following from the coastal refuge model are 1062consistent with the observed hominin fossil and paleoclimate records. However, we have not 1063yet rigorously tested the model. Vrba (2007) pointed out that is difficult to test hypotheses on 1064environmental causes of hominin and other biotic evolution, because the data come from 1065different subdisciplines each with their own set of biases and errors. A major challenge for the 1066coastal refuge model is the fact that at present there is not a single Pliocene hominin fossil 1067known from the Coastal Forest of Eastern Africa. The primary test of the coastal refuge 1068hypothesis would be to find early hominin fossils in east coastal deposits, thus corroborating 1069the model. Or alternatively, a good test would be to find a rich and diverse fossil fauna in east

1070coastal deposits aged ~4.5-2.5 Ma, containing no hominin fossils (cf. Lothagam, section 3.3), 1071thus rejecting the model. 1072Since fossilization potential in the CFEA is very low, extra efforts are required to identify and 1073explore possibly fossiliferous areas near former Indian Ocean coastlines and in specific places 1074within the coastal zone. Fortunately, there is increasing scientific interest in the coastal area. 1075For instance, as mentioned before, the new project at Gorongosa in Mozambique conducted by 1076Susana Carvalho and colleagues (Carvalho et al., 2017; Habermann et al., 2018) is starting to 1077find evidence of forested conditions in a coastal setting in the Mio-Pliocene Mazamba 1078Formation, with so far limited but encouraging presence of vertebrate fossils. Habermann et al. 1079(2018) state that absence or presence of primates in the fossil record from Gorongosa will be 1080key for testing paleobiogeographic hypotheses of primate evolution in coastal forests. We are 1081hopeful that their continuing work in Gorongosa will yield such fossils. 1082Other examples of promising fossil exploration areas would be sediments trapped in limestone 1083caves located in the Indian Ocean coastal areas, notably along the coasts of Kenya (e.g.Vipingo 1084and Similoni caves; Kilifi area), Tanzania (e.g. Matumbi and Amboni caves) and Mozambique 1085(Marean and Shea, 1996; Younge et al., 2002; Helm et al., 2012; Pickford, 2012; Gonçalves et 1086al., 2016). Recently, Shipton et al. (2018) excavated to 3 m depth in the Panga ya Saidi cave in 1087the coastal forest north of Mombasa (Kenya), and found a record of human activity back to ~ 108878 ka. This inspires confidence that it would in principle not be impossible to find evidence of 1089hominin occupation in the CFEA. It could be worth while to dig deeper than 3 m in caves such 1090as Panga ya Saidi in order to look for older deposits containing signs of hominin presence, 1091analogous to the discoveries made in deep trenches in the cave Liang Bua on Flores in 1092Indonesia (Sutikna et al., 2018). 1093

1094In addition to the hominin fossil record, non-hominin faunal and floral fossil records are 1095particularly useful for testing the coastal refuge model. For example, the model posits that 1096hominins (especially before ~3 Ma) were predominantly associated with the humid 1097forest/woodland biome, instead of with a more open savanna biome. Hence, we can infer that 1098mammals constituting a forest/woodland faunal community would show a similar distribution 1099pattern in space and time as hominin fossils before ~3 Ma. Providing there is sufficient age 1100control and temporal resolution in the dataset, this can be tested by quantitatively analyzing 1101presence/absence/abundance of typical “forest” and “non-forest” (and “water-dependent” and 1102“dry-tolerant”) species over time in a similar way as we did for hominin fossils, by plotting 1103numbers of fossils (or preferably percentages of total fauna) per basin over time (as in Fig. 12). 1104Since relatively large numbers of non-hominin mammal fossils are (and will become) available 1105for this (Bobe et al., 2007; Bobe and Leakey, 2009; Rannikko et al., 2017), it should be possible 1106to use statistical analyses to test significance of the patterns found (e.g. Rowan et al., 2015). It 1107would also be worthwhile to apply ecometric analysis to fossil mammal teeth of different time 1108periods in order to detect possible evidence of the variable-stable climate periodicity and 1109associated periodicity in seasonality, notably the role of long dry seasons (Žliobaite et al., 11102016). The more fossils become available for study, and the better constrained in time, the more 1111feasible it will be to discern statistically significant patterns in time and space that can be 1112interpreted in terms of environmental change, evolution and dispersal. 1113 1114Another way of testing the model is to conduct genetic/genomic analyses on extant flora and 1115fauna in different parts of Africa. The molecular phylogeographic work of Hemp et al. (2015), 1116Brya et al. (2017) and Barratt et al. (2017, 2018), resolving evolutionary relationships and 1117timing of separation/connection events between coastal and inland populations of forest- 1118dwelling insects, rats, and amphibians, has already been mentioned. Such approaches could also

1119be applied to test Kingdon’s predictions on the coastal-inland sister-species complexes, such as 1120squirrels of the genus Paraxerus (Kingdon, 2003; see also Grubb, 1978) and bushbabies of the 1121genus Paragalago (Groves, 2015; Masters et al., 2017). Presently, we are using comparative 1122genetic analyses (mitochondrial genomes in combination with several nuclear genes) on the 1123extant fish species Synodontis schall and Hydrocynus forskahlli, in combination with 1124occurrence data from the fish fossil record in Africa (see e.g. Pinton et al., 2013), to calculate 1125phylogenetic trees and estimate timing of the last gene flow (as proxy for drainage connection) 1126between populations of these species in the Turkana Basin and the Chad Basin (Otero et al., 11272016). For instance, based on the model and the pattern in the hominin 1134fossil record (Fig. 11), it would be expected to find fossils of early Australopithecus afarensis 1135in ~3.8 My old deposits in the Turkana Basin, similar to fossils of that age in the Afar Basin 1136and Laetoli. 1169We have explored the relevance of the Coastal Forest of Eastern Africa in hominin 1170biogeography and proposed that the coastal forest served as a core area for early hominin 1171evolution and dispersal. From this hypothesis, we developed the conceptual coastal refuge 1172model that combines dimensions of time and space to investigate how climatic and 1173environmental changes are reflected in the presently known hominin fossil record. The most 1174striking temporal feature of the pattern in the hominin fossil record is the dividing line that can 1175be drawn at ~3 Ma (Fig. 11). 1176Before ~3 Ma, the Afar Basin appears to have been a hominin (sub-)core area, and in that 1177respect similar to (and/or part of) the CFEA. The other inland fossil sites then were marginal 1178areas for hominin habitation, with the Turkana Basin being relatively less marginal than the 1179other inland EARS basins. Between ~4.5 and 3 Ma, the 100 and 400 ky eccentricity cycles were 1180main climatic drivers of evolution and dispersal. Hominin evolution occurred notably during 1181variable climate episodes in Earth’ eccentricity maxima (as proposed by Maslin and Trauth, 11822009; Potts, 2013). We add an important component to the existing viewpoint: hominin 1183dispersal from the coast to (and between) inland basins occurred via vegetated humid corridors 1184notably during stable climate episodes in eccentricity minima. Seasonality, especially the length 1185of the dry season, must have been a critical factor constituting a threshold for establishment and 1186persistence of vegetated conditions and viable hominin populations in inland areas. 1187After ~3 Ma, when NHG intensified, increasingly arid and cool conditions resulted in 1188latitudinal contraction of the CFEA. We infer that this forest contraction and habitat 1189fragmentation caused a tripartite geographic split of the hominin population then residing in the 1190coastal forest zone along Indian Ocean and Red Sea coasts. As a consequence, three separated 1191populations found themselves isolated from each other in different ecological conditions, which 1192resulted in different evolutionary trajectories (Fig. 14). We conclude that between ~3 and 2.5 1193Ma, NHG exerted the all-important climatic influence on hominin evolution, likely causing a

1194major cladogenetic event in the hominin lineage. We posit that also after ~3 Ma, the 100 ky and 1195400 ky climate cycles will have continued to affect the dispersal and evolution patterns of 1196hominin populations by recurrently, during stable climate episodes, connecting the contracted 1197coastal forest with inland EARS basins. 1198 1199A major challenge for the coastal refuge model is the fact that so far, no (hominin) fossils have 1200been found in the CFEA. We have provided explanations for the lack of fossils, and indicated 1201how this could be remedied with targeted search efforts in the coastal area. Furthermore we 1202have discussed how the model can be tested, e.g. with molecular phylogeography approaches, 1203and used to predict new hominin fossil locations. The coastal refuge model recognizes and 1204includes the influence of climatic and environmental variability as drivers of early hominin 1205evolution (Potts, 1996, 1998, 2013; Kingston, 2007, Maslin and Trauth, 2009), and significance 1206of climatic stability and mesic corridors to facilitate hominin dispersal (Castañeda et al., 2009; 1207Drake et al., 2011; Larrasoaña et al., 2013; Grove, 2014; Grove et al., 2015; Cuthbert et al., 12082017). Also, it agrees with deMenocal (2004, 2011) who invoked major global climate change 1209related to onset and intensification of NHG as driver for hominin evolution. Our model 1210integrates the spatial component into these accumulated insights and proposes a unifying 1211testable climate-forced ecological mechanism explaining distribution of early hominin taxa in 1212Africa. Even so, we realize that such a conceptual model will at best be a mere approximation 1213of actual complexity in hominin taxic diversity and evolution.
