**Abstract**

Cell-intrinsic mechanisms of nutrient sensing are intimately linked to adap- tive metabolic responses, and these pathways play critical roles in the complex and dynamic nutrient environment of a growing tumor. Nutrient-responsive transcription factors (e.g., HIF, SREBP, ATF4) and signaling pathways (e.g., mTORC1, AMPK) allow tumor cells to tune their metabolic output and strategies to fluctuations in nutrient availability, thus balancing tumor cell proliferation and survival with a combination of anabolic and adaptive re- sponses. Coupling these nutrient-sensing mechanisms to the control of re- cycling and scavenging processes, such as autophagy and macropinocytosis, further enhances the adaptability to nutrients within tumors. Here, we dis- cuss the key nutrient-sensing pathways active in cancer cells, how oncogenic events influence these pathways, and their likely contributions to tumor growth and survival. A better understanding of nutrient-sensing strategies and metabolic adaptations within the tumor microenvironment is critical to defining and targeting metabolic vulnerabilities in cancer.

**INTRODUCTION**

Cancer cells undergo metabolic adaptations to reinforce their ability to survive and proliferate. Cell proliferation, both normal and neoplastic, requires the production of biomass in the form of proteins, nucleic acids, and lipids from available nutrients and energy, and cancer cells display an enhanced capacity to produce these macromolecular building blocks (DeBerardinis & Chandel

2016). To satisfy these anabolic requirements, cells must be able to monitor available resources to coordinately tune metabolic responses to the supply of nutrients. Therefore, cellular nutrient sensing and metabolic control are intimately linked. In solid tumors, the availability of exogenous nutrient resources is believed to fluctuate extensively over the course of tumor development and progression. The ability of cancer cells to sense and adapt to these changes can be theoretically influenced by their underlying genetic drivers, tissues of origin, and the dynamic tumor microen- vironment. We have learned much about how cells sense intracellular nutrients and energy and respond accordingly with alterations in cellular metabolism. However, how these sensing mech- anisms function in tumors and influence the growth, survival, and metastatic potential of cancer cells remains poorly understood. This review focuses on cell-intrinsic nutrient-sensing pathways and their role in tumorigenesis.
#### NUTRIENT-SENSING TRANSCRIPTION FACTORS

#### AND CELL-INTRINSIC METABOLIC ADAPTATIONS

A major mechanism by which cells alter their metabolic state in response to changes in nutrient availability is through the induction of gene expression programs affecting the levels of nutri- ent transporters and metabolic enzymes. Individual transcription factors have evolved to sense the deprivation of specific nutrients and, in turn, stimulate an adaptive response to overcome this deficiency. Many of these factors have been found to contribute to tumor development and progression, and we discuss three of these below (Figure 1).

#### Hypoxia-Inducible Factor

Oxygen is a key nutrient that supports aerobic metabolism, cellular bioenergetics, and specific biochemical reactions. Thomlinson & Gray (1955) made the pioneering discovery that necrotic lung tumors display gradient changes in oxygen exposure, with the necrotic core being the most oxygen-limited region. We now know that, like normal cells, tumor cells sense and respond to lim- iting oxygen levels through transcription factors called hypoxia-inducible factors (HIFs) (Wang et al. 1995) (Figure 1). Importantly, studies have shown that HIF1α and HIF2α have both redundant and distinct target genes and appear to play opposing roles in some cancer settings, such as renal cell carcinoma (RCC) (Keith et al. 2012). HIF1α is the major isoform that induces the expression of glycolytic enzymes (Hu et al. 2003; Iyer et al. 1998; Semenza et al. 1994, 1996). However, HIF2α stabilization underlies RCC driven by loss of the VHL tumor suppressor, which occurs in about 50% of RCCs (Kim & Kaelin 2004), and HIF1α contributes to tumor growth and angiogenesis in tumor models of a variety of other cancers (Kondo et al. 2002, 2003; Raval et al. 2005; Semenza 2003; Zimmer et al. 2004). HIF1α and HIF2α exert opposing control over two major drivers of anabolic tumor metabolism, the transcription factor c-Myc and the protein kinase mechanistic target of rapamycin complex 1 (mTORC1; see below), with HIF1α attenuating and HIF2α promoting their functions, at least in some settings (Dang et al. 2008, Keith et al. 2012). Although it is established that the HIF proteins play an important role in the adaptive response to oxygen deprivation in tumors, how their response cooperates with other nutrient-sensing programs and oncogenic signaling pathways in tumors remains an active area of research.

#### Sterol Regulatory Element-Binding Proteins

Given the need for lipids to support cell growth, it is not surprising that increased expression of lipogenic enzymes such as fatty acid synthase (FASN) and enhanced de novo lipid synthesis are long recognized metabolic features of tumors (Medes et al. 1953, Menendez & Lupu 2007). Sterol regulatory element-binding proteins (SREBPs) are a family of transcription factors that induce nearly every enzyme required for the de novo synthesis of fatty acid and sterol lipids (Horton et al.

2002). This lipogenic gene expression program is controlled by lipid-sensing mechanisms inherent to SREBP (Figure 1), but there is also increasing evidence of SREBP activation downstream of oncogenic signaling pathways (Duvel et al. 2010, Guo et al. 2009, Porstmann et al. 2008, Ricoult et al. 2016). Inactive SREBP is associated with SREBP cleavage-activating protein (SCAP) in the endoplasmic reticulum (ER) membrane, where it senses cholesterol and perhaps other lipid species (Horton et al. 2002). Upon sterol deprivation, the SREBP-SCAP complex is released from ER retention and traffics to the Golgi complex, where it is proteolytically cleaved by two proteases, resulting in the release of the N-terminal domain of SREBP, which is the functionally active transcription factor. This mature form of SREBP enters the nucleus to activate its lipogenic gene targets, which possess sterol regulatory elements (SREs). In this manner, SREBP serves to sense lipids and, upon their depletion, induce a program to restore their levels. In cancer cells, increased de novo lipid synthesis facilitates the production of cell surface and organellar membranes to support cell growth and division. SREBP targets such as ATP citrate lyase, FASN, and stearoyl-CoA desaturase (SCD) are often upregulated in human cancers, and SREBP activation has been implicated in cancer cell proliferation, survival, and tumor progression (Griffiths et al. 2013, Guo et al. 2009, Lewis et al. 2015, Liu et al. 2016, Peck et al. 2016, Ricoult et al. 2016, Williams et al. 2013, Young et al. 2013). #### Activating Transcription Factor 4

Cellular dry mass consists of about 50% protein; thus tumor cell proliferation is accompanied by a great demand for amino acids to support increased protein synthesis, as well as other amino acid–dependent processes such as de novo nucleotide and glutathione synthesis. A major amino acid–sensing mechanism employed by all eukaryotic cells to adapt to amino acid depletion in- volves induction of the activating transcription factor 4 (ATF4), also known as CREB2 (Figure 1). In response to amino acid depletion, uncharged transfer RNAs (tRNAs) accumulate and bind to a tRNA synthetase homology domain at the C terminus of the Ser/Thr kinase GCN2, which activates the kinase (Dong et al. 2000). GCN2 subsequently phosphorylates the eukaryotic trans- lation initiation factor eIF2α on S51, thereby attenuating cap-dependent translation initiation and preserving existing pools of amino acids (Hinnebusch et al. 2016). However, this phosphorylation promotes the selective translation of a subset of messenger RNAs (mRNAs), the best character- ized of which is ATF4, whose levels rapidly increase upon depletion of intracellular amino acids. Glucose deprivation or hypoxia can also activate ATF4 through eIF2α phosphorylation, but this occurs through the GCN2-related kinase PERK, which is activated by ER stress apparently caused by ER protein misfolding under conditions of insufficient glucose or oxygen (Blais et al. 2004, Koumenis et al. 2002, Liu et al. 2008, Scheuner et al. 2001). ATF4 induces the expression of many target genes aimed at recovering from nutrient depriva- tion and other cellular stresses (Han et al. 2013, Harding et al. 2003). Importantly, ATF4 promotes the transcription of amino acid transporters and enzymes involved in the synthesis of nonessential amino acids. Thus, GCN2 activation leads to both decreased amino acid consumption, via the attenuation of protein synthesis, and the induction of transporters and enzymes to acquire or pro- duce more amino acids through ATF4 activation. An increasing number of studies have examined the role of ATF4 in cancer metabolism. ATF4-mediated expression of the enzymes required for synthesis of the nonessential amino acids asparagine and serine has been found to play an impor- tant role in cancer cell survival and proliferation (DeNicola et al. 2015; Krall et al. 2016; Liu et al. 2016; Ye et al. 2010, 2012). The neutral amino acid transporter SLC1A5 (ASCT2), encoded by an ATF4 target gene, has also been found to contribute to tumor growth in breast and prostate cancer models (van Geldermalsen et al. 2016, Wang et al. 2015). Importantly, unresolved nutrient deprivation can result in cell death, and ATF4 activation has been found to contribute to the induction of apoptosis under such conditions (Iurlaro et al. 2017, Qing et al. 2012). Thus, ATF4 appears to drive the response to amino acid depletion in cancer and is likely important for both adaptive tumor cell survival and, with persistent nutrient starvation, the promotion of cell death.

#### AMP-ACTIVATED PROTEIN KINASE

The Ser/Thr AMP-activated protein kinase (AMPK) maintains cellular energy homeostasis by sensing and responding to energy stress resulting from decreased ATP production or excessive ATP consumption, which it directly senses through an increase in the ratio of AMP to ATP under such conditions (Hardie 2014) (Figure 2). For instance, the depletion of major catabolic Downloaded from www.annualreviews.org. Importantly, AMPK induces ATP-producing catabolic processes including glucose uptake, mitochondrial respi- ration, and fatty acid oxidation while inhibiting ATP-consuming anabolic processes such as protein and lipid synthesis. One major mechanism by which AMPK promotes this shift from anabolic to catabolic metabolisms is through the inhibition of mTORC1 (Gwinn et al. 2008, Inoki et al. 2003). Given that AMPK activation promotes catabolism to maintain energy balance but also inhibits the anabolic processes underlying tumor growth, it is not surprising that AMPK activation has been found to be both beneficial and detrimental to tumors in different settings (Faubert et al.

2013, Jeon et al. 2012, Laderoute et al. 2006, Leprivier et al. 2013). It is most widely believed that AMPK has tumor suppressor–like activity due to the fact that it is activated in response to energy stress by the upstream kinase LKB1, a bona fide tumor suppressor whose function is frequently lost in human cancers (Hardie & Alessi 2013, Shackelford & Shaw 2009). However, LKB1 is also an essential upstream activator of 12 additional protein kinases that are related to AMPK but do not respond to energy depletion, any of which could also contribute to the role of LKB1 as a tumor suppressor. Similar to the activation of nutrient-sensing transcription factors (see above) and autophagy (see below), AMPK activation likely serves as a key adaptive mechanism supporting tumor cell survival, especially under conditions of nutrient or energy stress ( Jeon et al. 2012, Leprivier et al. 2013). Therefore, despite its ability to limit tumor growth through its inhibition of mTORC1 signaling and other anabolic programs, AMPK activation could facilitate the survival of cells within growing tumors that experience a deprivation of catabolic substrates.

**MACROAUTOPHAGY**

The process of macroautophagy (autophagy) involves the sequestration and lysosomal degradation of cytosolic macromolecules and organelles, thereby recycling these cellular constituents into their nutrient components, which can be subsequently catabolized for energy production or mobilized for biosynthetic processes to make new macromolecules (Kaur & Debnath 2015). Importantly, whereas basal and selective autophagy can occur in cells as part of protein and organelle quality control, autophagy is strongly induced upon nutrient deprivation, where it serves to salvage cellular nutrients from macromolecular constituents. Autophagy is induced upon nutrient and energy depletion, at least in part, through attenuation of mTORC1 signaling and activation of AMPK. Under nutrient-rich conditions, mTORC1 serves as a potent inhibitor of autophagy through phosphorylation and inhibition of the autophagy-initiating complex ULK1-ATG13-FIP200 and other autophagy components (Ganley et al. 2009, Jung et al. 2009, Kim et al. 2011). AMPK stimulates autophagy through both its inhibition of mTORC1 and direct phosphorylation and activation of ULK1 (Egan et al. 2011, Kim et al. 2011). How the interplay between mTORC1 and AMPK signaling and the regulation of autophagy influence tumor growth and survival in different nutrient environments requires further investigation. It has been proposed that cancer cells use autophagy to relieve oxidative stress, prevent in- flammatory responses, avoid energy crisis, and acquire both catabolic substrates and metabolic precursors (Santana-Codina et al. 2017). However, autophagy seems to play distinct roles in tu- mor development and progression that depend on the tumor stage. Genetic studies have found that loss of autophagy and its ability to maintain normal cellular homeostasis can promote tu- mor initiation (Qu et al. 2003, Rao et al. 2014, Takamura et al. 2011, Yue et al. 2003). This tumor-suppressive function of autophagy in tissues is likely due to its role in preventing ROS and inflammation. #### SENSING AND ACQUISITION OF NUTRIENTS DURING TUMOR

#### DEVELOPMENT AND PROGRESSION

It is likely that access to nutrients varies significantly from tumor initiation to malignant invasion and metastasis (Figure 4). Early genetic events leading to cellular transformation often affect growth factor signaling pathways, rendering cell survival and proliferation independent of exoge- nous growth signals. Due to their contact with normal vasculature at this stage, these neoplastic cells generally have an ample supply of oxygen and other nutrients to support cell proliferation. Oncogenic driver mutations, such as those leading to aberrant activation of mTORC1 or c-Myc, promote nutrient uptake and anabolic conversion to the macromolecules necessary for cell growth and division. As nascent tumors outgrow the normal vasculature, they must adapt their metabolism and strategies for acquiring nutrients accordingly. #### PERSPECTIVES AND FUTURE CHALLENGES

Cellular nutrient-sensing pathways are intimately tied to the control of cellular metabolism, and the extension of this relationship to cancer underlies the metabolic plasticity of tumors and the

inherent challenges in therapeutically targeting cancer metabolism. Anatomic, genetic, and phys- iologic properties of the tumor, including the tissue of origin, oncogenic lesions, and the con- stituency of the stroma, and of the host, including dietary factors and the systemic metabolism, all influence the metabolic strategies and vulnerabilities of cancer cells in ways that we are just begin- ning to discover. For instance, analyses suggest that there is a greater overlap in metabolic gene expression between tumors and their tissues of origin than between tumors of different lineages (Hu et al. 2013). However, distinct oncogenic drivers can also contribute to key differences in the use of metabolic substrates within the same cancer lineage (Yuneva et al. 2012). Likewise, a single oncogenic driver can differentially influence nutrient uptake and metabolism in distinct tissue settings (Mayers et al. 2016, Yuneva et al. 2012). Understanding the molecular mechanisms and physiology underlying these differences is of central importance for harnessing such observations toward improved treatment or imaging modalities. Systemic nutrients and integrated metabolic responses can also influence tumor growth. It is well established that dietary restriction, meaning reduced calorie intake without overt starvation, hinders tumor growth in rodent models (Kritchevsky 2001). A decrease in circulating insulin and insulin-like growth factor levels is likely to contribute to this effect by dampening tumor cell– intrinsic signaling through the PI3K-mTORC1 pathway, with tumor cells that exhibit sustained activation of this pathway showing resistance to dietary restriction (Curry et al. 2013, Kalaany & Sabatini 2009). Other glucose- and insulin-lowering dietary interventions, such as ketogenic diets, have also been found to decrease tumor growth in some rodent models (Otto et al. 2008, Zhou et al. 2007), whereas high-fat diets can enhance tumor growth and metastasis in other models (Pascual et al. 2017, Xia et al. 2017). Interestingly, diets lacking the nonessential amino acids serine and glycine, which play a central role in protein, nucleotide, and glutathione synthesis, as well as processes requiring one-carbon metabolism, have been found to reduce tumor growth and enhance survival in a variety of xenograft and genetic mouse tumor models (Maddocks et al. 2013, 2017). However, with few exceptions, the direct contributions of dietary nutrients to tumor development, growth, and progression are poorly understood. The biggest challenge in the study of nutrient sensing as it relates to cancer metabolism is an inadequate ability to precisely measure nutrient availability within a given tumor. In fact, outside of oxygen, very little is known regarding the nutrients that are truly limiting in vivo for tumor growth and the diffusion properties of specific nutrients within the tumor microenvironment. Interpreta- tion of standard metabolomic and lipidomic data from homogenized tumor tissue is challenging due to a varying abundance of stromal cells and tumor heterogeneity. Mass spectrometry and nuclear magnetic resonance–based measurements of nutrients from interstitial fluids collected from tumors versus adjacent normal tissues provide one tool toward understanding tumor nu- trient availability. Recently developed desorption electrospray ionization mass spectrometry and nanostructure-initiator mass spectrometry have shown potential for identifying region-specific metabolic properties within tumors (Faubert & DeBerardinis 2017).
