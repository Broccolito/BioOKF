While classical microbial taxonomy relied on microbial cultures, most of the current microbial diversity remains uncultured. Conse quently, microbial taxonomy has been further revamped with the advent of the SeqCode (Hedlund et al., 2022), finally allowing the formation of permanent prokaryotic names based on sequence data. SeqCode relies on a set of nomenclature rules, including rule 15, stating that the name of a family, order, class, or phylum is derived from the name of its type genus (“The SeqCode” 2023). As Asgardarchaeota currently lacks a type genus, this phylum name has no standing in nomenclature under the SeqCode rules. This study addresses this gap by establishing a robust, SeqCode-compliant nomenclature for Asgard archaea at multiple taxo nomic ranks, based on phylogenomic analyses. Here, we describe the MAG serving as the nomenclatural type for the novel archaeal species, Asgardarchaeum abyssi gen. nov. spec. nov., a distinct lineage within the phylum Asgardarchaeota. Our phylogenomic analysis provides compelling evidence for its classification as a member of a new family, order, and class within the phylum Asgardarchaeota. Based on these results, we propose Asgardarchaeum gen. nov. as the type genus for Asgardarchaeaceae (fam. nov.), Asgardarchaeales (ord. nov.), *Asgardarchaeia (class. nov.), and Asgardarchaeota (phyl. nov.), and have* submitted these names for validation under the SeqCode. Our efforts aim to preserve taxonomic congruence given the prominence of the taxon Asgardarchaeota in the scientific literature. **Materials and methods** *Sample acquisition, sequencing, and assembly* Sampling, sequencing, assembling and binning have been previously described in detail for this sequencing effort (Gong et al., 2022; Langwig et al., 2022; Carlton et al., 2023). Briefly, we collected sediment push cores with the human-occupied vehicle Alvin from the Guaymas Basin (GB) in the Gulf of California (27.388 N 111.24560 W). On cruise AT15- 56, Dive 4573 sampled Aceto Balsamico mat (AB), a site known for high acetate and methane concentrations (Teske et al., 2016; Zhuang et al.,
2019). We binned the MAG AB3033_2
TS from core 23 between 30–33 cm in depth. Geochemical data collected at this depth include temperature (22◦C), sulfate (0.36 mM) and methane concentrations (6.4 mM) with a stable isotopic methane composition of 45 δ 13 C-CH4. We extracted DNA for each subsection of the core using a DNAeasy PowerSoil kit (Qiagen, Germantown, Maryland, USA) and quantified it with a QUBIT 2.0 fluorometer (Thermo-Fisher, Singapore). Library preparation and short-read sequencing (Illumina HiSeq 4000) was conducted by the Michigan State University RTSF Genomics Core. Sickle v1.33 ([https://github.com/najoshi/sickle](https://github.com/najoshi/sickle)) and IDBA-UD v1.0.9 were used for trimming and assembly, respectively (Peng et al., 2012). Scaf folds larger than 2 kbp were binned with Concoct v.0.4.0 (Alneberg et al., 2014) and Metabat v2.12.1 (Kang et al., 2019). DAS Tool v1.0 (Sieber et al., 2018) aggregated the scaffolds across the two binned sets and produced a non-redundant set of bins. We obtained metrics for this MAG using Biokit v0.0.4 (Steenwyk et al., 2022), and estimated its quality using CheckM v1.1.2 (Parks et al.,

2015). We ensured that the contig containing the predicted 16S rRNA gene was correctly binned, we performed a blastx (Johnson et al., 2008) analysis against NCBI’s non-redundant database (Pruitt,Tatusova,and Maglott, 2007) using default settings. *Gene content analysis* We predicted protein-coding, rRNA and tRNA genes using Prokka v1.14.6 (Seemann, 2014). We detected CRISPR spacers using CRISP RDetect v2.4 (Biswas et al., 2016). We estimated optimal growth tem perature, pH and salinity using GenomeSPOT v1.0.1 (Barnum et al.,
2024). A second estimate for optimal growth temperature was obtained
using OGT_Prediction v1.0.4 (Sauer and Wang, 2019). Orthogroups were detected using OrthoFinder v2.5.5 (Emms and Kelly, 2019) with default parameters.

*Metabolic predictions*

We predicted open reading frames in AB3033_2 TS with Prodigal v2.6.3 and we then annotated with KofamKOALA v1.3.0 (KEGG), Interproscan v5.61-93.0-64, dbCAN2 (CAZy), MEROPS v12.1, and HydDB (Aramaki et al., 2019; Jones et al., 2014; Zhang et al., 2018; Rawlings,Barrett,and Bateman, 2009; Søndergaard,Pedersen,and Greening, 2016; Table S1). We further filtered the dbCAN2 results only including those annotations by at least two tools (HMMER, dbCAN_sub, DIAMOND). Cellular localization of peptidases, CAZymes, and HydDB- identified [NiFe]-hydrogenases and formate hydrogenase were deter mined with Psortb v3.0.3 (Yu et al., 2010). We checked annotations across databases, which was supported by domain searches and blast when necessary.

*Reverse gyrase phylogenetic analyses*

We used the hidden Markov model (HMM) of TIGR1054 with the ‘hmmsearch’ program in the HMMER v3.4 software suite (Eddy, 2009) with the profile gathering cutoff parameter ‘–cut_ga’ to search for reverse gyrase sequences in the Asgardarchaeota and Thermoproteota species-representative predicted proteomes of GTDB r207. We per formed MSA using the same HMM using the ‘hmmalign’ program with ‘–trim’. We trimmed the MSA with trimAl v1.4.rev22 (Capella-Guti´er rez,Silla-Martínez,and Gabaldon, ´ 2009) using the ‘-gappyout’ algorithm. We used ModelFinder (Kalyaanamoorthy et al., 2017) as implemented in IQ-TREE v.2.1.3 (Minh et al., 2020) to identify the best model among all combinations of the LG, WAG, and Q.pfam model with the mixture model +C20 and rate heterogeneity variations (none, +R4 and +G4).

*Metagenome based environmental profiling*

We ran Sandpiper v0.2.2 (Woodcroft et al., 2024) with the query GTDB class “c__JAGGXT01” (added in GTDB r214) based on the only *Asgardarchaeia* representative: AUK159 (Accession number JAGGXT010000000). We excluded environments with less than three samples.

*Dataset selection for phylogenetic placement*

We retrieved all publicly available Asgardarchaeota genomes from NCBI (NCBI Resource Coordinators, 2018) up to October 6, 2022, and one recent large-scale study (Eme et al., 2023): a total of 493 Asgard archaeal genomes. To reduce redundancy, we dereplicated this initial set to include species-rank representatives, using dRep v3.2.2 (options “-sa 0.95 –S_algorithm fastANI conW 2 comp 40”) (Olm et al., 2017). We performed gene prediction for all genomes with Prokka v1.14.6 (Seemann, 2014). We downloaded species representative genomes and 16S rRNA gene sequences from the Genome Taxonomy Database (GTDB) r207 from the phylum-rank clades Thermoproteota (excluding members of the class Korarchaeia), Hadarchaeota, and Methanobacter iota_B (Table S1).

*Phylogenetic placement*

Since the partial 16S rRNA sequence of AB3033_2 TS belongs to a small contig of 5.4 kb, we ensured it was binned to the correct MAG by performing a blastx analysis of the contig, including this sequence against NCBI’s nr database. Accordingly, this search identified one gene with a single significant hit (e-value: 1e-28) to a protein belonging to the closely related Asgard archaeal MAG, AUK159. While no other 16S rRNA gene sequences have yet been recovered for Asgardarchaeia, the presence of a gene from a closely related MAG in this contig corroborates that it was appropriately binned, and therefore that the 16S rRNA gene from AB3033_2 TS can be considered a representative Asgardarchaeial sequence with a sequence identity ranging from 63-83 % to other Asgardarchaeota. Phylogenetic analyses using 16S rRNA sequences and GTDB marker genes indicate that AB3033_2 TS belongs to a distinct high-rank taxon. A Bayesian-inference 16S rRNA phylogeny of AB3033_2 TS and other archaea indicated no close homologous sequences, indicating a possible, yet unsupported close relatedness with an Asgardarchaeota sequence from the class Baldrarchaeia (Fig. 2A). *Taxonomic and nomenclatural considerations*

The Asgardarchaeia display a distinct phylogenetic placement within the Asgardarchaeota, and a consistent thermophilic lifestyle that differs from their relatives (e.g., Thorarchaeia are estimated to grow optimally between 20 and 50◦C) (Eme et al., 2023). Based on these factors, we define AB3033_2 TS as the nomenclatural type for a new genus and species: Asgardarchaeum abyssi *TS* gen. nov. spec. nov. (Table 1), and we propose Asgardarchaeum *TS* as the nomenclatural type for the family *Asgardarchaeaceae fam. nov., the order Asgardarchaeales ord. nov., the* class Asgardarchaeia class. nov., and the phylum Asgardarchaeota phyl. nov (Table 2).

**Conclusion**

In summary, our analysis of Asgardarchaeum abyssi sp. nov. high lights its significance as a novel archaeal species with distinctive genomic features and a phylogenetic placement within the phylum *Asgardarchaeota. The proposal of Asgardarchaeum gen. nov. as the type* genus for the associated higher ranks, including the class Asgardarchaeia and the phylum Asgardarchaeota, highlights the importance of SeqCode to facilitate validated taxonomic names for the description of unculti vated lineages, providing a critical service to the scientific community. The phylum Asgardarchaeota has been highly alluded to in the scientific literature (527 references in Google Scholar as of June 7th, 2024), and

its validation as a taxon name under SeqCode ensures continuity and clarity in scientific parlance.
