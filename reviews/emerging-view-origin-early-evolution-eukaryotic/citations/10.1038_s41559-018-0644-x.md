26 27 28 Examining the emergence of Life and its subsequent evolution has traditionally been carried out via 29 interpretation of the fossil record. However, this record, especially when looking at the earliest 30 scions of life, is minimal and made harder to interpret due to difficulties substantiating relationships 1,2 31 within the earliest branching lineages of the tree of life. Despite being problematic the fossil 32 record has been the main source of information informing the timeline of life’s evolution. We 33 attempt to shed light on this early period by presenting a molecular timescale utilising the ever- 34 growing collection of genetic data and explicitly incorporating uncertainty associated with fossil 1,3-5 35 sampling, ages, and interpretations. 36 37 Calibrations are a crucial component of divergence time estimation. Relative divergence times can 38 be inferred using alternative lines of evidence, e.g. horizontal gene transfers6. However, an absolute 39 timescale for evolutionary history can only be derived when calibrations are included in the 7,8 40 analyses. We derived a suite of calibrations, following best practice4 for the fundamental clades 41 within the Tree of Life, drawing on multiple lines of evidence, including physical fossils, biomarkers 42 and isotope geochemistry2. Two key calibrations, for the last universal common ancestor (LUCA) and 43 the oldest total-group eukaryotes, constrain the whole tree by setting a maximum on the root, while 9,10 44 also informing the timing of divergence of eukaryotes within Archaea. Putative records for life 11,12 14,15 45 extend back to the Eoarchaean, including microfossils, stromatolites13 and isotope data from 16-18 46 the ~3.8 Ga Isua Greenstone Belt (Greenland). However, these records have been contested. 47 Microfossils from the ~3.4 Ga Strelley Pool Formation, Australia, are the oldest conclusive evidence 48 to constrain the age of LUCA19. The fossils, many of which are arranged in chains of cells, have been 49 demonstrated through nanoscale imaging and Raman spectroscopy, to exhibit a complex 50 morphology with a central, usually hollow, lenticular body and a wall that is either smooth or in some cases reticulated; these features are beyond the scope of pseudofossils2. The Strelley Pool

52 Formation also contains other microfossils 20-22 , in association with both distinct ∂ 13 Corg and ∂ 13 Cinorg23 53 and pyrite indicative of sulphur metabolisms24, along with stromatolites that exhibit biological 54 structure25. Overall these data allow us to confidently use the Strelley Pool Biota as the oldest, 55 undisputable, record of life. For a maximum constraint on the age of LUCA we considered the 56 youngest event on Earth that life could not have survived. Conventionally, this is taken as the end of 57 the episode of late heavy bombardment, but modelling has shown that this would not have been 58 violent enough for planet sterilization26. However, the last formative stage of Earth’s formation, the 59 Moon-forming impact, melted and sterilised the planet. The oldest fossil remains that can be 60 ascribed to crown-Eukaryota is ~1.1 Ga Bangiomorpha pubescens 27,28 , which can be confidently 61 assigned to the red algal total group (Rhodophyta). Older fossil remains from the >1.561 Ga 62 Chitrakoot Formation have been tentatively interpreted as red algae29, however, current knowledge 63 of their morphology does not allow for an unequivocal assignment to crown-Archaeplastida. The 64 oldest fossil remains that can be ascribed with certainty to Eukaryota are acritarchs from the 65 >1.6191 Ga Changcheng Formation, North China30, discriminated from prokaryotes by their large size 66 (40-250μm) and complex wall structure including striations, longitudinal ruptures, and a trilaminar 67 organization. However, these structures do not indicate membership to any specific crown- 68 eukaryote clade, only allowing us to use these records to constrain minimally the timing of 69 divergence between the Eukaryota and their archaebacterial sister lineage, Asgardarchaeota 9,10,31 . As 70 there is no other evidence to maximally constrain the time of divergence between Eukaryota and 71 Asgardarchaeota, we used the same maximum placed on LUCA, i.e. the Moon-forming impact. These 72 key time constraints were combined with nine others (See SI) to calibrate a timescale of life 73 estimated from a dataset of 29 highly conserved, mainly ribosomal, universally distributed proteins 74 (see SI) using a relaxed molecular clock modelled in a Bayesian framework. 75 76 **Results**

77 Analytical choices can deeply affect molecular clock posterior age estimates32 and we explored a 78 range of prior probability distributions to model our fossil calibrations and estimate conservative 79 credibility intervals for our divergence times. Initially, we applied a hard maximum of 4.52 Ga (the 80 age of the Moon forming impact) to the root of our tree and used uniform age priors (reflecting 81 agnosticism about divergence timing relative to constraints) to the other fossil calibrations (Fig. 1a). 82 These analyses assumed an uncorrelated molecular clock model and produced the amino acid 83 substitution processes using optimal gene-specific substitution models. Subsequently, we explored 84 the impact of using calibration protocols based on non-uniform age priors. Firstly, we implemented a 85 truncated Cauchy distribution with the mode located halfway between the minimum and maximum 86 bounds, reflecting a prior view that true divergence times should fall between the calibration points 87 (Fig. 1b). We then skewed the Cauchy distribution such that the mode shifted towards the minimum 88 or the maximum constraint, reflecting prior views that the fossils used to calibrate the tree are 89 either very good (Fig. 1c) or very poor (Fig. 1d) proxies of the true divergence times. Our results 90 proved robust to the use of different calibration strategies, only identifying some variability in the 91 size of the recovered credibility intervals (Fig. 2a-c). 92 93 We explored the impact of different strategies for modelling both the molecular clock (Fig. 1e) and 94 the amino acid substitution process (Fig. 1f). Only minimal differences in posterior ages were found 95 between analyses using an uncorrelated or an autocorrelated clock (Fig. 2d). Consistently, Bayesian 96 cross-validation indicated that the two models do not differ significantly in their fit to the data (Cross 97 validation score = 0.7 +/- 2.96816 in favour of the uncorrelated clock). In contrast, using a single 98 substitution model across the 29 genes, or using an optimal set of gene-specific substitution models 99 inferred using PartitionFinder33 resulted in very different age estimates (Fig. 1f, 2e). Using a single 100 substitution model recovered larger credibility intervals (Fig. 2e) with a more homogeneous 101 distribution of branch lengths across the tree, and older divergence times (compare Fig. 1f and Fig. 1a-d). An Akaike Information Criterion (AIC) test indicated that the partitioned model provides a

103 significantly better fit to the data (AIC-score = 565.21 in favour of 29 gene-specific models), allowing 104 the rejection of the divergence times obtained with a single substitution model. As expected, 105 divergence times estimated from individual genes were much less precise, although posterior age 106 estimates overlap well (SI S4.1). This indicates that the genes comprising our dataset encode a 107 congruent signal and the timescale inferred from the combined analysis is not biased by single gene 108 outliers. Furthermore, their combination improves the precision of the clade age estimates (Fig. 2f-j), 109 which are clearly informed by the data (SI S4.2). We tested the effect of taxonomic sampling by 110 doubling the number of cyanobacteria and alphaproteobacteria in our dataset. We then explored 111 the effect of phylogenetic uncertainty by dating a tree compatible with Woese’s three domains 112 hypothesis34 and by dating all the 15 trees in the 95% credible set of trees from our phylogenetic 35 113 analysis (S4.3, S4.4). Further analyses that used co-estimation of tree and topology (S4.5) did not 114 reach convergence (S4.6), but recovered results that are congruent with those obtained from well 115 converged analyses (reported in S.4.4) where topology and time were sequentially inferred – see 116 S4.5 for a discussion. Overall, it appears that our results are robust to topological uncertainty and 117 the use of differential taxonomic sampling (SI S4.3 – S4.5). 118 119 It is not possible to discriminate between the competing calibration strategies which reflect different 120 interpretations of the fossil record, similarly, our model selection test indicated that the 121 autocorrelated and the independent-rates clock models fits the data equally well. Thus, in 122 establishing an accurate timescale of life we integrated over the uncertainties associated with 123 results from all these analyses (Fig. 3). The joint 95% credibility intervals reject a post late heavy 36 124 bombardment (~3900 Ma) emergence of LUCA (4519-4477 Ma). The crown clades of the primary 125 divisions of life, Archaebacteria and Eubacteria, emerged over a billion years after LUCA in the 126 Mesoarchaean-Neoarchaean. The earliest conclusive evidence of cellular life (Strelley Pool 127 Formation, Australia2) falls within the 95% credibility intervals for the ages of the last common

**Discussion** Methanogenesis is classically associated with Euryarchaeota. Our estimate for the age of crown- Euryarchaeota (2881-2425 Ma) is consistent with carbon isotope excursions indicating the presence of methanogens by 2 Ga37 but is substantially younger than the earliest possible evidence of biogenic methane in the geochemical record at ~3.5 Ga 38,39 . If the geochemical evidence is correct, our timescale implies that methanogenesis pre-dated the origin of Euryarchaeota. This hypothesis would be consistent with recent environmental genomic surveys indicating that other archaeal lineages may also be capable of methane metabolism40 or methanogenesis41, and that metabolisms using the Wood-Ljungdahl pathway to fix carbon minimally evolved in stem-archaebacteria 42(WAS6),43 and might have been a characteristic of LUCA 43-45 .

The GOE (~2.4 Ga) was perhaps the most significant episode in the Proterozoic46, fundamentally changing the chemistry of Earth’s atmosphere, oceans, and likely altering temperature. It has been causally associated with the evolution of cyanobacteria, as a consequence of their oxygen release 47,28 , and implicated as an extrinsic driver of eukaryotic evolution48. Our timescale indicates that crown-Cyanobacteria and crown-Eukaryota significantly postdate the GOE. Crown- Cyanobacteria diverged 1947–1023 Ma, precluding the possibility that oxygenic photosynthesis emerged in the cyanobacterial crown ancestor. However, the cyanobacteria separated from other eubacterial lineages (Fig. 3), including the non-photosynthetic sister group of the Cyanobacteira (Melanibacteria; SI 4.3) in the Archaean, prior to the GOE, consistent with the view that oxygenic photosynthesis evolved along the cyanobacterial stem49 and compatible with a causal role of the Total group Cyanobacteria in the GOE.

154 Crown-Eukaryota diverged considerably after both the Eukaryota-Asgardarchaeota split and the 155 GOE, in the middle Proterozoic (1842-1210 Ma). Our study strongly rejects the idea that eukaryotes 156 might be as old, or older than prokaryotes50, and agrees with a number of other studies that dates 51-53 157 the Last Eukaryote Common Ancestor (LECA) to the Proterozoic (~1866-1679 Ma). Within 158 eukaryotes, the main extant clades emerged by the middle Proterozoic, including Opisthokonta 159 (~1707-1125 Ma), Archaeplastida (~1667-1118 Ma) and SAR (~1645-1115 Ma). The symbiotic origin 160 of the plastid occurred among stem archaeplastids (~1774-1118 Ma), and our 95% credibility interval 28,50,53 161 for the origin of the plastid overlap with the results of other recent studies. The relatively long 162 stem-lineage subtending LECA is intriguing. It is found using both uncorrelated and autocorrelated 163 clock models (Fig. 1e, 2d), and disappears only if a poorly-fitting, single substitution model is used 164 (Fig. 1f, 2e), suggesting that it is not a modelling artefact. Analyses excluding the hitherto unknown 9,31 165 immediate living relatives of Eukaryota, Asgardarchaeota, had no significant impact on the span 166 of the eukaryote stem-lineage, suggesting that its length is robust to taxon sampling (SI 4.7). 167 168 Our timescale for eukaryogenesis rejects the hypothesis of an inextricable link between the GOE and 169 the origin of eukaryotes48. Competing hypothesis for eukaryogenesis hinge on the early versus late 55,56-59 170 acquisition of mitochondria relative to other key eukaryote characters. Absolute divergence 171 times cannot discriminate between theses hypotheses. However, as the only proposed evidence in 172 support of the mitochondria late57 hypothesis have been shown to be artifactual58, the similar age 173 estimates for Alphaproteobacteria and LECA, at this stage are most conservatively interpreted as 174 indicating that the process of mitochondrial symbiosis drove a rapid process of eukaryogenesis. This 175 process involved large transfer of genes from the genome of the alphaproteobacterial symbiont to 59,60 55,61 176 that of the archaeal host, as predicated on metabolism. 177 178 The search for the earliest fossil evidence of life on Earth has created more heat than light. Though the fossil record remains integral to establishing a timescale for the Tree of Life, it is not sufficient in

180 and of itself. Proteomes 190 were downloaded from GenBank62 and putative orthologs were identified using BLAST63. The top 191 hits were compiled and aligned into gene specific files in MUSCLE64 and trimmed to remove poorly 192 aligned sites using Trimal65. To minimise the possible inclusion of paralogs and laterally transferred 193 genes, we generated gene trees (under CAT-GTR+G) in PhyloBayes66 and excluded sequences when 194 the tree topology suggested that they might have been paralogs. The sequences were then 195 concatenated into a supermatrix using FASconCAT67, and phylogenetic analyses were performed 196 using PhyloBayes66. The superalignment was initially analysed under both GTR+G and CAT-GTR+G68. 197 RogueNaRok69 was used to identify rogue taxa, and analyses were repeated (under both GTR+G and 198 CAT-GTR+G) after having excluded unstable taxa. One final analysis was performed that included 199 only the eukaryotic sequences in our dataset (under CAT-GTR+G). For all Phylobayes analyses 200 convergence was tested in Phylobayes using BPCOMP and TRACECOMP. 201 202 **Calibrations** 203 In total we used 11 calibrations spread throughout the tree but mainly found within the Eukaryotes 204 as this group has the best fossil record. Calibration choice was carried out conservatively using

205 coherent criteria70. Full details of each calibration used can be found in the supplementary 206 information. 207 208 **MCMCTree analysis** 209 For our clock analyses, we used a constraint tree based on our CAT-GTR+G and GTR+G trees (Fig. 210 S3.2, S3.3 and S4) – see results of phylogenetic analyses in SI for details. The complete phylogeny 211 was rooted to separate the Bacteria from the other lineages (i.e. Archaea and Eukayota). To select 71 212 the amino acid model to be used in our molecular clock analyses we used PartitionFinder v1.1.1. 213 Divergence time estimation was carried out using the approximate likelihood calculation in 72 214 MCMCTree v4.9. We set 4 different calibration density distributions, uniform, skewed towards the 215 minimum, skewed towards the maximum and midway between these two dates. For this we used 216 the Uniform and Cauchy models within MCMCTree, which can be set to place the maximum 217 probability of the node falling in a certain space between the calibrations, the values for these were 218 first produced using MCMCTreeR73 code in R74. We investigated two strategies to model amino acid 219 sequence evolution: a single WAG+G model or the optimal partitioned model suggested by 220 PartitionFinder. The latter used 29 gene-specific models (28 LG+G and one WAG+G). The Akaike 221 Information Criterion was used to test whether using a single model or a partitioned model provided 222 a better fit to the data. Rate variation across lineages was modelled using both an autocorrelated 223 and an uncorrelated clock model. Bayesian cross-validation was used to test whether one of the two 224 considered, relaxed molecular clock models best fitted the data (implemented in Phylobayes). 225 226 In all our molecular clock analyses a soft tail of 2.5% was applied to the upper calibration bound and 227 a hard minimum. This apart from the root node to which a hard maximum was applied, and the 228 nodes calibrated using Bangiomorpha75 to which a soft minimum tail of 2.5% was applied.
