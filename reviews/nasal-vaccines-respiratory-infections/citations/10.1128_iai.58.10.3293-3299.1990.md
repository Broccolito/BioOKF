Pneumococcal Surface Protein A (PspA) Is Serologically Highly Variable and Is Expressed by All Clinically Important Capsular Serotypes of Streptococcus pneumoniae

MARILYN J. CRAIN,12* W. DOUGLAS WALTMAN II,2t J. SCOTT TURNER,' JANET YOTHER,2 DEBORAH F. TALKINGTON, t LARRY S. McDANIEL,2 BARRY M. GRAY,"2 AND DAVID E. BRILES12,3 Departments ofPediatrics,' Microbiology,2 and Comparative Medicine,3 University ofAlabama at Birmingham, Birmingham, Alabama 35294

Received 28 February 1990/Accepted 18 July 1990

Pneumococcal surface protein A (PspA) has been shown previously to elicit antibodies protective against pneumococcal infection and to be necessary for full pneumococcal virulence in mice. The protein was originally defined by the two mouse monoclonal antibodies Xi64 and Xi126, which together recognized PspA on 14% of pneumococcal isolates. Some PspA molecules reacted with both antibodies, but most reacted with only one or the other. In the present study we demonstrated that PspA is produced by all pneumococci, confirming our hypothesis that there are variants of PspA which are not detected by Xi64 and Xi126. We produced a rabbit antiserum and five additional monoclonal antibodies specific for PspA for these studies. The rabbit antiserum reacted with each of 95 pneumococcal isolates tested, comprising 16 capsular serotypes. One or more of the seven monoclonal anti-PspA antibodies reacted with 95% (53 of 57) of pneumococcal isolates tested. The specificity of the monoclonal and polyclonal antibodies to PspA was confirmed in two ways: (i) by detection of molecules on wild-type pneumococci that are identical in molecular weight to those detected in Western blots (immunoblots) with Xi64 and Xi126 and (ii) by the use of mutants of Streptococcus pneumoniae that fail to produce PspA or that produce a truncated form of PspA. By using the seven monoclonal antibodies, we observed 31 PspA types among the 57 isolates. When the 53 strains reactive with the monoclonal antibodies were analyzed by capsular type as well as by serologic type and molecular weight of PspA, we observed 50 different clonotypes of pneumococci.

Pneumococcal infections pose a threat to the health ofthe very young, the very old, and immunocompromised individ- uals of all ages. The currently available pneumococcal vaccine, composed of purified capsular polysaccharides, does not offer protection to the children at greatest risk, since infants less than 2 years of age do not normally mount an adequate antibody response to polysaccharide antigens (6, 10). One potential solution to this problem would involve the identification of a protein antigen on the surface of pneumococci capable ofeliciting protective antibodies. Such an antigen could then be used as a vaccine itselfor could be conjugated with pneumococcal polysaccharides. Pneumococcal surface protein A (PspA) is a potential candidate for such an antigen. McDaniel et al. demonstrated that two monoclonal antibodies to PspA, Xi64 and Xi126, could protect mice from fatal infection with a type 2 and a type 3 strain (11-13). The ability ofPspA to elicit protection was demonstrated in experiments in which Xid mice were injected with nonencapsulated PspA+ pneumococci and their isogenic PspA- mutants. PspA+, but not PspA-, pneumococci elicited a response protective against other- wise fatal doses of pneumococci (13). The antigenic deter- minant of PspA recognized by Xi126 is pepsin and trypsin sensitive (11, 12). PspA has also been shown to be polymor- phic, as evidenced by wide variations among strains in the

In a previous study, only 14% of 499 pneumococcal isolates reacted with one or both of the monoclonal antibod- ies Xi64 and Xi126 (18). However, because of the antigenic variation in PspA (13, 18), it seemed likely that many of the strains failing to react with Xi64 or Xi126 might express PspA molecules antigenically distinct from those detected by these antibodies. We have used two approaches to test this hypothesis. First, we produced a polyclonal rabbit antiserum which is capable of detecting PspA. Second, we have identified five new monoclonal antibodies that recognize different epitopes ofPspA. Pneumococcal strains chosen for these studies included 16 capsular serotypes of clinical importance (7, 9). The rabbit antiserum detected PspA on each of 95 strains tested. By using the expanded panel of monoclonal antibodies (five new and the two previously described), we could detect PspA on 95% of the pneumo- coccal isolates tested. These data provide strong evidence that PspA is broadly expressed among clinically important serotypes of pneumococci.

Specificity of antibodies for PspA. To determine whether PspA is present on all pneumococcal strains, we prepared a rabbit antiserum and five additional monoclonal antibodies reactive with PspA. One pneumococcal strain in our collec- tion, the encapsulated type 3 strain WU2, reacted with each of the five new monoclonal antibodies, as well as the previously described monoclonal antibodies Xi64 and Xi126 (Fig. 1; data for Xi64 not shown). All of the monoclonal antibodies recognized a 91.5-kilodalton (kDa) band on WU2. To confirm the specificity ofthe monoclonal antibodies for PspA, the five new antibodies, in addition to Xi126, were tested for their reactivity with JY1123, a mutant ofWU2 that produces a truncated form of PspA (detected by Xi64 and Xi126) that is released into the medium. The cell lysate of this mutant contains neither the full-length nor the truncated PspA (J. Yother and D. E. Briles, unpublished data), and none of the monoclonal antibodies reacted with components of the mutant bacterial lysate. Each monoclonal antibody, however, detected the 46-kDa truncated PspA released into the medium by strain JY1123 (Fig. 1). None of the seven antibodies reacted with the culture medium supernatant of WU2 unless the cells were harvested during stationary phase, when PspA was present as result of autolysis (data not shown). Figure 1 also illustrates that the rabbit antiserum is reac- tive with PspA, since it detects the 91.5-kDa PspA band of strain WU2. The panel of seven monoclonal antibodies (Xi64, Xi126, and the five new monoclonal antibodies) was used to study PspA in 57 strains ofpneumococci, as shown in Table 2. The frequency of strains reactive with each individual antibody was as follows: Xi64, 19%; Xi126, 35%; SR4W4, 49%; 2A4, 31%; 1A4, 39%; 7D2, 25%; and XiR278, 42%. Only 4 of the 57 strains failed to react with any of the monoclonal anti- bodies specific for PspA (the type 6 strains DBL1, BG25-9, BG5-8A, and the type 3 strain BG6380). PAO was the PspA type designation for strains which failed to react with any ofthe monoclonal antibodies. Since no two antibodies gave the same pattern of reactivity, each ap- peared to detect a different epitope ofPspA. PspA types PAl to PA30 exhibited relatively close relationships to one an- other, as can be seen in Fig. 3A. All of the PspA types formed a contiguous group in which each reactivity pattern differed by the gain or loss ofonly a single specificity from at least one other type in the group. By using the method of identical pairs (4), it was estimated that there are likely to be 58 different serologic types of PspA. There was only a 5% chance that the number of PspA types exceeded 150. Each of the seven capsular serogroups or types, when more than one strain was tested, exhibited more than one PspA type, except for the three capsular serogroup 9 strains included in these studies. In the case of the capsular type 3 (Fig. 3B) and serogroup 6 strains (data not shown), the PspA types were relatively unrelated to one another. In addition to the serologic variability described above, PspA is known to exhibit variability in molecular weight (12, 17). This size variation can be seen for nine strains in Fig. 4, where the rabbit antiserum has been used to develop a Western blot of electrophoresed pneumococcal lysates. DISCUSSION

The data presented here show that PspA can be detected

on 100% of pneumococcal isolates with a polyclonal antise-

rum and on 95% of the isolates with a panel of seven monoclonal antibodies. In an earlier study, the two mono-

clonal antibodies then available were reported to react with

only 14% of strains. A 550-base-pair DNA probe, then

believed (13) and subsequently proven (J. Yother and D. E.

Briles, unpublished data) to be part of the pspA structural

gene, was observed to react with only 21% of 102 pneumo- coccal strains. Together, the probe and monoclonal antibod-

ies detected 27% of strains (13). PspA was judged to be

variable in structure, since the probe and each of the two

antibodies reacted with different overlapping subsets of

strains.

At the time, it was assumed that the failure of the

antibodies and probe to react with all strains may have been

the result of genetic variation in the portions of PspA they

detected, rather than an absence of PspA from some virulent

strains. This assumption appears to be borne out by the

present study, which reveals serologic detection of PspA on 100% of isolates and very extensive serologic (and size)

variability. Although it may seem surprising that a DNA probe 550

base pairs in length did not recognize all variants of PspA, it

should be remembered that a 550-base-pair probe would be

expected to encode less than one-fourth of the pspA struc-

The new monoclonal antibodies reported in this study greatly increase our ability to characterize PspA. The fact that each monoclonal antibody recognizes different subsets of strains provides evidence that each recognizes a different epitope of PspA. This provides a means of serologically typing PspA. We observed 31 different patterns of reactivity in the PspAs produced by the 57 isolates examined. By applying a numerical analysis to these data (4), we estimated that there are likely to be 58 PspA types detectable with our seven monoclonal antibodies in the wild population from which our samples were drawn. When the 53 strains reactive with the monoclonal antibodies to PspA were analyzed by capsular serogroup or type, PspA type, and the molecular weight of PspA, 50 different phenotypes of pneumococci were observed. Each phenotype contained only one mem- ber, with the exception of the four serogroup 6A strains that exhibited PspA type 19, with a molecular mass of 92.5 kDa. These four 6A strains may be members of a single clone of pneumococci. Of 19 serogroup 6 strains reactive with the monoclonal antibodies, 16 different phenotypes were ob- served. These findings emphasize the extreme diversity of pneumococci. In previous studies we reported that PspA elicits protec- tive antibodies in mice (5, 11, 13). It has frequently been observed that bacterial antigens capable of eliciting protec- tion exhibit a high degree of serologic variation, as is the case for M protein of the group A streptococci (8), Hae- mophilus influenzae outer membrane proteins (14, 15), and the pneumococcal capsule (1, 2). This variation presumably is an evolutionary attempt by the pathogen to escape the effects of protective antibodies elicited by previous infec- tion. The high degree of serologic variation we have ob- served in PspA is suggestive that PspA may elicit protective immunity in humans, its natural hosts. Of the 31 protein types identified, none differed by more than a single specificity with respect to its nearest neighbor. Thus, PspAs show evidence ofa common ancestor. It was of interest, however, that the distribution of PspA types within capsular types showed no evidence for a clustering ofclosely related PspAs in the capsular type 3 or type 6 strains. This observation, together with the observation that many PspA types are expressed by strains of several capsular serotypes, strongly suggests that there is some genetic exchange of PspA and/or capsular-type genes in the wild. The rate of exchange is apparently not rapid, however, since patients whose pneumococci exhibit the same PspA, as characterized by molecular weight and reactivity with Xi64, Xi126, and SR4W4, have been monitored over a period of 12 to 18 months (17; W. D. Waltman II, B. M. Gray, C. Svanborg, R. Facklam, and D. E. Briles, submitted for publication). It has also been observed that the frequency of different PspA types among type 9N and 9V strains varies over decades rather than months or years (D. W. Waltman II et al., submitted). This apparent genetic stability of PspA over time may be important to its potential use as a vaccine. In this regard, it is also encouraging that there exists considerable cross- reactivity between PspA molecules. In fact, the PspA of strain WU2 (PspA type 1), which is detected by all seven of the monoclonal antibodies, shares antigenic epitopes cross- reactive with 53 of 57 strains examined. Furthermore, the rabbit antiserum, raised against a single PspA, detected PspA on each of 95 pneumococcal isolates. Future studies will be required to determine whether epitopes of PspA which elicit protection also exhibit broad cross-reactivities.
