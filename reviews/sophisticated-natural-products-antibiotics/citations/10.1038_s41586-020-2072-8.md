## Abstract

*Mycobacterium tuberculosis* (Mtb) is an obligate human pathogen and the causative agent of tuberculosis[1](#ref-CR1 "Gopinath, K. et al. A vitamin B12 transporter in Mycobacterium tuberculosis. Open Biol. 3, 120175 (2013)."),[2](#ref-CR2 "Gopinath, K., Moosa, A., Mizrahi, V. & Warner, D. F. Vitamin B12 metabolism in Mycobacterium tuberculosis. Future Microbiol. 8, 1405–1418 (2013)."),[3](/articles/s41586-020-2072-8#ref-CR3 "Jankute, M., Cox, J. A. G., Harrison, J. & Besra, G. S. Assembly of the mycobacterial cell wall. Annu. Rev. Microbiol. 69, 405–423 (2015)."). Although Mtb can synthesize vitamin B12 (cobalamin) de novo, uptake of cobalamin has been linked to pathogenesis of tuberculosis[2](/articles/s41586-020-2072-8#ref-CR2 "Gopinath, K., Moosa, A., Mizrahi, V. & Warner, D. F. Vitamin B12 metabolism in Mycobacterium tuberculosis. Future Microbiol. 8, 1405–1418 (2013)."). Mtb does not encode any characterized cobalamin transporter[4](#ref-CR4 "Korkhov, V. M., Mireku, S. A. & Locher, K. P. Structure of AMP-PNP-bound vitamin B12 transporter BtuCD-F. Nature 490, 367–372 (2012)."),[5](#ref-CR5 "Rempel, S., Colucci, E., de Gier, J. W., Guskov, A. & Slotboom, D. J. Cysteine-mediated decyanation of vitamin B12 by the predicted membrane transporter BtuM. Nat. Commun. 9, 3038 (2018)."),[6](/articles/s41586-020-2072-8#ref-CR6 "Santos, J. A. et al. Functional and structural characterization of an ECF-type ABC transporter for vitamin B12. eLife 7, e35828 (2018)."); however, the gene *rv1819c* was found to be essential for uptake of cobalamin[1](/articles/s41586-020-2072-8#ref-CR1 "Gopinath, K. et al. A vitamin B12 transporter in Mycobacterium tuberculosis. Open Biol. 3, 120175 (2013)."). This result is difficult to reconcile with the original annotation of Rv1819c as a protein implicated in the transport of antimicrobial peptides such as bleomycin[7](/articles/s41586-020-2072-8#ref-CR7 "Domenech, P., Kobayashi, H., LeVier, K., Walker, G. C. & Barry, C. E. III. BacA, an ABC transporter involved in maintenance of chronic murine infections with Mycobacterium tuberculosis. J. Bacteriol. 191, 477–485 (2009)."). In addition, uptake of cobalamin seems inconsistent with the amino acid sequence, which suggests that Rv1819c has a bacterial ATP-binding cassette (ABC)-exporter fold[1](/articles/s41586-020-2072-8#ref-CR1 "Gopinath, K. et al. A vitamin B12 transporter in Mycobacterium tuberculosis. Open Biol. 3, 120175 (2013)."). Here, we present structures of Rv1819c, which reveal that the protein indeed contains the ABC-exporter fold, as well as a large water-filled cavity of about 7,700 Å3, which enables the protein to transport the unrelated hydrophilic compounds bleomycin and cobalamin. On the basis of these structures, we propose that Rv1819c is a multi-solute transporter for hydrophilic molecules, analogous to the multidrug exporters of the ABC transporter family, which pump out structurally diverse hydrophobic compounds from cells[8](#ref-CR8 "Shukla, S., Abel, B., Chufan, E. E. & Ambudkar, S. V. Effects of a detergent micelle environment on P-glycoprotein (ABCB1)–ligand interactions. J. Biol. Chem. 292, 7066–7076 (2017)."),[9](#ref-CR9 "Aller, S. G. et al. Structure of P-glycoprotein reveals a molecular basis for poly-specific drug binding. Science 323, 1718–1722 (2009)."),[10](#ref-CR10 "Kim, Y. & Chen, J. Molecular structure of human P-glycoprotein in the ATP-bound, outward-facing conformation. Science 359, 915–919 (2018)."),[11](/articles/s41586-020-2072-8#ref-CR11 "Alam, A., Kowal, J., Broude, E., Roninson, I. & Locher, K. P. Structural insight into substrate and inhibitor discrimination by human P-glycoprotein. Science 363, 753–756 (2019).").
