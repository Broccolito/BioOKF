## In Vivo and In Vitro Effects of a ClpP-Activating Antibiotic against Vancomycin-Resistant Enterococci

### Autumn Brown Gandt,aElizabeth C. Griffith,bIda M. Lister,aLisa L. Billings,aAngel Han,aRajendra Tangallapally,bYing Zhao,b

### Aman P. Singh,bRichard E. Lee,bMichael D. LaFleura

a Arietis Pharma, Boston, Massachusetts, USA b Department of Chemical Biology and Therapeutics, St. Jude Children's Research Hospital, Memphis, Tennessee, USA

**ABSTRACT** Antibiotics with novel bactericidal mechanisms of action are urgently needed. The antibiotic acyldepsipeptide 4 (ADEP4) activates the ClpP protease and causes cells to self-digest. The effects of ADEP4 and ClpP activation have not been char- acterized sufficiently for the enterococci, which are important pathogens known for high levels of acquired and intrinsic antibiotic resistance. In the present study, ADEP4 was found to be potently active against both Enterococcus faecalis and Enterococcus faecium, with MIC90s of 0.016 g/ml and 0.031 g/ml, respectively. ClpP purified from E. faecium was found to bind ADEP4 in a surface plasmon resonance analysis, and ClpP activation by ADEP4 was demonstrated biochemically with a-casein digestion assay. In addition,

*E. faecium ClpP was crystallized in the presence of ADEP4, revealing ADEP4 binding to* ClpP in the activated state. These results confirm that the anti-enterococcal activity of ADEP4 occurs through ClpP activation. In killing curve assays, ADEP4 was found to be bactericidal against stationary-phase vancomycin-resistant E. faecalis (VRE) strain V583, and resistance development was prevented when ADEP4 was combined with multiple classes of approved antibiotics. ADEP4 in combination with partnering antibiotics also eradicated mature VRE biofilms within 72 h of treatment. Biofilm killing with ADEP4 anti- biotic combinations was superior to that with the clinically used combinations ampicillin-gentamicin and ampicillin-daptomycin. In a murine peritoneal septicemia model, ADEP4 alone was as effective as ampicillin. ADEP4 coadministered with ampicillin was significantly more effective than either drug alone. These data suggest that ClpP- activating antibiotics may be useful for treating enterococcal infections. **KEYWORDS** ADEP, ClpP, Enterococcus, VRE he genus Enterococcus contains opportunistic nosocomial pathogens which cause **T**bacteremia, endocarditis, and surgical site and urinary tract infections. The CDC recognizes vancomycin-resistant enterococci (VRE) as a serious public health threat. Unlike most Gram-positive pathogens, which are susceptible to cell wall-active antibi- otics such as ampicillin (AMP) or vancomycin, enterococci do not die when exposed to these drugs; rather, their growth is inhibited (1–3). The enterococci are also relatively impermeable to aminoglycosides, and the concentrations required for killing are higher than what can be achieved clinically in patients (2, 4). Bactericidal activity against even susceptible strains of enterococci requires a combination of a cell wall-active agent and an aminoglycoside, usually ampicillin or penicillin with gentamicin or streptomycin (5). Using this synergistic combination, killing occurs as the penetration of the aminogly- coside is increased in the presence of the cell wall-active antibiotic due to increased cell permeability (6). However, for the combination to be effective, the pathogen must be susceptible to the-lactam and lack high-level resistance to the aminoglycoside, and this is increasingly rare (2, 5, 7, 8). Bactericidal agents are preferred for serious infections, such as endocarditis (9). Both clinical studies and animal models suggest that bactericidal therapies correlate with better therapeutic outcomes than those obtained with bacteriostatic agents for difficult-to-treat indications (12). In addition to their intrinsic tolerance to some antibiotic classes, pathogenic Entero- *coccus strains readily acquire resistance determinants through horizontal gene transfer,* including genes that confer resistance to vancomycin, erythromycin, chloramphenicol, -lactams, and tetracycline as well as high-level resistance to aminoglycosides (2, 13). Nonsusceptible phenotypes have also been reported for more recently approved antibiotics, including daptomycin (DAP), linezolid (LZD), tigecycline (TGC), and orita- vancin (ORI) (5, 14, 15). The rapid spread of resistance as well as the benefits of bactericidal therapy point to the need for an antibiotic with a novel mechanism of action and bactericidal activity. Acyldepsipeptides (ADEPs) are natural product antimicrobials originally isolated from Streptomyces hawaiiensis (16). ADEPs bind and activate the ClpP protease, causing dysregulated proteolysis and cell death (17, 18). ADEPs are effective against Gram- positive bacteria, including the medically important species Staphylococcus aureus, *Streptococcus pneumoniae, and Enterococcus faecalis (17). While their novel mechanism* of action suggests that ADEPs may be active against multidrug-resistant (MDR) Gram- positive species, limited data are available in this regard. Previously, a potent ADEP analog, ADEP4, was found to offer protection in a murine model of lethal systemic infection with E. faecalis (17). Recently, a vancomycin-resistant MDR strain of Entero- *coccus faecium with a constitutively active stringent response was isolated from an* immunocompromised patient. Although the isolate remained susceptible to both linezolid and daptomycin during normal growth, biofilm-mediated tolerance to these agents was demonstrated, and only ADEP4 was effective against biofilms (19). The goal of this study was to evaluate the antimicrobial activity of ADEP4 as a model ClpP activator against E. faecalis and E. faecium. A panel of 73 Enterococcus isolates was tested in broth microdilution MIC assays. E. faecium ClpP was expressed and purified, and its ability to be activated by ADEP4 was confirmed biochemically. Time-dependent killing of stationary-phase cultures and biofilms was performed to test the effects of ADEP4 and combinations of approved drugs on VRE viability. A murine model of peritoneal septicemia was also used to evaluate the effects of ADEP4 and ampicillin, a standard of care, against VRE.

The collection con-* sisted of commensal, clinical, laboratory, and agricultural isolates, including 18 VRE and 13 MDR strains (see Table S1 in the supplemental material). On average, a 4-log10reduction in bacterial burden compared to that of the vehicle control group was detected for combinations of ADEP4 and AMP. **DISCUSSION** The ClpP-activating antibiotic ADEP4 was found to inhibit the growth of a panel of
*E. faecalis and E. faecium strains, including vancomycin- and multidrug-resistant clinical* isolates. No preexisting resistance was detected among the isolates which were tested, as may be expected for a compound with a novel mechanism of action. In stationary- phase killing curve experiments, ADEP4 was found to be rapidly bactericidal against E. *faecalis strain V583. Killing was more pronounced than that by other antibiotics or even* combinations of these agents, such as daptomycin plus ampicillin. The prominent and unique killing of high-density, stationary-phase cells is likely a consequence of ClpP activation. This mechanism of action contrasts with those of traditional antibiotics, which inhibit processes that are required during growth or division but are less active during stationary phase, such as DNA replication, protein synthesis, and cell wall synthesis (30). Inoculum effects, where antibiotics are less effective against bacteria at high densities, have been reported for many antibiotics (31). Inoculum effects or low target activity may contribute to the lack of efficacy for traditional antibiotics for some infections and may be less applicable to ClpP-activating antibiotics, such as ADEP4. The ability of enterococcal ClpP to bind to and be activated by ADEP4 was confirmed by three independent biochemical or biophysical assays. SPR experiments demonstrated the high affinity (K*D*14 nM) and long residence time ( 357 s) of ADEP4-EfClpP binding. We speculate that the actual binding affinity of ADEP4 for EfClpP may be higher (subnanomolar) than our reported K*D*. Data analysis in this study was conser- vative given the surface heterogeneity originating from immobilization conditions and the complex multimeric binding mode for binding of ADEP4 to EfClpP. Nonetheless, the robust association rate (k*a*) indicated fast kinetics of ADEP4 binding to EfClpP, an observation corroborated by in vitro enzymatic assays in which ADEP4-activated ClpP exhibited rapid-casein degradation kinetics.-Casein degradation assays confirmed that binding of ADEP4 to EfClpP results in a proteolytic active state. Structural data further supported the hypothesis that anti-enterococcal activity of ADEP4 occurs through ClpP activation. ADEP4-EfClpP binding results in N-terminal pore opening and access to the serine catalytic triad in a manner that is independent of chaperones. This finding is consistent with structural studies of ClpP acyldepsipeptide activation in multiple bacterial species (32). A more detailed examination of the *EfClpP-ADEP4 binding site revealed a high degree of homology between the ADEP* binding pockets of multiple bacterial species. The hydrophobic alkyl chain binding pocket of EfClpP is made up of Leu 49, Ala 53, Phe 50, Leu 24, Arg 23, Asp 27, and Ile 29 (Fig. 4B, yellow residues). These residues show a high degree of homology to ADEP-bound ClpP proteins of other species represented in the Protein Data Bank (see
Fig. S1 in the supplemental material). The difluorophenyl group occupies a hydropho-
 bic pocket at the base of the larger ADEP4 binding pocket, made up of Leu 49, Phe 83, Thr 80, Leu 115, Met 93, Ile 91, Ile 45, and Tyr 63 (Fig. 4B, green residues). There is also a high degree of sequence homology in this area between structurally represented species. Some variability is present at Ile 45 (which is a Val residue in nonenterococcal species) and Met 93. The hydrophobic character at these positions remains consistent (Fig. S1). A series of critical hydrogen bonding interactions occurs between ADEP4 and Tyr 63, anchoring the ligand into the binding pocket (Fig. 4B, black dashed lines). These interactions have been observed in all of the available structural data, and this residue is conserved between species. The remainder of the lactone core is supported by the Gln 89, Phe 113, Met 190, and Tyr 61 residues, which exhibit less homology than that

for other structurally represented species (Fig. 4B, pink residues). This suggests that variation within this portion of the binding pocket might have less of an impact on ADEP4 binding (Fig. S1). E. faecium TX16 and E. faecalis V583 ClpP proteins share 90% sequence identity. Within the active site, these two species share complete homology, with the exception of E. faecium Met 93, which is a Leu residue in E. faecalis. This variation maintains the hydrophobic character of the position. It should be noted that this residue is not well conserved between species; it shows the highest degree of variability within the binding pockets of ClpP sequences with reported structural data for the protein in complex with an acyldepsipeptide. The high degree of homology within the ADEP4 binding pocket demonstrated in this study supports the hypothesis that ADEP4 binding allows for a broad spectrum of activity within Gram-positive bacterial species. In the time-dependent killing experiments, development of resistance to ADEP4 was detected within 72 h, similar to data reported for S. aureus (33). However, bactericidal activity was retained and resistance development was not detected when ADEP4 was combined with other antibiotics. Resistance to ADEP4 monotherapy was also detected in the biofilm killing studies, as there was a stepwise increase in colony counts from 24 to 72 h. This resistance takeover was not detected in biofilm experiments when ADEP4 was combined with antibiotics of other distinct classes, such as DAP, LZD, or AMP. The combination of ADEP4 and the glycopeptide ORI caused a 4-log10reduction in survival against a planktonic stationary-phase culture, but biofilm killing was less pronounced. These data suggest that a biofilm-specific mechanism of resistance or antibiotic antag- onism may be responsible. The wide range of potential partnering antibiotics suggests that the second antibiotic acts to prevent the outgrowth of ADEP4-resistant ClpP mutants. Given the novel mechanism and advantage of ClpP-activating antibiotics in killing stationary-phase and biofilm cells, development of combined therapeutics ap- pears to be justified. This approach fits with the call to action to assess whether single-target antibacterials can be used in combination to overcome resistance (34). Combination antimicrobial therapy, a mainstay for tuberculosis, is becoming increas- ingly common for other difficult-to-treat infections (35). A variety of virulence defects have been reported for S. aureus clpP mutants. In vitro, *clpP phenotypes include slow growth (36), growth defects under anaerobic conditions* (36), temperature sensitivity (37, 38), increased sensitivity to hydrogen peroxide (38), increased autolysis (36), decreased hemolysin production (38), lower MICs of protein synthesis inhibitors (39), and increased susceptibility to killing by antibiotics (33). ClpP was also found to be required for virulence in a skin abscess model (38), and decreased bacterial burdens were found in kidney, liver, and other tissues when clpP null mutants were examined in murine models of septicemia (40) and implanted catheter infection (41). A review of the literature suggests that a detailed examination of the role of ClpP in enterococcal virulence has not been performed. In the present study, ADEP4 monotherapy caused a 2-log10decrease in kidney burden in the peritoneal septicemia model, and nonresponders, which may be indicative of resistance development, were not detected. However, these studies were limited in the number of animals and duration of antibiotic exposure. Additional studies examining multiple days of dosing may suggest whether resistance to a ClpP activator monotherapy or combinations of traditional antibiotics with a ClpP activator is likely to occur in the clinic. ADEP4 had activity similar to that of the clinical standard of care, ampicillin, in the acute murine peritonitis model despite having greater activity in the stationary-phase killing curve studies. The advantage of ClpP-activating antibiotics over traditional antibiotics may be more evident in chronic, recurrent, biofilm, complicated, or high-burden bacterial infections, especially where cells may be in a dormant or nongrowing state. In summary, the ClpP activator ADEP4 was highly potent against a panel of Entero- *coccus strains, including 18 VRE and 13 multidrug-resistant strains. ADEP4 was dem-* onstrated to directly bind and activate *EfClpP.* When ADEP4 was combined with antibiotics from distinct mechanistic classes, improved killing was detected, without resistance development. ADEP4 monotherapy was found to decrease V583 bacterial

burdens in the kidneys, and a combination with AMP was more effective than each antibiotic alone. Thus, ClpP-activating antibiotics have the potential to be combined with multiple distinct classes of antibiotics, including the-lactams. These data suggest that ClpP-activating antibiotics may be useful for treating VRE infections, and additional studies are warranted.

### MATERIALS AND METHODS

**Bacterial strains and growth conditions. Strains were routinely grown with brain heart infusion** (BHI) broth or agar at 37°C. Liquid cultures were grown in shaking incubators at 180 rpm. E. faecalis strain V583 was used for the in vitro stationary-phase and biofilm killing experiments as well as the in vivo peritoneal septicemia model. Bacterial strains were obtained through the ATCC or BEI Resources or from academic labs (see Table S1 in the supplemental material). **Antibiotics. Antibiotics were made up as 10-mg/ml stock solutions, and aliquots were stored at** 20°C. Ampicillin (AMP), daptomycin (DAP), and gentamicin (GEN) were dissolved in water. Linezolid (LZD), rifampin (RIF), tigecycline (TGC), and acyldepsipeptide analog 4 (ADEP4), which was custom synthesized, were dissolved in dimethyl sulfoxide (DMSO). Ciprofloxacin (CIP) was dissolved in 0.1 M sodium hydroxide. Oritavancin (ORI) was dissolved in water containing 0.002% polysorbate 80 (P-80). **MIC assays. MIC assays were performed using the broth microdilution method (42). E. faecalis strain** V583 was evaluated concurrently with each MIC test as a quality control. Serial 2-fold dilutions of antibiotics were made in DMSO, water, or water containing 0.002% polysorbate 80, as appropriate. Cation-adjusted Mueller-Hinton broth (CAMHB) was used as the growth medium for all MIC assays. Colonies were suspended in CAMHB, the optical density was measured, and the culture was diluted to give a final concentration of approximately 5 105CFU/ml in each well. Plates were incubated for 18 to 20 h prior to measuring the turbidity. The MIC was determined to be the lowest concentration of drug that inhibited visual growth. The medium was supplemented with 50 g/ml Ca2 for DAP assays and with 0.002% polysorbate 80 for ORI assays. **ClpP expression and purification. The mature sequence of E. faecium strain TX16 (43) ClpP was** synthesized and cloned into the NcoI and XhoI sites of the pET21 vector by GenScript. EfClpP with a C-terminal six-histidine tag was expressed in E. coli BL21(DE3) for8hat18°C. The protein was purified in a two-step process incorporating affinity chromatography with a HisTrap High Performance column (GE Healthcare) and size exclusion chromatography with a Sephacryl S-200 (16/60) column in buffer containing 50 mM Tris, pH 8, 100 mM NaCl, and 2 mM dithiothreitol (DTT). Purity was assessed using SDS-PAGE analysis. **SPR analysis. Direct binding of ADEP4 (molecular weight [MW], 770.87 Da) to EfClpP (monomeric** MW, 23,269.4 Da) was measured via surface plasmon resonance (SPR) analysis using a FortéBio Pioneer FE instrument. The Ni-nitrilotriacetic acid (Ni-NTA) biosensor was conditioned with a pulse of 0.5 M EDTA, followed by 50 M NiCl2 at a flow rate of 25 l/min for 5 min.
