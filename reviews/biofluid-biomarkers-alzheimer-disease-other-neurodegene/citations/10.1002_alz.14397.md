**Highlights** ∙ Different plasma phosphorylated tau (p-tau) species reflect different pathological processes of Alzheimer’s disease (AD), with p-tau231 showing the greatest asso- ciation with the earliest increases in brain amyloid beta (Aβ) accumulation, while p-tau217 shows greater association with both brain Aβ and early tau pathology, and other p-tau and tau fragment species show greater association with later stages of brain tau pathology. ∙ Plasma p-tau217 has proven to be an excellent biomarker for AD pathology due to its close association with both brain Aβ and tau pathology, as well as its large dynamic range. ∙ Many different assays with varying performance exist for the same p-tau species, with mass spectrometry assays performing uniformly well, and several immunoas- says achieving comparable performance. ∙ “Round robin” head-to-head studies have been performed to compare different assays for several key plasma biomarkers, including p-tau181 and p-tau217, but additional head-to-head studies are needed, especially for new analytes and for measuring performance in diverse populations. ∙ Plasma immunoassays have the potential to increase accessibility of early diagnostic testing for a broad population, including diverse historically under-represented and under-served populations, due to the potential to be implemented globally, including in primary care settings; however, further research is needed to validate the optimal cutoffs for each assay for real-world clinical usage. ∙ Eventually, clinical implementation of a two-step workflow may allow standalone use of plasma testing in certain contexts, minimizing the need for confirmation with costly and less accessible cerebrospinal fluid/positron emission tomography testing.

### 1 INTRODUCTION

The underlying neuropathology of Alzheimer’s disease (AD) has been widely studied, and the framework for diagnosis and staging of AD based on amyloid beta (Aβ), tau, and neurodegeneration (ATN) biomarkers developed by the National Institute on Aging and the Alzheimer’s Association (NIA-AA) working group is commonly used to characterize the disease in individuals (Figure 1A).1A revision of this framework has recently been published by the working group to reflect recent developments,2such as the regulatory approvals of disease- modifying therapies (DMTs), the emergence of plasma biomarkers, and the fact that imaging and fluid biomarkers within a category may not be equivalent for many use cases. The fundamental tenet is that AD is defined based on biochemical alterations rather than clinical symp- toms. Core biomarkers are brain Aβ plaques, “A,” which can be mea- sured in fluids (cerebrospinal fluid [CSF] or blood) as Aβ42/40 ratio or by brain Aβ positron emission tomography (PET) imaging, and brain tau neurofibrillary tangle (NFT) pathology, “T,” measured by tau PET. In flu- ids, the equivalence of various “T” criterion biomarkers has been more difficult to establish; the current proposal envisions certain tau species (phosphorylated tau [p-tau]205, C-terminal, and non-phosphorylated

tau fragments) as “T” markers. Other p-tau species (such as p-tau217) are associated with both Aβ and tau pathology, that is, they appear to reflect Aβ-related tau phosphorylation and release/secretion, which is predictive, but not a direct marker, of NFT pathology. These various tau species will be described further in the following sections of this review. The revised criteria also incorporate non-core AD biomarkers, such as neuronal injury/dysfunction, “N,” measured as neurofilament light (NfL) in fluids or by structural brain imaging, as well as inflam- matory processes, “I,” such as astrocytic activation, reflected by fluid glial fibrillary acidic protein (GFAP), as well as vascular injury (V) and synucleinopathy (S). Aβ pathology, which can be identified up to approximately two decades prior to the onset of AD clinical symptoms,3occurs early in the progression of AD, followed by tau pathology and neurodegeneration, which represent a more advanced stage of AD. The revised criteria for diagnosis and staging of AD enables a more accurate categorization of the disease based on biological criteria rather than clinical symptoms, which benefits clinical practice especially at early stages of disease. Moreover, these revised criteria play a crucial role in advancing preci- sion medicine approaches, and have the potential to aid in the design and implementation of clinical trials for DMTs in AD.4–6

Drug development strategies for precision medicine–based DMTs depend on the identification of individuals who exhibit AD pathology, even at very early stages of the disease (i.e., pre-symptomatic).7–9The inclusion of participants lacking the targeted pathophysiology, and/or with too advanced pathology, are considered to be some of the pri- mary factors that contribute to high rates of clinical trial failure.10–12 By incorporating biomarkers associated with Aβ and tau, the biomarker framework improves participant selection, staging, endpoint mea- surements, treatment evaluation, and subgroup analyses, eventually contributing to a more in-depth understanding of AD and improved therapeutic strategies.13–17 Certain core biomarkers within the revised criteria, such as Aβ and tau PET imaging and CSF analysis, are already used in clini- cal practice for diagnosing AD, particularly in specialized memory clinics, and their use is expected to increase for diagnosis and stag- ing as well as for therapeutic decision making.18Tau measurements have emerged as particularly clinically relevant biomarkers for AD staging due to a strong association with cognitive deterioration19,20 and neurodegeneration.21Other than neuropathology, tau PET imag- ing is regarded as the gold standard biomarker for assessing tau pathology.19,20,22Flortaucipir (Tauvid, aka AV1451 or T807) is the first and only US Food and Drug Administration (FDA)-approved PET tracer for imaging tau pathology in AD.23–25While PET imaging is presently being used for diagnosis, factors such as high cost, limited availability, radiation exposure, and scaling issues pose challenges for wider use. The aim of this review is to provide a summary of immunoassays for plasma p-tau species, including the detailed assay characteristics, and to highlight the strengths and limitations of the various assays in dif- ferent contexts of use, with a focus on use in clinical trials for AD, as well as to suggest future areas of research to evolve the understanding and application of these biomarker assays, including in clinical practice.

Initially, CSF t-tau was generally accepted to represent the “N” of the ATN frame- work, as it was thought to reflect neurodegeneration not specific to a particular etiology.27However, across the neurodegenerative demen- tias, CSF t-tau increase is surprisingly AD specific: normal CSF levels of the marker are typically seen in several neurodegenerative pri- mary tauopathies, such as frontotemporal dementia and progressive supranuclear palsy.28Along similar lines, CSF p-tau was initially pro- posed to represent the “T” of the ATN framework, but this has also been demonstrated to be AD specific and not increased in non-AD tauopathies.4,6Recent research indicates that fluid p-tau measures are more strongly associated with brain amyloidosis (the presence of Aβ plaques) than with neurodegeneration and tau deposition as detected by PET imaging or post mortem examination,29,30suggesting they reflect the earlier stages of AD pathology, hallmarked by Aβ rather than tau deposition. Blood-based biomarker tests are more suitable for large-scale implementation (i.e., in primary care settings globally) in AD diagnosis as they are less invasive, more accessible, and relatively inexpen- sive compared to PET imaging and/or lumbar puncture.18,31Thus, they could potentially provide improved access to screening among diverse populations, resulting in the inclusion of historically under- represented patient populations in clinical studies and more access to treatment, thereby addressing a major challenge in current AD research.32Furthermore, a single blood sample could provide the ability to assess the levels of Aβ, tau, neurodegeneration, and inflamma- tory markers, eliminating the need for multiple separate neuroimaging scans. Since many of the new blood tests are not yet approved as stand- alone diagnostic tests, they are often used as pre-screeners as part of the diagnostic work-up in specialized memory clinics for patients with cognitive symptoms, and the results are then confirmed with CSF or PET testing whenever possible.33It is anticipated that blood-based biomarkers will be widely used in specialty memory clinics over the next 3 to 5 years and, with regulatory approvals as in vitro diagnostic (IVD) tests, they may increasingly replace PET/CSF testing. It is likely that they will be used as diagnostic tools in primary care in combination with cognitive tests within the next 5 to 10 years to identify individuals who should be referred to specialists for more definitive testing.18A globally accessible screening tool of this nature could address the cur- rent limitations in AD diagnostic capacity within health-care systems, which is especially important with the advent of new DMTs.9 Until recently, measuring AD biomarkers such as Aβ42/40 and p-tau in blood was highly challenging due to their extremely low concen- trations and often poor separation between pathological and normal states. However, recent advances in detection techniques and high affinity antibodies have enabled rapid progress in the reliable measure- ments of blood p-tau as well as Aβ, which is expected to change clinical practice within the next several years.34,35 There is emerging evidence that in the early stages of AD, spe- cific fluid-based p-tau assays are more sensitive than tau PET (i.e., are elevated before tau PET reaches an abnormal threshold), although

not Aβ PET, suggesting they might become particularly valuable as accessible and inexpensive tools for identifying patients who could benefit from early disease intervention (Figure 1B).29,36Tau PET trac- ers bind insoluble paired helical filament (PHF) tau protein aggregates in the brain that form NFTs, which mainly accumulate during the symp- tomatic phases of AD and correlate with brain atrophy and cognitive decline.37–40This accumulation of NFTs is characteristic of the later stages of AD, wherein underlying neurodegenerative processes have already produced significant damage, resulting in irreversible cognitive impairment. In contrast, CSF and blood p-tau levels likely reflect active secretion of soluble p-tau from neurons, for example, via retromer dysfunction,41in response to Aβ accumulation during earlier stages of AD.42,43Consequently, p-tau species detected in CSF and blood are associated with Aβ pathology as well as tau pathology, suggesting that this marker reflects several phases of the Aβ cascade, differentiating fluid p-tau from both Aβ and tau PET.34,42,44 Along these lines, some research has suggested that Aβ induces changes in soluble tau phosphorylation and release (tau metabolism), followed later by tau aggregation (PHF/NFT formation).29This is supported by evidence that the effects of insoluble aggregated Aβ (detected by Aβ PET) on insoluble aggregated tau (detected by tau PET) are associated with and/or mediated by soluble p-tau (as measured in the CSF), whereas soluble t-tau shows less association/mediation, sug- gesting this relationship is mainly via increased tau phosphorylation.29 Also supporting this reasoning, primary tauopathies with tau PET pos- itivity and findings of NFTs on neuropathological examination typically have normal fluid p-tau levels, presumably because they lack Aβ– induced soluble p-tau formation.29,45–52Furthermore, the relationship between fluid-based p-tau and tau PET may depend on the stage of AD. Much research is being conducted to better understand this rela- tionship, but additional experimental studies are needed to directly address the hypotheses.38,53–56 These findings of early changes in fluid p-tau markers, prior to the onset of symptoms and significant increases in tau PET signal, sug- gest that there may be a key window for intervention targeting soluble tau pathology in individuals with Aβ pathology before significant tau aggregation has begun (i.e., biomarker profile A+, fluid p-tau+,PET T–), to prevent further aggregation and symptoms.29This possibility would make fluid-based p-tau biomarker determination particularly valuable in screening for preclinical and prodromal AD, especially with blood plasma/serum assays given the greater affordability and accessibility.

### 2.1 p-tau181

P-tau181 (phosphorylated tau at position 181, T181) is historically a core biomarker within the ATN paradigm that has been widely used in both CSF and blood assays for AD diagnosis. It is one of the most abun- dant p-tau species,57,58which may account for it being commonly used before the advent of more sensitive tools that allowed the quantifica- tion of less abundant p-tau fragments.59P-tau181 was the first p-tau species to demonstrate good accuracy for discriminating AD versus controls in plasma assays.47–49,52Early commercially available CSF p- tau181 immunoassays targeted mid-region tau fragments and did not perform as well in blood.60To address this limitation, newer ultrasen- sitive blood immunoassays target the N-terminus to detect fragments spanning from the N-terminus to mid-region,48,49,61,62which repre- sent the most abundant forms of p-tau in blood.34Several plasma assay technologies and platforms are now available for measuring p-tau181 in blood samples. Mass spectrometry (MS)-based assays, ultrasen- sitive single molecule array (Simoa) immunoassays, electrochemical reaction-based electrochemiluminescence immunoassays (ECLIA), and the recently developed immunoassay platforms offer higher sensi- tivity and specificity in detecting and quantifying p-tau181 in blood samples.34,48,49,61Several of the more common immunoassays are described in Table 1. Additional immunoassays for plasma p-tau181 are becoming available rapidly. Although plasma assays to detect p-tau181 have shown promise in distinguishing AD patients from controls, other AD-related p-tau species have been identified that are elevated at an earlier disease stage and/or have greater dynamic range (i.e., greater fold-change for AD vs. non-AD) than p-tau181,57,58,62–66suggesting that they may be even more useful for AD diagnosis than p-tau181; these other biomarkers are discussed below.

### 2.2 p-tau231

Studies have suggested that fluid p-tau231 may be a particularly valu- able biomarker for early stages of the AD spectrum (i.e., preclinical and prodromal AD). Neuropathological studies show that p-tau231 is a predominant component of pre-tangles, whereas p-tau181 is more prominently seen in mature NFTs.67In addition, p-tau231, but not p- tau181, is significantly increased at the earliest Braak stages in some studies,63and phosphorylation of tau at position 231 has been sug- gested to be one of the earliest events in the phosphorylation cascade that modulates tubulin assembly.68An MS-based study suggested that p-tau231 is often observed in conjunction with p-tau235, which is the dual phosphorylation epitope recognized by the AD-specific antibody PHF-27,69,70supporting the AD specificity of this species. Further- more, immunoassay-based studies indicate that CSF p-tau231 may be a more sensitive biomarker than p-tau181, especially at early stages of AD.62,64Since p-tau231 is present in lower quantities in the blood than p-tau181, its quantitative analysis is challenging, requiring highly sen- sitive biomarker detection techniques, such as the recently developed plasma immunoassays described in Table 1.

In particular, p-tau217 exhibited a 4-fold increase, whereas p-tau181 showed a 1.2-fold increase. A related finding in this study is that p-tau217 had lower abundance than p-tau181 in the CSF of control individuals, suggesting its specificity for AD.57 Consistent with the above study, another MS study72demonstrated that p-tau217 and p-tau262 were among the most relevant phospho- rylation sites in distinguishing AD from control brain tissue, showing performance superior to p-tau181. Also in that study, p-tau217 was one of the most upregulated phosphorylation sites in the CSF com- pared to brain homogenate samples, suggesting that upregulation of p-tau217 levels may occur concurrently with the release of tau from neurons, as opposed to the aggregation of tau into intraneuronal NFTs, which is a later-stage pathology. Thus, these studies suggest that p-tau217 may be a relevant marker of early-stage AD pathology. Assays for either CSF p-tau181 or p-tau217 epitopes both detect AD pathology with high accuracy. Notably, in a number of studies using MS58,73or immunoassay techniques in CSF,29,59,65,66p-tau217 outper- formed p-tau181 in detecting AD pathology (CSF and PET biomarker status) and AD dementia, including differentiating AD from other neu- rodegenerative diseases, consistent with p-tau217 having a greater specificity for AD. However, concentrations of p-tau217 are relatively low in blood compared to p-tau181, making quantitative measurement in blood extremely difficult until recently. In analogy to the CSF results, studies with blood plasma using highly sensitive MS techniques34revealed that plasma p-tau217 exhibited a larger dynamic range and showed greater specificity than plasma p-tau181 in identifying abnormal Aβ status, irrespective of cognitive status. A study by Barthelemy et al.34also showed that the reduction in the p-tau217/tau217 ratio from CSF to plasma is smaller than the reduction observed for p-tau181/tau181, which the authors suggested might reflect less dilution by unphosphorylated peripheral tau contri- bution for p-tau217 compared to p-tau181, increasing the specificity and dynamic range of plasma p-tau217. Consistent with other findings suggesting the specificity of fluid p-tau217 for AD, there is evidence that the association between Aβ and tau (measured by Aβ and tau PET) was more greatly mediated by CSF p-tau217 than CSF p-tau181, suggesting that p-tau217 may be more informative on the pathological state of tau associated with the formation of PHFs (as detected by tau PET).29Supporting this suggestion, a post mortem study showed that plasma p-tau217 concen- tration mediated the association between Aβ plaques and tau tangles (measured by neuropathology or tau PET), especially for tau pathology outside of the medial temporal lobe.50Furthermore, other tauopathies, such as progressive supranuclear palsy (PSP) and corticobasal degen- eration (CBD), were not associated with elevated plasma p-tau217 levels despite having similar NFT densities as AD on neuropathologi- cal examination, suggesting that plasma p-tau217 levels are associated specifically with Aβ plaques, which demonstrates specificity for AD.

In addition, a recent AD *post mortem* neuropathology study revealed immunostaining for p-tau217, but not other p-tau species, in vesicular structures, which likely represent granulovacuolar degenera- tion (GVD) bodies, in addition to the classic NFTs and neuropil threads (NTs). The area fraction of positive immunostaining correlated with the burden of Aβ plaquesandNFTs,aswellaswithante mortem plasma p-tau217, and it was elevated only in the AD brains but not in the non- AD (i.e., primary tauopathy) brains. Of note, these correlations were observed only in individuals with Aβ positivity, further supporting AD specificity of plasma p-tau217. The authors suggested that the pres- ence of p-tau217 in GVD bodies and its correlation with Aβ and NFT burden suggest a potential role for these structures in the secretion of p-tau217 from neurons in response to Aβ pathology. Although plasma p-tau217 has been frequently described as more AD specific than p-tau181, which is elevated in other neurodegen- erative diseases such as amyotrophic lateral sclerosis,75there have been some reports questioning the AD specificity of the p-tau217 phospho-epitope. A study reported that Aβ-negative individuals with the R406 W MAPT pathogenic mutation, which is associated with pri- mary tauopathies, showed an increase in CSF p-tau217 (measured using an immunoprecipitation-mass spectrometry assay) to a positiv- ity threshold defined by an AD cohort.76In addition, another study77 showed that p-tau217 distinguished both AD and Creutzfeldt–Jakob disease (CJD) from controls. In Aβ-negative individuals with CJD, levels of CSF p-tau217, but not p-tau231, were increased, and the extent of neurodegeneration was correlated with the levels of p-tau217. There- fore, further research is required to understand the extent to which plasma p-tau217 is specific for AD pathology. Two highly sensitive enzyme-linked immunosorbent assay (ELISA)- based plasma immunoassays using different platforms and targeting slightly different p-tau217 species, described below and in Table 1, have been well studied and characterized. Additional immunoassays for plasma p-tau217 are becoming available rapidly, such as the new ALZpath assay.78 Studies using either the Janssen Simoa p-tau217+ assay or the Lilly MSD p-tau217 assay have demonstrated that both assays accurately discriminate between preclinical AD and control groups,36,51,79,80and both show specificity in distinguishing AD from other neurodegen- erative diseases.51,52,79In addition, both assays predict abnormal Aβ and tau PET status across the clinical spectrum of AD and correlate with Aβ and tau PET imaging.36,51,52,79,81The new ALZpath assay has also shown high accuracy in identifying abnormal Aβ and tau PET or CSF status and correlations with Aβ and tau PET.82Overall, the con- cordance of findings across multiple p-tau217/p-tau217+ assays and studies support the relevance of this phospho-epitope in AD-related pathology. Both the Janssen Simoa p-tau217+ assay and the Lilly MSD/Simoa p-tau217 plasma assays, as well as the ALZpath assay, have demonstrated similar sensitivity and specificity across all stages of the AD spectrum. Additional plasma immunoassays for the p-tau217 epi- tope are being developed at a rapid pace, and more details about these newer assays will likely become available in the near future.

### 2.4 p-tau205

As discussed above, p-tau205, along with p-tau217, was identified by a highly sensitive MS study as one of the species that exhibited higher levels of hyperphosphorylation compared to p-tau181 in the CSF of individuals with AD versus controls.58P-tau205 in combination with p-tau202 (and frequently with p-tau208 as well), is the dual phospho- rylation epitope recognized by the AT8 antibody commonly used in immunohistochemistry and other detection methods to identify NFTs in AD and other tauopathies.83Another MS study53of CSF p-tau species in autosomal dominant AD (ADAD) found that, among the p- tau species examined (p-tau181, p-tau202, p-tau205, and p-tau217), p-tau205 showed the strongest correlation with measures of brain atrophy and hypometabolism. CSF p-tau205 increased prior to tau PET signal and clinical symptom onset, but after the rise of p-tau217 and p-tau181 levels (both of which coincided with Aβ PET increase, before Aβ reached an abnormal threshold). Importantly, all three p-tau species were correlated with Aβ PET signal throughout the brain, with p- tau217 exhibiting higher correlations than the others in most regions. Emerging evidence from studies of ADAD consistently suggests that elevated levels of CSF p-tau205 and p-tau217, but not p-tau181, mediate the relationship between Aβ and downstream biomarkers, including t-tau levels, cortical thickness, and cognitive impairment, and are associated with disease progression.84 Similarly, an MS study examining plasma p-tau217, p-tau181, p- tau199, p-tau202, p-tau205, and p-tau23185revealed that p-tau217, p-tau231, and p-tau205 were the biomarkers that best reflected Aβ PET and tau PET, with p-tau231 reflecting the earliest pathology, p- tau217 showing the greatest fold changes, and p-tau205 reflecting the latest pathology. P-tau205 was the only biomarker that was better associated with tau PET than Aβ PET. To the best of our knowledge, there have been no published immunoassays for the measurement of p-tau205. In addition to the above-described p-tau epitopes, which represent the most extensively studied to date, additional p-tau epitopes have emerged as promising biomarkers, including p-tau212.86The availabil- ity of assays for additional p-tau epitopes may provide more granularity in AD diagnosis and staging, as each epitope is thought to reflect a unique and/or complementary aspect of AD pathology.

### 3.1 p-tau231 versus p-tau181 immunoassays

In head-to-head comparisons between UGot Simoa plasma p-tau181 (using the AT270 capture antibody) and UGot Simoa plasma p-tau231 assays, both have shown comparable performance in identifying the symptomatic stages of AD as well as AD neuropathology.45,87,88How- ever, compared to the UGot Simoa plasma p-tau181 assay, the UGot Simoa plasma p-tau231 assay showed increased levels at earlier dis- ease stages (i.e., lower Aβ PET levels), better differentiated Braak stages across the entire spectrum, and showed more robust associa- tions with Aβ and tau PET in cognitively unimpaired (CU) individuals.45 These findings suggest that the UGot Simoa plasma p-tau231 assay may be more promising for detecting early AD-related changes. However, another head-to-head study44demonstrated that the ADx Simoa p-tau181 assay, which uses a capture antibody that does not show cross-reactivity with p-tau175 (ADx252), had significantly higher area under the curve (AUC) values than the ADx Simoa p-tau231 assay for detecting symptomatic and preclinical AD. Additionally, the ADx Simoa p-tau181 assay, but not the ADx Simoa p-tau231 assay, correlated with Aβ PET signals in CU individuals. The ADx Simoa p- tau181 assay also demonstrated a greater fold increase and AUC in distinguishing Aβ-positive CU individuals from Aβ-negative CU elderly individuals than previous studies using AT270-based plasma p-tau181 assays89–92(Table S1 in supporting information). These contrasting results suggest that the ADx Simoa p-tau181 assay may have better clinical performance than the AT270-based plasma p-tau181 assays. Interestingly, while the ADx Simoa p-tau181 assay demonstrated higher specificity and positive predictive value (PPV) compared to the ADx Simoa p-tau231 assay, no significant differences in sensitivity or negative predictive value (NPV) were observed between the two assays (Table S2 in supporting information). These findings imply that both assays may be useful in contexts that require high sensitivity, such as population screening for early AD diagnosis, while the ADx Simoa p- tau181 assay may be preferred in situations requiring high specificity, such as clinical trial recruitment for DMT development.44 The differences in performance of different assays for the same phospho-epitope demonstrated across studies highlight the impor- tance of assay characteristics in determining clinical performance. To ensure clarity and comparability, it is therefore of interest to com- pare different phospho-epitopes using similar assays for a more direct evaluation of the characteristics inherent to specific phospho-epitopes. For example, an MS study simultaneously measuring plasma p-tau181, p-tau199, p-tau202, p-tau205, p-tau217, and p-tau231 on the same platform85showed that p-tau231, along with p-tau205 and p-tau217 but not p-tau181, had the highest fold changes between individuals with A+T+ versus A–T– CSF biomarker profiles, the highest correla- tions with Aβ PET and tau PET, and increased at the lowest Aβ PET levels or tau PET Braak stages (Figure 3). In contrast, p-tau181 had smaller fold changes and weaker associations with Aβ PET and tau PET.

### 4 OPEN QUESTIONS AND FUTURE OUTLOOK

### 4.1 What factors influence the diagnostic

### performance of different p-tau biomarkers?

A caveat in interpreting the results of head-to-head comparisons of p- tau immunoassays is that different p-tau phospho-epitopes are often compared using distinct platforms and detection antibodies. For exam- ple, there is only a modest correlation (r = 0.66) between p-tau181 measured on MSD and p-tau181 measured on the Simoa assay.91The underlying reasons for potential differences in clinical performance are not yet fully understood. Differences could be attributed to technical factors, such as variations in antibody binding affinities/specificities or platform sensitivities, or to different degrees of matrix interference across assays.95Differences in diagnostic performance could also be explained by biological factors, including varying degrees of comorbid- ity, but also ethnic and sociodemographic differences, where there may be biomarker-specific vulnerabilities requiring further study (discussed further below). Technical factors such as cross-platform differences could be either due to the platform itself or specific assay characteristics: capture and detection antibodies, calibrators, incubation time and tempera- ture, and so on. However, even when immunoassays for p-tau217 and p-tau181 were compared using more similar techniques (both MSD, same calibrators, and same detection antibody), the assay for p-tau217 continued to show significantly greater accuracy than p- tau181 in identifying neuropathologically confirmed AD (although the differences in performance were smaller). Thus, there are likely some differences inherent to the phospho-epitopes.52,102 Based on potential inherent biological differences, it is possible that each p-tau species may be most useful in different contexts, although it remains uncertain whether the differences are clinically meaningful. Studies investigating the performance of CSF versus plasma p-tau assays in different cohorts and using different assays have reported conflicting results. ### 4.3 New methods on the horizon for plasma

### p-tau immunoassays

Beyond measuring p-tau concentrations, plasma p-tau to non–p-tau ratios have recently been investigated as potential biomarkers for AD. Currently, MS is used to measure these ratios, but in theory they could also be measured using immunoassays. A study by Janelidze et al.96 found that the MS-based pT217/T217 ratio measurement performed better than p-tau217 immunoassays for identifying CSF Aβ positivity and predicting progression to AD dementia. However, comparing the ratios to p-tau concentration measures in this study was confounded by the use of different technologies (MS vs. immunoassay). In contrast, the AHEAD study used MS to measure both ratios (pT217/T217 and pT181/T181) and concentrations (p-tau217 and p-tau181), and the ratios yielded slightly higher AUC values for predicting Aβ PET positiv- ity compared to the p-tau concentration measures.116As mentioned above, use of ratios may also partially compensate for confounding effects of comorbidities (Figure S1).114,116These findings suggest that plasma p-tau to non–p-tau ratios may hold promise as biomarkers for AD. Furthermore, there are novel fluid assays for a variety of tau epi- topes other than phospho-tau, such as C-terminal tau fragments, which unlike the above-described p-tau assays, reflect brain tau more than brain Aβ,117,118as well as the brain-derived tau assay,119which reflects AD-type neurodegeneration (“N” of ATN). However, further discussion of the assays for these other tau epitopes is outside the scope of this review.

### 4.4 How to best use plasma p-tau assays in

### clinical trials of AD DMTs?

Plasma p-tau assays are already in use in clinical trials of DMTs for AD in screening/pre-screening and exploratory surrogate outcomes.

4.4.1 Screening/pre-screening Studies suggest that plasma p-tau217 could serve as a valuable screen- ing biomarker in clinical trials. Power analyses of theoretical clinical trials have demonstrated that incorporating plasma p-tau217 as a

screening tool might reduce the necessary sample size.103,120–122 Plasma p-tau could also be used as a pre-screening tool to be con- firmed with PET imaging to reduce screen failures. For this purpose, the Janssen Simoa p-tau217+ plasma assay is currently being used in the Autonomy trial (NCT04619420) and the Lilly MSD p-tau217 plasma assay is being used in the TRAILBLAZER-ALZ studies (i.e., NCT05026866). Eventually, plasma screening may be used analo- gously in clinical practice.

4.4.2 Exploratory surrogate outcomes in AD DMT clinical trials Anti-Aβ DMT trials showed reductions in plasma p-tau181 with aducanumab treatment,123reductions in plasma p-tau217 with donanemab treatment,124and reductions in plasma p-tau181 and p-tau217 with gantenerumab treatment.125To our knowledge, these measures have not yet been published for anti-tau DMT trials. Longitu- dinal increases of plasma p-tau217 have been shown to be associated with clinical deterioration and brain atrophy in preclinical AD, support- ing the notion that this biomarker may hold promise as a surrogate outcome measure (i.e., see Figure 5).56 The potential use of plasma p-tau measures as surrogate outcomes will require greater understanding of how longitudinal changes in plasma p-tau relate to longitudinal changes in other biomarkers that may be used as surrogate outcomes, for example Aβ and tau PET, and, most importantly, how these changes relate to clinically meaningful measures such as cognition and function. Such relationships will need to be investigated in natural history studies as well as treatment trials. Changes in soluble p-tau have been suggested to reflect Aβ aggrega- tion early in the disease course, before tau PET increases; therefore, changes in these measures may precede tau PET changes by a signif- icant amount of time.29Data from recent and current DMT trials that include longitudinal measurements of plasma p-tau and Aβ/tau PET are expected to contribute to a greater understanding of this relationship (i.e., Pontecorvo et al.124).

### 4.5 How to use plasma p-tau assays in clinical

### practice for AD?

Since plasma p-tau measurements are more accurate than clinical diag- nosis in predicting AD pathology, they have the potential to improve the accuracy of, and access to, AD diagnosis and monitoring in clini- cal practice, once they receive regulatory approvals as IVD tests.126 However, development of robust and standardized assays is critical to ensure their widespread availability and adoption in regular clinical testing. Some of these plasma p-tau assays, especially the p-tau217– based assays, are very likely to be deployed on clinical assay platforms to be made available for large-scale global routine testing. ### 5 CONCLUSIONS

A better understanding of plasma p-tau biomarkers, including fac- tors such as different phospho-epitopes, assay designs, and platforms, is crucial for overcoming drug development–related challenges, by enhancing the acceptability and applicability of the biomarker frame- work to identify appropriate patient populations for clinical trials. Furthermore, if plasma p-tau biomarkers demonstrate robust corre- lations with disease progression, cognitive decline, or other relevant clinical endpoints, they could potentially be used as surrogate outcome measures to assess the efficacy of therapeutic interventions and facili- tate the development of more efficient and streamlined clinical trials in AD.9 The revised criteria for diagnosis and staging of AD leverage recent fluid and imaging biomarker data to specify the types of informa- tion that can be extracted from each marker (i.e., fluid p-tau217 will be scored in a different sub-category than fluid p-tau205 and MTBR-tau243). This granular approach takes into account the dif- ferent pathological processes reflected by different fluid tau species and imaging and is expected to advance the use of the framework in research and in the clinic. However, this approach should be used with caution until there is greater understanding of how the various biomarkers/assays perform across different laboratories and cohorts. In addition to use in clinical trials, plasma p-tau biomarkers have the potential to improve the accuracy of AD diagnosis and monitoring

in clinical practice, once further research has addressed outstand- ing questions on how to standardize assays and how to account for variability due to comorbidities, BMI, age, and racial/ethnic back- ground. Blood-based biomarker testing has great potential to provide improved access to AD screening and clinical care to historically under- represented and under-served diverse populations. These promising results inspire the medical and scientific communities, along with all relevant stakeholders, to investigate and engage in discussions regard- ing the possible implementation of AD blood biomarkers in the early clinical setting.
