### 2.5 CSF collection

CSF samples were collected passively in polypropylene tubes (Sarst- edt) via lumbar puncture, immediately centrifuged at 2000 × g for 10 minutes at 4◦C, and frozen until further analysis. The amyloid sta- tus of the participants of the validation cohort (amyloid positive [A+] and amyloid negative [A–]) was determined using in-house cut-offs of the ACE Alzheimer Center Barcelona for CSF Aβ42/40 ratio.18Par- ticipants with CSF Aβ42/40 ratio > 0.063 were defined as A–, while participants with CSF Aβ42/40 ratio ≤ 0.063 were defined as A+.

EDTA blood was collected with venous DBS cards (Capitainer B 50, Capitainer AB) from n = 8 individuals according to manufacturer’s instructions. For DBS biomarkers, one filter was used per assay and DBSvenouslevels were correlated with paired EDTA plasma samples. To validate these findings, DBSvenouswere also collected in the valida- tion cohort in n = 34 individuals and were correlated with paired EDTA plasma samples.

### 2.4 Biochemical analysis

Prior to analysis, both filter papers in a DPS card containing dried venous plasma were removed from the card, combined, and trans- ferred to a spin bucket with fritted bottom (Corning Costar Spin-X CLS9301, Corning Costar) and placed in a standard 1.5 mL Eppendorf tube. Samples were incubated shaking with 130 to 210 μL assay dilu- ◦ ent (depending on the needed volume of the respective assay) at 37 C and 500 rpm for 30 minutes. After incubation, the tube was spun at 20◦ C and 14,000 × g for 5 minutes. After spinning, the filters and spin bucket were discarded, and the eluate was analyzed. Plasma samples were thawed for 45 minutes at room temperature, vortexed for 30 sec- ◦ onds at 2000 rpm and centrifuged at 4000 × gand20 C for 10 minutes before analysis. The levels of GFAP, NfL, Aβ40, Aβ42, p-tau181, and p-tau217 in DPSvenousand EDTA plasma were measured using a Simoa HD-X ana- lyzer (Quanterix) and commercially available kits (Neurology 4-Plex E assay kit for GFAP, NfL, Aβ40, and Aβ42; kit lot #503246, calibrator lot #134101; pTau-181 Advantage V2 assay kit, kit lot #503703, calibrator lot #228603, Quanterix; ALZpath pTau217 assay for p-tau217, ALZ- path Inc.) at the Neurochemistry Laboratory, Sahlgrenska University Hospital, Mölndal, Sweden. CSF Aβ1-40, Aβ1-42, p-tau, and total tau levels were measured with chemiluminescent enzyme immunoassays (CLEIA) on the Lumipulse G600II analyzer (Fujirebio Europe N.V.) at the ACE Alzheimer Center, Barcelona, Spain.

### 2.6 Neurocognitive testing

In all participants of the clinical validation cohort, cognitive status was determined by the 30-point Mini–Mental State Examination (MMSE) 19 20 questionnaire and the Clinical Dementia Rating (CDR) scale. Par- ticipants were grouped according to the test results using predefined cut-offs (CDR) and equal distribution over three terciles (MMSE): no/questionable cognitive impairment (CDR ≤ 0.5, MMSE 28–30 points), mild cognitive impairment (MCI; MMSE 24–27 points), and moderate cognitive impairment (CDR ≥ 1, MMSE 12–23 points).

### 2.7 Statistical analysis

Associations between analyte concentrations in venous DPS and EDTA plasma samples were assessed using linear regression models and Spearman’s rank correlations. Biomarker concentrations in paired DPSvenousand EDTA plasma samples were compared using paired- samples t test procedures to evaluate concentration differences after DPSvenousprocessing. Statistical analyses for amyloid status (A+ vs. A–) and cognitive test results (high vs. middle vs. low score) were per- formed using two-sided t tests. To determine the relationship between

amyloid status and DPSvenousdepending on age, binary logistic regres- sions with age as covariate were performed. To evaluate the sample stability, linear mixed effect models were performed to check for time effects. For collection device testing, linear regression models were used. Differences of biomarker concentrations after spotting differ- ent volumes were tested using mixed effects analyses. For evaluation of diagnostic performance of DPSvenousand EDTA plasma biomarkers, we used the receiver operating characteristic (ROC) area under the curve (AUC) and DeLong test was performed for comparison between AUCs. To determine which combination of biomarkers enabled the best discrimination in the logistic regression, the Multi–Model–Inference R package version 1.43.17 was used that generates models with the best combinations of biomarker ranking based on Akaike information crite- ria (AIC). The model with the lowest AIC represented the best model fit, change lower than two points implied a similar fit, and was compared to subsequent models with the goal of retaining the most parsimonious models. Note that when performing the analyses, the biomarker inclu- sion was limited to the best performers to minimize the risk of random false-positive findings. All statistical analyses were performed in GraphPad Prism 9 for Windows, R-Studio Version 4.3.1., and IBM SPSS Statistics Version

29.0.0.0. Significance levels were set at P < 0.05.

### 3.1 Optimization of sample extraction from the

**DPS** **venous** **cards**

We first tested different filter systems that elute the proteins from the DPS filter discs by using spin buckets with and without nylon membrane. Signals (average number of enzymes per bead [AEB]) for DPSvenousGFAP, NfL, and Aβ40 were higher or similar when pro- cessed with spin buckets without membrane compared to spin buckets with nylon membrane. For DPSvenousp-tau181, on average higher sig- nals were reached when processed with spin buckets with membrane (Figure S2 in supporting information). Aβ42 could only be quantified in two out of eight samples and no paired samples of both conditions were available. We then compared DPSvenoussamples processed with spin buckets without membrane to DPSvenoussamples processed with three different 96-well filter plates; for all tested analytes, DPSvenoussamples prepared in the protein precipitation plate, as well as in the spin buck- ets without membrane, revealed the strongest correlation with the corresponding plasma sample (Figure S3 in supporting information). We then investigated the effect of blood volume spotted on the DPSvenouscards; for all tested analytes (GFAP, NfL, Aβ40, Aβ42, and p-tau181), no linear relation of volume and DPSvenousbiomarker concentrations were found (Figure S4 in supporting information). When evaluating three different storage conditions, we found that at both RT and 4◦C, DPSvenousGFAP concentrations changed signifi- cantly over time (P < 0.001/P < 0.001 [RT/4◦C] for time effect), while NfL (P = 0.368/P = 0.366 for time effect), Aβ40 (P = 0.152/P = 0.364 for time effect), and Aβ42 (P = 0.195/P = 0.051 for time effect) were

stable over the 6-month period. Compared to day 0, GFAP levels were decreased at week 1, both when stored at RT and 4◦C(β =−4.786, *P < 0.001 for RT and β =−4.018, P < 0.001 for 4*◦C). DPSvenousp- tau181 levels were stable over time at 4◦C(P = 0.151 for time effect), while there was a significant concentration drop at RT (P = 0.016 for time effect); compared to day 0, DPSvenousp-tau181 levels were lower at 6 months (β =−0.164, P = 0.020). Also, when stored at 37◦C, DPSvenousGFAP levels changed significantly over time (P = 0.002 for time effect); there was a concentration decrease in week 1 compared to day 0 (β =−1.909, P < 0.001). DPSvenousNfL (P = 0.488 for time effect), Aβ40 (P = 0.501 for time effect), and p-tau181 (P = 0.432 for time effect); however, remained stable over the 1-month period. For Aβ42, not enough data points were available for time effect analysis (Figure S5 in supporting information). Finally, we tested DBS alternative collection methods; the results obtained with DPSvenouswere reproduced for DBS GFAP, NfL, and Aβ40 in the discovery cohort (Figure S6 in supporting information). In four out of eight samples, DBSvenousAβ42 could not be quantified. In the validation cohort, GFAP from DBSvenouscorrelated significantly with plasma (Figure S7 in supporting information).

The validation cohort included 115 participants with suspected AD, displayed in Table 1. In all participants, AD biomarker concentrations were measured in the eluted DPSvenoussamples and compared to paired EDTA plasma samples. Applying the CSF Aβ42/40 ratio cut-off in the subgroup with CSF biomarker data (n = 99), 59 individuals were defined as A+ (CSF Aβ42/40 = 0.048 ± 0.009) and 40 as A– (CSF Aβ42/40 = 0.095 ± 0.017). As expected, A+ individuals also had sig- nificantly lower cognitive performance and higher CSF p-tau and total taulevelscomparedtoA–individuals(Table1).

### 4 DISCUSSION

In this study, we demonstrate that it is possible to quantify a wide array of biomarkers associated with amyloid and tau pathology, neurode- generation, and astrogliosis in DPS from venous collection. Further- more, biomarker measurements from DPSvenous, especially p-tau217, p-tau181, NfL, and GFAP, exhibited a robust correlation with their cor- responding plasma references. This could be validated in a memory clinic cohort comprising patients with suspected AD. Moreover, in this cohort, biomarker measurements from DPSvenous, especially p-tau217, had a good diagnostic accuracy discriminating amyloid pathology sta- tus, which was statistically the same as conventional EDTA plasma biomarkers. These results suggest that the measurement of AD-related and neurodegeneration biomarkers from DPSvenouscould pose a reli- able method for diagnostic and monitoring purposes, facilitating and decentralizing the utility of blood-based biomarkers to recurrent and remote sampling conditions. It should be noted that the concentrations detected in DPSvenous samples were substantially lower than those found in EDTA plasma. These findings align with the results of two previous studies, both

reporting lower levels of NfL in DPS/DBS compared to plasma.7,21 Consequently, consideration should be given to these concentration disparities when implementing AD-related biomarker analysis from venous DPS/DBS. Remarkably, and despite the concentration dif- ferences between EDTA plasma and DPSvenoussamples, the same biomarkers performed equally well in distinguishing A+ and A– patients. This includes p-tau217, which was shown to have an AUC of 0.87 and mean fold-change of 2.6 (± 2.8). These intriguing results emphasize the potential interchangeability of both sampling methods, which has not been demonstrated before and represents a novel and promising finding. Within both the discovery and validation cohorts, DPSvenousGFAP exhibited one of the most robust correlations with EDTA plasma. Moreover, DPSvenousGFAP correlated weakly but significantly with cognitive performance in the validation cohort and had moderate accuracy in discriminating individuals with and without amyloid pathol- ogy, pointing to its link with the emergence with amyloid pathology as shown in sporadic AD,15familial AD,22and Down syndrome.16 Furthermore, a recent study showed that DMTs for AD significantly reduced plasma GFAP levels.2Therefore, venous DPS sampling for GFAP measurement could be a useful supporting tool in monitoring anti-amyloid treatment effectiveness. In addition to a high stability and significant correlation with cog- nitive status, DPSvenousNfL demonstrated robust correlations with standard plasma measurements in both cohorts. However, the correla- tion was notably stronger in the discovery cohort than in the validation cohort, which may be attributable to potential age variations between the cohorts, as NfL levels tend to increase with age.23DPSvenousNfL exhibited limited accuracy in distinguishing A+ from A– individuals, which aligns with previous findings suggesting that while NfL serves as a biomarker for neurodegeneration, it lacks specificity for detecting AD pathology. Increasing evidence also emphasized p-tau217 as state marker of Aβ pathology in preclinical AD,13,32highlighting the remarkable potential of DPSvenousp-tau in the very early diagnosis of AD. Further- more, a recent study reported that anti-amyloid therapies significantly reduced plasma p-tau217.2Thus, DPSvenouscollection for p-tau217 could offer a simple and effective method to regularly monitor and evaluate the therapy efficacy in patients undergoing DMTs. We also see a great potential for DPSvenousp-tau to be used in large-scale clinical trials. Although DPSvenousAβ40 and Aβ42 displayed significant correla- tions with plasma in the discovery cohort, these correlations were not consistent in the validation cohort. Our storage experiments indicate that Aβ40 and also Aβ42 levels, when detectable, remain stable over time when stored at room temperature or in a refrigerator. However, it is crucial to acknowledge recent findings that suggest both Aβ40 and Aβ42 concentrations in plasma may decline significantly after just 6 hours at room temperature.33Therefore, it cannot be completely ruled out that less controlled conditions during transportation would have had an impact on biomarker levels in the validation cohort. Addition- ally, it is possible that the drying process initially impacts Aβ,makingits detection in DPSvenouschallenging and resulting in weak correlations with corresponding plasma samples stored at ultra-low temperatures. Our method development experiments revealed that Aβ42 could not be quantified in > 50% of the samples due to low abundance in DPSvenoussamples. Moreover, DPSvenousAβ40, Aβ42, and Aβ42/40 did not differ between A+ and A– individuals, displayed limited perfor- mance in distinguishing CDR groups, and showed no association with cognitive status. Given that EDTA plasma Aβ has been shown not to be a robust biomarker for AD due to marginal reductions between con- trols and AD patients,31it is not surprising that we do not see clinical utility for this biomarker in DPS. DPS sampling offers significant advantages in both sample collec- tion and analysis procedures. First, DPS sampling requires substantially less volume compared to standard procedures, making it an effi- cient option. Second, our findings confirmed the stability of DPSvenous over weeks, even without refrigeration at high ambient tempera- tures, highlighting a great potential of DPS collection in clinical or research scenarios in which an immediate processing and temperature- dependent storage of blood samples is not feasible. DPS sampling could have a tremendous relevance in rural areas with reduced laboratory infrastructure, where AD as a result may be highly underdiagnosed.34 Furthermore, DPS sampling could be used to monitor biomarker lev- els associated with AD-specific pathology, along with biomarkers that help detect potential side effects, thus aiding in treatment decision making. Conversely, DPSvenousbiomarkers may also facilitate the ongo- ing assessment of pharmacotherapy success and serve as a basis for dosage adjustments. Strengths of this study encompass tight control and standardiza- tion of sample collection, processing, and analysis. Moreover, we have

validated our findings from method development experiments using a well-characterized validation cohort. Notably, our study reveals that biomarkers related to AD and neurodegeneration, especially GFAP, NfL, p-tau181, and p-tau217, are detectable in DPSvenousand exhibit robust correlations with standard measures. Additionally, we have demonstrated that DPS biomarkers distinguish amyloid pathology and correlate with neurocognitive testing, highlighting their clinical rele- vance. One limitation of the study pertains to the unknown pathology of the participants of the discovery cohort. It is noteworthy that the correlations of DPSvenousbiomarkers were stronger within the dis- covery cohort than in the validation cohort. This may be attributed to the variances in underlying pathologies present within the dis- covery cohort, which may have exerted an influence on the results. Consequently, these associations should also be evaluated in a larger population with a broad spectrum of (pre)clinical AD patients and healthy controls, to verify the diagnostic value of DPSvenousmeasure- ments. Also, as we began our experiments before the evolution of p-tau217 as a state biomarker for AD, we collected DPSvenoussamples for p-tau217 from additional participants and could not investigate the performance of p-tau217 in combination with the other biomarkers in discriminating amyloid pathology and cognitive status. Last, while the signal for NfL, Aβ40, Aβ42, and p-tau remained stable over time at 4◦C—the selected temperature for the study—in the storage exper- iment, it exhibited a drop for GFAP between day 0 and week 1. The measurements in the current study were conducted within week 1, which may not be the optimal condition for GFAP but is still deemed acceptable. Given the nature of the study, which aims to extend the use of AD biomarkers to remote settings, it would be impractical to measure at day 0. Therefore, we performed all the analyses within week 1. In summary, our findings bear major implications: (1) the con- cordance between DPSvenousand plasma measures reflecting also cognitive performance and amyloid pathology, and (2) it is an easy and widely accessible application allowing a quick sample processing. Taken together, DPS collection emerges as an easy-to-implement and feasible alternative to conventional blood processing, particularly in scenarios in which remote sampling is highly desirable. We believe that innova- tive tools for blood biomarker sampling, such as DPS, can increase the possibilities to detect early changes related to AD and other neu- rodegenerative disorders. This would translate into a better patient management, for example, by early access and improved efficacy of DMTs.
