### Review article

**Key points**

•• Disease staging systems are used across medicine to measure disease severity, estimate patient prognosis, determine eligibility for clinical trials and guide clinical care.

•• Staging systems provide a model of the natural history of a disease as well as a framework to validate new biomarkers and test new interventions.

•• Alzheimer disease (AD) has a long preclinical phase in which multiple biological changes are observable in the absence of symptoms; biomarker-based staging systems are well suited to detect and monitor these changes over the natural history of AD.

•• Biomarker-based staging using the topography of PET ligand uptake provides information about AD severity and aids prediction of clinical outcomes.

•• Recent work has highlighted the potential of fluid biomarker panels for the staging of AD and the development of plasma biomarker panels will substantially increase the accessibility of biomarker-based AD staging.

•• Biomarker-based AD staging might help to guide clinical decision- making; for example, evidence suggests that individuals with lower levels of tau tracer uptake on PET respond better to anti-amyloid monoclonal antibody therapy.

**Introduction** Alzheimer disease (AD) is a progressive neurodegenerative disease and is the primary cause of dementia worldwide, contributing substantially to global mortality and morbidity1. Pathologically, AD is defined by the presence of amyloid-β (Aβ) plaques and neurofibrillary tangles (NFTs) composed of hyperphosphorylated tau (p-tau) in the brain 2,3 . Notably, AD pathology starts to accumulate in the brain approximately 10–20 years before the onset of symptoms (the preclinical phase of the disease). The clinical syndrome typically associated with AD pathology is characterized by insidious onset, gradual progression and, usually, early amnestic symptoms4. However, studies have shown that 15–30% of individuals with a clinical diagnosis of probable AD dementia (without AD biomarker support) have insufficient levels of Aβ plaques and NFTs at autopsy5 or on PET imaging6 to fulfil the criteria for AD, even when the diagnoses were made at expert centres. Such studies, in tandem with evidence from human genetics and observational cohorts 7–10 , have highlighted the need to include biomarkers reflecting the underlying pathology in the definition of AD2. In this framework, AD exists along a continuum, beginning with early, clinically silent pathological changes and progressing to initial symptoms and, ultimately, to AD dementia. In recent years, a large number of clinical trials have drawn atten- tion to the need for AD staging rather than simply noting the presence or absence of AD pathological changes. In this Review, we outline key concepts of disease staging in medicine and how they inform the ration- ale for biomarker-based staging of AD. We provide a state-of-the-art summary of the disease staging systems that are currently used for AD and consider future staging systems based on cerebrospinal fluid (CSF) or plasma biomarkers. We discuss how disease staging might aid

the design and interpretation of clinical trials and provide information beyond the dichotomization of disease into presence versus absence. We conclude by outlining the potential clinical implementation of biomarker-based AD staging and discuss the potential benefits and drawbacks of this approach.

**Disease staging** Measurement of disease severity is crucial in many fields of medi- cine to allow assessment of the accuracy of initial clinical diagnoses, direct patient care and determine when specific therapeutic interven- tions are likely to be most effective, among many other uses11 (Box 1). Disease staging systems offer a structure for gauging the severity of a disease through the identification of key milestones in the progres- sion of the disease. In oncology, the tumour–node–metastasis (TNM) staging system combines information about tumour size (T), spread to nearby lymph nodes (N) and spread to other organs beyond the primary site (M). For each letter, a number indicating the severity of the specific pathological feature is provided, and the letter–number combination (for example, T3N2M1) is used to assign a specific stage to the cancer12. Although the disease itself is a naturally continuous process, the TNM system allows the communication and study of cancer in a standardized manner and helps appropriate treatment strategies to be determined for different disease stages12. This example highlights a crucial function of disease staging systems: they provide a standardized language surrounding disease severity, which aids the interpretation of observational and interven- tional studies across different centres with different measurement techniques. Although dichotomization of disease into presence versus absence has many uses, most diseases occur on a continuum of severity. Disease staging systems are helpful to evaluate diagnostic accuracy, for example, in determining whether a new diagnostic test can identify early disease in addition to advanced disease. Many staging systems, including TNM staging and some AD staging systems, use the anatomi- cal distribution of pathology as a marker of disease severity: diseases with greater spread are considered to be more advanced.

**Current staging systems for AD** A disease staging system could help to guide the management of patients with AD. For example, evidence suggests that anti-amyloid therapies are likely to be most effective in early symptomatic disease, before severe tau pathology and neurodegeneration are present13. Even in individuals who are not eligible for disease-modifying interventions, clinical staging of dementia is helpful for counselling patients and their families about their prognosis, to allow them to prepare for the future and obtain additional assistance as needed14. Currently, bio- logical AD staging systems are based on neuropathology, which can be applied only after death, whereas in vivo staging of AD is based largely on cognitive symptoms. These two approaches are briefly outlined in this section.

**Clinical staging of dementia** The severity of cognitive symptoms lies on a continuum. Though not specific to AD, clinical staging of dementia symptoms allows the deline- ation of important features that characterize the disease experience for people living with dementia and their caregivers15. ## Principles of disease staging

One of the primary dificulties in various medical fields is determining the severity of a disease to guide patient care and assess the efectiveness of treatment. Disease staging methods categorize a disease on the basis of increasing levels of severity, whereby the later stages indicate a worse outcome. These staging techniques identify specific milestones in the progression of a disease that are detectable, reflect current or future symptomatic severity and, ideally, inform the selection of therapeutic interventions. The stages of a disease can be based on its clinical history, origin, anatomical distribution of pathology, or biological characteristics and can be determined through physical examination, biomarker testing or a combination of both. In the case of Alzheimer disease, biomarkers are currently used to aid in the identification of the disease, whereas severity is determined on the basis of the clinical manifestation. Positive amyloid-β and tau biomarkers can be observed at all stages of the disease, from the preclinical stage, when no cognitive impairment is evident, through mild cognitive impairment to dementia.

The CDR is a widely used clinical tool that assesses dementia sever- ity by interviewing the patient and a knowledgeable informant (if available). A global CDR score is determined on a scale from 0 to 3, with 0 indicating no dementia and increasing numbers denoting increased dementia severity18. A score of 0.5 (very mild dementia) is closely associated with the syndrome of mild cognitive impairment19, and CDR scores of 1, 2 and 3 denote mild, moderate and severe dementia, respectively. The 2018 NIA-AA clinical staging system also uses a numerical approach but is intended to apply only to individuals on the AD con- tinuum, that is, with biomarker evidence of AD pathological change 2,20 . In this clinical staging system, stage 1 indicates normal cognitive func- tion. Stage 2 also corresponds to cognitive function within the normal range plus subjective decline as reported by the participant, a history of decline from the previous level of functioning or evidence of mild neurobehavioural changes not attributed to life events. Stage 3 denotes cognitive performance below a specific range of functioning without significant impairment in the ability to perform activities of daily living. Stages 4, 5 and 6 correspond to mild, moderate and severe dementia, respectively. Although clinical stages provide important and accessible infor- mation to the patient and caregiver, clinical dementia severity is typi- cally the result of multiple neuropathological processes, especially with advanced age21. Neuropathological assessments can determine the severity of AD and also uncover other neurodegenerative diseases that may be contributing to the cognitive symptoms to varying degrees. In the next section, we briefly review neuropathological staging sys- tems for AD and consider how they have informed clinicopathological relationships.

**Neuropathological staging** Neuropathological evaluation at autopsy remains the gold standard for the definitive identification of AD. Post-mortem staging of AD is based on a semi-quantitative assessment of the anatomical distribution of specific pathological changes. The current 2012 NIA-AA staging model involves an ABC scoring system, which assigns stages to Aβ plaques (A) using the Thal system, NFTs according to the Braak stage (B) and neuritic plaques based on the Consortium to Establish a Registry for AD (CERAD) score 3,22

(C). The Thal staging system classifies Aβ plaque topography
into five stages23. Aβ appears first in the neocortex (phase 1), followed by the allocortex (in particular, the hippocampus; phase 2), the striatum and diencephalon (phase 3), brainstem nuclei (phase 4) and, finally, the cerebellum (phase 5) 23 . NFTs are staged using the Braak system, in which the pathology starts in the transentorhinal cortex (stage I) and progresses to the entorhinal cortex, hippocampus (stage II), infe- rior and mesial temporal cortex, including the amygdala (stage III), association cortices (stages IV and V), and primary sensory cortices (stage VI) 24,25 . Neuritic plaques — a feature of degenerating neurons that consist of an Aβ core surrounded by dystrophic neurites containing aggregated tau — are ranked by density in the neocortex (none, sparse, moderate or frequent) using the CERAD scoring system26, instead of being staged according to topographical distribution as in the Thal and Braak staging systems. To classify neuropathological change in an individual, an ABC score is assigned, ranging from A0 (indicating no Aβ plaques; Thal 0), B0 (no tau tangles; Braak 0), C0 (no neuritic plaques; CERAD 0) to A3 (Thal stage 4–5), B3 (Braak stage V–VI), C3 (frequent neuritic plaques). These ABC scores are then converted into four levels of AD neuropatho- logical change: none, low, intermediate or high. Braak stages III–IV and above, accompanied by significant Aβ and neuritic plaques (together indicating intermediate or high AD neuropathological change), are considered sufficient to explain dementia 3,22 . Therefore, AD staging systems can determine not only whether AD was present or absent (diagnosis) but also the severity of the disease, which can be used to examine the potential relationship to the symptoms. Despite the limitations of post-mortem assessments, these staging systems have nevertheless enabled AD diagnosis to be standardized27. Importantly, AD neuropathological change staging is reported inde- pendently of clinical history, such as the presence or stage of cognitive impairment. Because AD pathology accumulates for many years before the onset of AD symptoms, many individuals with AD pathology lack cog- nitive impairment28, perhaps reflecting some degree of cognitive reserve. Although neuropathological staging of AD is limited to post-mortem application, the cognition-independent nature of these staging systems has provided a precedent for the development of in vivo biomarker clas- sification systems that are similarly independent of cognitive status. One such classification system, the AT(N) system, groups AD biomarkers according to three broad categories: Aβ, tau and neurodegeneration29. Neurodegeneration is placed in parentheses to indicate that it is a feature of all neurodegenerative diseases and not specific to AD. Aβ biomark- ers include amyloid PET, CSF concentrations of Aβ (most frequently, Aβ42 or the Aβ42/40 ratio) and, most recently, plasma Aβ. Tau biomark- ers include tau PET and biofluid p-tau levels. Neurodegeneration bio- markers include 18 F-fluorodeoxyglucose PET, structural MRI and total tau, among other fluid markers of neuronal injury.

**The need for biomarker-based AD staging** For decades, AD was nearly synonymous with the clinical symptoms of progressive amnestic dementia30. However, neuropathological

examinations showed that relatively mild AD pathology was frequently observed in the brains of individuals without cognitive symptoms 31,32 or with mild cognitive symptoms that did not meet the criteria for dementia 33,34 and, conversely, that cognitive symptoms could be observed in the absence of AD pathology. With these studies came an understand- ing that the clinical manifestations associated with probable AD dementia are often the result of advanced pathological changes, especially substan- tial NFT pathology (Braak stages III–IV and above) 35 . In subsequent in vivo studies, elevated amyloid PET tracer uptake was frequently reported in individuals without cognitive impairment 28,36–38 , confirming that early AD-associated pathological changes are often asymptomatic. Well-established biomarker models suggest that the dementia stage of AD is the tail-end of a pathological process that has been under way for approximately two decades 7–10,39 . Although the survival time in individuals diagnosed with dementia varies depending on a number of factors, including sex, age and medical comorbidities, several esti- mates suggest that individuals live for approximately 6–10 years after a diagnosis of AD dementia 39–41 . The dementia phase of AD is estimated to account for about one-third of the natural history of the disease, and the fact that the majority of the disease course is clinically silent highlights the need to stage AD (and in particular the preclinical phase) using biomarkers that reflect AD neuropathology. Early proposals for biomarker staging of AD focused on the preclinical phase of AD, with different stages indicating more advanced disease42. According to the 2011 preclinical AD framework, stage 1 corresponds to a positive Aβ biomarker (PET or CSF) without evidence of neuronal injury or of subtle cognitive change, stage 2 corresponds to a positive Aβ biomarker with evidence of neuronal injury but without evidence of subtle cogni- tive change, and stage 3 indicates elevated Aβ, biomarker evidence of neurodegeneration and evidence of subtle cognitive change. The value of staging AD biologically is further underscored by the observation that AD pathology is more prevalent than dementia37. Therefore, the number of individuals who might benefit from AD-modifying therapies exceeds the number of individuals with symp- tomatic AD, provided that ongoing randomized controlled trials in preclinical populations (such as the AHEAD 3, AHEAD 45, DIAN-TU and TRAILBLAZER-ALZ 3 studies) provide evidence of efficacy. Overall, the long preclinical phase of AD can be framed as a window in which multiple different therapeutic strategies can be employed. Staging systems using AD biomarkers will be important tools to determine which individuals are likely to respond optimally to specific therapeutic strategies. Asymptomatic individuals with abnormal amyloid PET scans rep- resent a heterogeneous group with variable risk of clinical progression to mild cognitive impairment and/or all-cause dementia. However, multicentre longitudinal studies indicate that asymptomatic individu- als with abnormal amyloid PET and tau PET biomarkers have a high risk of cognitive decline 43,44 . Crucially, the risk of clinical progression to all-cause dementia varies according to the topography of tau PET abnormality, suggesting that biological staging of AD in the preclini- cal phase has important implications for understanding the risk of cognitive decline. Biomarker-based disease staging not only has greater sensitiv- ity than clinical assessments owing to the ability to detect preclini- cal changes but also has greater specificity and could enable us to estimate what proportion of cognitive impairment is attributable to AD and what proportion is attributable to other causes. Because dementia is frequently associated with multiple co-existing neuro- pathological diseases21, understanding when AD is the likely cause of symptoms will be crucial given that Aβ positivity might be incidental

in some individuals with clinical dementia45. The recent development of biofluid assays for α-synuclein, a common non-AD pathology, has highlighted how non-AD co-pathologies, such as Lewy body disease, can contribute to cognitive decline in individuals with abnormal Aβ and tau biomarkers 46,47 . As biomarkers for other neurodegenerative diseases continue to develop 48–50 , biomarker-based disease staging stands to contribute to personalized medicine approaches for AD and other neurodegenerative diseases.

**PET-based disease staging** Advances in molecular imaging are anticipated to help us refine current diagnostic classification systems for AD 48,51 . Although most research to date has concentrated on categorizing amyloid PET and tau PET scans as either positive or negative, the spatial resolution of PET also allows staging based on the anatomical distribution of pathology. Staging systems have the potential to provide additional information by utiliz- ing the topographical distribution of PET uptake, which can aid in the monitoring of patients during AD progression. Because Aβ and tau are the hallmark pathologies of AD, staging of AD according to these pathologies could provide more precise estimates of AD severity than other PET biomarkers of neurodegeneration52 or neuroinflammation53, which might be abnormal in other neurodegenerative diseases.

**Amyloid PET** Several publications have proposed data-driven staging systems based on the regional burden of Aβ plaques imaged by PET. Although the pre- cise sequence of regional Aβ accumulation might vary slightly between studies and populations (for example, sporadic versus autosomal domi- nant AD), the general pattern indicates initial accumulation in medial neocortical association cortices, such as the medial prefrontal cortex, posterior cingulate and precuneus, followed by sensorimotor regions and the striatum 54–56 . However, achieving replicability across different radioligands, cohorts and analytical methods can be challenging56. One study of over 650 participants from the AD Neuroimaging Initiative identified four stages of amyloid PET tracer uptake, with the later stages being observed more frequently in individuals with AD dementia57. Subsequent studies based on this staging model identified longitudinal trajectories of Aβ deposition58 and specific patterns of future cognitive decline in relation to the baseline amyloid PET stage of a participant59. Furthermore, a multicentre study involving over 3,000 individuals scanned with different radioligands found that stages based on regional Aβ distribution were associated with distinct risk pro- files for cognitive decline60. Another study in which the spatial extent of Aβ pathology was staged by amyloid PET also reported associations between the amyloid PET stage and elevated CSF p-tau concentra- tions, tau PET binding patterns and risk of future cognitive decline61. A further study indicated that the number of ‘positive’ brain regions on amyloid PET visual reads correlated closely with global tracer uptake values62. These studies suggest that staging systems based on regional Aβ pathology provide important information about biomarker changes in early disease as well as prognostic information for future cognitive decline. However, a limitation of amyloid PET staging is that tracer uptake values plateau in the early symptomatic stages of AD 63,64 , so its utility for staging might be limited beyond the preclinical stage. An alternative to topographical staging of Aβ pathology is the measurement of global Aβ burden, facilitated by the Centiloid scale. This scale is a framework to standardize amyloid PET standardized uptake value ratios (SUVRs) measured with different imaging agents65. A Centiloid value of 0 represents the mean of a group of Aβ-negative

young adults (under 45 years of age), whereas a Centiloid value of 100 represents the mean of a group of individuals with mild-to-moderate AD dementia65. Centiloid values can exceed 100 but rarely go consider- ably below 0. Various cut-off points for amyloid PET positivity on the Centiloid scale have been proposed but most converge around 20–25 Centiloids66, suggesting that the majority of the Centiloid scale exists within the ‘amyloid-positive range’, which might provide additional information about the natural history of AD beyond dichotomiza- tion. Case-to-autopsy studies have suggested that a Centiloid value above 20 is associated with at least moderate density of Aβ plaques and Centiloid values above 50 are strongly associated with a neuropatho- logical diagnosis of AD, including substantial NFT and neuritic plaque pathology67. Furthermore, 19 Centiloids was identified as the point at which amyloid PET SUVRs reliably began to increase over time68, and a smaller study identified 24 Centiloids as a threshold where amyloid PET SUVRs became more likely to increase69. These findings indicate that staging of AD based on the magnitude rather than the regional distribution of Aβ pathology can provide insights into the severity of AD-related biological change and risk of cognitive decline.

**Tau PET** The validation of tau PET radioligands has benefited substantially from knowledge of the canonical distributions of NFT pathology described in autopsy studies. Several tau PET studies provided evidence that the distribution of tracer uptake aligns with Braak stages (with most studies combining stages I–II, III–IV and V–VI) 70–74 . Early-stage tau pathology can be observed in individuals without cognitive impairment, either in the presence or absence of notable Aβ pathology 43,63,72,75,76 . An elevated tau PET signal in the medial temporal regions in the absence of Aβ pathology might indicate primary age-related tauopathy 77–79 . Markedly increased tau tracer uptake in regions corresponding to Braak stages III–IV (tem- poral neocortex and association cortices) is predominantly observed in individuals with abnormal Aβ biomarkers and is typically accompanied by at least mild cognitive symptoms 63,75 , and increased tracer uptake in brain regions corresponding to advanced Braak stages (association

cortices and primary sensory cortices) is predominantly observed in individuals with Aβ positivity and cognitive impairment 63,75,80 . These findings extend observations from autopsy studies, which suggest that advanced Braak stages are largely incompatible with normal cognitive function 81,82 . Taken together, these results indicate that the clinical mani- festations of AD represent advanced AD neuropathological changes83 and highlight the utility of tau PET for the staging of AD severity. Owing to the close association of tau pathology with disease symptoms, tau PET-based disease staging has considerable potential to estimate the risk of clinical deterioration. In a multicentre study, asymptomatic individuals with an elevated neocortical tau PET signal had an increased risk of clinical progression to all-cause dementia compared with individuals in whom the signal was restricted to the medial temporal lobe43. Rates of cognitive decline were also higher in individuals with elevated neocortical tau than in other groups. By contrast, asymptomatic individuals with imaging-based evidence of isolated Aβ pathology without concomitant tau pathology had rela- tively low rates of cognitive decline and clinical progression43. These results, together with other longitudinal tau PET prediction studies 84,85 , highlight tau PET-based staging as a useful strategy for the prediction of clinical outcomes. To model the natural history of AD, disease staging systems using tau PET-based staging systems can be combined with other techniques to investigate changes in a range of biomarkers in relation to the pro- gression of tau pathology. For example, a study in which tau pathology was staged using the PET tracer 18 F-MK-6240 found that both amyloid PET SUVRs and various p-tau biomarkers in CSF plateaued at Braak stage IV63 (Fig. 1). Subtle memory decline could be observed at the group level starting at PET-based Braak stage II, and more advanced PET-based Braak stages seemed to be incompatible with normal cog- nition. Knowledge of biomarker-based disease stages would also be useful for future studies aiming to validate novel biomarkers and understand their association with AD severity86. Similar to amyloid PET, tau PET-based staging need not be restricted to the topographical distribution of tau pathology.

Alternative methods include assigning stages based on quantitative values within specific meta-regions as was performed in the phase II and III randomized trials of the anti-amyloid monoclonal antibody donanemab 13,87 . Although we would anticipate reasonable correspond- ence between disease staging systems based on the regional extent of pathology and quantitative values within specific regions, these two approaches differ in several ways. Staging based on regional abnormal- ity is likely to be more sensitive to early disease; for example, the early accumulation of tau in small regions of the medial temporal lobe is not detectable using larger temporal regions of interest (ROIs), which dilute the medial temporal signal 63,88 . Uptake within a reporter ROI might also be helpful in later disease; for example, once an individual reaches Braak stage VI, only quantitative values can be used to indicate more advanced disease. Therefore, the optimal staging methodology will probably depend on the population being evaluated. However, recent work has focused on quantifying PET tracer uptake without the need to specify an a priori ROI 89–91 , which might circumvent some of the limitations of both regional and global approaches. Proof of concept for the utility of disease staging based on biomarkers was provided by the donanemab randomized controlled trials: in both phase II87 and phase III13 trials, individuals with intermediate tau PET tracer uptake had better responses to donanemab therapy than did individuals with more advanced disease. These results lend support to the idea that anti-amyloid therapy is likely to be most beneficial early in the disease course before substantial tau accumulation and neurodegeneration have taken place. The capacity of biomarker-based disease staging to determine patient eligibility for specific interventions highlights the need for more accessible biomarkers. The need for amyloid PET to confirm the presence of Aβ plaque pathology, tau PET to stage disease and multiple MRI scans to monitor for adverse events places a large burden on both patients and the health-care system and severely limits access to treat- ment for patients in non-urban areas. As we discuss in the next section, fluid biomarkers (especially blood-based biomarkers) can measure multiple different pathologies at once from a single sample and might circumvent the need for multiple PET scans for the majority of patients.

**Fluid biomarker-based disease staging** An alternative to imaging techniques for staging AD in vivo is the use of fluid (CSF and blood) biomarkers. Multiple fluid biomarkers have been developed that demonstrate strong associations with AD neuropathology and PET measures of Aβ92 and tau burden as well as with clinical diagnosis and prognosis (reviewed elsewhere48). Of note, fluid biomarkers reflect the balance between production, secretion or release, and clearance of the analytes of interest at the time of fluid collection, which correlates with but differs from imaging measures, which visualize the lifetime accumulation of pathology93. In some stud- ies, fluid measures have been demonstrated to detect Aβ pathology earlier than amyloid PET 94,95 . Therefore, individuals with abnormal fluid Aβ biomarkers but normal amyloid PET might have very early AD pathology, and fluid biomarkers could enable staging to start earlier than with imaging measures. However, the main advantages of fluid biomarkers are logistical: compared with imaging methods, they need less complex infrastructure and are considerably cheaper, and multi- ple biomarkers can be measured using a single biofluid sample. Fluid biomarker panels, which include multiplexed immunoassays or mass spectrometry-based measurements, could be useful in AD staging. Studies in autosomal dominant96 and sporadic AD 63,86,97 indicate that levels of different p-tau species increase at different stages of

the disease88. Plasma and CSF p-tau231 levels are thought to increase early in the disease course and are associated with Aβ pathology 97–101 . Elevations in plasma and CSF p-tau217 levels also correlate with Aβ pathology, even in cognitively healthy individuals with preclinical AD 102–107 . In addition, p-tau217 can identify NFT pathology at the earli- est stages 99,102–105,108–110 . Plasma p-tau217 levels increase longitudinally, making the mean fold change in the symptomatic stage of AD higher than for p-tau181 and p-tau231 (ref. 98). However, p-tau181, p-tau217 and p-tau231 biomarkers are all limited in their ability to differentiate between intermediate (Braak III–IV) and late (Braak V–VI) stages of tau accumulation 63,111,112 . CSF 86,96,111,113 and plasma86 p-tau205 levels are altered in the later stages of AD and are more strongly associated with tau PET measures than are p-tau231 or p-tau217. The same is true for the microtubule-binding region (MTBR) tau variants MTBR-tau243 (refs. 114,115) and tau368 (refs. 116,117). Other fluid tau biomarkers, including p-tau235 (ref. 118) and N-terminal tau biomarkers, such as NTA 119,120 and NT1 (refs. 121,122), also show increases within the AD continuum. Overall, more research is needed to further validate the ‘late’ fluid biomarkers that are most closely related to NFT pathology. Because the levels of some biomarkers, such as CSF Aβ42, p-tau181, p-tau217 and p-tau231 (refs. 63,96,123), become abnormal very early in AD and subsequently plateau, the use of single analytes might provide limited information regarding the severity of disease. Previous studies have proposed that tau undergoes a sequential phosphorylation process in AD, in which phosphorylation of specific sites primes phosphorylation at other sites124. A neuropathological study demonstrated that the pattern of tau hyperphosphorylation was associated with different stages of NFT maturity125. In one study, CSF Aβ42/40, p-tau217, p-tau205, MTBR-tau243 and non-phosphorylated tau were used to create a staging model for the whole AD continuum in two independent cohorts112. The CSF biomarkers became abnor- mal in a similar order in all participants, starting with reductions in the Aβ42/40 ratio, followed sequentially by increases in p-tau217, p-tau205, MTBR-tau243 and, finally, non-phosphorylated tau. This panel of CSF biomarkers was used to stage AD, and more severe stages were associ- ated with a higher rate of amyloid and tau PET positivity and cognitive impairment. The CSF stages also predicted the longitudinal trajectories of other biomarkers as well as clinical progression. Translation of this approach to blood-based biomarkers could enable AD staging based on a single blood sample126 (Fig. 2). Although fluid biomarkers are highly promising, important limi- tations must be addressed. Different assays designed to measure the same analyte might vary in their performance 92,108,127–130 ; therefore, a staging model may need to be assay specific. Some common medi- cal comorbidities could affect fluid biomarker levels (for example, chronic kidney disease in the case of blood biomarkers 131–133 or white matter lesions in the case of CSF biomarkers134) although this prob- lem might be mitigated through the use of biomarker ratios rather than absolute values 135,136 . Pre-analytical conditions, such as how the biomarker is stored or how many times it is thawed and re-frozen, may affect biomarker values, and best practices should be followed to minimize these concerns 137,138 . Furthermore, in general, the mean fold change between healthy controls and individuals with AD is lower in plasma than in CSF 130,139,140 , especially for plasma Aβ42/40 (ref. 140). **Subtypes versus staging: space and time** The shift towards a biological definition of AD2 has allowed the rec- ognition of a diverse range of clinical syndromes secondary to Aβ and tau pathologies. Traditionally, AD was linked predominantly to amnestic-predominant dementia144, but retrospective studies identi- fied AD pathology as the cause of various non-amnestic focal cortical syndromes, including posterior cortical atrophy, primary progressive aphasia, behavioural variant frontotemporal dementia and corticobasal syndrome 145–147 . With the advent of tau PET, seminal studies suggested that variations in the topographical distribution of NFTs were closely associated with different clinical presentations of AD 148,149 , which has now been confirmed for a diverse range of clinical syndromes using tau PET 150–155 and neuropathological assessments156. These studies high- lighted substantial individual-level variability in the topographical distribution of tau pathology, diverging from the group-level averages described in the Braak staging system. Multicentre tau PET studies have provided strong evidence for the existence of disease subtypes, classified according to the distribution

of tau PET signal. One study involving over 2,300 individuals identi- fied four distinct patterns of tau accumulation, each associated with specific clinically relevant characteristics and prognostic features157. In addition, a multicentre study revealed unique patterns of neocortical tau PET tracer uptake in individuals with preclinical AD, character- ized by either asymmetrical distribution in the temporal lobe or high uptake in the precuneus158. These findings support the suggestion that multiple AD subtypes exist (at least as characterized by NFT distribu- tion patterns) and, importantly, are more prevalent than previously estimated158. Although several studies have highlighted considerable variability in tau accumulation, a consistent general pattern is observed, con- sisting of initial accumulation in the medial temporal lobe, followed by aggregation in the temporal neocortex, association cortex and, finally, the primary sensory cortices 63,75,157 . This pattern might serve as a foundation for tau PET-based AD staging despite the acknowledged heterogeneity of the disease. Alternatively, in vivo staging systems could be developed for different subtypes of AD if the existing medial temporal–widespread temporal–primary sensory staging system proves not to be useful. Another potential option in clinical settings would be to classify tau PET scans visually, which circumvents the problem of differences in regional uptake patterns, or to employ meth- ods to quantify tau PET tracer uptake without the need to specify an a priori ROI 89–91 . The existence of disease subtypes, as defined by tau PET pat- terns or other subtyping systems159, does not negate the utility of disease staging systems. Subtyping and staging have different goals in the study of AD as illustrated by individuals with different clini- cal variants of AD. Individuals might have similar degrees of global cognitive impairment (for example, CDR score of 0.5) but differ dra- matically in terms of the focus (for example, amnestic-predominant or visuospatial-predominant). Furthermore, such individuals could be at a similar Braak stage (for example, stage V) with different relative distributions of pathology within brain regions that encompass stage V. In such cases, subtyping provides information that is not captured by staging. Nevertheless, disease staging systems are well suited for understanding the natural history of AD and identifying important

points in the disease course that might respond to specific therapeutic interventions.

**Clinical trial applications** From a biomarker-based staging perspective, many disease-modifying trials conducted in recent years have targeted individuals with mild clinical symptoms but severe AD pathology 87,160–162 . As reviewed above, by the time an individual is symptomatic, advanced AD pathological changes, including mid-stage to late-stage neocortical tau pathology, are likely to be present 7,8 . Published data from clinical trials indicate that many other bio- logical changes had already taken place when symptomatic individuals were enrolled. In Fig. 3, we illustrate this concept by assigning Centiloid values to individuals from the Translational Biomarkers in Aging and Dementia (TRIAD) cohort163 on the basis of their global amyloid PET SUVR and plotting abnormalities in other disease features, including CSF Aβ42/40 and p-tau217, medial temporal and neocortical tau PET signal, and neurodegeneration and cognitive decline, in relation to Centiloid values. At the time of enrolment in published anti-amyloid monoclonal antibody trials, the participants probably already showed marked changes in p-tau217, medial temporal tau and neocortical tau, and neurodegeneration had already occurred. According to this model, randomized trials targeting ‘early’ sympto- matic AD recruited individuals with advanced AD pathological changes in the brain, including Aβ and tau pathology and neurodegeneration. These findings suggest that disease-modifying interventions have been timed

suboptimally in the studies to date. A post-mortem examination of a patient treated with the anti-amyloid drug aducanumab revealed sparse Aβ plaques but advanced NFT pathology164 (Braak stage V). Although the patient had a lower density of neurofibrillary changes compared with a reference group of individuals at Braak stages V–VI164, Aβ plaque reduction in this advanced disease stage might have only mild effects on NFT aggregation. Additional case-to-autopsy studies of individuals treated with anti-amyloid therapies will refine our understanding of the effects of anti-amyloid treatment on downstream tau accumulation; mixed results have been reported with tau PET to date 13,87,160 . Figure 3 also provides examples of ongoing anti-amyloid drug trials that target much earlier AD pathological change in the asymp- tomatic phase of the disease, such as the AHEAD 3 and AHEAD 45 studies165. DIAN-TU, a study to test the anti-amyloid drug lecanemab and the anti-tau agent E2814 in asymptomatic individuals with domi- nantly inherited AD, is also in progress166. The results of these trials will not be known for several years, but targeting earlier disease stages is expected to result in better outcomes as irreversible processes, such as neurodegeneration, will have unfolded to a lesser degree at these stages. Despite negative results for primary outcomes in the A4 study167, which tested an antibody against soluble Aβ in individuals with preclinical AD, the agents that are being used in the ongoing trials have already demonstrated evidence of substantial Aβ plaque reduction and clinical benefit. **Limitations and unanswered questions** The limitations of disease staging must be considered. Staging systems are simplifications based on key disease features that tend to happen in sequence and, consequently, no single staging system will apply to every individual. For example, the existence of tau PET subtypes and atypical clinical presentations of AD are reminders that disease staging systems cannot explain all of the important features of AD. Furthermore, most diseases are naturally continuous processes and the selection of specific milestones might, thus, be artificial. A simi- lar concern is often raised with biomarker dichotomization; disease staging at least partially attenuates this concern through the use of multiple stages. Reproducibility is another concern. For example, although increasing the number of stages provides a more granular picture of the natural history of a disease, it can also decrease reproducibility. This phenomenon has been noted for Braak staging at autopsy180, hence the collapsing of stages I–II, III–IV and V–VI to increase agreement between pathologists and academic centres24. Similarly, the simulta- neous use of multiple in vivo biomarker categories opens the door to lower reproducibility owing to measurement error. One advantage of staging systems is their ability to provide a common language for disease severity that can be understood across centres, but more work is needed to determine the extent of inter-method biomarker stage agreement, for example, whether an individual will be classified as the same stage using different tau PET ligands. Another limitation of AD staging is the relatively low amount of longitudinal biomarker staging data beyond amyloid PET. Our understanding of relative and absolute risks of biological AD stage

progression with respect to age, apolipoprotein E ε4 carrier status, and other clinical and demographic factors is likely to be refined in the com- ing years. Correspondingly, determining the association of biological AD stage with relative and absolute risk of dementia, hospitalization and mortality181 will be necessary to inform clinical practice and counsel patients. Data from representative samples are required for accurate clinical translation as individuals recruited from community-based studies might have more medical (and neurological) comorbidities than some highly selected research cohorts21, which could complicate the relationship between AD biomarker stage and dementia stage. Importantly, biomarker-based staging should complement but not replace clinical staging of dementia. In fact, discordance between the clinical dementia stage and the AD biomarker stage might provide valuable information. Brain and/or cognitive resilience is expected to modify associations between biological AD severity and severity of cognitive impairment; for example, it is conceivable that individuals with exceptional cognitive reserve (related to education or other social determinants of health) will display less severe cognitive symptoms than would be expected based on their biological stage 182–184 . Several questions remain unanswered regarding the biomarker-based staging of AD. For example, which biomarker-based staging system is most predictive of clinical deterioration? In this con- text, it would be instructive to evaluate whether tau PET-based staging provides meaningful prognostic information above and beyond amy- loid PET staging and, if so, whether this is mediated by clinical impair- ment at baseline. The use of both amyloid PET-based and tau PET-based staging might be particularly beneficial in preclinical disease as, at later stages, amyloid PET measurements will have plateaued and are unlikely to be prognostically meaningful. Furthermore, because amyloid PET and tau PET signals evolve in an interrelated fashion, the additional information that comes from staging with both biomarkers should be evaluated empirically. Another question pertains to the context of use for biomarker- based disease staging in AD. Staging systems can aid disease diagnosis, determine clinical trial eligibility, estimate patient prognosis and pre- dict response to therapy, among many other potential uses. Therefore, the details of which staging system to employ will differ according to the context of use. For example, in individuals without cognitive impairment, trial eligibility might best be determined using amy- loid PET-based staging, whereas in those with cognitive impairment, tau PET-based staging might be more useful. If the goal is to predict cognitive deterioration, tau PET-based staging may be more appropri- ate than amyloid PET-based staging. Longitudinal studies are needed to compare the performance of fluid biomarker-based staging sys- tems with more established PET staging systems. Finally, in diagnostic settings, biomarkers, such as amyloid PET, fluid Aβ42/40 or p-tau217, might be best placed to indicate the presence or absence of AD, with tau PET-based staging indicating the extent to which AD is driving a clinical phenotype.

**Conclusions** Advances in biomarker-based in vivo staging of AD are increasing our understanding of the natural history of AD and could inform the selec- tion of participants for disease-modifying drug trials. The application of disease staging systems for AD will also provide increased sensitivity to detect early disease, for example, by examining early tau PET signals or assessing fluid biomarkers that are elevated early in the disease course such as p-tau231 and p-tau217. In turn, other biomarkers that become elevated late in the natural history of AD, such as neocortical

tau PET tracer uptake, p-tau205 in CSF 111,185 , and plasma86 and CSF levels of the MTBR fragments tau368 (ref. 116) and MTBR-tau243 (ref. 115), can help increase specificity for AD and indicate the presence of advanced disease. Further development and validation of plasma biomarker signatures will substantially increase the accessibility of AD staging systems in clinical settings. Caution and care must be applied when translating novel biomarkers to unselected populations, particularly if they consist of groups that have been traditionally under-represented in medical research 133,186,187 .
