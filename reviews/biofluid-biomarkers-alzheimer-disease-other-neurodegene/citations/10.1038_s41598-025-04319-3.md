**1,2** **, Erin M. Jonaitis** **1,2,3** **, Carol A. Van Hulle,** **Rachael E. Wilson, Gwendlyn Kollmorgen4, Clara Quijano-Rubio5, Ozioma C. Okonkwo,** **Barbara B. Bendlin, Sanjay Asthana** **1,2,3** **, Kaj Blennow** **6,7** **, Henrik Zetterberg &** **Sterling C. Johnson**

**Amyloid burden impacts cognitive decline in the pre-dementia stages of Alzheimer’s disease (AD),** **but there remains significant variability in cognitive trajectories that may be explainable by markers** **of synaptic function and neurodegeneration. Leveraging longitudinal data from two harmonized,** **at-risk but predominantly unimpaired cohorts, we examined how several proteins measured from** **cerebrospinal fluid (CSF) differ by amyloid status, cognitive status, and impact cognitive decline** **measured using a Preclinical Alzheimer’s Cognitive Composite. Four hundred and thirty-four** **individuals provided CSF biomarkers of amyloid-beta42 (ab42), phosphorylated tau (181), from** **which we identified Aβ + individuals using a cutoff based on a p-tau181/Aβ42 ratio. Other markers** **of neurodegeneration (N) included neurofilament light, neurogranin, SNAP-25, and NPTX2. Most** **biomarkers of N were higher in the Aβ + group and lower in cognitively unimpaired individuals, though** **NPTX2 exhibited the opposite pattern. Even in the face of Aβ+, NPTX2 was higher in individuals** **who showed slower rates of cognitive decline, suggesting NPTX2 may be associated with cognitive** **resilience. Moreover, a SNAP-25/NPTX2 ratio of synaptic dysfunction explained more variance in** **cognitive decline than other biomarkers alone. This work provides support for potentially useful** **biomarkers of synaptic function implicated in the clinical syndrome of AD, aiding in future diagnosis** **and staging efforts.**

#### Keywords Cognitive decline, Amyloid, Alzheimer’s disease, Synaptic dysfunction

Alzheimer’s disease (AD) is characterized by biological changes that lead to cognitive and functional decline 1–3 . Amyloid-beta plaques (Aβ) and neurofibrillary tau tangles (ptau) that accumulate in the brain are Core Biomarkers of AD; with the main vehicles for diagnostic and staging criteria in the revised diagnostic and staging framework being amyloid-PET (a Core 1 marker) tau-PET imaging (a Core 2 biomarker, indicated with T2) 4 . However, PET imaging that accurately identifies Core A and T2 biomarkers may not always be available, and we propose that biological staging may be informed by the degree of neurodegeneration or synaptic loss ascertained in samples of cerebrospinal fluid (CSF). Here we examine several CSF biomarkers in addition to Core 1 A and T1 markers which may impact trajectories of cognition associated with advancing stages of cognitive and functional decline in a sample at higher risk for AD.

One candidate CSF biomarker of neurodegeneration included in the recent revised criteria for diagnosis and staging of AD is neurofilament light (NfL) 4 . NfL is considered a “non-specific” marker associated with AD pathophysiology that is released by cells as damage occurs, with higher CSF levels of NfL indicating neurodegeneration and increasing likelihood of AD 5–7 , and lower cognitive scores 8,9 . Though NfL is an important indicator of neuronal structure, it does not provide information on synaptic dysfunction that may relate to AD progression. Here, we examine how, in addition to AD-specific biomarkers (A and T1) and non-specific NfL, CSF markers of synaptic dysfunction may be useful in understanding the time course of AD and associated observable clinical features. Several synaptic proteins show promise as biomarkers of non-specific processes involved in both the pathophysiological and clinical syndrome of AD10. Here, we focus on three such proteins: neurogranin (Ng), synaptosomal-associated protein 25 (SNAP-25), and neuronal pentraxin 2 (NPTX2). Ng is a post-synaptic neural protein that contributes to cell signaling. Ng is implicated in synaptic plasticity and is related to learning and memory processes as well as other markers of neurodegeneration 11,12 . SNAP-25 plays a critical role in pre- synaptic functioning and intracellular transport13, with concentrations of CSF SNAP-25 higher in patients with an MCI or dementia diagnosis, and cognitively unimpaired people who are Aβ+ 10 . NPTX2, part of a family of three extracellular scaffolding proteins, influences synaptic function and neurotransmission14. Lower concentrations of CSF NPTX2 suggest synaptic dysfunction, with CSF NPTX2 decreasing at a faster rate in patients with MCI compared to healthy controls15, as well as being significantly lower in participants with post-mortem diagnosis of AD compared to controls16, In addition, the ratio of these two proteins (SNAP-25/NPTX2) has been shown to improve prediction of neurodegenerative outcomes and cognitive decline 10,17 . Our goal was to identify additional potentially useful CSF biomarkers that may be associated with diagnostic markers (A and T1) of AD as well as cognitive decline in a relatively young, predominantly healthy, yet at-risk cohort. To do so, we leveraged data from two longitudinal cohort studies enriched for AD risk beginning in midlife. CSF was assayed for core AD specific biomarkers Aβ42, p-tau181, as well as a nonspecific biomarker of neurodegeneration (NfL) and several synaptic functioning markers (Ng, SNAP-25, and NPTX2). Our first aim was to examine the association of biomarkers of neurodegeneration and synaptic dysfunction to each other and to known biomarkers of AD (Aβ42 and p-tau181), as well as compare how these same markers vary across age, cognitive status and Aβ status, using diagnostic hybrid ratio p-tau181/Aβ42 as a cutoff4. Our second aim was to examine whether NfL, Ng, SNAP-25, NPTX2, or SNAP-25/NPTX2 moderated Aβ status *age trajectories of cognitive decline using a locally derived Preclinical Alzheimer’s Cognitive Composite (PACC) 18 . To that end, we conducted a series of model comparisons between a base model that included Aβ status*age and five additional models adding a 3-way interaction of ‘other biomarker’*Aβ status*age (i.e., separate models for each of the biomarkers). Model comparisons were used to determine which neurodegeneration or synaptic dysfunction biomarker explained the most variability in cognitive decline relative to each other.

### Method

#### Study cohorts

Data were drawn from the Wisconsin Registry for Alzheimer’s Prevention (WRAP) and Wisconsin Alzheimer’s Disease Research Center (WADRC) using the November 2022 data freeze. WRAP is a prospective longitudinal cohort study that started in 2001. The cohort is enriched for AD risk by enrolling a high proportion of participants with a parental family history of AD (71.23%, with < 1% missing). Most WRAP participants did not have diagnosed dementia and were predominantly cognitively unimpaired (CU) and between ages 40–65 at enrollment (mean age 54 years). Follow-up study visits occur approximately every two years. Full study details are described elsewhere19. The WADRC study began enrollment in 2009 and comprises three cohorts of adults: CU adults aged 45–65 followed biennially and older CU adults followed annually, as well as adults of any age with either mild cognitive impairment (MCI) or dementia followed annually. The WADRC sample is also enriched for dementia risk by enrolling a high proportion with a parental history of dementia (60.27%, with < 1% missing). Follow-up visits occur yearly for those with MCI or dementia, and biannually for the CU individuals. At the time of the data freeze used for these analyses, 1753 from WRAP and 1085 from WADRC had enrolled in the respective main studies; Participants in both studies are invited to participate in a separate biomarker visit that includes lumbar puncture, MRI, and/or PET imaging. Human subjects participation in these studies was approved by the University of Wisconsin Health Sciences Institutional Review Board, in accordance with the Helsinki declaration. All participants provided informed consent.

#### Study participants

Of those participants who had completed at least one lumbar puncture, the selected CSF biomarker assays were available for 213 participants from WRAP and 210 participants from ADRC (see additional assay details in Sect.  2.3.2). This sample (n = 423) was used to address Aim 1. To facilitate model comparisons for Aim 2, only those with all CSF samples assayed for this particular set of analyses and at least one PACC score were included, leaving a total of N = 346 adults (40—85 years at cognitive baseline, n(%) = 235 female; 91% cognitively unimpaired, 7% MCI, or 2.5% dementia) with complete biomarker and cognitive data from WRAP (n = 183) and WADRC (n = 163) studies. Cognitive outcome: PACC The primary cognitive outcome was performance on a modified Preclinical Alzheimer Cognitive Composite (PACC) developed using locally-available tests similar to those in21. The PACC is a well-known composite used to assess cognitive function and is sensitive to the earliest stages of cognitive decline. PACC scores typically combine tests that assess learning, memory, executive function, and/or global cognition, and have been shown to be sensitive to preclinical cognitive decline in AD cohorts 18,21,22 . Our PACC score was comprised of the Rey AVLT total learning trials23, Wechsler Memory Scale–Revised Logical Memory Story A delayed recall score, cross-walked where necessary based on Craft Story performance 24,25 , and the (re- verse scored) Trail-Making Test Part B26. Higher scores on all tests indicate stronger performance on cognitive assessments. Each of the three scores were first standardized against the first available testing occasion for a cog- nitively unimpaired group before creating an unweighted average composite score, which was then converted to a standard scale score (mean = 100, s.d. = 15) for ease of interpretation. PACC creation for harmonized WRAP and ADRC data is described in detail elsewhere18.

Cognitive status determination Cognitive status was determined after each visit by a consensus review pro- cesses. For participants in the WADRC, a multidisciplinary committee reviews all data from each visit (e.g., neuropsychological testing, informant ratings, medications, etc.) and participants are categorized as cognitively unimpaired (CU), mild cognitive impairment (MCI), dementia, or other (non-MCI) impairment. In WRAP, participant visit data is reviewed first by an algorithm to flag people showing potential signs of clinical impair- ment; those participants’ data are reviewed by a multidisciplinary committee and status is categorized as CU, MCI, dementia, or other (non-MCI) impairment (see27 for details). In both studies, MCI and dementia ratings are based on NIA-AA criteria 28,29 without reference to biomarkers. We used the cognitive status at the time of

lumbar puncture to identify cognitive status groups. Of note, only 3 individuals were classified as “other im- paired”, and due to unknown etiology of impairment were excluded from analyses.

*Cerebrospinal fluid biomarkers* Lumbar puncture and CSF collection CSF samples were collected using a uniform protocol for WRAP and WADRC. At least 2-22 mL of CSF fluid was collected via gentle extraction into polypropylene syringes and com- bined into a single 30 mL polypropylene tube. Within 15 min from collection, samples were gently mixed and then centrifuged at 2000 g for 10 min at 4 °C; 0.5 mL CSF was aliquoted into 0.5 mL Sarstedt tubes. All samples were stored at − 80 °C.

CSF assays Samples collected in Sarstedt tubes through 2022 were sent to the Clinical Neurochemistry Labo- ratory, University of Gothenburg for assaying. Core AD biomarkers of p-tau181 and Aβ42 were measured using the Elecsys ® Phospho-Tau (181P) CSF and Elecsys βAmyloid (1-42) CSF assays on a fully automated Cobas ® e 601 instrument; the necessary quality control steps as outlined in the published methods and performance data were taken 30–33 . In addition to AD specific markers, we also assayed markers of neurodegeneration and synaptic dysfunction: NfL, Ng, SNAP-25, and NPTX2. These biomarkers were measured using the NeuroToolKit, a panel of robust prototype assays, on a Cobas e 411 instrument (both Roche Diagnostics International Ltd, Rotkreuz, Switzerland) using quality control checks in accordance with Roche recommendations. Of note, the follow- ing markers were assayed but not included in analyses (total Tau, α-Synuclein, glial fibrillary acidic protein, sTREM2, and YLK40). We also calculated the SNAP-25/NPTX2 ratio in addition to individual SNAP-25 and NPTX2 biomarkers. SNAP-25 shows higher concentrations in AD and NPTX2 shows lower concentrations, re- sulting in a ratio that likely amplifies information on synaptic functioning10. If participants completed multiple lumbar punctures, the most recent was selected for analysis.

p-tau181/Aβ42 ROC analysis Following34 and others 35,36 , amyloid positivity (i.e., Aβ+) was defined by a p-tau181/Aβ42 ratio cutoff determined from a receiver operating characteristic (ROC) analysis. ROC analyses used PiB-PET measured within 2 years of a CSF collection (N = 235, 90% cognitively unimpaired) as the stand- ard of comparison. Participants with a PiB-PET DVR ≥1.19 were considered amyloid positive (N = 57). ROC analyses were conducted using R package pROC37. The optimal threshold for p-tau181/Aβ42 discrimination was based on Youden’s Index38. ROC analyses indicated high diagnostic consistency between PiB-PET amyloid positivity and p-tau181/Aβ42. Area under the curve for p-tau181/Aβ42 was 0.969 (0.949–0.990) and the ROC derived threshold for biomarker positivity was 0.0195 for p-tau181/Aβ42 (87% negative agreement, 95% positive agreement). Individuals with a p-tau181/Aβ42 ratio greater than 0.0195 are considered Aβ +.

#### Statistical analyses

Analyses were conducted in SAS 9.4 39 with visualization conducted using R40. Sample characteristics in Table 1 are presented for the full sample and stratified by Aβ + and Aβ- group. Comparisons between Aβ + and Aβ- groups were conducted using independent t-tests or chi-square tests depending on the distribution of the variable. Raw values for biomarker concentrations (pg/mL) are shown in Table 1, but we converted all continuous biomarker concentrations into z-scores using the mean and s.d. for the entire sample prior to study analyses. This helps increase interpretation and compare standardized effects across biomarkers. To address Aim 1, we examined Spearman correlations of biomarkers with age, as well as among the biomarkers of interest overall and separately for the Aβ + and Aβ- groups. Next, we compared biomarker CSF concentrations of Aβ42, p-tau181, NfL, Ng, SNAP-25, NPTX2 as well as the two derived ratios (p-tau181/Aβ42 and SNAP-25/NPTX2) across cognitive status (CU, MCI, and Dementia) using ANOVAs. Finally, we compared biomarker concentrations across individuals who were determined to be Aβ + versus Aβ- (using the p-tau181/ Aβ42 cutoff described in 2.3.2.3). Cohen’s d effect sizes were calculated for two-group comparisons, and eta- squared (η2) for three-group comparisons; post hoc group comparisons for clinical diagnosis are described in Supplementary Table 1. For Aim 2 analyses, we used linear mixed effects models41 to investigate whether NfL, Ng, SNAP-25, NPTX2, or SNAP-25/NPTX2 was associated with cognitive decline on our modified PACC outcome in the presence of Aβ+ (i.e., p-tau181/Aβ42 above the threshold) and to examine which of these markers explained the most variability in cognitive decline. Specifically, after determining that person-level intercepts and age slopes (unstructured covariance) were appropriate random effects to include in all models, we compared an “amyloid only” model with each of five other “amyloid + biomarker” models. All six of these models used maximum likelihood estimation and included the following covariates as fixed effects: time (modeled using linear and quadratic age terms, centered at age = 60), and covariates of sex (reference = female); education level (reference = BA/BS or higher); time-varying practice (i.e., number of prior exposures to the PACC tests minus one); and time (years) between baseline PACC and the CSF sample used in these analyses. Because data came from two cohorts (WRAP and WADRC) and we utilized data from both CU/MCI individuals (n = 415) and those with dementia (n = 8) at the time of the lumbar puncture, we included cohort (effect coded so that 0 = average participant) and diagnosis (reference = CU/MCI) in all models as fixed effects covariates to adjust for mean level differences across cohort and diagnosis. Because we wished to compare the amyloid only model with those that include biomarkers, we limited the sample for Aim 2 to participants who had complete biomarker and cognitive data (n = 346). Model comparisons used the corrected Akaike Information Criteria42, which is indicated for non-nested models with small sample size. Conventionally, a change in AICc greater than 2 between two comparison models indicates that the model with the lower AICc has a superior model fit, with changes greater than 10 providing substantial support for the model with lower AICc 43,44 . In the presence of significant interactions, we calculated the simple effects of the biomarker of interest on cognition for each Aβ status group at varying ages using the “interactions” package in R45. In doing so, we were able to visualize and interpret both group differences and the quadratic age effects associated with each biomarker46.

### Results

#### Sample characteristics

Sample characteristics for those eligible for Aim 1 analyses (n = 423) are shown in Table 1 for the overall sample as well as by Aβ status group. Parallel descriptive statistics for individuals included in the Aim 2 analyses are in Supplemental Table 1. Patterns of descriptive characteristics were similar across both samples. Approximately 30% of the sample were Aβ+; those who were Aβ + tended to be older, had completed more cognitive testing occasions, and were less likely to be CU at baseline and more likely to have MCI. Additionally, we compared individuals who were in the limited sample (n = 346) to those who were not included (n = 77), with results indicating that those individuals included in the limited sample were more likely to be women, have completed more cognitive testing occasions, and have higher concentrations of Aβ42 and SNAP-25.

#### Biomarker comparisons and group differences

We first examined relations among biofluid concentrations and age (Supplemental Fig. 1). Age was significantly related to all biomarkers of interest, except NPTX2, which showed no relation with age. Spearman correlations of the standardized biomarkers in the full sample and stratified by Aβ-/Aβ + status are presented in Fig. 1. Overall, Aβ42, a core indicator of AD pathology, was positively related to Ng, SNAP-25, NPTX2 (with correlations ranging from 0.225 to 0.486), and inversely related to the SNAP-25/NPTX2 ratio (rho=−0.349). p-tau181 was also positively related to NfL, Ng, SNAP-25, NPTX2, and the SNAP-25/NPTX2 ratio, with correlations ranging from 0.258 to 0.925. The p-tau181/Aβ42, which we used to determine amyloid pathology, was positively related to NfL, Ng, SNAP-25, and the SNAP-25/NPTX2 ratio, with stronger correlations in the Aβ + group. Rhos ranged from 0.119 to 0.212 in the Aβ- group, and 0.399 to 0.507 in the Aβ + group. The synaptic proteins (Ng, SNAP- 25, and NPTX2) were all associated with each other and also with NfL, with rhos ranging from 0.199 to 0.882. Results for comparing biomarkers across group (Aβ-/Aβ+) and cognitive status are presented in Table 2, with post hoc comparisons in Supplemental Table 2. There were significant differences between Aβ-/Aβ + groups in all biomarkers with the exception of NPTX2. Aβ + individuals had higher concentrations on average of NfL, Ng, SNAP-25, and SNAP-25/NPTX2 ratios (see Supplemental Fig. 2 for boxplots of raw values); effect sizes for the overall group effects (eta-squared,η2) ranged from 0.017 (NPTX2) to 0.582 (p-tau181/Aβ42; Table  2) with Cohen’s d comparing effect sizes between Aβ+/Aβ- individuals ranging from − 1.00 to −2.555 for the same biomarkers, respectively. Biomarker concentrations also differed by cognitive status, such that individuals characterized as Cognitively Unimpaired at the visit closest to the CSF collection had significantly higher Aβ42 and lower p-tau181, p-tau181/Aβ42 ratio, NfL, and SNAP-25/NPTX2 ratio than both the MCI and Dementia groups. 2. Individuals identified as CU at CSF collection also had higher NPTX2 than the dementia group, and lower Ng and SNAP-25 than the MCI group. ### Discussion

Understanding pathophysiological correlates of Core 1 biomarkers of AD (Aβ42 and p-Tau181) and cognitive decline that implicate neurodegeneration or synaptic dysfunction could provide useful information for staging of AD. Here, we showed that the latter have perhaps stronger associations with decline than known biomarkers of AD and structural degradation (NfL) that are proposed in the revised NIA-AA criteria for diagnosis and staging of AD4. Pre- and post-synaptic biomarkers were first identified in CSF over twenty years ago47, but have only recently been a focus of AD research48. Characterizing these biomarkers in a mostly pre-clinical but at-risk sample may further elucidate patterns among biomarkers across multiple physiological processes that precede the occurrence of Core diagnostic biomarkers of AD. Comparisons of CSF biomarker concentrations by amyloid group (Aβ-/Aβ+), indicated that NfL, Ng, SNAP-25 and the SNAP-25/NPTX ratio were significantly higher in Aβ + individuals. In contrast, NPTX2 was independent of amyloid status. Previous work has found that NPTX2 may be higher in healthy, cognitively unimpaired individuals compared to those with subjective cognitive decline, MCI, and other dementias (frontotemporal dementia) 15,49 . Thus, NPTX2 may broadly identify synaptic resilience and/or loss across multiple diseases not specific to AD, and therefore would not vary by markers specific to AD. We also compared CSF biomarkers across cognitive status (CU, MCI, Dementia). As expected, cognitively unimpaired participants had significantly higher concentrations of Aβ42, and lower concentrations of p-tau181 and their combined effects (p-tau181/Aβ42), than individuals who were identified as MCI or with Dementia, with no differences between MCI and Dementia groups. This pattern persisted for NfL, a protein associated with structural change that is perhaps a downstream effect of amyloid burden. For the synaptic proteins (Ng and SNAP-25), CU individuals had lower concentrations than those with MCI, with no differences found between the Dementia group vs. others. There were no significant differences for NPTX2; it is possible that we were not able to identify potential differences in our sample, with relatively fewer MCI and Dementia cases. In addition, our sample was limited to those with objectively measured cognitive decline and did not identify other types of dementias. Future work in other cohorts may be better able to differentiate between CU groups and other more nuanced groups representing cognitive decline (i.e. participants with subjective cognitive decline). Our second goal was to identify whether CSF biomarkers could provide additional information on cognitive decline in the presence of amyloid positivity. To do so, we constructed a series of models which compared more established biomarkers associated with cognitive decline due to AD (p-tau181/Aβ42, NfL and Ng) to emerging biomarkers (SNAP-25, NPTX2) that have emerged more salient in recent works10. Using the same sample enables us to compare these models (using AIC), to determine if one biomarker better predicts cognitive decline than another. Though these findings need to be replicated in other samples, we provide strong support that CSF synaptic proteins are uniquely associated with cognitive decline over and above amyloid positivity in our at-risk cohort. Specifically, Ng, SNAP-25, NPTX2, SNAP-25/NPTX2 all improved the model fit relative to the base model (p-tau181/Aβ42 only) or a model that added a marker of neurodegeneration (NfL) suggesting that synaptic dysfunction may account for some of the variation in cognitive trajectories in the preclinical phases of AD clinical syndrome. Interestingly, even though NPTX2 did not differentiate cognitive status (CU, MCI, Dementia), it did perform better than p-tau181/Aβ42 alone in predicting cognitive decline, similar to15.

Synaptic communication could be a key factor in preclinical decline associated with AD clinical syndrome, and protecting synapse loss may provide resilience to cognitive decline in the face of physiological changes due to amyloid burden. A recent clinical study measuring changes in amyloid plaques among dominantly inherited Alzheimer’s disease showed that plaque removal (post-pharmacological intervention) improved CSF levels of Ng but not NfL in a relatively young (mean age 44) cohort50. It is important to note that our findings do not offer an underlying causal mechanism. It is possible that synaptic proteins SNAP-25 and NPTX2 could be indicators of other factors related to cognitive resilience, rather than affecting amyloid-related impairment directly. However, together with our findings, results from50 highlight the importance of collecting additional biomarkers beyond the Core diagnostic markers Aβ and T1 or T2 outlined in the revised NIA-AA framework; indeed, our results indicate that targeting synaptic function may prove useful and be a potential avenue for future research on the diagnosis and staging of AD. It is worth considering that the presence of synaptic markers in CSF, though tied to neurodegeneration, could also be tied to synaptic protein turnover, or the process of regular replacement in order to maintain function and to prevent damage51. Work using animal models indicates that the continuous synthesis and degradation of these synaptic proteins can also influence their levels in the CSF 51,52 . Nonetheless, lower levels of CSF SNAP- 25 and higher levels NPTX2 were indicative of slower rates of cognitive decline. It could be that, in the case of NPTX2, healthy turnover and use of this protein may be protective. The use of biomarker ratios in AD research is increasing17 as these ratios often show stronger associations with cognitive decline and cognitive status than the Core 1 and 2 biomarkers alone. Here, the SNAP-25/NPTX2 ratio utilized to indicate amplified effects of synaptic dysfunction showed the strongest delineation between cognitive status groups. SNAP-25/NPTX2 was able to identify CU participants in the “unimpaired” disease state with some clarity. Finally, SNAP-25/NPTX2 provided the best-fitting model of cognitive decline. Emerging evidence suggests that higher SNAP-25 differentially relates to AD clinical syndrome compared to other dementia diagnoses, suggesting that SNAP-25 could identify synaptic dysfunction associated with AD clinical syndrome with some specificity 10,48 , though results are mixed with SNAP-25 also showing relations with frontotemporal dementias49. Nonetheless, our results highlight the impact synaptic processes have on AD-related pathology and associated cognitive decline. Interestingly, each of the synaptic biomarker models also included significant quadratic effects for rates of cognitive decline, whereas the amyloid only and NfL models did not. Synaptic dysfunction appears to show stronger effects at older ages for markers of synaptic function and depending on amyloid status. It is possible that as brains age, there is an increased vulnerability to the effects of other biomarkers, even in the presence of amyloid. For example, estimates from the “p-tau181/Aβ42 + SNAP-25/NPTX2” model indicated that at age 60, the simple effect of SNAP-25/NPTX2 is −3.351, corresponding to a model-predicted PACC score of approximately 112 for a female participant with a BA level of education. By age 80, the simple effect of SNAP-25/NPTX2 increases nearly three-fold to − 11.323, corresponding to a model-predicted PACC score of approximately 96 for the same participant. Though we were able to use model comparisons to compare biomarkers of neurodegeneration or synaptic function in relation to cognitive decline in an at-risk AD cohort, we were not able to do so across different neurodegenerative diseases. NPTX2 in particular seems to be disease-agnostic, and shows reduced concentrations not only in AD, but also frontotemporal and Lewy body dementias 49,53 , as well as in some psychological disorders such as schizophrenia and anxiety 54,55 . In contrast, SNAP-25 and Ng are both known synaptic biomarkers with specific links to AD pathology and clinical syndromes48. We highlight the potential relative import of each for future clinical utility in AD diagnosis and staging, but are not able to understand relative salience of each across other known dementias. In addition to being limited to an at-risk for AD cohort, our group sizes of

Aβ + individuals were small. However, even in our relatively healthy cohorts, the addition of SNAP-25 and NPTX2 enhanced prediction of cognitive decline compared to amyloid positivity alone, suggesting that these two synaptic markers may provide information on potential mechanistic processes involved in decline, from which future researchers could base therapeutic intervention for multiple etiologies. Participants provided years of repeated measures cognitive data, yet often only one biomarker assessment was available. While this design can reveal important associations between biomarker levels and cognitive trajectories, establishing a casual pathway requires prospective longitudinal biomarker assessments. Our future work will include longitudinal biomarker assessments as they become available. Moreover, although we included two Core 1 AD biomarkers (Aβ42 and p-Tau181) measured in CSF, recent evidence suggests that more 56,57 contemporary biomarker assays (plasma p-Tau217) may prove useful in diagnosing and staging AD. As WRAP and ADRC continue to enroll new participants and collect increasingly cutting-edge biomarker data, we hope to expand on this work to include relations among synaptic biomarkers with PET and plasma based indicators of AD. Indeed, crucial advances using blood-based markers may provide a useful, and cost-effective, antidote to the more invasive measures of PET and CSF collection. In conclusion, in addition to known markers of neurodegeneration (NfL), key synaptic function markers – Ng, SNAP-25 and NPTX2 – may provide useful diagnostic and mechanistic information on AD diagnosis and staging. Future work will explore how these CSF proteins relate to other markers of AD pathology and clinical syndromes, including neuroimaging, blood-based markers, and also multiple cognitive outcomes.
