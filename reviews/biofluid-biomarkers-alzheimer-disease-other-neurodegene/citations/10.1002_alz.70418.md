**Abstract** **INTRODUCTION: The Consortium for Clarity in Alzheimer’s Disease and Related** Dementias Through Imaging (CLARiTI) Return of Results Core aims to develop tools and a framework for disclosing individual results at Alzheimer’s Disease Research Centers (ADRCs). An understanding of current disclosure practices is necessary to generate this protocol. **METHODS: All 37 ADRCs received a survey between January and April 2024; 36** provided valid responses. **RESULTS: Most ADRCs disclose diagnosis and cognitive results to participants with** impairment, and disclosure of biomarker data (e.g., amyloid and tau positron emis- sion tomography) has accelerated since 2019. Though less common, disclosure to unimpaired participants has increased since 2019. Motivators for disclosure include to thank participants, for recruitment/retention, and to help inform health-care deci- sions. Barriers include limited expertise and infrastructure, concerns about clinical actionability, and risks to participants. **DISCUSSION: The ADRC network is invested in sharing research results. While some** concerns remain, CLARiTI will critically evaluate a standardized approach to sharing these results.

### 1 BACKGROUND

Alzheimer’s disease and related dementias (ADRDs) pose significant personal and societal challenges with the aging of the US population.1 To meet this challenge, the National Institute on Aging (NIA) began funding Alzheimer’s Disease Research Centers (ADRCs) in 1984, with 37 centers currently established. ADRCs provide ADRD resources and education to local communities, train new investigators in the field, facilitate representative and inclusive recruitment into studies, and catalyze cutting-edge research around center-specific themes. Each center maintains a highly characterized longitudinal cohort of adults across the cognitive spectrum and with etiologies including Alzheimer’s disease, Lewy body disease, vascular injury, frontotempo- ral lobar degeneration, and others ([https://naccdata.org/requesting-](https://naccdata.org/requesting-) data/data-summary/uds). Annual assessments include demographic and health information; cognitive testing; neurological examination; and optional neuroimaging, blood draw, and other research proce- dures. These publicly accessible data are pooled across sites through the National Alzheimer’s Coordinating Center (NACC).2 The coordination of the ADRC network has played a part in earlier and more accurate identification and treatment of individuals at risk for cognitive decline. Advancements in biomarker testing3–5and the approval of disease-modifying treatments6,7(DMTs; e.g., anti-amyloid monoclonal antibodies) have shaped research being conducted within the network. The availability of DMTs underscores the importance of biomarker testing for cognitively impaired participants and may sug- gest future relevance for unimpaired participants. The benefits of AD biomarker testing also extend beyond eligibility for DMTs to include etiologic and prognostic clarification that may inform clinical, social, and financial decisions.8The ADRC Consortium for Clarity in ADRD Research Through Imaging (CLARiTI)9aims to leverage the ADRC net- work to capture heterogeneous ADRD profiles in a diverse sample through standardized imaging and plasma collection at each center.10 Driven by greater understanding of the meaning of biomarkers for disease risk and shifts in research attitudes toward bidirectional communication between studies and participants, ADRD research studies have increasingly sought to return neuropsychological and biomarker results to participants. The most recent NIA ADRC

request for applications requires sites to “assure appropriate disclo- sure of biomarker and other results.” Therefore, we must understand how results can be effectively, ethically, and pragmatically relayed back to participants. To date, most research on returning results has focused on apolipoprotein E (APOE) genetic information12–14and amyloid positron emission tomography (PET).15These studies demon- strate that such information is generally well understood and does not cause sustained or significant distress in participants across the cogni- tive spectrum.16,17However, these studies have important limitations. They include relatively homogenous samples of participants with high educational attainment, considerable resources, and low psychiatric burden, which limits the generalizability of results. The protocols for returning results are also time- and resource intensive, thus limiting scalability. While several frameworks have been developed,18–20no universal protocol exists. The CLARiTI Return of Results (RoR) Core, made up of interdisciplinary experts in disclosure from four ADRCs, aims to produce a flexible, community-informed “Toolkit” for return- ing individual research results at centers nationwide. The Toolkit will provide and evaluate the effectiveness of pre-test education to sup- port participants in making informed decisions about learning their results. Additionally, training materials and templates for communi- cating complex biomarker information will be offered. This work has the potential to improve recruitment and retention, build trust with research participants, and leverage the ADRC network in collecting detailed post-disclosure outcomes data in a national sample. Results will also inform biomarker disclosure approaches in clinical settings, in which imaging and plasma biomarkers are becoming increasingly com- mon for the purpose of etiologic clarification and determining eligibility for DMTs. A scalable, ethical CLARiTI RoR framework must be informed by the current disclosure practices of ADRCs and by their capacity, inter- est, and needs. In 2019, researchers from the Advisory Group on Risk Evidence Education in Dementia (AGREED)21,22surveyed the ADRCs about disclosure practices, motivations, and perceived barriers and facilitators. Given the accelerated developments in biomarker testing and the expansion of the ADRC network, an updated survey on current practices was warranted. Here, we report findings from a 2024 survey of the 37 established and exploratory centers.

**METHODS**

The RoR Core developed the current questionnaire based on the 21 Roberts et al. 2019 survey. Roberts et al. aimed to assess what information was shared with participants, how this information was communicated and by whom, and why sites elected to return—or not return—research results. Given the rapidly changing landscape of biomarkers since 2019, including the approval of multiple anti-amyloid therapies, the RoR Core created additional items to explore nuanced aspects of training for and the execution of returning research results. More specifically, domains covered the types of results returned (e.g., cognitive test results, research diagnosis, amyloid PET, tau PET, flu- orodeoxyglucose [FDG] PET, structural neuroimaging, APOE or other genetic results), by whom, to whom, and under what conditions (e.g., visit modality and length). We gathered additional details regard- ing factors motivating or facilitating the return of research results, compared to barriers or perceived risks. 23 The survey was coded in REDCap and disseminated by NACC to each of the 37 ADRCs between January and March 2024. Sites were asked to identify one individual to complete the survey based on familiarity with local practices around disclosure; each site therefore provided only one questionnaire. Of the 37 sites invited to partic- ipate, all provided responses; however, a review of the responses determined that one site provided invalid data. Descriptive statistics were generated in R version 4.4.2 and graphics generated by R Studio. The 36 sites represented the geographic distribution of the ADRCs nationally.

**RESEARCH IN CONTEXT**

1. **Systematic review:** The Consortium for Clarity in Alzheimer’s Disease Through Imaging (CLARiTI) Return of Results Core surveyed all 37 participating Alzheimer’s Disease Research Centers (ADRCs) regarding practices around returning individual research results (e.g., cog- nitive data, biomarkers) to participants. Specific details regarding the disclosure of amyloid and tau positron emission tomography results were also requested.
2. **Interpretation: Our findings demonstrate growing inter-** est in and implementation of individual results disclosure in the ADRC network. Centers are highly motivated by participants’ desire for their results and by the associated positive impact disclosure may have on trust building, recruitment, and retention.
3. **Future directions:** Specific practices commonly used across sites will inform the CLARiTI Return of Results framework being developed as part of a toolkit for sites. Barriers to returning results must also be addressed through a combination of capacity building, training and resources to limit logistical challenges, and policy change to reduce risks to participants.
### 3.1 Return of research results to participants

### with cognitive impairment

Consistent with the 2019 survey, most sites disclose neuropsychologi- cal test results gathered through annual participant evaluations (75%) as well as research diagnoses rendered through standard consensus review of the cognitive evaluation (75%). Proportions are relatively consistent with responses in 2019. Fifty-eight percent of sites cur- rently share structural magnetic resonance imaging (MRI) results,

representing an increase since 2019 (40%). Almost half (47%) are shar- ing amyloid PET results with impaired participants, similar to 43% of sites in 2019. In contrast, disclosure of amyloid measured through other modalities, or of other biomarkers, was relatively rare, occurring in less than one quarter of centers. While tau imaging (17%), cere- brospinal fluid (CSF) biomarker results (22%), and APOE results (14%) are among those results shared by relatively few sites, the number reporting disclosure of these results doubled from 2019, suggesting significant growth in this area. FDG PET and other genetic test results are completed and returned by a small proportion of sites (17% and 6%, respectively) and less frequently than in 2019.

### 3.2 Return of research results to cognitively

### unimpaired participants

Return of cognitive test results and participant research diagnoses to cognitively unimpaired participants occurs at most sites (69% each), and at a similar number of sites as in 2019. In contrast, amyloid PET (44%) and structural MRI (61%) are disclosed at more sites compared to 2019 (27% amyloid PET and 33% structural MRI in 2019). Though still shared by only a few sites, there was an increase in the number of centers returning tau PET (11%), CSF biomarker (14%), and APOE results (14%) compared to 2019. As with cognitively impaired partic- ipants, fewer sites disclose FDG PET (11%) and other genetic results (6%) to cognitively unimpaired participants compared to 2019.

### 4 DISCUSSION

Approximately three quarters of sites provide cognitive testing and research diagnosis, more than half provide structural MRI results, and almost half provide amyloid PET results. Less than one quarter return additional result types. There has been notable growth since 2019 in the disclosure of structural MRI, amyloid and tau PET, CSF biomark- ers, and APOE genotype. Return of cognitive testing/research diagnosis is similar to 2019, while return of FDG PET and non-APOE results has decreased. Given our focus on amyloid and tau PET results, we additionally evaluated components of and motivations for/deterrents to return- ing amyloid and tau PET. More sites return amyloid PET to cognitively healthy participants compared to 2019, which aligns with literature demonstrating that unimpaired participants desire and leverage their results to drive risk reduction and advanced planning.24,25Return of results protocols must be adapted to the clinical stage. For instance, disclosure of amyloid PET to cognitively unimpaired participants should emphasize adaptive health behaviors and clinical monitor- ing (as opposed to DMTs or other treatments). We expected an

increase in the proportion of sites returning amyloid PET to cogni- tively impaired participants, as this procedure is now commonly used in clinical and research26settings for etiologic clarification and treat- ment planning27in the context of US Food and Drug Administration (FDA)-approved DMTs.28Though sites already disclosing amyloid to cognitively impaired participants cited the clinical utility of amyloid PET as a major motivator for doing so, the overall proportion of sites returning these results did not significantly increase. Given the more recent development of tau PET tracers and associ- ated visual rating systems,29–31as well as limited clinical actionability of tau PET results, it is less surprising that few sites currently disclose tau PET. However, studies have illuminated the potential utility of tau measures to enhance clinical prognostication32and as endpoints for clinical trials.33We therefore expect greater future demand for tau dis- closure methods as validity, interpretability, and potential actionability of these markers increase. Indeed, most ADRCs expressed interest in disclosing tau PET in the future. The CLARiTI RoR Toolkit, launched in April 2025, already contains a protocol and resources supporting the disclosure of amyloid PET results. Integration of tau into the Toolkit is a critical and time-sensitive goal set for fall 2025. In addition to citing potential clinical benefit to participants, ADRCs recognized and were motivated by participants’ desire for their individ- ual results10and saw disclosure as a potential strategy for enhancing recruitment and retention in ADRD research. While we are unaware of any published literature directly linking the return of participant-level results to enhanced research engagement, a variety of studies sup- port this association. Participants34,35and study partners36suggest that personal benefit gained from ADRD research, including learning individual or family health results, can facilitate enrollment and reten- tion in studies. In contrast, a lack of disclosure of results serves as a barrier.37 Among sites that do not currently return amyloid or tau PET results, interest in future disclosure is near ubiquitous, highlighting the need to better understand and address implementation barriers. The most common barrier was purely logistical: disclosure was not a planned part of most ADRC longitudinal cohorts. This finding underlines that PET disclosure would require not only standalone protocols and resources for communication of this information, but rather adaptation of exist- ing workflows and documents to ensure return of results is feasible, sustainable, and effective across the diverse contexts in which ADRCs operate. CLARiTI is not the only impetus for disclosure; many large, national multi-site studies such as the Alzheimer’s Disease Neuroimag- ing Initiative (ADNI),18the Longitudinal Early-Onset Alzheimer’s Dis- ease Study (LEADS),38and the US Study to Support Brain Health and Prevent Cognitive Decline (POINTER)39have already launched return of results programs. Indeed, the latest funding announcement for the ADRCs requires some element of disclosure to individual participants. The RoR Core will continue to partner closely with sites and lever- age expertise from other studies to develop guidance around these changes. Additional logistical barriers include lack of expertise in disclosing results and additional time burdens for staff. Data from the survey themselves demonstrate the feasibility and efficiency of returning

results, with most sites indicating that sessions are completed within 30 minutes using virtual or in-person modalities. This finding reflects a growing consensus that remote return of results is a viable option.40,41 Indeed, using technology to conduct remote visits can increase site capacity and return-of-results feasibility. Though “face-to-face” in-person and video may be linked to lower participant distress,42 participants may be given the choice of modality for receiving their results18,40,41to balance the needs of sites (lower time and effort burden) and participants (lower time and travel burden, enhanced capacity to involve out-of-area support persons). Survey results also suggest that ADRCs effectively use a multidisciplinary disclosure team, including physicians and advanced practice providers (e.g., nurse practitioners, physician assistants), but also neuropsychologists, counselors, social workers, and advanced trainees. This may assuage concerns about both expertise and time demands for any single team member. There are no established qualifications or competencies required to communicate biomarker results; however, various studies have commented on key practices and styles that may result in bet- ter post-disclosure outcomes.42The CLARiTI RoR Core must meet concerns regarding a lack of expertise with a variety of multimodal learning opportunities for clinicians of diverse training backgrounds. The first of these trainings was accomplished in April 2025 with a national webinar through the NACC ([https://clariti.naccdata.org/for-](https://clariti.naccdata.org/for-) researchers/for-adrcs#webinars). Additional in-person and asyn- chronous educational offerings will be developed in the coming months and will reference published or publicly available training around biomarker testing for clinicians and researchers.43 Additional barriers to disclosure implementation include concerns about unintended harms to participants. There is growing evidence to alleviate this concern; studies of post-disclosure outcomes demon- strate that participants across the cognitive spectrum rarely experi- ence new or exacerbated psychiatric symptoms,44,17and do not regret learning results.45Nonetheless, participants may experience transient test-specific distress, worry, and experiences of stigma after learning of biomarker positivity.44,46These “risks” must be discussed pre- testing and monitored post-disclosure. Furthermore, protocols should incorporate the presence of a support person42,47and approach the discussion of results with clarity, compassion, and validation,42as these factors are associated with lower post-disclosure distress. The CLARiTI RoR Toolkit will incorporate suggested language and considerations for working with both participants and their loved ones as part of the dis- closure session. Additionally, the RoR Core will provide validated pre- and post-disclosure screening measures to track changes in mood, as well as recommendations for how and when they should be applied. Another concern for researchers is the potential for participants to experience medicolegal discrimination should research results be shared with medical or insurance providers, employers, or other entities.48Half of the sites reported sharing results with medical providers with participant approval, and very few include PET results in the electronic health record directly. Site-specific context regard- ing electronic health record entry is important in that some ADRCs are stand-alone centers, whereas others are embedded within a clinic, wherein adding a result to an electronic health record is part of the

standard of care. Risks and benefits of sharing results with medical providers/electronic health records should be carefully discussed prior to testing, ideally. Parameters around data sharing must be clearly defined to avoid accidental breach of confidentiality. Overall, sites already sharing amyloid PET generally align with pub- lished guidelines for ADRD biomarker disclosure that recommend

(1) determining appropriateness of returning results, (2) providing pre-test education and seeking consent, (3) returning results to the individual and their support person, and (4) following up to promote recipient(s) well-being.19,20,49–51Most ADRCs that disclose amyloid or tau provide pre-disclosure education and post-disclosure resources (e.g., clinical referrals, lifestyle recommendations). When disclosing, most sites provide a qualitative label (most commonly “elevated” or “not elevated”) as opposed to a quantitative value or graphic. While consistent with the FDA-approved visual read process for amyloid PET, several sites reported participants’ desire for quantitative and graphical information, as reported elsewhere.52Future work is needed to evaluate the effectiveness and utility of standardized quantita- tive values (e.g., Centiloids) and graphical risk representations. Fewer sites provide a written report to participants, despite literature sug- gesting that written reports may be helpful for improved participant experience and recollection of results.53,54 This study had several limitations. First, while results from the ADRCs may generalize to other research and even clinical settings, ADRCs have strict parameters and workflows that do not exist in other contexts. Relative risks and benefits of testing for and disclos- ing biomarker information differ greatly based on these settings, as does capacity for pre-test counseling and post-disclosure follow-up. Second, while this survey represents an updated review of return of results practices at ADRCs, it does not capture the nuances of disclosure at each site. For instance, we did not assess the exact lan- guage used to communicate results to participants (e.g., how dementia risk is communicated based on the qualitative result) or the timeline and nature of follow-up. As indicated in one recent scoping review,49 more information is needed to inform comprehensive and standard- ized guidelines for returning amyloid PET and other biomarkers.
