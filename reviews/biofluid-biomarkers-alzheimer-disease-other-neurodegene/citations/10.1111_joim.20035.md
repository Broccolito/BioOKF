**Background.** In 2015, a selective decrease in the glomerular filtration of middle-sized molecules such as cystatin C compared to small molecules such as creatinine was first described and tenta- tively termed “Shrunken pore syndrome.” Numer- ous studies have thereafter found an associa- tion between this syndrome (defined by a low eGFRcystatin Cto eGFRcreatinineratio) and mortal- ity and morbidity. In 2023, the syndrome was renamed selective glomerular hypofiltration syn- dromes (SGHS) as shrunken pores are not the only pathophysiological mechanism. Recently, some studies have used the difference between eGFRcystatin Cand eGFRcreatinineto describe a simi- lar disorder, and this investigation compares the two measures.

**Methods.** Using a cohort of 2781 adults with a median follow-up of 5.6 years, referred for determination of glomerular filtration rate (GFR), estimated GFR (eGFR) was determined using four equations. SGHS was defined using the eGFRdifferenceand the eGFRratioand association to mortality investigated through adjusted Cox proportional hazard models. From each adjusted regression model, Harrell’s C-index and 95% con- fidence intervals were calculated.

**Results. Both measures were associated with mor-** tality.Nosignificantdifferencesconcerninghazard

ratios or Harrell’s C-index were found between the twomeasurestoestimatemortality,andbothiden- tified SGHS and increased mortality in a subpop- ulation of 567 “healthy” individuals with no prior diagnosis and with no kidney disorder according to the kidney disease improving global outcomes- criteria.

**Conclusion. The eGFR**differenceis not superior to the eGFRratioin diagnosing SGHS or estimating mor- tality. However, as the two measures do not iden- tify the same subpopulation, using them simulta- neously might improve risk stratification. **Keywords:** creatinine, cystatin C, GFR, hypofiltra- tion, kidney, mortality, shrunken pore syndrome

## Selective glomerular hypofiltration

**Introduction**

Glomerular filtration rate (GFR), expressed as mL/min or normalized to a body surface area of 2

1.73 m, is used to monitor kidney function in most clinical situations. Measuring GFR (mGFR) involvesinvasiveandtime-consumingprocedures, andestimatesofGFR(eGFR)thereforedominatein clinicalpractice.CreatinineorcystatinChasbeen usedasmarkersofGFRsince1926[1,2]and1979 [3–5],respectively,andbothcystatinC-basedGFR- estimates, eGFRcystatin C(eGFRCYSin tables), and creatinine-based estimates, eGFRcreatinine(eGFRCR in tables) are used in modern health care [6–11]. The most reliable way of estimating GFR was in 2010suggestedtodeterminebotheGFRcystatin Cand eGFRcreatinineandusingtheaveragevalue,eGFRmean unless a known nonrenal influence on cystatin C or creatinine was present [12, 13]. In most indi- viduals eGFRcystatin Cand eGFRcreatinineagree, so that the eGFRcystatin C/eGFRcreatinineratio, hence- forth eGFRratio,is about 1. However, after use of this estimation system for about 5 years, it became evident in 2015 that a significantproportionofpatientsdisplayedalower value of eGFRcystatin Cthan of eGFRcreatininein the absence of any known nonrenal influence on cys- tatin C or creatinine [14]. This was interpreted as a selective general decrease in the glomeru- lar filtration of molecules of the size order of cys- tatin C (13.3 kDa) as the filtration of other 10– 30 kDa proteins was also decreased in compar- ison with creatinine (0.11 kDa) [14]. A similar selective decrease in the glomerular filtration of molecules of the size order of cystatin C had, in 2002, also been observed in the third trimester of pregnancy [15, 16]. Such a selective reduction of the glomerular filtration of 10–30 kDa molecules might be caused by shrinking of the glomerular pores according to the pore model for glomerular filtration, and the syndrome was therefore tenta- tively designated Shrunken Pore Syndrome, SPS [14]. In 2016, it was observed that patients with SPS, defined by an eGFRratiobelow 0.70, and sub- jectedtocoronaryarterybypassgraftingdisplayed a very strong increase in mortality compared to patients without SPS [17] and, in the same year, thatthesamewastrueforhealthyseniorswithSPS compared to healthy seniors without SPS [18, 19]. These observations of a strong increase in mortal- ity and/or morbidity, of patients or healthy people with a reduced eGFRratio, that is, SPS, have subse- quently been extended to many more populations with varying characteristics [20–39]. **Journal of Internal Medicine, 2025, 297; 79–92**
It has also recently been discovered that not only shrunkenporesbutalsoelongatedpores,forexam- ple, in diabetes patients with increased thick- ness of their glomerular basement membrane, will result in a selective reduction of the glomeru- lar filtration of 10–30 kDa molecules [40]. It has been suggested that shrunken and elongated pore syndromes represent a new class of kid- ney disorders, selective glomerular hypofiltration syndromes (SGHS) [36, 41], which often cannot be diagnosed using the kidney disease improving global outcomes (KDIGO)-criteria for kidney disor- ders, although these syndromes are connected to strong increases in mortality and morbidity [36, 41]. A particularly interesting aspect of SGHS is that it can be diagnosed in individuals indepen- dent of kidney function, with no kidney disorder according to the KDIGO-criteria and with no other diagnosis and that these “healthy” individualsdis- play a very high hazard ratio for all-cause mortal- ity[18,19,27,36,42–44].Arecentstudyindicates thatanadequatedietaryproteinintakemayreduce the risk of all-cause and cardiovascular mortality in SGHS [45], emphasizing the importance of an early and reliable diagnosis of the syndrome.

Some nephrological studies have since 2020 reported a similar increase in mortality or morbid- ity in patients with an eGFRcystatin C-value signifi- cantly lower than their eGFRcreatinine-value [46–50] but used the difference between eGFRcystatin Cand eGFRcreatinine(eGFRdifferencehenceforth), rather than eGFRratiooriginally used, to identify these patients [14,17–44].Noexplanationforthechangeindiag- nostic procedure was offered, and no comparison oftheperformanceofthetwomeasurestoestimate mortality was carried out.

The aim of the present work was to study whether the difference between eGFR and eGFR is superior to the cystatin C creatinine eGFR /eGFR ratio in predicting the cystatin C creatinine mortality of patients with SGHS and if the mea- sures identify the same patient subpopulations.

Glomerular filtration rate estimating equations

The cystatin C-based Caucasian Asian Pediatric Adult(CAPA)-equation[6]andthecreatinine-based Lund-Malmö revised (LMR)-equation [52] were used for the estimation of GFR in all patients. For comparison, the cystatin C- and creatinine-based CKD-EPIequationswerealsousedtoestimateGFR (cystatinC-basedchronickidneydiseaseepidemio- logic collaboration equation [CKD-EPIcystatin C]and creatinine-based chronic kidney disease epidemi- ologic collaboration equation [CKD-EPIcreatinine]) in all patients [53]. The CAPA-equation does not require race- or sex coefficients in contrast to the other equations [6, 36, 52, 53].

**Results**

To study if eGFRdifferencewas more efficient than the originally used eGFRratioin predicting mor- tality of patients with SGHS, a clinically well- characterized population of adults ≥18 years was investigated. Table 2 shows, stratified by mortal- ity status, the cystatin C and creatinine levels, measuredGFR,andeGFRcystatin CandeGFRcreatinine, estimated by the CAPA- and LMR-equations, for the population. As expected, both measures indi- cate worse kidney function for the sub-cohort of patients who died during the observation period. Both eGFRdifferenceand eGFRratioalso displayed a pattern of higher differences and lower ratios, respectively, for the deceased population, suggest- ing a higher prevalence of selective glomerular hypofiltration disorders in the deceased popula- tion.WheneGFRdifferenceandeGFRratioweregrouped

into different intervals, the largest difference and thelowestratiodominatedinthedeceasedpopula- tion, suggesting that the bigger the difference and the lower the ratios, the higher the mortality, as observedintheprimarystudiesofSPS[14,17,22, 24].WhentheCKD-EPIequationswereusedtocal- culate eGFRcystatin Cand eGFRcreatinine, the results were virtually identical to those obtained by the CAPA- and LMR-equations (Table S1).

All-cause mortality overall

Cox regression analysis for all-cause mortality evaluated by eGFRdifferenceor eGFRratio, using the CAPA-andLMR-equations,showedthatbothmea- sures were significantly associated with mortal- ity (Table 4). This was true both for the unad- justedpopulationandafteritwasadjustedforage, sex, cancer, CVD, DM, CKD, BMI grouped, mGFR grouped, eGFRcystatin Cgrouped, and eGFRcreatinine grouped. Excluding patients with BMI <18.5 did not change any of the statistical conclusions (data notshown).WhentheCKD-EPIequationsreplaced the CAPA- and LMR-equations in the analysis, the results did not differ significantly (Table S2).

Harrell’s C-index based on adjusted Cox regres- sions did not show any difference in the capacity of the two measures to estimate mortality neither when the CAPA- and LMR-equations were used (Table 5) nor when the CKD-EPI equations were used (Table S3).

**Discussion**

Thetwomeasures,eGFRdifferenceandeGFRratio,were compared concerning their capability to estimate themortalityinabigpopulationwithknownmGFR and large variation in clinical status and includ- ing individuals with no signs of disease, many of which represented potential kidney donors [27]. The eGFRratiowas used in the initial studies of the

Several cystatin C-and creatinine-based GFR-estimation equations have been described, and, as their diagnostic performance differs, the pair of cystatin C-and creatinine-based GFR- estimationequationschosenfordiagnosingSGHS, forexample,SPS,willinfluencethevalueofthetwo measures used in this and previous studies [28, 36]. We therefore used two of the previously most used pairs of equations, that is, the CAPA- and LMR-equationsandtheCKD-EPIcystatin C-andCKD- EPIcreatinine-equations[28,36].Bothmeasures,irre- spective of the pair of equations used, showed, as expected,lowervaluesforeGFRratioandhigherval- ues for eGFRdifferencein the sub-cohort of patients who died during the study period (Table 2 and Table S1).

Cox regression analysis for all-cause mortality of the full population showed that both measures were significantly associated with mortality irre- spective of the pair of equations used (Table 4 and TableS2),andHarrell’sC-indexbasedonadjusted Cox regressions did not show any significant dif- ference between the two measures for estimation of mortality (Table 5 and Table S3).

When these analyses were repeated for the three sub-cohorts of the population repre- senting the mGFR intervals <60, 60–89, and ≥90 mL/min/1.73 m2, similar results were obtainedforallthethreesub-cohortsandindicated no advantage for the eGFR over eGFR in difference ratio estimating mortality (Tables 6 and 7 and Tables S4 and S5). On the contrary, a graphical rep- resentation of the association between the two measures with mortality suggested that eGFRratio was more efficient in the estimation of mortality in the sub-cohort with mGFR <60 mL/min/1.73 m2 (Fig. 1).

A decrease in eGFRratioused in the initial stud- ies of SPS and SGHS has been shown to identify a subpopulation with a strong increase in mor- tality among “healthy” individuals with no prior diagnosis and with no kidney disorder accord- ing to the KDIGO-criteria [17–19, 27, 36, 42, 43]. Interestingly, as described in this investigation, eGFRdifferencecan also be used to identify such a subpopulation with an increase in mortality, but not more efficiently than the originally used eGFRratio.

Although it is known that both eGFRdifferenceand eGFRratiocan be used to identify subpopulations with increased mortality, it has not been inves- tigated previously whether these subpopulations completely agree or not. This was done here by comparing the classification of the population by different levels of eGFRratioand eGFRdifference(Fig. 2 andTable8).Formostofthe2781individuals,the classifications agreed, but for 172 of them, con- siderable classification differences were observed (Table8).Tostudywhethertheseclassificationdif- ferences were of biomedical importance, the mor- tality of different combinations of eGFRdifferenceand eGFRratiowas investigated (Table 9). The obser- vation that the highest mortality was not asso- ciated with the lowest eGFRratioand the largest eGFRdifferencewas surprising (Table 9) and strongly indicatesthatthesubpopulationidentifiedbyalow eGFRratiois not identical with that identified by a largeeGFRdifference,neitherfromabiostatisticalnor fromabiomedicalpointofview.Theobservedmor- tality in the group with low ratio was higher for those individuals with a small difference, that is, those with a low GFR. It is an interesting chal- lenge to find out if this subpopulation differs from othersubpopulationsinanyotherrespectthanits higher mortality. It should be observed that fac- tors other than GFR might also explain the dif- ference in mortality. Nevertheless, these observa- tionssuggestthatsimultaneoususeofeGFRdifference and eGFRratiomight be the best way to estimate the mortality of patients with a significantly lower eGFRcystatin Cthan eGFRcreatinine,for example, in SGHS.

Itshouldbeobservedthatstudiesofdifferentways of estimating mortality in SGHS do not only con- cern the evaluation of risk factors for patients but also hypotheses about the pathophysiology of and possible treatment of SGHS [28, 36, 41–43] and differentiationbetweenSGHSandotherconditions with low eGFRratio, for example, sarcopenia [54].

Strength and limitations

Thestrengthofthisinvestigationisthatitisbased upon a large population with measured GFR and known diagnoses at recruitment and known death causes and diagnoses after an extended observa- tion period [27]. One limitation is that information about albuminuria is not available for all individ- uals, which means that we to some extent might underestimate the prevalence of CKD. As creati- ninelevelsmaybeinfluencedbyfactorsotherthan renal filtration, detailed information on nutritional data would have been an advantage.

Another limitation is that the conclusions are based on the specific population used in the presentworkandmoreinvestigationsarerequired toevaluatethetwomeasuresinestimatingmortal- ity and morbidity in other populations with differ- ing characteristics.

**Conclusions**

The eGFRdifferenceis not superior to the eGFRratio in diagnosing SGHS or estimating mortality. How- ever, as the two measures do not identify identi- calsubpopulations,simultaneoususeofbothmea- sures might improve the risk stratification as sug- gested by this study.
