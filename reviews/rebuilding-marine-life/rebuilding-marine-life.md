## Abstract

Sustainable Development Goal 14 of the United Nations aims to “conserve and sustainably use the oceans, seas and marine resources for sustainable development”. Achieving this goal will require rebuilding the marine life-support systems that deliver the many benefits that society receives from a healthy ocean. Here we document the recovery of marine populations, habitats and ecosystems following past conservation interventions. Recovery rates across studies suggest that substantial recovery of the abundance, structure and function of marine life could be achieved by 2050, if major pressures—including climate change—are mitigated. Rebuilding marine life represents a doable Grand Challenge for humanity, an ethical obligation and a smart economic objective to achieve a sustainable future.

1.  Dennis Fritsch 17 April 2020, 14:57

    Duarte et al. offer a timely argument that rebuilding marine life is both doable and economically viable. We believe the investment thesis for ocean health can be further strengthened.

    The authors particularly highlight Marine Protected Areas (MPAs) as an impactful tool to restore ocean health. We suggest that the required global network of large-scale MPAs, including for the High Seas, can be managed using a satellite-based ocean data infrastructure that can service multiple users and therefore may have significantly lower operating costs than the numbers historically identified. We argue that, as a first step, a realistic case for necessary conservation measures will need to be developed and innovative finance approaches1 can then be discussed to best deliver the funding required.

    Various business sectors that would benefit from improved ocean health and could contribute to such investment are mentioned. Indeed, a number of industries have undertaken efforts to reduce their impacts on the marine environment, such as The Global Tuna Alliance by improving seafood traceability, or ORRAA by identifying ocean risk solutions. We posit, however, that the main constraints for adequate investment into the sustainable “blue” economy result from a general lack of awareness of the ocean’s significance to the global economy, alongside few investable formats and pathways available to financial institutions, rather than from a lack of funds2.

    Recent investor initiatives, such as UNEP FI’s Sustainable Blue Economy Finance Initiative, can assist in facilitating such investment pathways and help raise awareness. Sizeable investments to rebuild ocean health require a pro-active approach, including public-private partnerships and a broader ocean finance architecture that aligns blue and green finance approaches3. These need to engage both impact-focused and more traditional financial institutions to promote “blue” finance and broader sustainability approaches to deliver the funding at the scale and speed required.

2.  Saleem H. Ali 17 April 2020, 16:10

    While we applaud Duarte et al for providing a comprehensive review of marine recovery efforts, their policy recommendation table takes a non-empirically grounded assertion which must be challenged. In their key actions column in Table 1 they make a sweeping suggestion to “impose a moratorium on deep-sea mining until technologies free of impact are available.” For sure the authors realize that “free of impact” is a scientifically insurmountable threshold, since in the next sentence they note “improve environmental safety of oil and gas operations." If risks and benefits can be balanced for oil and gas extraction which involves flammable and explosive resources, the same logic can be applied to deep sea minerals extraction which is essentially a dredging and recovery operation under highly controlled conditions.

    Extraction of minerals from the deep sea should of course proceed with caution and that is why a highly detailed and deliberative process of ecological review is being carried forth by the International Seabed Authority under the Law of the Sea Convention. Risks of mining in the ocean need to be compared with mining on land and the need for critical metals for green technologies. To this end, criteria such as ecosystem service impacts can be applied (J. Lee et al. Topical Studies in Oceanography 137, 486–503; 2017). Furthermore, with appropriate monitoring and regulatory enforcement, deep-sea mining could in fact be a vehicle for further research of this fragile environment.

3.  Alejandro Martínez-Abraín 23 April 2020, 12:18

    Duarte et al. document relatively rapid rates of recovery of marine life, habitats and ecosystems following past conservation interventions. The authors conclude that substantial rebuilding of many components of marine life by 2050 is an achievable goal for science and society, provided that major pressures (including climate change) are mitigated. Although I do not doubt that marine life can rebound in many cases faster than previously thought, I doubt the 2050 horizon is a realistic one. Net Primary Productivity (NPP) of the world oceans has shown a decreasing trend over the last few decades and this trend will most likely continue with rising sea surface temperatures. Even with a growing network of Marine Protected Areas, the full density recovery of many species throughout the marine trophic web will be jeopardized by decreasing levels of NPP. On the other hand, relying on the oceans as an increasing source of food for humans could be a bit misleading. On the contrary, NPP of terrestrial ecosystems is increasing (>20% during the last 50 years) with increasing levels of CO2, and this trend will continue in the near future at least for C3 plants, as long as temperatures do not increase beyond the point in which carbon fixation rates start decreasing with increasing temperature. Current projections ([https://earthobservatory.na...](https://earthobservatory.nasa.gov/images/3835/changes-in-ocean-productivity\) "https://earthobservatory.nasa.gov/images/3835/changes-in-ocean-productivity)") expect NPP increases at temperate-latitude terrestrial ecosystems and decreases in NPP at tropical latitudes. It could well be that our focus for food provisioning at the mid run should be put on mid latitude terrestrial systems, together with the world oceans.
