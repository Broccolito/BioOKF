### SUMMARY

Long-term delivery of anti-HIV monoclonal antibodies (mAbs) using adeno-associated virus (AAV) vectors holds promise for the prevention and treatment of HIV infection. We describe a therapy trial in which four rhesus monkeys were infected with SHIV-AD8 for 86 weeks before receiving the AAV-encoded mAbs 3BNC117, 10–1074, and 10E8. Although anti-drug antibody (ADA) responses restricted mAb delivery, one monkey successfully maintained 50–150 μg/ml of 3BNC117 and 10–1074 for over two years. Delivery of these two mAbs to this monkey resulted in an abrupt decline in plasma viremia, which remained undetectable for 38 successive measurements over 3.0 years. We generated two more examples of virologic suppression using AAV delivery of a cocktail of four mAbs in a 12-monkey study. Our results provide proof-of-concept for AAV- delivered mAbs to produce a “functional cure”. However, they also serve as warning that ADAs may be a problem for practical application of this approach in humans.

### INTRODUCTION

While anti-retroviral drug therapy of HIV-infected humans, or SIV- or SHIV-infected monkeys, may suppress viral replication, as reflected by plasma viremia, to below levels detectable in clinical assays for years, it is not a cure. Virus can still be recovered from peripheral blood mononuclear cells (PBMC) of treated individuals and removal of antiviral drugs results in a rebound of plasma viral loads in the vast majority of individuals. “The Berlin patient”, Timothy Ray Brown, is considered the only individual who has been cured of HIV infection (Allers et al., 2011; Hutter et al., 2009; Yukl et al., 2013). As part of the treatment for his acute myeloid leukemia, he received whole body irradiation and bone marrow ablation prior to receiving a stem cell transplant of CD34+ stem cells from a C-C chemokine receptor type 5 (CCR5) Δ32 homozygous donor. T cells from CCR5 Δ32 individuals are not susceptible to infection by HIV-1 strains that utilize CCR5 as second receptor for entry into cells (Berger et al., 1999; Dean et al., 1996; Wilkinson et al., 1998). By all measures, he is free of any replication-competent HIV from his body (Allers et al., 2011; Yukl et al., 2013). This remarkable cure has stimulated great interest in seeking

The last decade has witnessed the characterization of an impressive array of monoclonal

### Author Manuscriptantibodies (mAbs) with potent neutralizing activity against a broad range of HIV isolates.

Not only have these mAbs been used to guide vaccine design for the elicitation of such antibodies, they have also been directly administered to monkeys and to people by passive transfer (Sok and Burton, 2018). A number of studies have shown significant antiviral effects against SHIV replication in rhesus monkeys (Borducchi et al., 2018; Hessell et al., Author Manuscript2016) and against HIV replication in humans (Bar et al., 2016; Bar-On et al., 2018; Caskey et al., 2017; Mendoza et al., 2018; Scheid et al., 2016). While these passive transfer experiments have demonstrated an ability to suppress viral replication to varying degrees, repeated infusions are continually needed to maintain the mAb concentrations. Gene transfer using adeno-associated virus (AAV) holds promise for achieving very long term delivery of Author Manuscript mAbs from a single administration of the AAV vector (Fuchs and Desrosiers, 2016). Long- lived cells such as those of skeletal muscle have the potential to produce transgene products for more than a decade from a single administration of AAV vector (Mueller et al., 2017; Rivera et al., 2005; Schnepp et al., 2016).

Here we present evidence for a functional cure of a SHIV infection in a chronically infected rhesus macaque following long-term delivery of the potent broadly-neutralizing monoclonal antibodies 3BNC117 and 10–1074 from a one-time administration of AAV vector.

### RESULTS

#### Long-term virologic suppression

Four Mamu B*08-neg B*17-neg Indian-origin rhesus monkeys were infected by intravenous injection of SHIV-AD8eo (Shingai et al., 2012) produced by transfection of cloned DNA into HEK-293T cells. This SHIV is derived from a molecular clone of defined sequence, is CCR5 using, is able to maintain persistent chronic phase viremia and to induce progressive CD4+ cell depletion and AIDS. Plasma viremia peaked in the four monkeys at 106 to 107 viral RNA copies per ml of plasma at 2 to 3 weeks following inoculation. Chronic phase viral loads in the four monkeys (the median of measurements weeks 32 to 62 after infection) were 74,000, 50,000, 11,000, and 330 viral RNA copies per ml of plasma (Fig. 1A). These monkeys never received anti-retroviral therapy: not before, during, or after recombinant AAV (rAAV) administration.

At 86 weeks post infection, each monkey received intramuscular injections of AAV-1 vectors encoding IgG1 versions of the potent broadly-neutralizing mAbs 10E8 (Huang et al.,

2012), 3BNC117 (Scheid et al., 2011) and 10–1074 (Mouquet et al., 2012) (Fig. S1). The IC50 values of these three mAbs for neutralization of SHIV-AD8 are shown in Table S1. 10E8 recognizes an epitope in gp41, 3BNC117 recognizes the CD4 binding site on the gp120 trimer, and 10–1074 recognizes sequences in the C2-V3 region of gp120 in a carbohydrate-dependent fashion. were converted to authentic rhesus monkey constant regions (c-rhesusized). The c-

Rhesus monkey rh2438, with a plasma viremia level of approximately 10,000 viral RNA copies per ml of plasma at the time of rAAV administration (Fig. 1A), persistently maintained concentrations of 3BNC117 and 10–1074 in the range of 50 to 150 μg/ml in serum following the rAAV administrations (Fig. 2A–C). rAAV administration was followed Author Manuscript within weeks by a sharp decline in viral loads to below the limit of detection of 15 copies per ml in this monkey. Viral loads have remained suppressed to below the limit of detection for 38 successive measurements over a period of 3.0 years (Fig. 1B). Ultra-sensitive viral load quantitation was performed on plasma samples obtained 10, 23, 31, 39, 56, and 76 weeks after the AAV administration. Five of the six samples were below the limit of detection of 1 copy of viral RNA per ml; only the week 56 sample yielded a value of 2. The level of virological suppression seen in animal rh2438 was not evident in the other three monkeys that received the same rAAV administrations but did not achieve such high Ab levels in circulation (Fig. 1C).

#### Extremely low level of recoverable virus

Blood samples were obtained from rh2438 at weeks 53 and 62 after rAAV administration for attempts at SHIV recovery. From the week 53 sample, 52 × 106 PHA-stimulated PBMCs from rh2438 were co-cultured with a similar number of PHA-stimulated PBMCs from a SHIV-naïve monkey donor in the presence of IL-2. We also co-cultured 16 × 106 PHA- stimulated PBMCs from the week 53 sample with 221 cells, which are permissive for SHIV- AD8 replication (Alexander et al., 1997). No SHIV was recovered. From the week 62 sample, 50 × 106 CD8+ T cell-depleted PHA-stimulated PBMCs from rh2438 were co- cultivated with 221 cells and 50 × 106 with CD8+ T cell-depleted PHA-stimulated PBMCs from a naïve monkey donor. Following culture for 31 days in IL-2, no SHIV was recovered.

Two attempts have been made to adoptively transfer infection from rh2438 to a naïve rhesus monkey recipient. A lymph node was removed from rh2438 at 74 weeks post rAAV administration, a single cell suspension was prepared, and the entire amount was infused intravenously into a naïve recipient rhesus monkey. 38 million lymph node cells were transfused. Measurements of plasma viremia remained negative in the recipient monkey. Three separate clusters of lymph nodes were removed from rh2438 at 78 weeks post rAAV administration, single cell suspensions were prepared, and the entire amount was transfused

into a separate naïve recipient monkey. 319 million lymph node cells were transfused. This

#### Continuing difficulty with ADA

In an attempt to repeat and extend the findings in rh2438, 12 additional monkeys were infected with SHIV-AD8. At 36 weeks after the SHIV infection, the monkeys were

separated into two groups of six monkeys each for rAAV administrations: the “bi-group” to receive AAV-delivered crhesusized 3BNC117 and 10–1074 mAbs; the “quad-group” to receive c-rhesusized versions of N6 (Huang et al., 2016), PGT128 (Walker et al., 2011), PGT145 (Walker et al., 2011), and 35022 (Huang et al., 2014). ### Author Manuscriptthings were planned differently for this 12-monkey experiment. In contrast to the AAV-1-

encapsidated vectors that were used in the initial study, the week 36 rAAV administrations used the AAV-8 serotype which has been reported to be less immunogenic and more tolerogenic with respect to host responses to the expressed transgene product (Mays et al., 2014; Sharland et al., 2010). And at week 60, a booster inoculation was planned using rAAV-1 to deliver the same mAbs to each monkey (Greig et al., 2016).

Following the rAAV-8 administrations at week 36, there was a readily apparent drop in viral loads in the ensuing weeks in at least 9 of the 12 monkeys (Fig. 4A). However, the decrease in viral loads was transient in 7 of these 9. Monkeys r14121 and r14097, both in the quad- group, did have a more sustained suppression of viral loads (Fig. 4B). Except perhaps for r14097, the booster inoculation at week 60 (about half a year after the rAAV-8 administrations) exerted little or no effect on viral loads. Plasma viral loads in r14121 have been ≤15 SHIV RNA copies/ml for 15 of the last 17 measurements beginning 4 weeks after the initial rAAV-8 administration. Plasma viral loads in r14097 have been ≤15 SHIV RNA copies/ml for 10 of the last 18 measurements beginning 3 weeks after the initial rAAV-8 administrations. Plasma viremia in both r14121 and r14097 prior to the rAAV-8 administrations at week 36 was in the range of 1,000 to 2,000 SHIV RNA copies/ml (Fig. 4B). The measured serum levels of delivered mAbs in the different animals over the course of the experiment (Fig. S3) are consistent with the changes in viral loads that were observed. r14121 and r14097 appeared to have better delivery in general of two or more mAbs compared to the other four monkeys in the group (Fig. 4C and Fig. S3). The rise and fall of delivered mAb levels in all 12 monkeys were again consistent with the levels of ADA that emerged (Fig. S4).

### DISCUSSION

It is somewhat surprising that variant virus resistant to both 3BNC117 and 10–1074 did not emerge following the AAV delivery of these two antibodies to rh2438. It is important to note, however, that Klein et al. have described the properties of HIV-1 variants resistant to

these two antibodies that emerged in HIV-infected humanized SCID mice treated with these

### Author Manuscript

two antibodies (Klein et al., 2014). These resistant variants were extreme, tier 1, neutralization-sensitive viruses. Monkey rh2438 had been infected with SHIV for 86 weeks prior to AAV delivery of the mAbs and likely had developed antibodies with a broad range of anti-Env specificities. Thus, it is reasonable to think that the anti-Env antibodies that the monkey was making, working in conjunction with the two AAV-delivered mAbs, were

### Author Manuscript

responsible for the failure of resistant viruses to emerge. It is also possible that the relatively high concentrations of these two mAbs present in rh2438 made it difficult for resistant virus to emerge.

The early failures to recover virus, the major decline in anti-p27 antibodies, and particularly

### Author Manuscriptthe failure to transmit infection following adoptive transfer of large numbers of lymph node

cells from rh2438 raised the possibility of an actual eradicative cure. However, the SHIV recoveries at weeks 84, 88, and 93 unambiguously demonstrate the persistent presence of proviral genomes capable of yielding replication-competent SHIV. The QVOA and the sequence analyses nonetheless indicate that very large numbers of PBMCs were necessary to Author Manuscript recover replicating virus even after CD8+ T cell depletion, PHA stimulation, IL-2, and co- culture with MHC non-matched cells. The absence of resistance mutations and the limited divergence from the cloned virus used for infection strongly suggest that the virus recoveries were not derived from actively replicating virus. Each virus recovery thus appears to have been derived from a long-lived latent reservoir of cells. Thus, a failure to adoptively transfer infection using large numbers of lymph node cells may not always be a reliable measure of a cured infection. Why virus recovery was not able to be accomplished prior to 80 weeks but was repeatedly accomplished after 83 weeks remains a mystery. It is possible that it is just a statistical chance whether the one in approximately 50 million cells just happened to be present in the sample used. It is also possible that individual clonal reservoirs were being expanded over time to the point of being more likely to yield recoverable virus. In fact, it has been shown that individual cells harboring replication-competent provirus may be extensively expanded in a clonal fashion over time (Hosmane et al., 2017; Simonetti et al.,

2016). We also cannot rule out the possibility that the lymph node biopsies and repeated blood sampling may have contributed to a facilitated ability to recover virus. While 2 of the 16 monkeys described in this report did exhibit spontaneous control of their SHIV-AD8 infection, that control was gradual and very slow to develop. In contrast, rh2438, r14097 and r14121 had immediate, precipitous declines in viral loads immediately after the AAV delivery. Thus, the virologic control in these three monkeys is almost certainly a result of the recombinant AAV administration. Nonetheless, other factors, including host genetics and the host’s own antiviral immune responses, may be contributing importantly to the long- term suppression. While a reasonable level of suppression was achieved in two of the monkeys in the currently-described studies, only one additional monkey (rh2438) showed an extreme, very long-term, level of suppression from the single administration of the recombinant AAV vectors. While only a single individual, this occurrence, like that of the Berlin patient, is an example of what is possible and represents a proof-of-concept for the research community.

Clearly, however, further work is needed to achieve much greater consistency in the ability

### Author Manuscript

to deliver each mAb.

Our previous work (Fuchs et al., 2015; Martinez-Navio et al., 2016) and the work of others (Saunders et al., 2015b) have consistently observed a severe problem with ADA associated with impeded AAV delivery of mAbs. The specificity of the ADA response has been entirely Author Manuscriptor almost entirely directed to the variable regions of the immunoglobulin G (IgG) and its magnitude correlated directly with the distance of these variable regions from germline (Martinez-Navio et al., 2016). This is worrisome because the potent broadly-neutralizing mAbs that one would want to deliver to fight HIV have for the most part variable domains that are extremely diverged from germline (Burton and Hangartner, 2016; Corti and

### Author ManuscriptLanzavecchia, 2013). Comparison to published reports from various sources suggests that

the immunogenicity of these IgGs in monkeys may be greater when delivered via AAV as compared to passive infusion (Gautam et al., 2018; Saunders et al., 2015a). A recent report presented at a meeting suggests that ADA is not just a problem for AAV delivery of anti- HIV mAbs to monkeys; recombinant AAV expressing the human anti-HIV mAb PG9 quite

### Author Manuscript consistently elicited anti-PG9 antibodies in human volunteers and little or no PG9 was

detected in serum (Jefferys, 2018). Even passive infusion of commercial antibodies such as Humira® and Remicade® for the treatment of other diseases frequently elicits ADA associated with the loss of clinical benefit (Radstake et al., 2009).

Despite the problems with inconsistent delivery, the results with rh2438 demonstrate the potential of this approach. If these findings can be consistently translated to use in humans, one can envision that a one-time administration of AAV vector could protect against HIV for the rest of one’s life. ADA responses are likely to be the major barrier preventing this image from becoming reality. Research will need to be directed to find simple, practical, readily- applicable ways to circumvent this ADA barrier for AAV delivery of potent broadly- neutralizing anti-HIV mAbs.
