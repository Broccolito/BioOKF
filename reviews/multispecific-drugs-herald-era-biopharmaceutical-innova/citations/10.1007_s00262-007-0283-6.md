## ORIGINAL ARTICLE

**Adoptive therapy of head and neck squamous cell carcinoma with antibody coated immune cells: a pilot clinical trial**

**Herbert Riechelmann · Markus Wiesneth · Peter Schauwecker ·** **Peter Reinhardt · Silke Gronau · Anita Schmitt · Carsten Schroen ·** **Judith Atz · Michael Schmitt**

Received: 12 September 2006 / Accepted: 29 December 2006 / Published online: 2 February 2007 © Springer-Verlag 2007

**Abstract** *Background* Catumaxomab is an antibody that binds with one arm epithelial cell adhesion molecule (EpCAM) positive tumors and with the other arm CD3+ T cells. Intravenous application of therapeutic antibodies may result in intravascular cytokine release. *Aim* In this pilot trial we assessed whether cytokine release can be controlled by ex vivo cell opsonization and cytokine wash-out before administration of catu- maxomab, preserving its anti-cancer activity. In addi- tion, preliminary data on safety of and clinical response to catumaxomab coated autologous immune cells were acquired. *Methods* Peripheral blood mononuclear cells (PBMNC) of four patients with recurrent head and neck carcinoma were collected by leukapheresis, incubated ex vivo with catumaxomab for 24 h and cleared from released cytokines. Each patient received an escalated number of antibody-coated PBMNC

equivalent to 1 £ 10 4 , 1 £ 10 5 , 1 £ 10 6 and 1 £ 10 7

CD3 + cells/kgBW intravenously at bi-weekly intervals. *Results* After opsonization, PBMNC released sub- stantial amounts of interferon (IFN) and tumor necrosis factor (TNF) in vitro, which were removed before administration. Catumaxomab up-regulated CD25, CD69, and CD83 on PBMNC, and catumax- omab loaded PBMNC released IFN and granzyme B when coincubated with EpCAM + BHY cells, suggest- ing cell activation and target directed biological activity. During the study period, one patient died of aspiration pneumonia and one patient needed a trache- otomy. Treatment related adverse events (AE) occurred at the highest cell dose in two patients, whereas 1 £ 10 6

loaded CD3 + cells/kgBW were well tolerated by all patients. One patient showed stable disease for 6 months and one patient is in complete remission for 27 months. *Conclusion* Ex vivo opsonization of PBMNC with catumaxomab provided biologically active, tumor tar- geting cells. Extracorporeal PBMNC coating may be an option to control intravascular cytokine release induced by therapeutic antibodies.

**Introduction**

In adoptive immunotherapy, cellular eVectors of anti- tumor immunity are directly administered, bypassing host factors that might prevent the generation of an eVective immune response in vivo [9]. To activate and

direct immune cells toward their target, they may be opsonized with antibodies speciWc for tumor-antigens [15]. Epithelial cell adhesion molecule (EpCAM) or tumor-associated antigen GA733 is, with some vari- ability, over expressed in head and neck squamous cell carcinoma (HNSCC) [5, 18] providing a target for adoptive immunotherapy. Catumaxomab is an anti- body bridging CD3 + T cells and EpCAM positive tumor cells by its two speciWcities; in addition, due to the unique combination of immunoglobulin heavy chains, catumaxomab selectively binds to and activates acces- sory cells expressing Fc receptors type I (CD64) and III (CD16) [14, 28]. Catumaxomab and comparable antibodies have demonstrated substantial anti-tumor activity in various cell culture experiments [20, 28], in vivo mouse tumor models [21] and clinical trials [8, 10]. The addition of an EpCAM-positive cell line derived from oropharyngeal carcinoma (BHY) to catu- maxomab-opsonized peripheral blood mononuclear cells (PBMNC) from healthy donors resulted in activa- tion of CD83 + antigen presenting cells, secretion of interferon (IFN), and granzyme B (GranB) medi- ated lysis of targeted BHY cells by redirected CD8 +

T-cells [23]. In an ex vivo culture system employing disaggregated tumors of patients with HNSCC, enhanced anti-tumor activity of combined antibody and autolo- gous PBMNC was found when compared with applica- tion of catumaxomab alone [6]. Based on the enhanced anti-tumor activity of catu- maxomab coated immune cells [6], extracorporeal coating of immune cells with catumaxomab and subse- quent intravenous application of catumaxomab-opson- ized immune cells appeared a promising approach to translate eVects observed in preclinical trials into clinical practice. Moreover, this approach might over- come the eVects observed by intravascular cytokine release following intravenous application of therapeu- tic antibodies [1, 4, 11, 24, 27]. In this clinical pilot study, feasibility, tolerable cell dose, safety, immuno- logical parameters, and clinical response of adoptive immune therapy with catumaxomab coated immune cells were investigated in four patients with otherwise intractable recurrent HNSCC.

## Patients and methods

Overview

In this prospective, dose escalating, clinical pilot trial, four patients with EpCAM-positive recurrent HNSCC were treated with catumaxomab-coated PBMNC. Fol- lowing inclusion, a total of about 1 £ 10 PBMNC

was harvested by leukapheresis and cryopreserved at ¡130°C. For treatment, PBMNC doses equivalent to 1 £ 10 4 CD3 + cells/kg at day 1; 1 £ 10 5 CD3 + cells/kg at day 15; 1 £ 10 6 CD3 + cells/kg at day 29; and 1 £ 10 7

CD3 + cells/kg at day 43 were opsonized with 0.25 g catu- maxomab/4 £ 10 6 CD3 + cells/ml and incubated for 24 h at +37°C to allow ex vivo cytokine release. Cells were then washed to remove released cytokines and administered intravenously over 3 h (Fig. 3). For cell transfusion, patients were monitored for 24 h and various laboratory parameters were assessed. The outcome was evaluated on days 57 and 85. A positive vote of the Ethics Commit- tee of the University of Ulm was obtained (67/2003) and required documents for cell processing were approved by appropriate state and federal agencies.

Eligibility and measurement of tumor lesion at baseline

Patients between 18 and 75 years with recurrent EpCAM-positive HNSCC not amenable to conven- tional treatment were eligible. For inclusion, CT or MR scans obtained within 4 weeks prior to therapy had to verify a measurable recurrent lesion with a minimal diameter of 1 cm [26]. Patients with acute or chronic infections, signiWcant hematologic, pulmonal, hepatic, renal, cardiac, autoimmune, psychiatric or endocrine disorders, or requiring systemic steroid treatment were excluded. Moreover, patients with brain metastasis, catumaxomab positive skin prick test, or patients oth- erwise considered at particular risk by the treating phy- sician were not included.

## Leukapheresis, cell processing, and quality control

Leukaphereses were performed on a cell separator (Spectra, COBE BCT, Lakewood, CO, USA) using anticoagulant (ACD-A; Fresenius HemoCare, Nether- lands) at a ratio of 1:15 to whole blood. By sterile dock- ing (Terumo Cooperation, Kofu Plant, Yamanashi, Japan) 10 ml of the apheresis concentrate was trans- ferred to a separate sterile small blood bag for further analysis [22]. Cells were then cryopreserved with 5% DMSO in appropriate aliquots to achieve 1 £ 10 4 CD3 +

cells/kgBW for the Wrst dose at day 1; 1 £ 10 5 CD3 +

cells/kgBW for the second dose at day 15; 1 £ 10 6 CD3 +

cells/kgBW for the third dose at day 29; and 1 £ 10 7

CD3 + cells/kgBW for the fourth dose at day 43.

## Flow cytometry

PE or FITC-conjugated rabbit anti-mouse-IgG, and clinical grade antibodies for CD3, CD4, CD8, CD14, CD25, CD69, and CD83 were obtained from B&D (Heidelberg, Germany). For Xow cytometry, samples were stained with the chosen antibodies, washed and resuspended in PBS. Isotype controls were performed with PE labeled anti-mouse IgG. The samples were then submitted to Xow cytometry on a FACScalibur Xow cytometer (Becton Dickinson, Heidelberg, Ger- many) for forward and sideward scatter (FSC/SSC) and Xuorescence measurements. Data were acquired and analyzed employing CellQuest™ software.

## Cytokine ELISA

For the assessment of cytokine levels in the supernatants collected before opsonization, following opsonization, and following opsonization and washing, ELISA Kits for human IFN, Interleukin-2 (IL-2), and TNF were employed (R&D Systems GmbH, Wiesbaden, Ger- many). Assays were performed in triplicates according to the manufacturer’s instructions and evaluated on a microplate reader (Easy Reader EAR400AT, SLT-Lab- instruments Austria GmbH, Altdorf, Austria).

## Enzyme-linked immunospot (elispot) assay

Before administration of catumaxomab-coated cells, their potential to elicit an immune response after

contact with EpCAM + tumor cells was assessed in an elispot assay employing 96-well plates (Millipore, Schwalbach, Germany). Antibodies to IFN and GranB were coupled to nitrocellulose membranes and incubated over night at 37°C with the cell suspension aliquots. After washing, the biotinylated detection anti- body DK1-1 (Mabtech, Nacka Strand, Sweden) and Strepavidin/alkaline phosphatase were added and the reaction products were visualized after BCIP/NBT stain- ing with an elispot reader (C.T.L., Reutlingen, Ger- many). The following cell suspensions were analyzed: BHY-cells alone (1 £ 10 4 cells/well), patients PBMC alone (1 £ 10 5 cells/well), BHY cells (1 £ 10 4 cells/well) together with patients PBMC (5 £ 10 4 cells/well), catu- maxomab-coated PBMC alone (1 £ 10 5 cells/well) and BHY-cells together with catumaxomab-coated PBMC. All elispot assays were performed in triplicates.

## Assessment of clinical outcome

Clinical response was assessed according to the Response Evaluation Criteria in Solid Tumors [26]. For safety evaluation, heart frequency, temperature, and blood pressure were continuously monitored dur- ing and until 1 h after transfusion of opsonized cells. These parameters were further assessed at regular intervals until the patient was discharged on the follow- ing day. Prior to cell transfusion, various laboratory parameters including a complete and diVerential blood count, glucose, albumin and total protein, electrolytes, kidney and liver tests including alanine amino transfer- ase (ALT), aspartate amino transferase (AST), and gamma glutamyl transferase (GGT) were done. All adverse events (AE) were documented and catego- rized according to the Common Terminology Criteria (CTC) for AE V3.0 [16]. Patient serum for the deter- mination of Human anti-murine antibody and anti-rat antibody (HAMA/HARA) was collected before treat- ment and on day 57. Two grade 3 CTC toxicities would have prevented the further administration of the same cell dose in order to protect the patients.

**Results**

Between October 2003 and February 2004, four patients were eligible and included in this trial. All patients were males with recurrent HNSCC. Patient 1 was 45 years old and initially suVered from a T3N0M0 laryngeal carcinoma. A recurrence 7 months after Wrst line concomitant radio-chemotherapy (70 Gy, 5-FU/ Mitomycin C) was treated by salvage laryngectomy and neck dissection. Despite additional treatment with

carboplatin, he developed a second hypopharyngeal relapse 5 months after laryngectomy with inWltration of the prevertebral fascia. Patient 2 (46 years old) initially suVered from a T4N1M0 supraglottic carcinoma treated by partial laryngectomy and postoperative radiother- apy with 67 Gy. After 1 year the patient developed recurrent disease involving the posterior Xoor of the mouth and tongue base. Patient 3 was 41 years old and initially had a T4N2bM0 hypopharyngeal carcinoma treated with laser debulking and subsequent radiother- apy with 70 Gy with concomitant 5-FU/cisplatin treat- ment. One year later, the tumor recurred at the primary site and in the neck. Patient 4 was 52 years old and initially had a T4N1 hypopharyngeal tumor requir- ing an emergency tracheotomy. He was then treated with hyperfractionated radiotherapy with 60 Gy and 5- FU/cisplatin concomitantly. From all patients, tumor specimens were assessed for EpCAM positivity before inclusion. The percentage of EpCAM-positive tumor cells ranged between 50 and 80%. All patients were heavy drinkers and cigarette smokers. Concomitant diseases included chronic obstructive pulmonary dis- ease in three patients, and alcoholic liver disease in two patients.

**Discussion**

Catumaxomab

BispeciWc antibodies may direct eVector cells of the immune system to tumor cells [11]. Catumaxomab is a chimeric anti-EpCAM £ anti-CD3 antibody recruiting T-cells and Fc receptor positive accessory cells into the direct environment of EpCAM-positive tumor cells. Catumaxomab completely eliminated tumor cells in malignant ascites Xuid of patients with ovarian can- cer [8]. In combination with high dose chemotherapy, a long lasting remission of metastatic breast cancer has been reported [24], and a signiWcant antineoplastic eVect has been demonstrated in explants of genuine HNSCC [6]. However, systemic treatment with thera- peutic antibodies may result in intravascular cytokine release. In a prospective dose escalating trial, we stud- ied the feasibility and tolerability of an adoptive immunotherapy with catumaxomab-coated PBMNC. Catumaxomab was used to arm immune eVector cells outside the body (ex vivo) to prevent adverse eVects due to the concomitant cytokine release. After the removal of the released cytokines, the catumaxomab- armed cells were retransfused. Leukapheresis and cellular composition of the leukapheresis product

No leukapheresis related AE were observed. Consis- tent with a recent report [12], peripheral blood lym- phocytes were low in the four patients with active disease. Moreover, relative numbers of monocytes were markedly elevated in all four patients. Increased relative numbers of monocytes in patients with HNSCC have been reported by Ostwald and co-work- ers [17]. Recently, low CCR5 expression and functional impairment of peripheral monocytes, as well as sponta- neous ex vivo apoptosis of peripheral blood monocytes from patients with HNSCC have been reported [13], suggesting functional impairment of blood monocytes in HNSCC patients.

## Extracorporeal release of cytokines

Before administration, the cryopreserved cells were thawed and coincubated with catumaxomab ex vivo. The optimal conditions for catumaxomab incubation were evaluated in preclinical experiments (Figs. 1, 2). In the four leukapheresis products, »50–95% of lym- phocytes were successfully loaded with the antibody.

## Immune cell activation

Catumaxomab-opsonization was associated with an increase of CD25 + , CD69 + , and CD83 + cells as well as up-regulation of these activation markers reXecting immune cell activation (Table 1). Since CD25 is the IL- 2 receptor, it is assumed that the increase of CD25 +

cells correlates with increased IL-2 secretion of catu- maxomab-stimulated T cells and accessory cells. CD69 appears to be the earliest inducible cell surface glyco- protein acquired during lymphoid activation. The mol- ecule is involved in lymphocyte proliferation and functions as a signal transmitting receptor in lympho- cytes, natural killer (NK) cells, and platelets [3]. In peripheral blood, CD83 is preferentially expressed on dendritic cells indicating activation and maturation of this cell population [29]. ## Clinical course

In this study, four patients with recurrent HNSCC of the larynx and hypopharynx not amenable to standard treatment were included. Catumaxomab-coated PBMNC were administered in increasing doses in bi- weekly intervals. During the observation period, one patient died of intercurrent aspiration pneumonia and one patient needed a tracheotomy due to laryngeal obstruction. These complications are frequently observed in patients with recurrent HNSCC and were considered unrelated to the experimental treatment. Treatment related AE were only observed with the highest cell dose given, whereas a cell dose equivalent 6 + to 1 £ 10 CD3 cells/kg BW were well tolerated. The median survival of patients with recurrent HNSCC despite intensive treatment ranges between 6 and 12 months [2]. Thus, the oncologic results of this study are consistent with disease prognosis. Interest- ingly, a complete remission was observed in one patient with an observed survival of 28 months. Occa- sionally, patients with recurrent HNSCC survive for a prolonged period [7], and prolonged survival has also been reported in catumaxomab treated breast cancer patients [24], however documented complete responses are rare. Factors possibly contributing to this favorable result include the small tumor volume, the high per- centage of EpCAM + tumor cells, the high PBMNC coating eYciency, and the strong GranB release of coated PBMNC when coincubated with EpCAM +

BHY cells in the elispot assay, suggesting particularly high biologic activity of the adoptive treatment in this patient. In conclusion, we could demonstrate that extra- corporeal coating of PBMNC with tumor-targeting antibodies is feasible. Coating of PBMNC with catu- maxomab provided highly active cells for adoptive immunotherapy. A substantial fraction of coating related cytokine release could be accomplished before the antibody was applied. In the four patients observed, the treatment was well tolerated at a dose of 10 6 cells/kgBW. Therefore, extracorporeal PBMNC coating may be an option to control cytokine release induced by immunomodulating agents as, e.g., tri- functional antibodies. Adoptive therapy with Catu- maxomab-coated PBMNC may be an option for adjuvant therapy in HNSCC and other EpCAM posi- tive tumors including cancer of the lung [19] and esophagus [25].
