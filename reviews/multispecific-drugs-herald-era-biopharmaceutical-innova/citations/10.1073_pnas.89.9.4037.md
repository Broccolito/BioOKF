Antiestrogen ICI 164,384 reduces cellular estrogen receptor content by increasing its turnover

SOPHIE DAUVOIS, PAUL S. DANIELIAN, ROGER WHITE, AND MALCOLM G. PARKER*

Molecular Endocrinology Laboratory, Imperial Cancer Research Fund, 44 Lincoln's Inn Fields, London WC2A 3PX, United Kingdom

Communicated by Elwood V. Jensen, December 30, 1991 (receivedfor review August 21, 1991)

ABSTRACT The ability ofestrogens to stimulate the tran- scriptional activity ofthe estrogen receptor can be inhibited by a diverse range ofestrogen antagonists. Here we show that the antiestrogen ICI 164,384, N-(n-butyl)-11-[3,1713-dihydroxy- estra-1,3,5(10)-trien-7a-yl]N-methylundecanamide, rapidly reduces the levels of receptor protein transiently expressed in cells without affecting receptor mRNA abundance. The reduc- tion in the levels ofreceptor protein is dose dependent, revers- ible by estradiol, and mediated by the hormone-binding do- main ofthereceptor. Pulse-chase experiments indicate thatthe half-life of the receptor is reduced from -5 hr in the presence of estradiol to <1 hr by ICI 164,384. A similar reduction in estrogen receptor levels is demonstrated in human breast cancer cellstreated with ICI 164,384. We discussthe possibility that the increased turnover of the receptor might be a conse- quence of impaired receptor dimerization.

Estrogens regulate cell growth and differentiation by binding to specific receptors that function as transcription factors (1-3). Hormone binding is required for the dissociation of an inactive oligomeric receptor complex to allow receptor dimer- izationand high-affinity DNA binding (4,5)and toproduce the full transcriptional activity of the receptor (6-9). Stimulation of transcription is mediated by two transcriptional activation functions (TAF)-namely, TAF-1 and TAF-2, the activity of TAF-2 being dependent upon estrogen binding. Since estro- gens have been found to act as mitogens in at least 30o of breast cancers, a large number of estrogen antagonists have been developed as therapeutic agents. One type, which in- cludes the nonsteroidal breast cancer drug tamoxifen (10), can also act as partial agonists in a number of physiological responses; while another type, which includes ICI 164,384, has been reported to be devoid ofagonist activity (11, 12). ICI 164,384 is N-(n-butyl)-11-[3,17,3-dihydroxyestra-1,3,5(10)- trien-7a-yl]N-methylundecanamide. The mechanism of action of some steroid hormone antag- onists is beginning to emerge. It has been shown that tamox- ifen promotes high-affinity DNA binding ofthe receptor but fails to induce the formation ofTAF-2 inthe hormone-binding domain, which accounts for its antagonist activity (7, 9). The agonist effect of tamoxifen has been proposed to be derived from TAF-1 in the N-terminal domain ofthe receptor, which is constitutively active (13). The effect of the so-called pure antiestrogen ICI 164,384 is controversial. In our studies with the mouse estrogen receptor (MOR), we demonstrated that ICI 164,384 inhibited DNA binding of the receptor and proposed that this may be a consequence of impaired recep- tor dimerization (14). This impairment might result from steric interference produced by the large 7a-alkylamide ex- tension present on ICI 164,384 (12), since we have shown that the steroid binding pocket is at or near the dimer interface (15). However, otherworkers havefound thatICI 164,384did not inhibit DNA binding and proposed that it also interferes with a subsequent step required for receptor-mediated gene transcription (16, 17). The variation in the ability of ICI 164,384 to inhibit the DNA binding activity of the estrogen receptor might be explained by differences in the stability of receptor dimers. Since it may not always be possible to dissociate preformed dimers and inhibitDNA binding in cell-free extracts, we have investigated whether ICI 164,384 was able to prevent DNA binding in intact cells. In this paper we show that ICI 164,384 treatment causes a decrease in cellular content of estrogen receptor protein by markedly reducing its half-life and sug- gest that this might be caused by impaired receptor dimer- ization. RESULTS

Effect of Pretreatment of COS-1 Cells with ICI 164,384 on the Ability of MOR To Bind DNA. Previous experiments showed that ICI 164,384 inhibited the DNA binding activity of the MOR in cell-free extracts when it had been overex- pressed in insect cells (14), but this was reported not to be the case in a number of other cell types (16, 25). Therefore, we investigated the effect of incubating intact cells with the antiestrogen prior to the analysis ofits DNA binding activity. When transfected COS-1 cells were pretreated with either no hormone or estradiol, the DNA binding activity of the receptor was similar in the presence or absence ofanti-MOR MP16 (Fig. lA). After a 24-hr treatment with ICI 164,384, there was a 90% reduction in DNA binding activity that was unaffected by the addition of MP16. Since MP16 was able to restore the DNA binding activity of MOR complexed with ICI 164,384 in cell-free extracts (14), these observations suggest that ICI 164,384 treatment of intact cells results in a loss of receptor protein. In contrast, treatment with the partial agonist tamoxifen had no effect on the amount of receptor detected in a gel-shift assay (data not shown). This effect of ICI 164,384 was not part of a general effect upon gene expression because the activity ofluciferase, used as an internal control, was unaffected (S.D., unpublished results). The steroidal estrogen antagonist ICI 164,384hasbeen shown to function as a "pure" antagonist in vivo (11, 12, 26), in breast cancer cell lines (27-29), and intransiently transfected cells (13, 14). In this paper, we show that ICI 164,384reduces steady-state levels of estrogen receptor by at least 90-95%. The turnover of the receptor is increased as a result of a reduction in the half-life of the protein from 5 hr in the presence ofestradiol to <1 hr in the presence ofICI 164,384. In contrast, nonsteroidal antiestrogens have been shown to have little effect on estrogen receptor turnover (30). The half-life of the human estrogen receptor in MCF-7 human breast cancer cells was determined to be 3 hr in the presence of estradiol, comparable to that of the MOR in COS-1 cells, and 3.5-5.0 hr in the presence of nafoxidine or C1628 (30). Tamoxifen has also been shown to increase the expression of estrogen receptors (31). Thus, the pure antiestrogen ICI 164,384 and nonsteroidal partial agonists differ markedly in their effects on the turnover of the estrogen receptor. In previous studies we identified residues in the hormone bindingdomain that are involved indimerization and overlap a region essentialforestrogen binding (15). This possibility would be consistent with the observation that a side-chain length of 16-18 atoms was optimal for both the inhibitory effects of antiestrogens on dimerization and DNA binding in cell-free extracts (N. In view of these results we favor the suggestion that the increased turnover ofestrogen receptors in the presence ofICI 164,384 may occur as a consequence of impaired receptor dimeriza- tion. It appears that ICI 164,384 binding is required for a reduction in the half-life of the protein, since the deletion mutant MOR 1-339 is not significantly affected by the anti- estrogen. This form of the human estrogen receptor lacking the hormone binding domain has been shown to dimerize via a weak dimerization activity in the DNA binding domain (5) and to stimulate transcription constitutively (7, 8). The demonstration that ICI 164,384 can act prior to DNA binding contrasts with the proposal that this antiestrogen is more likely to inhibit a subsequent step involved in gene activation (16, 17). Evidence to support this proposal was based on two types ofobservations. First, chimeric receptors that contain the heterologous GAL4 DNA bindingdomain or the VP16 transcriptional activation domain appear to bind DNA in transiently transfected cells even in the presence of ICI 164,384 (7, 17). One explanation for these results is that chimeric receptors might be processed differently from the normal protein, possibly due to the fact that these heterolo- gous proteins may contain additional dimerization functions (7, 17, 32). Second, ICI 164,384 failed to inhibit the in vitro DNA binding activity of estrogen receptors from either calf uterus (16) or HeLa cells (24) or when translated in vitro (9). However, other studies have demonstrated that the DNA binding activity ofestrogen receptor isolated from human or pig uterus (33) and the MOR expressed in insect cells is inhibited by ICI 164,384 (14), suggesting that the inhibitory effect of the antiestrogen may vary depending upon the source of the receptor protein. These discrepancies might also be explained by differences in the specific binding conditions used in the gel-shiftassays, sincetheDNA binding activity of the human estrogen receptor was shown to be affected by cation concentration and temperature (34). Since estrogen receptors appear to exist as dimers in cell-free extracts (5, 15, 16), variations in the effectofICI 164,384may reflect differences in the stability ofreceptor dimers in cells. Recently it has been demonstrated that ICI 164,384 also reduces the estrogen receptorcontent inmouse uterine tissue (35), consistent with the observation that the antiestrogen acts as a pure antiestrogen in the uterus in vivo (11, 12). However, ICI 164,384 has been found to stimulate proges- terone receptor protein levels inhuman endometrial Ishikawa cancer cells in vitro (36). At the present time it is not known whether the partial agonist effect of ICI 164,384 is solely receptor-mediated or depends on other transcription factors such as Jun (37). In conclusion, ICI 164,384 binding results in a reduction in steady-state levels ofestrogen receptor protein in transiently expressing cells, human breast cancer cells, and mouse uterus (35). The reduction is caused by a decrease in the half-life of the receptor protein and is likely to be a general feature of the mechanism of action of ICI 164,384 in target cells.
