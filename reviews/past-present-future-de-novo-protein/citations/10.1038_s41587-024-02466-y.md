T cell immunity relies on interactions between major histocompatibility complexes (MHCs) and T cell antigen receptors (TCRs) to trigger an antigen-dependent response, with presentation of antigen on MHCs a central aspect of the process. Class II MHCs (MHCIIs), which are found on antigen-presenting cells (APCs) and other cell types, including

### Author Manuscript

transformed cells1, play a role in detecting foreign antigens2–4, driving autoimmune diseases5,6 and potentially mediating microbiome tolerance7,8. Recent studies have shed light on the relationship between APCs, MHCII and antigen presentation9–11, but much of the fundamental biology and theragnostic potential remain to be explored.

Currently, TCRs and TCR-like antibodies are the only approaches for targeting peptide– MHCs (pMHCs)12–16. Molecules developed to target MHCII are primarily directed at autoimmune self-peptides17, despite MHCII’s expansive role in other diseases, such as cancer and viral infection-linked autoimmunity18,19. Developing TCR-based targeting and therapeutic modalities commonly begins with identifying natural TCRs against the antigen of interest as a starting point. However, to escape the negative selection process in the thymus, natural TCRs must maintain a low affinity to self-antigen. Notably, MHCII peptides adapt a more highly constrained extended conformation than MHCI20. Additionally, MHCII-binding TCRs generally, but not exclusively21,22, have narrower docking orientation distributions and weaker affinity than their MHCI binding counterparts23. This is likely due to the extended peptide structure on MHCIIs, where there are few structural features for binders to engage and they require a specific docking conformation to access the peptide residues. The intrinsic low affinity makes both the identification of an initial MHCII- binding clone and the downstream engineering process difficult. Structurally, to achieve discrimination of diverse epitopic peptides, the canonical TCR docking pose on MHCII allows up to four complementarity-determining region loops to align along the peptide24–26. The involvement of multiple flexible loops to encode peptide specificity also complicates the engineering process. TCR-like antibodies have been used to bridge the affinity gap from natural MHCII-binding TCRs14,17,27, but binders produced from the directed evolution process can result in substantial change in the binding orientation28,29. In general, the de novo development of TCRs and TCR-like antibodies to a specific target requires large combinatorial libraries and extensive screening efforts27,30,31. Although recent advances in computational protein structure prediction and design have advanced our understanding and engineering capabilities of protein–protein interactions32,33, they have not contributed

### Results

#### Molecular engineering inspired by the superantigen MAM

Superantigens are typically produced by bacteria, viruses and other microorganisms. They broadly activate T cells by bridging MHCII and TCRs, acting as a molecular glue overwriting antigen restriction between an APC and a T cell35. MAM is a superantigen with a unique binding mode to MHCII (Protein Data Bank (PDB) ID: 2ICW34), featuring an L-shaped structure consisting of two domains. Its N-terminal domain contacts pMHCII, whereas both N- and C-terminal domains jointly form the interface with the TCR. MAM engages MHCII in an orthogonal orientation relative to the peptide binding groove, primarily only contacting the α-chain of MHCII. The native MAM structure also has a stretch of flexible residues at the beginning of the chain (residues 1–20), which is located above the peptide binding groove on MHCII in the bound complex (Fig. 1b). This flexible loop is conveniently located above the peptide in the MHCII groove, but, despite the loop’s proximity, native MAM is agnostic to the peptide36. MAM’s unique structure led us to consider the possibility of engineering MAM as a general binding platform for targeting pMHCII in a peptide-specific manner (Fig. 1a). Additionally, as a superantigen,

MAM’s natural compatibility with multiple HLA alleles suggests a possible mechanism for

### Author Manuscript

multiallelic, peptide-focused binding36.

In the ternary TCR–MAM–MHCII complex structure, the manner that the N-terminal loop wraps perpendicularly around the three-helix bundle that makes up the N-terminal domain of MAM to form a binding interface is unusual. We found that the deletion of the flexible loop

### Author Manuscriptfrom MAM completely abolishes its pMHC binding (Supplementary Fig. 1), which revealed

a truly surprising aspect of the MAM structure. In the unbound MAM crystal structure (PDB ID: 3KPH), the first 15 residues are too labile to be resolved, so their critical contribution to binding is unanticipated. We had expected residual binding signals from the helical bundle alone, but the complete loss of binding makes MAM an even more attractive scaffold, as Author Manuscriptthis suggests minimal background binding to MHCII. It appears that the loop is the critical element that allows MAM to make a crowbar-like shape to saddle over MHCII’s α1-helix, with the loop being the prying tip. That MAM has no notable affinity for MHCII without the loop would make any positive binding signal truly peptide focused.

Next, we undertook extensive engineering efforts to transform MAM into an MHC–antigen

### Author Manuscript

binding platform. When developing a superantigen into a targeting platform, the intrinsic toxicity from engaging T cells is of primary concern. MAM can be rendered inert by eliminating its C-terminal domain, which abolishes MAM’s ability to bind the TCR, and the N-terminal domain alone is sufficient to bind pMHCII (Supplementary Fig. 2).

In our design scheme, we planned to turn the N-terminal flexible loop into the antigen recognition element (ARE). To develop it into a segment that can accommodate a wide range of possible antigens, the natively disordered loop would need to be anchored at the free end to tolerate the introduction of randomized mutations in this region. To achieve this, we computationally designed a specific disulfide bond that anchors the loop to the helical bundle scaffold (Fig. 1c, line 1). This modification involving residues 5 and 119 simultaneously reduced loop flexibility and improved overall stability. The design was incorporated into the TRACeR-II scaffold (Supplementary Fig. 3).

To further optimize the structure, we identified a few nonideal structural features associated with the initial helical bundle and computationally redesigned these regions with RosettaRemodel37 (Fig. 1c, lines 2–4). First, we redesigned one of the α – α turns. The native α – α turn between the second and third helices has a nonideal loop connection, with a twisted geometry and a missing N-cap of the third helix. We redesigned this turn into a more energetically favorable GBA loop38, featuring a Gly 67 break in the second helix and an Asp 68 cap on the third helix (Fig. 1c, line 2). Our experimental results confirmed that this redesign resulted in a more than 100-fold increase in recombinant protein production yield, denoting a huge improvement on scaffold stability. We also addressed several other stability issues, including the mutation of G61D with repacking of surrounding residues to reduce the glycine’s helix-breaking effect (Fig. 1c, line 3) and the L47K and L50E mutations to reduce solvent-exposed hydrophobicity (Fig. 1d, line 4). The immunogenicity of the optimized helical bundle was evaluated in mice through multiple injections over a 2-week period. No signs of toxicity or immunogenicity were observed based on no changes

in body weight and no notable development of antibody titers, respectively, during this study

#### A unique antigen recognition mechanism enabling direct structural design

The agreement between the cryo-EM structure and the original MAM complex suggests that the binding mode for TRACeR-II is indeed constant. This opens the possibility

that specific MHCII antigen-binding ARE sequences can be directly designed into this very constrained conformational space. The simplistic loop-on-loop interaction reduced the modeling problem to a tractable scale. We tested this notion with an antigen of unknown complex structure (a severe acute respiratory syndrome coronavirus 2 (SARS-CoV-2) peptide on HLA-DR1 with the sequence GAALQIPFAMQMAYRF; Fig. 4e).

We incorporated Rosetta modeling and AlphaFold2 prediction tools to attempt the design challenge. The protocol is presented in Fig. 4e. We first predicted the tightest peptide binding frame to the MHCII with the NetMHCIIpan online server55. Residues were then threaded onto the MAM–HLA-DR1 complex structure (PDB ID: 2ICW) following the Author Manuscriptpredicted frame. We used Rosetta to model the ARE and its interactions with the antigen. We forced the same cysteine residues on the TRACeR scaffold and relaxed the environment near the disulfide. During the design process, the same five residues used in the original library were allowed to mutate. These five residues and all the neighboring residues (including MHC, peptide and TRACeR) within 12 Å were allowed to move during the FastDesign and FastRelax steps (Supplementary Appendices 1 and 2). The resulting models were then scored by Alphafold2 and ranked by their pLDDT scores56. The top ten designs were tested with yeast surface display, and one of the sequences showed binding to the designated target (Fig. 4f, top). All the models scored similarly by pLDDT, and the ARE sequence that showed binding (FRHGV) was not among the best-scored models ranked by Rosetta metrics. We inspected the differences between the design models and noticed a positive ϕ backbone angle at the fourth position in the ARE loop, where a glycine was designed onto the successful sequence while other designs have polar residues (asparagine or arginine). A strict design rule dictates that glycine should have been used for this position, and the design of polar residues is likely a Rosetta artifact. We then tested variants of the unsuccessful sequences with glycines in their fourth position to account for the Rosetta artifact. We rescued two more peptide-specific designs with better affinity (Fig. 4f, bottom). The ARE sequence that yielded the highest binding signal on yeast was purified as a recombinant protein and had an SPR-measured Kd of 3.78 μM (Supplementary Fig. 26). ### Discussion

MHCIIs play a central role in adaptive immune responses, as they are responsible for

### Author Manuscript

antigen presentation that leads to CD4+ T cell activation and differentiation. As a critical hub for antigen information exchange, MHCIIs are strongly implicated in human diseases, from autoimmune diseases to cancer, and thus the characterization and discovery of MHCII- related antigens are fundamental for the studies of pathogenesis and potential theragnostic solutions. The demand for peptide-specific MHCII-binding proteins is high, but despite the immense progress with TCRs and TCR-like antibodies, the labor-intensive engineering processes have not met the theragnostic needs. We discovered that the unique binding features of the superantigen MAM could potentially offer a simple solution by reducing the peptide recognition interface to a single loop. A single loop tethered on one end with a disulfide bond could achieve discrimination of single mutations on MHCII-presented peptides. It can encode peptide selectivity with as few as five residues in the ARE, and this region can be expanded in case additional interactions with the peptide are needed.

TRACeR-II’s single loop-based interface inspired us to carry out computational designs using only the peptide’s sequence. As a proof of concept, we successfully designed a TRACeR against an arbitrarily chosen epitopic peptide. A design pipeline like this could potentially be automated to address the expanding antigen repertoire. The success with this effort could greatly reduce the amount of labor and time required for developing binders for pMHCII antigens.

The TRACeR-II platform has several limitations that could benefit from further optimization. First, the current footprint by which TRACeRs interact with the peptide is relatively small, which might restrict the range of peptides that could be targeted effectively. The affinity and specificity for each antigen can vary substantially depending on the chemical composition of the peptide; thus, extending the length of the ARE region or incorporating additional structural features could potentially enhance these interactions. Second, although we saw very little anti-TRACeR responses in our animal trials, additional investigations into its potential immunogenicity in humans will be necessary to fully assess the suitability in clinical applications.

The crystal structure of the ternary complex of MAM, TCR and MHCII has been available

### Author Manuscript

in the PDB since 2007. As a superantigen, MAM presents several intriguing features, such as it is the only known naturally occurring superantigen that binds near the peptide, it interacts with the α-chain of MHCII, and it can bind not only different HLAs but also mouse MHCs. Wild-type MAM is not known to be peptide specific and has not been explored in that regard. However, through protein design, we were able to build from MAM

### Author Manuscript

a highly stable, versatile and designable MHCII-binding platform. We have demonstrated that peptide-specific binders for multiple HLA molecules, including HLA-DR1, HLA-DR3, HLA-DR4, HLA-DR15 and HLA-DQ2.5, can be selected from a single ‘peptide-focused’ ARE library. Our results present an alternative strategy for addressing the challenge of MHCII peptide targeting, providing a promising foundation for developing potent tools to

### Author Manuscript

explore MHCII biology and applications in cancer, viral infection and autoimmune diseases.

### Methods

#### Library design and production

TRACeR library with NNK degenerate codon at the five loop positions was ordered from Integrated DNA Technologies as Ultramer. The Ultramers were then assembled and amplified.

For library transformation, Saccharomyces cerevisiae yeast EBY100 cells were transformed with insert DNA and linearized pCTCON2 plasmid using the established protocol57. After transformation, cells were grown overnight in SDCAA medium at 30 °C, passaged once and stored in 20% glycerol solution at −80 °C.

#### Yeast display and library screening

Transformed yeast cells were grown in SDCAA medium. For induction of expression, yeast cells were centrifuged at 2,000g for 5 min and resuspended in SGCAA medium supplemented with 0.2% glucose at a cell density of 1 × 107 cells per ml and induced at 30 °C for 16–24 h. Cells were washed with PBS supplemented with 0.5% bovine serum albumin (BSA) and labeled with pMHC tetramer or monomer, together with anti-c-Myc– FITC (Miltenyi Biotec). After incubation for ~1 h at room temperature, cells were washed twice, resuspended in PBS supplemented with 0.5% BSA and run on a Sony SH800 cell sorter.

#### Next-generation sequencing

Up to 1 × 108 yeast cells from a growing culture were taken to perform a Zymoprep using a Zymoprep II kit (D2004, Zymo Research). The TRACeR sequences were amplified from the

extracted plasmids, and the adapter sequences were added by PCR. The PCR product was

### Author Manuscript

gel purified and sent for next-generation sequencing (Amplicon-EZ, Genewiz). The most enriched clones were taken for subsequent experimental characterization.

Two micrograms of insert was transformed into competent EBY100 cells with linearized pCTCON2 vectors.

#### Construction of HLA-specific T2 cell lines

To test the binding affinity and specificity of TRACeR with naturally processed pMHCII complexes, cell lines expressing a single HLA-DR1 allele were constructed by stable transfection of HLA-DR1 or stable cotransfection of HLA-DR1 and HLA-DM (HLA-DMA and HLA-DMB). T2 is an MHCII-deficient (HLA-II−/DM−/DO−) TxB hybrid cell59,60. HLA-DM catalyzes CLIP peptide dissociation from HLA-DR molecules and facilitates loading of other peptides; this function is critical for the exchange of CLIP, particularly for molecules with high CLIP affinity, such as HLA-DR1 (refs. 45,61). Thus, at the cell surface, T2 HLA-DR1 transfectants express CLIP-associated HLA-DR1 complexes, whereas T2 HLA-DR1/HLA-DM transfectants express primarily HLA-DR1 complexes with other peptides. T2 cells and its transfectants were cultured in IMDM supplemented with GlutaMAX (Thermo Fisher Scientific), 10% HI FBS, 1% penicillin/streptomycin and 0.5 mg ml−1 G418 (to maintain selective pressure on HLA-DR transfectants) at 37 °C with 5% CO2. Expression of the HLA-DR protein was detected by anti-HLA-DR (clone L243), and CLIP- associated HLA-DR complexes were detected by anti-CLIP clone (CerCLIP.1). Expression was evaluated by flow cytometry before performing TRACeR binding experiments.

**Image processing**

All processing was performed in cryoSPARC63 (Supplementary Fig. 18). In total, 21,328 movies were motion corrected using patch motion correction. The contrast transfer functions of the aligned micrographs were determined using the patch contrast transfer function, and an initial stack of 100,993 particles was chosen from the first 704 micrographs by blob picking. After successive rounds of reference-free two-dimensional (2D) class averaging, 2D classes corresponding to intact complexes were used as templates to pick 18,639,350

particles from the complete dataset. After 2D sorting to remove junk particles, a random

### Author Manuscript

set of 498,178 particles was used for ab initio reconstruction, followed by two rounds of three-dimensional sorting of the full set of 15,580,106 nonjunk particles. The resulting 3,706,646 particles were then local motion corrected, and the volume was improved by nonuniform refinement. Due to mobility of the Fab constant domains, local refinement was performed using a mask to exclude these domains, and this 2.34-Å-resolution map was used

### Author Manuscript

for subsequent real space refinement in phenix. Additional sharpening was performed with deepEMhancer64.
