## PepPrCLIP performs comparably to RFDiffusion on

## structured targets in silico

Before experimental testing, we computationally benchmarked the full PepPrCLIP pipeline via AlphaFold-Multimer, a massively im- proved method for peptide-protein docking as compared to previ- ous docking strategies (32, 33). Specifically, the predicted interface TM (ipTM) score is a verified metric within AlphaFold2-Multimer

protein complex, thus serving as a quantitative assessment of gen- eration quality (32, 33). To define a rigorous “hit rate” for PepPrCLIP, we first cofolded 209 PDB-validated peptides in PepPrCLIP’s un- seen, nonhomologous, held-out “strict” test set and retrieved their ipTM values. We then cofolded the top PepPrCLIP-generated pep- tide and the top RFDiffusion-generated peptide of equivalent length with the corresponding target protein sequence (data file S1). To establish a very strict but normalized threshold on binding capacity, we calculated the hit rate as the percentage of generated peptides that have an ipTM score greater than or equal to that of the corresponding test set peptide over all tested target proteins. To note, this is a very high standard, as this indicates the rate at which peptides outperform experimentally validated, high-affinity, crys- tallizable peptide binders. As all target proteins are PDB-validated structures likely in its training set, RFDiffusion performs strongly as expected, with an in silico hit rate of 30.1% (Fig. 2B). Without ex- plicitly leveraging any structural information or seeing these target proteins in its training set, PepPrCLIP achieves an in silico hit rate of 17.7% (Fig. 2B). We further calculated the percentage of peptides from each model that achieves an ipTM ≥ 0.7, demonstrating that 22% PepPrCLIP-generated peptides form stable interactions com- pared to 40% of RFDiffusion-generated peptides, which has a com- parative advantage on these targets (33,  34). We also compare the ipTM scores of PepPrCLIP-generated peptides and RFDiffusion- generated peptides and find that PepPrCLIP outperforms RFDiffu- sion on 33% of this set of targets (Fig. 2C). In addition to these performance metrics, it is important to highlight the significant difference in inference runtime between the two ap- proaches. PepPrCLIP, leveraging only sequence embeddings, benefits from much faster generation and prioritization, generating ~1000 pep- tides per minute on a standard T4 TPU and requiring ~1 min to rank 100,000 peptides per protein target. In comparison, RFDiffusion reports around 2 min for the design of a single binder (17). This efficiency makes PepPrCLIP particularly advantageous when screening large li- braries of peptides for given target proteins, whether or not structural information exists. As such, our in silico results motivated us to experi- mentally explore PepPrCLIP’s generation quality in application-relevant settings for more difficult, conformationally diverse target proteins.

**PepPrCLIP-** **generated peptides inhibit the catalytic activity** **of UltraID** Proximity-dependent biotinylation identification (BioID) proteomics is a powerful and popular approach to study protein-protein interac- tions and intracellular organization. It relies on the enzymatic activity of biotin protein ligase to biotinylate proteins in proximity to a bait of interest. UltraID, a conformationally stable enzyme (pLDDT = 96.2) evolved from the original Escherichia coli BirA biotin protein ligase

(Fig. 3A), stands out as the smallest efficient enzyme currently avail- able for BioID (35). Its high enzymatic kinetics and low background activity make it highly promising for diverse proteome discovery ap- plications. Here, we report the development of a “caged” version of UltraID, wherein it is held in an inactive state, by using IPs. We rea- soned that it may be feasible to cage UltraID by linking it with an IP capable of binding to the catalytic core and inhibiting its activity (Fig. 3B). It is noteworthy that UltraID uses both adenosine 5′-triphosphate and biotin as substrates for its biotinylating function (35). Therefore, the success of inhibition by the IP likely hinges on its competitive

tive site or rendering the binding pocket inaccessible because of in- duced conformational changes. As UltraID is a highly structured, novel protein that is not present in either model’s training set, we evaluated the ability of both PepPrCLIP and RFDiffusion to generate IP targeting sequences of the UltraID catalytic pocket, which was predicted on the basis of AlphaFold2 structural models and the known structure of BirA (Fig. 3A). This was followed by experimental validation of their inhibition efficiency in cells. Providing only the target sequence of UltraID and confirm- ing their catalytic pocket targeting via AlphaFold-Multimer, we gener- ated 20 candidate IPs (IP_PpC_1 to IP_PpC_20) with PepPrCLIP (table S1 and data file S1). Providing the AlphaFold2-predicted structure of UltraID as input and specifying the catalytic pocket as a binding hotspot, we generated 19 IPs (IP_RFD_1 to IP_RFD_19) from RFDiffusion for comparison (table S1 and data file S1). We gen- erated plasmids expressing each of these IPs, fused to the UltraID DNA coding sequence via several flexible linkers and a hemaggluti- nin antibody (HA) epitope. These plasmids were then transfected into human embryonic kidney (HEK) 293T cells, which were treated with 50 μM biotin for 30 min before fixation and fluorescence stain- ing. To assess inhibition efficiency, we evaluated the ratio between biotinylation level (streptavidin fluorescence level) and transfection efficiency (HA fluorescence level). Our results reveal that among the 19 RFDiffusion-generated IPs, 17 of them led to UltraID inhibition, and 3 (IP_RFD_2, IP_RFD_4, and IP_RFD_17) inhibited the enzy- matic activity of UltraID by more than 75%, highlighting its strong binder generation capability on structured targets (Fig. 3C). Of the 20 PepPrCLIP-generated IPs, 19 showed successful UltraID inhibition, with 4 (IP_PpC_2, IP_PpC_8, IP_PpC_18, and IP_PpC_19) exhibit- ing more than 75% inhibition efficiency (Fig. 3C). To further evaluate the overall performance of PepPrCLIP- generated IPs compared with RFDiffusion-generated IPs, we per- formed an unpaired Welch’s t test on the two groups of peptides. Notably, the PepPrCLIP-generated IPs, on average, exhibited a high- er level of UltraID inhibition than the RFDiffusion-generated IPs (P = 0.0364; Fig. 3C). Even among the top candidates generated by the two models, the PepPrCLIP-generated IPs still significantly out- performed the RFDiffusion-generated IPs (P =  0.0055; Fig. 3D), underscoring the strong potential of PepPrCLIP in designing pep- tides that not only bind to but also inhibit the activity of the target protein. These results highlight the potential utility of PepPrCLIP in generating peptides capable of modulating the function of enzymes through predicted binding to catalytic sites. We also observed that RFDiffusion-generated IPs tend to have more repetitive sequences compared to PepPrCLIP-generated IPs (table S1). In contrast, PepPrCLIP prioritizes diverse sequences that promote strong inhibitory binding interactions to UltraID, which can further be advantageous when targeting varied protein

All presented in silico metrics were done with train/validation/test splits indicated in Results and the “Dataset generation” section. For experimental peptide derivation, a model trained on the entire data- set was used to ensure maximum performance.
## Generation of peptide candidate sequences

Candidate peptides are generated using all of the peptides in the training set. Each peptide is embedded within ESM-2-650M pLM ([https://huggingface.co/docs/transformers/model_doc/esm](https://huggingface.co/docs/transformers/model_doc/esm)) in Py- Torch. For a given peptide embedding, the variance (σ2) is computed over all dimensions of the embedding. For each residue in the source peptide, noise is sampled from the standard normal distribution *N(0, k*) and multiplied by the variance σ to create a perturbation 2 2

*p* *r_i. This perturbation is added to the embedding for its respective* residue. The noise scaling factor k can be varied to produce peptides that are closer or farther from the source peptides in the ESM phase space. At inference time, source peptides are randomly sampled from the training set (usually on the order of 100 to 1000). For each source peptide, 1000 peptides are generated through the noising method described above, varying the value of k to sample at various distances from the source peptide. Last, these peptides (on the order of 100,000) are fed into the CLIP model and ranked on the basis of predicted binding to a user-provided target sequence. For generation of peptides for this study, k values in the range of 5 to 22 were selected. We computed the mean Hamming distance between generated peptides and their source peptides as we varied the value of k. We did this for an ensemble of 100 source 18-nucleotide oligomer peptides, creating 500 generated peptides for each. The plot of Hamming distance as we varied k is depicted in Fig. 2A. We observed that at k = 5, we see mutations on the order of one amino acid, and the mean Hamming distance increases until k =  22, at which point almost the entire sequence is mutated. Thus, we chose that k would range between 5 and 22, sampling the full range of se- quence transformations. All peptide sequences evaluated in this study can be found in tables S1 and S2.
