## Abstract

De novo design of protein catalysts with high efficiency and stereoselectivity provides an attractive approach toward the design of environmentally benign catalysts. Here, we design proteins that incorporate histidine-ligated synthetic porphyrin and heme ligands. Four of 10 designed proteins catalyzed cyclopropanation with an enantiomeric ratio greater than 99:1. A second class of proteins were designed to catalyze a silicon-hydrogen insertion and were optimized by directed evolution in whole cells. The evolved proteins incorporated features unlikely to be generated by computational design alone, including a proline in an α helix. Molecular dynamics simulations showed that as the proteins evolved toward higher activity, their conformational ensembles narrowed to favor more productive conformations. Our work demonstrates that efficient de novo protein catalysts are designable and should be useful for manifold chemical processes.

* * *

De novo protein design provides an approach to test the mechanistic basis of protein function while offering a medium to engineer macromolecules with desirable functions, such as catalysis of new-to-nature reactions that have so far only been approached by modifying and engineering natural proteins ([1](#R1)–[3](#R3)). Compared with natural proteins, de novo proteins have many possible advantages for catalytic processes ([Fig. 1A](#F1)): (i) Their small size (<13 kDa) provides relatively favorable efficiency in terms of moles per gram of catalyst relative to most natural proteins; (ii) thermal stability allows use in reactions up to 100°C ([4](#R4)–[6](#R6)); and (iii) a variety of nonnatural organic, organic-metal, and metal ion cofactors can be incorporated ([7](#R7)–[16](#R16)). Moreover, we expected that the functionally essential tertiary structures of de novo proteins might be highly stable to green organic solvents such as ethanol ([17](#R17)), which greatly enhance the solubility of organic substrates and influence reactivity through solvent effects. De novo proteins can also be improved by directed evolution (DE) ([18](#R18)), although the full power of DE, including biosynthetic incorporation of complex cofactors together with screening in intact cells, has yet to be brought to de novo proteins. Finally, the Michaelis constant (*K*M) values of natural substrates tend to match the relevant concentration of substrates (micromolar to millimolar range)—weaker binding leads to low fractional occupancy of the active site, whereas tighter binding can lead to deep energy wells that can impede overall reactivity ([19](#R19)). By contrast, for practical catalytic applications, substrate concentrations are high, ideally 10 to 20% weight/volume (0.1 to 2.0 M). At these concentrations, the probability of a substrate encountering a protein active site per unit time is high, so there is less need for tight binding or construction of deep substrate-binding pockets.

Here, we show that this full list of desirable properties can indeed be combined into a single de novo protein. Previous examples of de novo proteins have demonstrated that some of these favorable properties are achievable, including stability to high concentrations of 2,2,2-trifluoroethanol ([9](#R9), [20](#R20), [21](#R21)). However, there are few examples of de novo protein catalysts and none that combine so many desirable characteristics. From the earliest days, it has been possible to design minimally sized de novo proteins with high thermodynamic stabilities ([4](#R4)–[6](#R6)). Also, the de novo design of proteins that bind both natural and unnatural cofactors, particularly metal ions and porphyrins, has been accomplished ([22](#R22)–[27](#R27)). However, rate enhancements and stereoselectivity have been modest in the absence of extensive rounds of DE ([28](#R28), [29](#R29)). And although heme cofactors have been biosynthetically incorporated into de novo proteins ([30](#R30), [31](#R31)), there have been no attempts to evolve them in bacteria. Here, we design a class of highly active heme and synthetic porphyrin-binding proteins, which we name NovoChromes.

Arnold, Fasan, and co-workers have previously shown that natural ferrous heme proteins can be repurposed and evolved to catalyze cyclopropanation ([32](#R32)–[35](#R35)), Si–H insertion ([36](#R36)), and other reactions involving metallocarbene insertions ([37](#R37)). The cyclopropanation reaction has also been assessed in the context of de novo protein, although these studies have been limited to natural heme cofactors. One designed protein, which loads heme in vivo, was shown to be a highly stable, selective catalyst for this reaction, although it did not show the full spectrum of desirable properties listed above ([Fig. 1A](#F1)) ([38](#R38), [39](#R39)). Here, we show that highly efficient catalytic proteins containing ferrous heme or synthetic ferrous porphyrin catalysts can be designed into structurally simple scaffolds by using modern methods for de novo protein design ([40](#R40)–[43](#R43)) in conjunction with careful consideration of the reaction mechanism and desirable reaction conditions.

## Design of highly selective and robust cyclopropane synthases

Cyclopropanation was chosen as a reaction with the challenge of stereoselective formation of two C–C bonds. Natural proteins have been engineered to catalyze this reaction, providing benchmarks of success ([32](#R32)–[34](#R34), [39](#R39), [44](#R44)). Metal-catalyzed cyclopropanation progresses through a metal carbenoid, which reacts with an alkene to form a cyclopropane. Here, a diazo compound serves as the carbene precursor ([45](#R45)), which asymmetrically reacts with a styrene ([46](#R46)). The reaction creates two stereogenic centers, leading to four possible stereoisomers. Biocatalysts for this reaction have been discovered by screening a library of heme proteins, followed by optimization through DE. We began by testing a previously designed porphyrin-binding protein, MPP1 ([27](#R27)), in complex with iron-diphenylporphyrin (FeDPP). The resulting iron protein, Fe-MPP1, catalyzed the cyclopropanation reaction between styrene (**1a**) and ethyl diazoacetate (EDA, **2**) with a low enantio and diastereo preference ([Fig. 1B](#F1)). We suspected that the problem might arise from the binding site being too small to accommodate substrates and the transition state (TS) ([fig. S1](#SD1)). To create a less crowded—but still chiral—environment at the binding site, we truncated a portion of the C-terminal helix that capped the upper roof of the binding site in MPP1 ([Fig. 1C](#F1), orange region). We then used variable backbone sequence design ([47](#R47)) to design ([Fig. 1D](#F1); [figs. S2](#SD1) and [S3](#SD1); and [supplementary materials](#SD1), [section 2.1.1](#SD1)) 10 protein sequences. All expressed well in bacteria, and the purified, reconstituted proteins catalyzed cyclopropanation in enantiomeric ratio (e.r.) ranging from 55:45 to 91:9, favoring the *trans*\- (*S*,*S*)- enantiomer ([table S4](#SD1)). The best one was named Porphyrin NovoChrome 1 (PNC1), which yielded a 96:4 diastereomeric ratio (d.r.) and 91:9 e.r. Two mutations of PNC1 \[Val19→Ile (V19I) and Gly108→Val (G108V)\] were made to help stabilize the packing of the C-terminal helix, which led to only a slight increase to 93:7 e.r.

Given the modest stereoselectivity of the double mutant, we determined its crystallographic structure. Although the overall structure matches the design model and the Evolutionary Scale Modeling (ESM)–predicted structures \[Cα root-mean-square deviation (RMSD) = 1.1 Å\], the essential details of the binding site deviated considerably from both the design model and ESM-predicted ([48](#R48)) structures ([fig. S4](#SD1) and [S5](#SD1)). A single turn of the N terminus of the third helix (H3) had unwound and instead adopted a more extended structure that meanders over the porphyrin, closing access to the active site ([Fig. 1E](#F1), cyan backbone). To achieve a better design, we used a backbone search algorithm, Method for Accelerated Search for Tertiary Ensemble Representatives (MASTER) ([40](#R40)), to discover a more favorable conformation for the hairpin between H2 and H3 ([Fig. 1F](#F1)). We then used LigandMPNN ([41](#R41)) to vary the sequence at all positions, except those engaged in primary and secondary metal interactions ([supplementary materials](#SD1), [section 2.1.2](#SD1)). We next generated 1000 porphyrin NovoChrome sequences, whose apo-structures were computed by using a protein structure prediction algorithm (Raptor-X) ([49](#R49)). Designs with high predicted local distance difference test (pLDDT) scores and low Cα RMSD were selected. Designs that accommodated the (*S,S*)-TS while clashing with the other isomers were further examined with ESMfold ([48](#R48)) to predict the structure of the un-complexed NovoChrome. Ten proteins that were predicted to have a highly preorganized binding site (pLDDT > 86 and RMSD < 0.9 Å; [table S5](#SD1)) were selected for bacterial expression. All were monomeric as assessed by size exclusion chromatography of the proteins after reconstitution with the FeDPP cofactor ([fig. S7](#SD1)).

We next evaluated the ability of the NovoChromes to catalyze cyclopropane formation from EDA and styrene: When reconstituted with ferrous cofactor, 7 of the 10 designs yielded >95:5 e.r. for the *trans* product; 4 yielded >99:1 e.r. ([table S6](#SD1)); and 1 design, PNC2, had an excellent *trans*/*cis* ratio of 98.5:1.5 and 99.8:0.2 e.r. for the *trans* product. The turnover frequency of PNC2 was 290,000 hours−1 ([table S7](#SD1)), and a total turnover number (TTN) of 40,000 (e.r. of 98.5 to 1.5 for the *trans* product) was determined at a styrene concentration of 0.25 M and a catalyst mole fraction of 10−5 relative to styrene. For comparison, a previously reported myoglobin-based catalyst reached a similar efficiency for the same reaction after being optimized through DE (e.r. 99.95:0.05 for the *trans* product and a TTN of 46,800) ([32](#R32)–[35](#R35)).

In the course of these studies, we found that the protein was highly stable in ethanol, which was added to solubilize the substrates. This prompted us to evaluate the stability of the tertiary structure of the PNC2 complex using circular dichroism (CD) spectroscopy. Apo- and holo-P NC2 are thermostable, with melting temperature (*T*m) of >90°C ([fig. S8](#SD1)). Although the diphenylporphyrin is achiral, it is held in a chiral environment when bound to the native conformation of the PNC2 protein, which induces a Cotton effect in the Soret region of the spectrum centered at 410 nm. Using this metric, we showed that the holoprotein retained its native structure at concentrations of ethanol as high as 70% ([Fig. 2A](#F2)). There was also a parallel decrease in the intensity of the ultraviolet (UV) bands that are characteristic of α-helical structure at 222 nm (n−π\*) as well as the π−π\* transition at 208 nm ([Fig. 2B](#F2)). Consistently, the protein also maintained its high catalytic performance in up to 70% ethanol \[99% yield and >99:1 e.r. for the (*S,S*)- isomer\] ([Fig. 2C](#F2)). The e.r. also remained high in 50% methanol, acetone, acetonitrile, and *N*,*N*-dimethylformamide ([table S8](#SD1)). This high stability in mixed aqueous organic solvents contrasts with natural proteins, which generally lose stability after adding >10 to 20% organic solvent ([50](#R50), [51](#R51)).

PNC2 retained its activity over a range of substitutions to the styrene substrate ([Fig. 2D](#F2)). The protein in 50% ethanol showed excellent activity for styrenes with an *ortho*\- (**3b**) or a *meta*-methyl (**3c**) substituent, which were converted in 99% yield to the *trans* isomer, with e.r. values of 99:1. The yields and stereoselectivity values remained high for substrates with a 4-fluorophenyl (**3e**) and 4-chlorophenyl group (**3f**) (96 and 95% yield, respectively, and e.r. > 99:1) and only began to drop when bulkier groups such as trifluoromethyl (**3g**) and methoxy (**3h**) were present (59 and 63%, respectively, and 99:1 and 90:10 e.r., respectively). Notably, α-methylstyrene maintained a similarly high yield of 99% and >99:1 e.r., furnishing a cyclopropane with adjacent quaternary-tertiary stereocenters (**3i**).

## Design of heme-based proteins for in-cell catalysis of Si–H insertion reactions

For many applications, it would be advantageous to design heme-based NovoChromes that biosynthetically load heme in cells rather than relying on reconstitution after cell lysis. Moreover, for general utility, it would be helpful to use DE, which is facilitated by in-cell screening ([37](#R37)). Considering the similarity between the synthetic porphyrin and heme, we expected that the natural heme, protoporphyrin IX, would also bind to the relatively open binding site with its propionic acid side chains pointed toward solvent. UV-visible spectroscopy indicated that PNC2 indeed bound ferric protoporphyrin IX ([fig. S9](#SD1)). We also screened all 10 of our designed proteins and found that they showed moderate to high e.r. for styrene cyclopropanation ([table S9](#SD1)).

We next evaluated the effectiveness of the heme derivatives of the 10 NovoChromes to catalyze Si–H insertion ([36](#R36)) using dimethylphenylsilane (**4a**) and ethyl 2-diazopropanoate (“Me-EDA,” **5**) as the model substrates ([Fig. 3A](#F3) and [table S10](#SD1)). To facilitate this goal, we first redesigned PNC2 to enhance its affinity for heme, which would allow biosynthetic incorporation and thus enable screening in intact cells. Beginning with the scaffold of PNC2, we first targeted the heme propionic acid groups to enhance specificity and affinity. To facilitate this process, we used van der Mers ([43](#R43)) to identify side chains that could engage the propionate carboxylate groups, resulting in substituting arginine, serine, and serine at positions 7, 57, and 67, respectively ([Fig. 3B](#F3)). With these residues fixed and the TS for the Si–H reaction in the active site, we next followed the same procedure for the design of PNC2 ([supplementary materials](#SD1), [section 2.1.3](#SD1)). A total of 200 designs were generated with LigandMPNN; 10 designs that accommodated the desired (*R*)-TS but clashed with the other undesired (*S*)-TS were chosen for expression in *Escherichia coli*. All 10 designs have >88 pLDDT and <0.9-Å Cα RMSD ([table S11](#SD1)). All 10 of these proteins catalyzed the Si–H insertion reactions with moderate to high e.r. in whole-cell conditions ([table S12](#SD1)). The protein with the highest e.r. of 94:6 was designated FeHP-1 ([Fig. 3C](#F3)). A spectral titration showed that FeHP-1 bound ferric protoporphyrin IX quite tightly (a sensitivity analysis of the parameters in a least-squares fit to the data showed that dissociation constant (*K*d) < 5 nM) ([fig. S10](#SD1)). The Soret band of the complex has a maximum at 405 nm ([fig. S10](#SD1)), which is typical for heme proteins with histidine as an axial ligand ([39](#R39)).

Although FeHP-1 was already a good stereoselective catalyst, we investigated whether the fold and sequence of this protein were amenable to DE, particularly using whole cells, which maintain the heme cofactor in the active, ferrous oxidation state ([33](#R33)). We began by making mutants that alter the four side chains (at positions 70, 74, 106, and 110) with the greatest van der Waals contact with the substrate ([Fig. 3D](#F3)). Site-saturation mutagenesis libraries were generated by using the 22c-trick method ([52](#R52)), and 88 clones per library were evaluated using intact *E. coli* cells in a 96-w ell plate format for the target Si–H insertion reaction.

In the first round of DE, we found the Ala70→Pro (A70P) mutation increased the e.r. from 94:6 to 97:3 ([Fig. 3E](#F3) and [tables S13](#SD1) and [S14](#SD1)). A second round of DE, which used FeHP-1 A70P as the parent, identified FeHP-1 A70P Phe106→Val (F106V) with an improved TTN of 19,200 and an e.r. of 99:1 ([table S14](#SD1)). A final round of DE targeting the two C-terminal residues 111 and 112 identified several highly active mutants ([table S15](#SD1)), including a triple mutant FeHP-1 A70P F106V Asn112→Ser (N112S), which maintained high stereoselectivity and an increase in TTN to 32,990 ([Fig. 3E](#F3) and [table S14](#SD1)). For practical purposes, a relevant measure of catalytic proficiency is the ability of a protein to turn over substantial quantities of substrate at a high micromolar to low molar concentration on the time scale of an hour. At a silane concentration of 0.2 M, the TTN was 30,400 ([table S16](#SD1)), which compares favorably with highly evolved natural proteins for the same reactions ([34](#R34)).

FeHP-1 A70P F106V N112S displayed substantial stability toward organic solvents: Including 60% methanol ([fig. S11](#SD1) and [table S17](#SD1)), 40% ethanol ([table S18](#SD1)) and the e.r. remained unchanged in 40% *N*,*N*-dimethylformamide or 40% dimethylsulfoxide ([table S19](#SD1)). Its turnover frequency in 50% methanol was 260,000 hours−1 ([table S20](#SD1)), which is 95-fold faster than previously evolved natural proteins.

With this evolved FeHP-1 A70P F106V N112S, we next examined the substrate scope of this biocatalytic enantioselective Si–H insertion reaction ([Fig. 3F](#F3)). Aryldimethylsilanes that had *ortho*\- (**6b**), *meta*\- (**6c**), or *para*-methyl (**6d**) substituents were readily accommodated by FeHP-1 A70P F106V N112S, providing the corresponding Si–C bond-forming products with excellent TTNs and enantioselectivity. Electron-donating groups such as a methoxy (**6e**) were also compatible. Silanes bearing an electron-withdrawing group, including chlorine (**6f**) and trifluoromethyl (**6g**), were also compatible with this Si–H insertion protocol, although reduced activity was observed for the trifluoromethyl-substituted substrate. Finally, sensitive functional groups, including a methyl ester (**6h**), a free amine (**6i**), and a free phenol (**6j**), were readily tolerated by the protein catalyst, giving rise to the corresponding silylation product with excellent activity and enantioselectivity. In particular, a TTN of 40,600 was observed for the phenol-containing substrate, representing the highest reported for Si–H insertion reactions among heme protein catalysts.

## Origin of stereoselectivity and how mutations consolidate the active site of FeHP-1 A70P F106V N112S

We performed classical molecular dynamics (MD) simulations to understand the origin of the high stereoselectivity of PNC2 and the evolved FeHP-1 A70P F106V N112S. For the Si–H insertion, we conducted three independent 500-n s simulations of the carbenoid intermediate and the near-attack conformation (NAC) ([supplementary materials](#SD1), [section 5.3](#SD1)) ([53](#R53)). The rigidity of the active site of the evolved FeHP-1 A70P F106V N112S increases substantially relative to the parent FeHP-1, with decreases in the root-mean-square fluctuations for both the silane substrate as well as the surrounding residue in the protein ([figs. S32](#SD1) and [S33](#SD1) and [table S24](#SD1)). The silane is oriented optimally to enter the lowest-energy TS in FeHP-1 A70P F106V N112S; by contrast, in FeHP-1, the substrate populates two different poses that lead to higher-energy orientations of the phenyl ring substituent as the silane reaches the TS ([fig. S15](#SD1) and [S23](#SD1)). H4, which helps frame the silane-binding site, was less dynamic in FeHP-1 A70P F106V N112S than in FeHP-1, which appeared to be a result of a tighter interaction with a neighboring H3 ([Fig. 4](#F4) and [fig. S20](#SD1)). The dynamics were most notable in the carbene intermediate, in which the unoccupied silane-binding site was highly preorganized in FeHP-1 A70P F106V N112S but not in FeHP-1 ([fig. S33](#SD1)). Similar results were observed for the cyclopropanation reaction ([fig. S36](#SD1) and [table S26](#SD1)).

Notably, the degree of rigidity in the silane-binding site increased incrementally in successive generations of the silylation NovoChromes. The rigidity increased in series of FeHP-1, the A70P mutant, and the double mutant (A70P F106V), although little change was observed upon introducing the third substitution, N112S ([figs. S27](#SD1) to [S31](#SD1)). This same progression is seen in the enantioselectivity for these variants, with FeHP-1 < FeHP-1 A70P < FeHP-1 A70P F106V ≈ FeHP-1 A70P F106V N112S. Moreover, the structural ensembles for this series of mutants provide a rationale for the stereoselectivity of FeHP-1 (94:6 e.r.) and its improvement in FeHP-1 A70P F106V N112S (99:1 e.r.). The stereochemical outcome of the reaction depends on the direction of approach of the silane to a plane formed by the carbon of the carbene and its two neighboring carbon atoms. In the predominant conformation for each complex, the carbenoid fits snugly into a shallow pocket with the neighboring methyl abutting H3. By design, larger groups are not sterically accessible at this site, so the ethyl ester is directed toward the solvent (see [figs. S25](#SD1) and [S26](#SD1) for hydrogen bond analysis). This orientation protects one face of the carbene from reaction with the silane, thereby favoring the TS that leads to the (*R*)-enantiomer of **6a**. In FeHP-1, Ala70 makes a key interaction with both the ester and methyl groups of the carbenoid species; when this residue is changed to proline (in each of the mutants), the larger pyrrolidine side chain forms even more extensive and shape-complementary interactions with the carbenoid and the heme cofactor ([Fig. 4](#F4), [A](#F4) and [C](#F4)). Additionally, substituting a large Phe106 in FeHP-1 with valine in the double mutant and FeHP-1 A70P F106V N112S further solidifies the interface between Helix3 and Helix4, resulting in a better fit with the phenyl group of silane, enabling favorable hydrophobic interactions with Phe77 and Tyr107 on the two helices ([Fig. 4](#F4), [B](#F4) and [D](#F4), and [fig. S19D](#SD1)).

The introduction of the A70P substitution also induces a local irregularity in the helical geometry and a slight kinking of H3, which results in a better match with the contour of the substrates. By comparison, the more idealized geometry of the parent protein FeHP-1 engenders less geometric complementarity with the substrate. It is notable that the most helix-promoting residue, alanine, was replaced by the most helix-disrupting residue, proline, to improve function during DE. Indeed, it might be difficult to predict this substitution a priori through programs that drive toward highly stable and regular secondary structures, illustrating the complementarity of de novo design and DE.

## Conclusions

Nature uses a single heme cofactor for diverse functions, including oxygen binding, oxygenase, peroxidase, oxidase, haloperoxidase, and electron transfer, among others ([54](#R54)). Heme iron has relatively high reactivity, so the role of the protein is to guide the reaction toward the desired function and inhibit other modes of reactivity while simultaneously protecting the cofactor. This is accomplished by incorporating auxiliary substrate-binding sites and controlling the electrostatics, sterics, and water accessibility of the heme’s environment. In a similar manner, we use de novo design to craft NovoChrome catalysts for nonnatural cyclopropanation and silylation reactions. Our results suggest that de novo is a powerful strategy for the development of stereoselective biocatalysts that function well directly from design or can also be rapidly optimized via DE. An advantage of nonnatural porphyrin cofactors is the ease with which their reactivity can be tuned by changing the metal ion or porphyrin substituents in future applications. More generally, de novo design is poised to bring the genetic control and structural programmability of proteins to transition-metal catalysis.
