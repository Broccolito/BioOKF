Amyotrophic lateral sclerosis (ALS), also known as Lou Gehrig’s disease, is a fatal neurodegenerative disease characterized by the degeneration of motor neurons in brain and spinal cord1. Te loss of motor neuros in ALS patients usually cause death within 2–5 years afer diagnosis due to respiratory failure2. Te prevalence of the dev- astating disease is unknow for much of the world, and a prevalence of 3.9 cases of ALS per 100, 000 persons in the 3

U.S was found during October 2010 to December 2011. Although 5–10% cases identifed as familial ALS which linked to genetic mutations, 90–95% cases being sporadic with the cause remains unknown4. Tere is no cure for ALS, the only drug approved by FDA is riluzole which was found to modestly extend life by approximately two to three months5. Terefore, there is an urgent need to better understand the etiology of ALS and subsequently develop more efective therapy for the devastating disease. Increasing evidence suggest that infammatory response in the central nervous system (CNS) that includes proinfammatory cytokines contribute to the pathogenesis of ALS6. Neuroinfammation has been identifed as a prominent pathological signature in ALS, both post-mortem and PET imaging studies revealed microglial acti-
7, 8 vation in the patients with ALS. Moreover, infltration of immune cells have been found in the CNS of ALS 9–11 patients, these include macrophages and T-cells at sites of motor neuron injury. In addition, clinical studies have also suggested that peripheral infammation is implicated in ALS, and the peripheral immune abnormalities include T-cells, cytokines, chemokines and other markers of infammation11. Te easy access to blood have led to more and more researchers to study peripheral blood cytokine and chemok- ine aberrations in ALS, in hope of gaining novel insights into the pathogenesis of ALS and potential early diagno- sis of the disease, and even disease modifying treatments. A number of studies have demonstrated elevated blood levels of infammatory cytokines and chemokines in patient with ALS, such as tumor necrosis factor-α (TNF-α), 12–15 interleukin-6 (IL-6) and monocyte chemotactic protein 1 (MCP-1). However, other studies suggested that the 16–18 levels of cytokines in ALS patients were unchanged compared to control subjects. To address the inconsistent

### Methods

Tis systematic review and meta-analysis was performed according to the guidelines that are recommended by the PRISMA statement (Preferred Reporting Items for Systematic reviews and Meta-Analysis) 19 .

**Search strategy and study selection.** Two independent researchers (Y.H. and C.C.) performed a system- atic review of peer-reviewed English-language articles from Pubmed and Web of Science. Te database search term was: (infammation or cytokine or chemokine or interleukin or tumor necrosis factor or interferon) AND (Amyotrophic lateral sclerosis or ALS). Te search date started on May 11, 2016 and ended on January 1, 2017. Original studies that comparing peripheral blood cytokines between patients with ALS and control subjects were included, and we undertook meta-analysis whenever individual infammatory cytokine data were available in three or more articles.

**Data extraction.** Data were extracted by two of us (Y.H. and C.C.). We extracted data on mean cytokine concentrations, standard deviation, p value and sample size to calculate efective size (ES) for meta-analysis. We also extracted data on age, sex, disease duration, control type and sampling source for potential between-study heterogeneity analysis (Supplementary Table).

**Statistical analysis.** Comprehensive Meta-analysis sofware (version 2; Biostat Inc) was used to perform all the statistical analyses in this study. ESs were generated from sample size and mean cytokine concentrations with standard deviation, or sample sizes and P values. In some studies, P values were reported as inequality rather than exact values, or reported as no signifcant diferences of cytokine concentrations between ALS patients and control subjects. We then contacted the corresponding authors of the original articles to request the necessary data to generate efective sizes. When the data were not available from the original articles or corresponding authors, we calculated the ESs as described previously 20, 21 . ESs were calculated as the standardized diferences in mean cytokine concentrations between ALS patients and controls, and then converted to Hedges g statistic, which adjusted the ESs based on sample sizes22. An ES estimate was calculated for individual cytokine when- ever the cytokine data were reported in at least three studies. A random-efects model was undertaken for the meta-analysis as we hypothesized that both within-study and between-study heterogeneity modulated the true ES23. Sensitivity analysis was undertaken to test the robustness of the outcome of the meta-analysis, this was achieved by excluding one study at a time to perform meta-analysis. Te I2 statistic and Cochrane Q test were used to analyze the between-study heterogeneity as described previously23. I2 statistic of 0.25, 0.5, 075 indicated small, moderate, and high levels of variance among studies. Unrestricted maximum-likelihood random-efects meta-regressions of ES24 were performed to test whether sam- ple size, patient age and gender (proportion of male individuals), and disease duration had moderating efects on the outcomes of the meta-analysis. Publication bias was determined by the Egger test as described previously25. All values for signifcances in this study were set at P < 0.05 except statistical diference for the Cochrane Q test, which was set at P < 0.10.

### Results

Te systematic review of the literature identifed 1224 records from PubMed and 1024 from Web of Science. Afer screening the titles and abstracts, 53 articles were selected for full text review. 28 studies were excluded because they reported individual cytokine data in less than three studies (13 studies), lacked necessary data (11 studies), lacked a control group (2 studies), reported cytokine levels in vitro (2 studies). Terefore, 25 articles comparing 14 blood infammatory cytokines between 812 ALS patients and 639 controls were included in this meta-analysis 12–18, 26–43 (Fig. 1).

**Main association of ALS with blood cytokine levels.** Random-efects meta-analysis demonstrated that blood TNF-α (Hedges’ g = 0.655; 95% CI, 0.284 to 1.026; p = 0.001), TNF receptor 1 (TNFR1; Hedges’ g = 0.741; 95% CI, 0.474 to 1.007, p < 0.001), IL-6 (Hedges’ g = 0.25; 95% CI, 0.074 to 0.427; p = 0.005), IL-1β (Hedges’ g = 0.296; 95% CI, 0.017 to 0.575; p = 0.038), IL-8 (Hedges’ g = 0.449; 95% CI, 0.258 to 0.641; p < 0.001) and vascular endothelial growth factor (VEGF; Hedges’ g = 0.891, 95% CI: 0.298, 1.485; p = 0.003) levels were signif- cantly elevated in patients with ALS compared with control subjects (Figs 2–4 and Table 1). ### Discussion

Tis meta-analysis included 25 case-control studies assessing 812 ALS patients and 639 control subjects, and found evidence of signifcant elevations of peripheral blood infammatory cytokines for TNF-α, TNFR1, IL1β, IL-6, IL-8 and VEGF in ALS patients compared with controls. For those cytokines signifcantly associated with ALS, medium to large ESs were found for TNF-α, TNFR1 and VEGF, whereas IL1β, IL-6, IL-8 showed small to medium ESs. Sensitivity analyses suggested that the signifcant associations between blood TNF-α, TNFR1 or IL-8 levels and ALS were not infuenced by an individual study, suggesting the robustness of these associations. However, the statistically signifcant associations observed in this meta-analysis for cytokines IL1β, IL-6 and VEGF could be infuenced by a single study. Tis is due to the small ESs associated the results for IL1β and IL-6, and the limited number of studies for VEGF (3 studies). Neuroinfammation is a prominent feature in neurodegenerative diseases such as Alzheimer’s disease (AD), Parkinson’s disease (PD), and ALS which are characterized by the appearance of reactive microglial and astroglial cells in central nervous system44. Moreover, a substantial number of studies suggested that peripheral infam- mation may also contribute to the pathological mechanisms of neurodegenerative diseases, with interests on the aberrant levels of peripheral blood infammatory cytokines in patients with AD, PD and ALS 11, 45 . Previous meta-analyses have been performed for peripheral blood cytokine levels in AD and PD. Similar to the fndings of our present meta-analysis in ALS, levels of TNF-α, IL-6 and IL-1β were elevated in patients with AD and PD 46–48 ,

suggesting that the elevations of blood infammatory cytokine TNF-α, IL-6 and IL-1β are not specifc in ALS. Furthermore, the increased concentrations of blood TNFR1 in ALS patients found in this meta-analysis may also not be specifc in ALS, because two studies have consistently demonstrated that patients with PD had increased levels of TNFR1 compared with control subjects 49, 50 . However, the infammatory cytokine IL-8, which the present meta-analysis showed signifcant association with ALS from 6 studies, and which zero heterogeneity was found among studies, was not associated with AD or PD in the respective meta-analyses 47, 48 . Another infammatory cytokine VEGF, which the meta-analysis identifed as the cytokine with largest ES in ALS studies, and which is also well known for its roles in angiogenesis and neuroprotection 51 , may be specifcally elevated in ALS. Tis is due to the two studies which showed no association between blood VEGF and PD 52, 53 , and our unpublished meta-analysis suggests that blood VEGF is not signifcantly associated with AD. Tese above results indicate that major neurodegenerative diseases including AD, PD and ALS may have both shared and distinct infammatory responses. A biomarker was defned as “a characteristic that is objectively measured and evaluated as an indicator of nor- mal biological processes, pathogenic processes, or pharmacologic responses to a therapeutic intervention.” by the National Institutes of Health Biomarkers Defnitions Working Group54. Biomarker research from blood have con- ducted intensively over the past two decades in neurodegenerative diseases including ALS, as it is non-invasive, easy to access and handle with low cost. ALS is diagnosed based on clinical evaluations and can be easy identifed at its full-blown presentation. However, there is a pronounced delay between the onset of symptoms and diag- nosis, with the diagnostic process as long as between 13 to 18 months55. Although only one drug (riluzole) with modest disease-modifying potency is available for ALS4, it is generally accepted that the more efective therapeu- tic interventions with earlier diagnosis. In addition, the lack of validated biomarkers in disease progression and patient prognosis of ALS may contribute to the failed efort for clinical trials over the last half century. Terefore,

there is an urgent need to fnd molecular biomarkers that could give reliable information on the onset and/or pro- gression of ALS. Peripheral blood infammatory cytokine would be of particularly attractive biomarkers, because there are multiple assays available for peripheral cytokines, and potentially additional mechanistic research to better understand the role of infammation in the devastating disease and subsequent therapeutic interventions. Indeed, clinical results from diferent groups suggested that the infammatory cytokines are promising biomark- ers for ALS, with a variety number of studies showed alterations of blood infammatory cytokines in patients with ALS when compared with controls 12–15 . In addition, one study showed that serum MCP-1 protein, MCP-1 mRNA, VEGF mRNA, smoking and alcohol consumption are the independent variables that diferentiated ALS and con- trols, with a sensitivity of 93.2% and specifcity of 86.2%. However, the alterations of cytokines were inconsistent for individual cytokines and between studies 15–17, 29 . In this meta-analysis, we for the frst time showed consistent aberrations of several cytokines in ALS from published literature. Concentrations of peripheral blood TNF-α, TNFR1, IL-6, IL-1β, IL-8, VEGF were signifcantly higher in patients with ALS when compared with controls. For those cytokines signifcantly associated with ALS, the ESs associated with the results of TNF-α, TNFR1 and VEGF were large, suggesting the potential usefulness of these cytokines as practical diagnostic biomarkers for ALS in the future. Additionally, these potential infammatory cytokine biomarkers from blood may be used to better estimate the rate of ALS progression and stratify patients with ALS in clinical trials. In fact, Babu et al. found that blood TNF-α levels are positively correlated with disease duration of ALS12. Lu et al. followed a cohort of ALS patients longitudinally and demonstrated IL-6 had signifcantly increased expression towards end-stage disease in the longitudinal analysis15. Te heterogeneity for the individual cytokines in our present meta-analysis varied from zero to high. For the cytokines significantly associated with ALS, high and moderate levels of heterogeneity were found for TNF-α and VEGF studies, respectively, whereas studies for TNFR1, IL1β, IL-6 and IL-8 did not show signifcant heterogeneity in this meta-analysis. Te strength of this study is that meta-analytic technique with subgroup and meta-regression analyses were used to adjust for the potential confounders that explained the high level between-study heterogeneity for TNF-α, although the limited number of studies for VEGF prevented us to fur- ther analyze the heterogeneity. Subgroup analysis stratifed by control type suggested that the ES and statisti- cal signifcance were increased for TNF-α studies when compared ALS patients with normal controls, whereas TNF-α levels were not signifcantly elevated in patients with ALS when compared with disease controls, although the heterogeneity remained high for studies comparing ALS patients and normal controls. Tis is reasonable as we have discussed above that the alterations of TNF-α levels in ALS may be not specifc in neurodegenerative dis- eases, and most disease control subjects in this meta-analysis were neurological disease. Meta-regression analysis demonstrated that age had moderating efect on the outcome of the meta-analysis, whereas other potential con- founders including gender, disease duration and samples size did not have moderating efects on the outcome of the meta-analysis, suggesting that the moderating efect of age was unlikely to be secondary to disease duration. Although this meta-analysis provided strong evidence of peripheral infammatory response in ALS, and the between-study heterogeneity was partially addressed by meta-regression analyses for TNF-α studies, this meta-analysis has some limitations. First, the meta-analysis of peripheral blood levels of infammatory cytokines in patients with ALS compared with controls provided us pooled results from case-control (cross-sectional) stud- ies, thus it is unclear whether the increased releases of cytokines are a cause or consequence for ALS onset. Second, studies on the associations between cytokines and disease duration or disease severity are limited, there- fore prevented us from assessing whether the blood infammatory cytokines have potential to be validated bio- markers to predict disease progression and/or patient diagnosis for ALS, and future studies are necessary to address this question. Tird, the limited number of studies with smaller sample sizes may have made observation of signifcant associations difcult for some cytokines. As examples are IL-10 and IL-17 which showed increased levels in patients with ALS, but did not research statistical signifcance (p = 0.081 and 0.087 respectively, see Table 1). Terefore, data from future studies on the cytokine levels in ALS that added into this meta-analysis may generate more cytokines that signifcantly associate with ALS. Fourth, what is the signifcance of the increased peripheral infammatory response related to CNS remains unclear, thus future analyses of CNS cytokine levels in ALS are necessary to better understand the etiology of the disease. Tese limits highlight the need for continued investigations into the aberrations of infammatory cytokines in ALS. In conclusion, this meta-analysis is the frst undertaken to investigate the alterations of infammatory cytokine levels in ALS patients, and demonstrated increased peripheral blood TNF-α, TNFR1, IL-1β, IL-6, IL-8 and VEGF levels in ALS patients compared to control subjects. Tese results strengthen the clinical evidence of an increased infammatory response in patients with ALS, thus providing a new insight into a potential molecular pathway that confers vulnerability to the onset and/or development of ALS, and underscores the potential role of cytokines as biomarkers for ALS, giving the robust and consistent associations between some cytokines and ALS. Terefore, more studies are needed in the future, hopefully with international cooperation, to translate the potential blood cytokine biomarkers into beneft of ALS patients.
