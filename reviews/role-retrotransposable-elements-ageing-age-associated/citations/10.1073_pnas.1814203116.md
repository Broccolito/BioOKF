Stoye) Human endogenous retrovirus-K (HERV-K) human mouse mammary tumor virus-like 2 (HML-2) is the most recently active endogenous retrovirus group in humans, and the only group with human- specific proviruses. HML-2 expression is associated with cancer and other diseases, but extensive searches have failed to reveal any replication-competent proviruses in humans. However, HML-2 proviruses are found throughout the catarrhine primates, and it is possible that they continue to infect some species today. To investi- gate this possibility, we searched for gorilla-specific HML-2 elements using both in silico data mining and targeted deep-sequencing ap- proaches. We identified 150 gorilla-specific integrations, including 31 2-LTR proviruses. Many of these proviruses have identical LTRs, and are insertionally polymorphic, consistent with very recent integra- tion. One identified provirus has full-length ORFs for all genes, and thus could potentially be replication-competent. We suggest that gorillas may still harbor infectious HML-2 virus and could serve as a model for understanding retrovirus evolution and pathogenesis in humans.

exogenous retroviruses (1–3). These sequences are the result of retroviral infection and integration of the provirus into the ge- nome of germ-line cells, and provide a record of past retroviral infections. Once integrated, such proviruses are permanent resi- dents of the host and will be present in all cells of progeny derived from the infected germ-line cell (4, 5). Most ERV sequences have numerous mutations that render them noninfectious. Addition- ally, homologous recombination can occur between the 5′ and 3′ LTRs of a provirus after integration, leading to the loss of internal coding sequence and producing a solo LTR. About 90% of ERV integrations have been reduced to solo LTRs. However, replication- competent ERVs have been found in a number of species, and recombination between defective ERVs can also lead to the pro- duction of infectious virus (6, 7). Human endogenous retroviruses (HERVs) constitute ∼8% of the genome (8), with ∼30 HERV groups represented (5, 9). The groups are currently named for the specific tRNA used for priming reverse transcription (10, 11), with the HERV-K group further divided into 11 subtypes that reflect their similarity to the infectious mouse mammary tumor virus (MMTV) (4, 12, 13). The human MMTV-like 2 (HML-2) subtype (hereafter called “HML- 2”) is particularly interesting for a variety of reasons. In addition to having members that possess a number of full-length ORFs, it contains the youngest known HERV sequences, and is the only one known to have human-specific integrations (14). These pro- viruses are further classified into three subtypes based on LTR phylogeny: LTR5A, LTR5B, and LTR5Hs. LTR5B contains the oldest insertions, and the LTR5A and LTR5Hs clades branch separately out of this group (14). LTR5Hs includes the most re- cently integrated sequences and is the only group that has human- specific integrations, whereas LTR5A and 5B appear to have ceased activity in the hominoid lineage before the split of humans

The youngest known HML-2 provirus may have integrated in humans as recently as 100,000 y ago, suggesting that this group was still active after the evolution of anatomically modern humans (15–17). Additionally, more than half of the known human-specific HML-2 proviruses are insertionally poly- morphic, with some insertions present in fewer than 5% of indi- viduals (12, 18–20). Despite evidence of evolutionarily recent activity (as well as occasional reports to the contrary), attempts by multiple labo- ratories have failed to find any unambiguous evidence for on- going HML-2 replication in humans. We previously reported our attempts to identify rare, recent HML-2 integrations in short- read sequence data from over 2,500 individuals in the 1000 Genomes Project (21). Although we were able to identify and EVOLUTION characterize rare HML-2 integrations, including one with full- length ORFs for all genes, none of the proviruses appeared to be derived from recent activity (22). Although no infectious pro- virus is known, the high numbers of relatively intact, insertionally polymorphic HML-2 proviruses in humans have led researchers to investigate this group for links to disease (8, 23). Significance

Human endogenous retrovirus-K (HERV-K) human mouse mam- mary tumor virus-like 2 (HML-2) is the most recently active endog- enous retrovirus group in humans. Their proviruses are also found within the genomes of all apes and Old World monkeys; however, no HML-2 provirus is known to be naturally infectious. Although these proviruses seem to be functionally extinct in both humans and chimpanzees, less is known about the profile and activity of HML-2 proviruses in gorillas. Our work here has identified gorilla- specific HML-2 elements that have characteristics consistent with very recent activity, and raises the possibility that gorillas may still contain infectious HML-2 virus. Thus, gorillas could serve as a model for how HML-2 functioned as a virus in humans, as well as shed light on its role in pathogenesis and host–virus evolution.

been observed in a number of disease states (24). Despite the abundance of research, no causative role has been proven for any HML-2 provirus in any disease. Attempts to prove such a role are hampered by insufficient knowledge of basic HML-2 biology, and of how HML-2 functions as a virus. Consensus HML- 2 proviruses have been shown to be weakly infectious in vitro; however, it is unclear how well these experiments recapitulate how HML-2 is replicated in vivo (25, 26). Although many HML-2 insertions are human-specific, there are a number of older HML-2 integrations at identical sites in all apes and Old World monkeys, dating their integration to as much as 35 million y ago, soon after the split with New World monkeys (14, 17, 27). Although these viruses may have become functionally extinct in humans, it is possible that active forms could currently exist in other primates, perhaps even in our closest relatives, chimpanzees and gorillas. Given the presence of shared HML-2 proviruses between these species, we thought it worthwhile to examine the possibility that chimpanzees and/or gorillas might have been subject to more recent—and possibly ongoing—infection and reintegration with HML-2 virus. Al- though chimpanzee-specific proviruses have been reported (28), the chimpanzee genome contains relatively low numbers of chimpanzee-specific HML-2 integrations in comparison with humans, suggesting even less recent activity in this species than in humans. In contrast, very little has been published on HML-2 proviruses in other hominoids, including gorillas. In this study, we apply to the gorilla genome two methodologies we have de- veloped for the identification of proviral integrations in the hu- man genome (22, 29). One method utilizes publicly available whole-genome sequence data published by the Great Ape Ge- nome Project (GAGP) (30), while a second method generates sequence libraries constructed to target, enrich, and identify HML-2 proviral integration junctions from genomic DNA. Using these methods, we have identified and sequenced 22 gorilla-specific 2-LTR proviruses not found in the reference gorilla genome. We identified an additional nine gorilla-specific 2-LTR proviruses present in the gorGor5 reference genome, and estimated the population frequency, time of integration, and cod- ing capacity of all proviruses identified. Most of the proviruses identified are insertionally polymorphic and appear to have in- tegrated very recently. Consistent with their recent origin, many contain one or more full-length ORFs. One of these proviruses has full-length ORFs for all retroviral genes, and could also potentially be replication-competent. Overall, the gorilla HML-2 proviruses comprise by far the most recently active HERV-related virus group in any known hominoid species and the most likely to represent still-active transmitted viruses.

Results Discovery of HERV-K (HML-2) Insertions. In this study, we used two separate methods to identify gorilla-specific HML-2 insertions not present in the current reference assembly of the Gorilla sp. genome. The first approach used searches of publicly available whole-genome sequence data from gorillas, using a data-mining technique that we previously applied to human samples from the 1000 Genomes Project (22). Though gorilla genomes have not been sequenced at anywhere near the scale of the 1000 Genomes Project, a smaller-scale study, the Great Ape Genome Project, finished in 2013, included high-coverage Illumina sequencing of 79 great apes from all six great ape species. We downloaded all available gorilla sequence data from this project as unaligned FASTQ files. In total, we obtained sequence from 30 individuals from 3 subspecies, including 26 Western lowland (Gorilla gorilla gorilla), Cross River (Gorilla gorilla diehli), and Eastern lowland (Gorilla beringei graueri). Analysis began with an align- ment step to the current gorGor5 long-read gorilla genome as- sembly (31). We retrieved unmapped reads from each aligned gorilla genome and searched reads with sequence matching the

(human) HML-2 5′ or 3′ LTR edges, filtering out any reads with junctions that matched known HML-2 proviruses in humans or gorillas and any reads with <10 bp of sequence flanking the in- sertion. From the 30 gorilla genomes screened, we identified 2,057 putative nonreference insertion sites, of which 184 had reads corresponding to both the 5′ and 3′ junctions (SI Appendix, Table S1). We focused our downstream analyses on this group of high-confidence, two-sided hits, though it is likely that many of the hits with reads corresponding to only 5′ or 3′ flanks are genuine. Of the two-sided 184 hits, 130 were found in a single subspecies, with 117 unique to Western lowland gorillas and 13 unique to the Eastern lowland gorillas. Thirty-two hits were found in the single Cross River sample; however, all of them were shared within the Western lowland subspecies. Of the 54 proviruses shared between two or more subspecies, 22 were found only in the Western and Eastern lowland gorillas, 18 only in Western and Cross River, and 14 sites were found in all 3 subspecies. As we show below, this extent of polymorphism far exceeds that seen in humans for the same provirus group. In addition to identifying nonreference HML-2 insertions, we took advantage of the recently released long-read gorilla genome assembly (gorGor5) to identify further gorilla-specific insertions (32). Our previous attempts to identify such insertions from earlier gorilla assemblies were hindered by very unreliable assembly of HML-2 elements, with artifactual “pseudolog” proviruses placed at the sites of known human-specific HML-2 insertions, as well as gaps in the assembly at sites of genuine gorilla-specific in- sertions. We were hopeful that the long reads used in the gorGor5 assembly would improve the assembly of large insertions such as ERVs. To identify gorilla-specific HML-2 proviruses from this assem- bly, we downloaded all LTR5Hs and LTR5 coordinates annotated by RepeatMasker (33) in the gorGor5 assembly available on the UCSC Genome Browser (32). We then cross-referenced these sites against the same data from the human, chimpanzee, and orangutan genomes, filtering out any sites where at least one of those three genomes had an annotated HML-2 insertion within 1kb.“Full-length” (or “2-LTR,” as distinct from solo LTRs) proviruses within this dataset were identified by filtering for sites with HML-2 internal genic regions, classified as “HERVK-int” by RepeatMasker. We identified nine gorilla-specific 2-LTR full- length or nearly full length proviruses present in the gorGor5 reference (Table 1, source: gorGor5), as well as 99 gorilla-specific solo LTRs (SI Appendix,TableS2). Our second approach to discovery of nonreference gorilla HML-2 elements adapted a linker-mediated PCR sequence li- brary preparation protocol first designed to specifically target and enrich human T-lymphotropic virus (HTLV) and HIV pro- viral integrations (34) to similarly identify HML-2 integrations in gorilla genomic DNA. We began our library preparation by attaching a linker to sheared DNA fragments to permit the spe- cific PCR enrichment of fragments containing HML-2 integrations. This PCR amplification step was designed to target the 5′ LTR region and the 5′ flanking genomic DNA of an HML-2 provirus using primers complementary to the linker and the untranslated leader sequence between the 5′ LTR and the gag gene. LTR- specific primers were not used in the first round to avoid ampli- fying solo LTRs. A second round of PCR amplification allowed for further enrichment of the 5′ flanking genomic DNA using an HML-2 primer designed to anneal near the end of the 5′ LTR, again in combination with a nested linker-specific primer. Library construction was conducted for each of three available Western lowland gorilla DNA samples. Libraries were sequenced on the Illumina MiSeq platform, aligned to the gorGor5 genome, and filtered to remove known HML-2 insertions. The resulting read data yielded 24 highly targeted loci not present in gorGor5. All 24 loci were investigated further, producing 18 previously unknown HML-2 integrations (Table 1, source: library).

Nomenclature for HML-2 Insertions. To distinguish among the thousand or so HML-2 proviruses (and solo LTRs) in the human genome, we have adapted the convention of naming specific proviruses by the chromosome band in which they are located (14, 35). In the absence of cytogenetic mapping of the gorilla genome, we adapted a related nomenclature convention. The chromosomal location of each newly found integration site was identified in the gorGor4 genome build (as the contigs in the gorGor5 assembly have not yet been assigned to chromosomes) and the provirus name was based on the chromosome number and megabase interval of each in- sertion, following the form chromosome.megabase (e.g., a provirus at chr9:32500000 would be named 9.32). Neighboring integrations within the same megabase interval will be given an additional letter corresponding to the order of discovery, though none was identified in this study.

PCR Validation and Frequency Estimations. The 24 nonreference insertions identified from the targeted library and 66 of the hits from the GAGP sample mining were validated with allele-specific PCR on genomic DNA from three Western lowland gorillas. Primers targeting the 5′ and 3′ genomic flanking DNA of each insertion were designed and used along with previously designed HML-2–specific primers (19, 22) to amplify both the insertion and the virus–host DNA genomic junction; 18/24 targeted library hits and 24/66 GAGP hits were confirmed in at least one sample. Four 2-LTR proviruses and 20 solo LTRs were identified from the GAGP, while all of the hits from the targeted library were 2-LTR

proviruses, as expected. In total, we have confirmed 22 gorilla- specific 2-LTR proviruses and 20 solo LTRs not present in the gorGor5 reference genome, in addition to 9 gorilla-specific 2-LTR proviruses and 99 solo LTRs from the gorGor5 assembly, for a total of 150 gorilla-specific HML-2 insertions. To estimate the frequency of the 2-LTR proviruses, we used the same allele-specific PCR assay on a larger panel of 10 Western lowland gorilla DNA samples, with the addition of primer sets for the gorilla-specific 2-LTR proviruses from the gorGor5 reference genome. The majority of the proviruses screened were insertion- ally polymorphic, with the exception of two proviruses identified in the reference genome which were homozygous for the insertion in all 10 samples screened. Many of the proviruses were present at quite low frequency, with over half present in less than 50% of the samples and nine found in only one sample (Fig. 1A and SI Ap- pendix, Table S3). In addition to PCR screening, we estimated the frequency of the 184 high-confidence hits across the 30 gorilla samples in the Great Ape Genome Project. There was a wide range of fre- quencies, with one hit being found in 100% of the samples, but the vast majority were found in less than 50% of the samples (Fig. 1B). Interestingly, 30 hits were found in only 1 of the 30 gorillas screened. Although it is likely that screening a larger sample would identify additional gorillas carrying some of these rare insertions, it is possible that some of them are unique to a single individual. Structure and Coding Capacity of Gorilla-Specific Proviruses. Using the same combination of primers used for genotyping, each non- reference insertion was amplified and sequenced. This analysis provided structural information about each insertion, such as putative ORFs, point mutations, large-scale deletions, and target- site duplications (which are characteristic of retroviral integra- tion). Twenty-four of the 31 2-LTR proviruses discovered had a full-length ORF for at least one viral gene (Table 1), with one of the proviruses, 9.32, displaying full-length ORFs for all four ret- roviral genes without the presence of any apparent inactivating mutations. Testing of this provirus for its ability to encode infec- tious virus is ongoing. Fig. 2 shows the overall structure of each identified provirus, excluding solo LTRs. Full-length ORFs, large deletions, small insertions, and inactivating mutations are in- dicated in the schematic images. All full-length HML-2 proviruses in hominoids can be divided into two structural categories: type 1 proviruses containing a characteristic 292-bp deletion in env,and type 2, with the 292-bp region intact. As in humans (36, 37), ap- proximately half of the proviruses identified are type 1 and half are type 2. Interestingly, nine of the gorilla proviruses shared another (1,492-bp) deletion (not seen in humans), which is found in both type 1 and type 2 proviruses, and which removes a large fraction of pro and pol, presumably rendering them completely incapable of infection without the aid of a replication-competent helper vi- rus. Also, two proviruses share a third, 1,372-bp, deletion in env (Fig. 2). Like the type 1 deletion, both of these novel shared de- letions are frameshifting, although the significance of this obser- vation is unclear.

Phylogenetic Analysis. We generated a neighbor-joining phylogeny of gorilla-specific insertions, using the LTR sequences of all 2-LTR proviruses identified (Fig. 3). For comparison, we also included the sequences of all of the 2-LTR proviruses known from humans and a

small selection of chimpanzee-specific LTRs extracted from the chimpanzee reference genome (panTro3) (32). As expected, most of the species-specific LTRs clustered in separate clades, with the human and chimp insertions clustering as sister clades and the gorilla-specific insertions forming an out- group to chimpanzees and humans. Strikingly, the gorilla-specific clade had noticeably shorter branch lengths than the human- or chimpanzee-specific clades, indicating much less sequence diver- gence in the gorilla-specific proviruses; indeed, two subclades in- clude multiple sequences with identical LTRs. This low level of divergence suggested to us that many of these insertions were very young, and that the HML-2s have been active in gorillas much more recently than in humans or chimpanzees. To investigate this possibility more thoroughly, we first used the 5′–3′ LTR comparison method to date those proviruses with two LTRs (13, 14). The mean age of the gorilla-specific provi- ruses was ∼380,000 y, with individual proviruses ranging in age from 1.93 million y (MY) to <300,000 y. Twelve proviruses had identical LTRs and thus we could only estimate that they in- tegrated at most ∼300,000 y ago, the average time for a single- nucleotide difference to arise between proviral LTRs. As these proviruses could have integrated any time between 300,000 y ago and the present, we used the midpoint of 150,000 y for these proviruses for calculating the mean provirus age. Although 5′–3′ LTR divergence is the preferred method to date individual proviruses, the limited number of 2-LTR proviruses in the ge- nome constrains the usefulness of this method for estimating the actual level of HERV activity over time. As in humans, there are about 10 times as many HML-2 solo LTRs (as well as truncated proviruses with only a single LTR) as there are 2-LTR proviruses in the gorilla genome, however, and we wanted to use this abundance of sequence information to get a better sense of the levels of HML-2 activity over time in gorillas. To estimate the insertion times of solo LTRs, we modified our 5′–3′ LTR com- parison method to use the divergence between each LTR and the nearest node on a neighbor-joining phylogeny. Using the same methods as we used to identify species-specific insertions in the gorilla reference genome, we downloaded and determined the species specificity of HML-2 LTR5Hs solo LTRs in the latest human and chimpanzee reference genomes (hg38 and panTro5, respectively); to this list we added the sequences of all known nonreference human-specific LTR5Hs LTRs. For each species, we made a neighbor-joining tree of all of the LTR sequences from that species and estimated the age of each LTR based on the branch distance to the nearest node on the tree (SI Appendix, Fig. S2). As previously, proviruses with identical 5′ and 3′ LTRs were assigned an age of 150,000 y. Similarly, solo LTRs with no sequence differences from their nearest neighbor were assigned an age of 300,000 y. To test the accuracy of this method compared with 2-LTR estimates, we used the same approach with the 5′ and 3′ LTRs of 2-LTR proviruses, using the distance to the nearest LTR not belonging to the same provirus. The ages for proviruses calcu- lated in this manner were indeed greater than the ages for the same proviruses calculated by 2-LTR comparison, though the difference was only statistically significant for nonhuman-specific proviruses (Fig. 4A). ficity and the 2-LTR method can produce young biased ages due to gene conversion (38, 39), so it seemed likely to us that the age estimates are roughly accurate (Fig. 4B). We applied these dating methods to our full dataset of human-, gorilla-, and chimpanzee- specific solo LTRs and proviruses, as well as those shared between these species (Fig. 4C). The chimpanzee-specific insertions were significantly older than the human-specific and gorilla-specific ones, with no insertions younger than 2 million y, and most in- sertions much older (Kolmogorov–Smirnov, P < 0.0001). As expected, the gorilla-specific proviruses are significantly younger than the human-specific proviruses (Fig. 4D); when we looked only at the ages of LTRs belonging to the clade containing most of the youngest insertions, they had a mean age of ∼0.9 MY, ∼1MY younger than the clade containing the youngest human-specific insertions (Kolmogorov–Smirnov, P < 0.0001).

We have higher confidence in the two-sided hits, as they all have the expected 4- to 7-bp target-site duplication characteristic of HML-2 integrations, and most of them were found in more than one individual. It is also likely that many of the library hits with amplicon numbers below our cutoff for further analysis also represented real provi- ruses. We had a limited number of gorilla DNA samples to test, and were unable to obtain DNA from the animals sequenced by the GAGP; it is likely that PCR screening of additional individuals would validate many of the unconfirmed or low-frequency hits. Additionally, the samples used for the GAGP were skewed heavily toward Western lowland gorillas, accounting for 26 of the 30 samples, which limits the interpretation and analysis of in- tegration patterns across all gorillas. Phylogenetic analysis of the LTR sequences from the gorilla- specific insertions showed that almost all of them clustered in a single clade, separate from and basal to the clades of known human- and chimpanzee-specific sequences. The phylogeny sup- ports a single shared origin of the three groups of species-specific HML-2s in the common ancestor of gorillas, humans, and chim- panzees and subsequent codivergence of each group with its host. The branch lengths of the gorilla-specific clade are noticeably shorter than those in the human-specific clade, and molecular clock analysis based on LTR divergence confirms that the gorilla-specific clade is significantly younger than the human- specific clade. The estimated mean time since integration of the gorilla-specific proviruses is ∼3 MY, compared with ∼3.7 MY for human-specific integrations. The age difference between 2-LTR proviruses alone is even more striking, with a mean age of ∼380,000 y for gorillas and ∼950,000 y for humans. A noticeable

feature of the gorilla clade is the presence of a large number of proviruses with identical 5′ and 3′ LTRs, many of which are also identical across multiple proviruses. While a few proviruses with identical 5′ and 3′ LTRs are known in humans, they do not cluster closely together phylogenetically, in contrast to the low-divergence gorilla sequences, which form a well-supported single clade, sug- gesting a much more recent common evolutionary origin. These age estimates are limited in their resolution by the lack of sequence divergence in the gorilla sequences. For proviruses with identical LTRs, we can only give a maximum age limit of ∼300,000 y, and it is possible some of them integrated much more recently. To account for this uncertainty, we assigned all such proviruses an estimated age of 150,000 y. It should also be noted that the age estimates in this study use a mutation rate based on a chimpanzee–human divergence time of ∼6 MY. The true divergence time of chimpanzees and humans is still a matter of some debate (30, 41, 42), and if the split took place signifi- cantly earlier or later than 6 MY, these age estimates would have to be adjusted accordingly. Although the solo LTR age estimates are in rough agreement with the 2-LTR estimates, they are sig- nificantly older, which is likely in part due to sequence differences accumulated during replication of the virus before integration. However, it is known that the 2-LTR method can also produce ages that are biased young due to inter-LTR gene conversion leading to elimination of sequence differences; indeed, several nonhuman-specific proviruses were excluded from this analysis due to evidence of such gene conversion leading to anomalously young age estimates. With a few exceptions, the age estimates produced by both methods correspond fairly well to the expected age ranges based on species specificity, with species-specific in- sertions giving dates younger than the species divergence times, and shared insertions dating older. Notably, the ages of insertions found in both humans and chimpanzees, but not in gorillas, are

clustered around ∼6 million y ago, matching what we would expect if they integrated in the relatively short period between the split of gorillas from humans and the split of chimpanzees from humans. Recent HML-2 replication in gorillas is also supported by the large numbers of insertionally polymorphic proviruses. We used two separate approaches to estimate the population frequency of the gorilla-specific proviruses. Of the 31 2-LTR proviruses iden- tified, all but 2 were shown to be insertionally polymorphic by PCR, with allele frequencies in the 10 samples screened ranging from 5 to 90%. Nine proviruses were only found in a single gorilla DNA sample. A similar overall pattern was seen with the GAGP data. In this dataset, we were limited to determining the presence or absence of each insertion, rather than genotypes. As with the PCR screening, a large fraction of the hits were present in only one individual; 30 of the 184 two-sided hits were present in only 1/30 gorillas screened. While it is likely that this analysis under- estimates the true frequency of these insertions, it is possible that some of them may represent de novo integrations unique to a single individual. Future studies of parent–child trios could test this idea conclusively. One hundred and thirty of the insertions were only found in one of the gorilla species investigated, and thus may have integrated after the split between Western and Eastern gorillas ∼200,000 y ago (30). Infection by HML-2 proviruses also requires the presence of functional ORFs for gag, pro, pol, and env. Our investigation into the structure of these novel gorilla-specific proviruses reveals that 24 have retained at least one full-length ORF. One provirus retained full-length ORFs for all retroviral genes, and could po- tentially be replication-competent. The proviruses can also be categorized on the basis of common deleted regions. Type 1 pro- viruses share a common 292-bp deletion at the pol–env junction that inactivates the two genes. Those proviruses that retain this region are called type 2. The proviruses identified in this study are approximately evenly divided between these two types, as are human proviruses (14). Nine of the proviruses identified share another deleted region located around the pro–pol junction that also likely renders them incapable of infection or retrotransposition without a helper provirus to provide the missing function. Like the type 1 proviruses, these proviruses do not appear to have diverged in sequence from the wild-type genomes, and are not focused in a single subclade, but rather are distributed across proviruses comprising the most recent node and thus presum- ably are replicated by copackaging and recombination with replication-competent HML-2 viruses and with one another. It is curious that these defective genomes seem to be maintained in the viral population, despite the absence of obvious benefit to the virus. The oldest type 1 proviruses predate the human– orangutan split (14), and have persisted in both the human- and gorilla-specific HML-2 lineages until at least the last few hun- dred thousand years, suggesting that they have been maintained for more than 10 million y of viral evolution. Taken together, the high copy number, high levels of poly- morphism, low sequence divergence, and presence of well- preserved proviruses strongly suggest that HML-2 has been ac- tive much more recently in gorillas than in humans, and could potentially still be infecting gorillas today.

Validation and Sequencing of HML-2 Insertions. All integration sites of interest were validated with allele-specific PCR using 100 ng of whole-genome

amplified gorilla genomic DNA. Primers corresponding to the 5′ and 3′ flanking genomic DNA for a site were designed and used to detect either the empty site or solo LTR alleles. Capillary sequencing was performed on at least one positive sample. 2-LTR provirus alleles were amplified in overlapping fragments from a single sample and sequenced to ≥3×, and a consensus was then constructed with the read traces from each site. Identification of HML-2 LTR5Hs Insertions. Our list of nonreference integra- tions was supplemented with known HML-2 insertions. To identify human-, chimpanzee-, and gorilla-specific insertions from their respective genome assemblies, as well as insertions specific to the human–chimpanzee clade and insertions with orthologs in all three species, coordinates of all annotated HML-2 LTR5Hs and LTR5 insertions were downloaded from the Repeat- Masker track of the human (hg38 build), gorilla (gorGor5 build), chimpanzee (panTro5 build), and orangutan (ponAbe2 build) reference genomes on the UCSC Genome Browser. Sequences <900 bp were excluded. Solo LTRs and 2-LTR proviruses were separated by the presence of internal proviral se- quence annotated as HERVK-int by RepeatMasker. Species-specific integra- tions were identified using BEDTools to filter out sites present in at least one of the other three species; coordinates were converted from one genome to another as necessary using the UCSC Genome Browser’s liftOver genome conversion tool. The same approach was used to identify loci present in both humans and chimpanzees, but not gorillas. Lastly, the remaining sites from the hg38, gorGor5, and panTro5 genomes were filtered with BEDTools to remove any sites not present in all three genomes to generate a set of shared orthologous insertions.
