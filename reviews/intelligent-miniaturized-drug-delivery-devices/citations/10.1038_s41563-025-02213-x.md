For bioimplants, including bioelectronic implants, foreign-body response (FBR) is the major biocompatibility issue that limits the functional longevity of the implants and causes side effects for patients1–7. As a type of immune-mediated reaction to foreign/synthetic materials, FBR typically starts from nonspecific protein absorption, which triggers the cascade of

### Author Manuscript

immune cell (e.g., macrophages, fibroblasts) recruitment, foreign body giant cell (FBGC) formation, collagen deposition, and eventually encapsulates the functional implants (Fig. 1a).8 For bioelectronic devices in particular, this often leads to a substantial increase of device-tissue interface impedance for the transduction of electrical or chemical signals7,9–11. With FBR being a material-initiated process, immunology studies have found that both the chemical5,12 (wettability, functional groups, etc.) and physical13–15 (stiffness, size, tomography, etc.) properties of implanted materials influence FBR outcomes.

To date, substantial efforts and progress have been made on the designs of non-electronic biomaterials to achieve suppressed FBR. For example, for hydrogels for drug delivery and tissue engineering applications, the strategies of incorporating anti-biofouling16–18 and immunomodulatory19–21 moieties have been shown to be highly effective in suppressing FBR. However, for electronic materials, in particular semiconductors which, in a lot of

### Author Manuscript

bioelectronic devices, need to be exposed on surfaces for signal transduction22,23, very few efforts have been made in FBR-suppressing designs. Despite the possibility of mitigating this material-originated issue by applying immune-compatible surface coating24–26 or releasing anti-inflammatory drugs25,27,28, achieving intrinsic immune-compatible properties on implantable semiconductors is more desirable in avoiding increased surface impedance and limited efficacy period (Supplementary Table 1).

To interface with biological systems, semiconducting polymers have been shown as one of the most promising electronic materials for simultaneously providing amplified biosensing, tunable optoelectronic response, mechanical flexibility, and chemical versatility22,23,29–32. Recently, significant efforts have been made to achieve tissue-like mechanical properties (i.e., softness2 and stretchability33,34), which indeed could help to suppress FBR. However,

### Author Manuscript major gaps still exist in developing immune-compatible semiconducting polymers. First,

there have not been any rigorous characterizations of FBR behaviors for semiconducting polymers. So far, the reported biocompatibility test often just focuses on in vitro cytotoxicity or cell adhesion23,35–37. As such, the influences of different chemical designs and physical properties of semiconducting polymers on FBR behaviors remain elusive. Second, for the molecular designs of semiconducting polymers, which is generally the primary factor dictating FBR, there has not been any design strategy reported for suppressing FBR. Here, we present a suite of design strategies for incorporating immunomodulatory moieties into both the backbones and side chains of semiconducting polymers, which substantially suppress the elicited FBR as indicated by as high as a 68% decrease in collagen density. By further carrying out a suite of systematic and rigorous immunological assays that are of little precedence for semiconducting polymers, which include PCR-based collagen quantification, cytokine assays, combined with an in vitro mechanistic study of anti-inflammatory effect, we obtained a relatively complete and mechanistic picture of the suppression effect on the FBR. Through measurement in organic electrochemical transistor (OECT) devices, these designs are shown to cause little sacrifice to the electrical performance of semiconducting polymers, with charge-carrier mobility maintaining ~1 cm2 V−1 s−1.

### Author Manuscript Immune-compatible designs of semiconducting polymers

The overall hypothesis of our design strategies is that the incorporation of immunomodulatory groups into either the backbone or the side chains (or both) of semiconducting polymers can serve to effectively suppress FBR induced by such polymers under implantation. For the backbone design, aromatic structures are the most preferred for keeping conjugation and efficient charge transport. Our inspiration was drawn from immunology studies38–43 that report antioxidant (and immunomodulatory) effects of selenium-containing agents, possibly through the capability to scavenge reactive oxygen species (ROS). Based on this, we hypothesize that selenophene—a structure that has been utilized as a replacement for thiophene in semiconducting polymers to achieve higher performance44,45—may significantly suppress FBR (Fig. 1b).

### Author Manuscript

Different from the backbone units, side chains of semiconducting polymers are better suited to accommodate non-aromatic structures without affecting charge transport. Taking advantage of this, our second design strategy is to incorporate two previously discovered immunomodulatory groups19, namely, triazole-tetrahydropyran (THP) and triazole-thiomorpholine 1,1-dioxide (TMO), at the end of side chains (Fig. 1c). To the best of our knowledge, these two groups and their immunomodulatory effect have only been studied on non-electrical polymers and hydrogels.19

To test these strategies, we utilize a base polymer structure of poly(3,3’-bis(2-(2-(2- methoxyethoxy)ethoxy)ethoxy)-2,2’:5’,2”-terthiophene) (p(g2T-T)) (Fig. 1d), which has been reported with the state-of-the-art performance in OECT for biosensing46,47. Our

### Author Manuscriptbackbone design strategy is implemented by replacing one thiophene with a selenophene in

every repeating unit, giving rise to the polymer p(g2T-Se). The side-chain engineering with THP and TMO groups is achieved through our previously reported click-to-polymer (CLIP) method48 with further optimization to achieve a 100% attachment ratio to the repeating units (Fig. 1e, Supplementary Figs. 1–3, Supplementary Table 2). As shown by both MTT assay and Live/Dead cell staining (Supplementary Fig. 4), these polymer designs do not show significant cell toxicity, which ensures their potential as biomaterials and justifies the following tests of immune compatibility.

### Author Manuscript Suppressed FBR in vivo

To study the elicited FBR from these polymers in vivo, we implanted these semiconducting polymers (as thin films supported by thin circular SEBS substrates) subcutaneously into mouse dorsal regions, for periods of one and four weeks. We first evaluated the acute inflammatory response to the implants after one week of implantation. From Masson’s trichrome (MT) staining (Supplementary Figs. 5 & 6), the collagen layer elicited by p(g2T-Se) polymer has a lower density (as measured by the blue pixel density) than that from the control p(g2T-T) polymer, which indicates less inflammation. After four weeks of implantation with the fibrotic capsule fully developed, the MT staining results in Fig. 2a show a collagen density of ~13% at the p(g2T-Se) polymer-tissue interface, which marks a ~50% decrease from the collagen density of 25% from p(g2T-T). Furthermore, the side-chain functionalization on p(g2T-Se) with the two immunomodulatory groups, i.e., THP

### Author Manuscript

and TMO, helps to further lower collagen density by another 35% and 39%, respectively (Fig. 2b, Supplementary Figs. 7–11).

As the collagen layer is mostly composed of type I and type III collagens,49,50 such a trend in the collagen density is further validated by quantifying mRNA expression levels for collagen types Ⅰ and Ⅲ in the tissue regions around the implants (Supplementary Table

3). As shown in Figs. 2c & d, the selenophene backbone engineering alone decreases the levels of both collagen types by ~20–40%, and the further side-chain engineering by THP and TMO can further bring the decrease extents to ~50–70% (Supplementary Fig. 12). Overall, as collagen deposition is the final-stage outcome of FBR, such substantial decreases in collagen density reflect the effective suppression of the FBR by our immune-compatible designs.

To better understand the FBR suppression effect, we performed immunofluorescence imaging for macrophages (with CD68) and myofibroblasts (with α-smooth muscle actin, α-SMA) after 4-week implantation for the four polymers (i.e., p(g2T-T), p(g2T-Se), p(g2T- Se)-THP, and p(g2T-Se)-TMO). The results in Figs. 2e & f show that both the backbone and side-chain engineering strategies help suppress immune cell recruitments. In particular, p(g2T-Se)-TMO achieved decreases in the populations of macrophages and myofibroblasts by ~68% and ~79%, respectively, compared to the control p(g2T-T).

To study the inflammatory signaling mechanism of the suppressed FBR, we further analyzed the expression levels of mouse inflammation-related markers (Fig. 3a–i, Supplementary

Fig. 13) at the end of the four-week implantation. The results indicate that both the

selenophene backbone engineering and the THP and TMO side-chain functionalization

downregulate the pro-inflammatory biomarkers (including C-C chemokine receptor type 7 (CCR7), interferon-γ (IFN-γ), granulocyte-macrophage colony-stimulating factor (GM- CSF), monocyte chemoattractant protein-1 (MCP-1), interleukin (IL)-23, IL-6, IL-1β, IL-27, IL-12p70, and IL-1α). At the same time, upregulations are observed for the anti-inflammatory biomarkers (i.e., IL-10, IL-4). Together, these results suggest that the selenophene backbone and immunomodulatory side chains lead to the recruitment or differentiation of macrophages in anti-inflammatory phenotypes (Mϕ2) while suppressing pro-inflammatory phenotypes (Mϕ1), which, together, helps to mitigate the FBR (Fig. 3j).

### Mechanistic study of the FBR suppression

In particular, the FBR-suppression effect discovered on selenophene is highly interesting. Despite the previous reports38–43 of antioxidant properties from selenium-containing compounds, these results above should be, to the best of our knowledge, the first observation of the effect in suppressing FBR. As the cytokine analysis above suggests some changes Author Manuscriptin macrophage behaviors during FBR, we further conducted a group of in vitro tests by directly exposing selenophene (or thiophene) to macrophages (Fig. 4a). To activate the inflammatory behavior, lipopolysaccharide (LPS), an inflammatory activator of immune cells, was added into the cell culture media. In the first group tests, we incubated RAW

264.7 macrophages in media with selenophene (or thiophene) small molecules of different concentrations. During these cell culture processes over time, serious cell toxicity was not observed even at the maximum treatment dose in the two groups (Supplementary Fig.
20). After 12 hours, we tracked the inflammatory activities of macrophages by measuring the concentrations of several representative intracellular pro-inflammatory biomarkers in macrophages51, including phospho-p38 (p-p38), phospho-extracellular signal-regulated kinases (p-ERK), nitric oxide synthase (iNOS), and phospho-c-Jun N-terminal Kinase (p- JNK) (Supplementary Fig. 21). As shown in Figs. 4b–e, the presence of selenophene indeed
### Author Manuscript can suppress the expression of these inflammatory biomarkers in a dose-dependent way. In

contrast, no such effect can be observed from thiophene (Figs. 4f–i). Furthermore, using flow cytometry to quantify the M1 phenotype and ELISA to measure pro-inflammatory cytokines and chemokines (Supplementary Figs. 22 & 23), we show that the inhibited intracellular inflammation signals in macrophages lead to suppression of subsequent derivation to pro-inflammatory macrophages and inflammatory properties of the cells.

Similarly, the second group of in vitro tests comparing p(g2T-Se) with p(g2T-T) polymers also show such an effect of selenophene in the polymer backbone in suppressing the inflammatory behavior of macrophages (Supplementary Figs. 24–26). Overall, these results suggest that the selenophene’s properties in scavenging the ROS (as confirmed in Supplementary Figs. 27 & 28), which is a type of key signaling molecules in inflammatory

### Author Manuscript

responses52–54, could possibly suppress the pro-inflammatory macrophage activation, and thereby mitigating FBR (Fig. 3j).

### OECT characterizations and applications

Next, the electrical performance of these immune-compatible designs of semiconductors was tested in OECT devices (Fig. 5a). All the polymers exhibit ideal transfer characteristics (Fig. 5b). The figure-of-merit transistor parameters including transconductance (gm) and charge-carrier mobility were calculated. The results show that selenophene in the backbone

gives a higher transconductance and mobility compared to a full thiophene backbone (Fig.

### Author Manuscript

5c, Supplementary Figs. 29 & 30). This is consistent with the previous observations44,45 of selenophene boosting mobility for donor-acceptor-type semiconducting polymers designed for organic field-effect transistors. For the side chain functionalization with THP and TMO that are relatively bulky in size, although the steric hindrance effect indeed leads to some minor decrease in the performance, the charge-carrier mobility still both stays around 1.0 cm2 V−1 s−1 for selenophene-containing backbones, possibly benefiting from the buffer effect of the tetra-ethylene-glycol units in the side chains.

To deepen the understanding of such structure-property relationships, we performed grazing incidence X-ray diffraction (GIXD) and UV-Vis spectra measurements on these polymers. The results show that selenophene in the backbone, compared to thiophene, gives a higher relative degree of crystallinity (rDoC) and smaller lamellar packing distances (Figs. 5d &

### Author Manuscript

e, Supplementary Fig. 31). From UV-Vis spectrum, a redshift for the absorption onset is observed (Fig. 5f, Supplementary Fig. 32), which indicates the decrease of the bandgap coming from the improvement in structural ordering. After side chain functionalization, both p(g2T-Se)-THP and p(g2T-Se)-TMO display decreased rDoC and increased lamellar packing distances relative to p(g2T-Se), which possibly caused the slight decrease in electrical performance. However, UV-Vis spectra indicate that these two functionalized polymers still mostly preserve the short-range aggregation level (Fig. 5f). The AFM images (Supplementary Fig. 33) also show similar surface morphology among the thin films of these polymers.

For the in vivo chronic applications of our immune-compatible semiconducting polymers in implanted OECT devices, we measured the change in their electrical performance during the

### Author Manuscriptimplantation periods of 1, 4 and 12 weeks. The results showed that the immune-compatible

designs have decent performance stability during chronic implantation (Supplementary

Fig. 34). To demonstrate the potential merits of the immune-compatible semiconducting

polymers in chronic bioelectronic functions, we further used the polymers to fabricate soft OECT devices (Supplementary Fig. 35) and demonstrated the chronic use as “implantable loop recorders”55 for subcutaneous recording of electrocardiography (ECG) signals in live mice (Figs. 5g–i). After 4 weeks of implantation, OECTs made using the p(g2T-Se)-THP polymer, on average, maintained higher ECG amplitudes compared to the devices made with the control polymer p(g2T-T), reflecting the benefit of the suppressed FBR from the immune-compatible designs. We also made such comparisons on the recording of electromyogram (EMG) signals, which showed the similar trend (Supplementary Fig. 36).

### Author Manuscript Outlook

Conceptually through innovating at the fundamental level of combining semiconductor physics with immunology in the designs of conjugated polymers, this study shows that the engineering of molecular structures can greatly suppress foreign-body responses elicited by such polymers as implants. Therefore, this is likely to be an important research focus for polymer bioelectronics in the upcoming years. Specifically, our work presents two design strategies for improving immune compatibility (Supplementary Table 4) while minimizing impacts on (or even benefiting) electrical performance: (1) incorporating

immunomodulatory elements (e.g., selenium) as heteroatoms in the backbone aromatic

### Author Manuscript

units and (2) grafting immunomodulatory groups at the ends of side chains. The FBR- suppressing effect of these designs was ascribed to the inhibition of macrophage activation. The versatility of these design principles makes them readily applicable to other functional categories of conjugated polymers, such as those used in biochemical sensing and fluorescence imaging. Further combination with low-modulus designs could mitigate the mechanical mismatch with interfacing tissues. Beyond the immediate scope, with conjugated polymers being a highly distinct type of chemical structure, the systematic study of FBR using immunological approaches from multiple dimensions—gene, chemical, cellular, and tissue levels—could generate new immunological understandings of FBR.

#### CLIP-method-based functionalization of THP and TMO groups on the side chains.

The semiconducting polymer p(g2T-T)-a or p(g2T-Se)-a was dissolved in chloroform. The solution was spin-coated on a n-octadecyltrimethoxysilane (OTS) layer modified substrate at 1000 rpm/min for 1 min and then annealed at 110 °C for 1 min in a nitrogen-filled glovebox. The precursor solution was prepared by dissolving the alkyne-attached precursor molecules (4-propargylthiomorpholine 1,1-dioxide or tetrahydro-2-(2-propynyloxy)-2H-pyran) (~2 mM), copper (I) bromide (~0.2 mM), and N,N,N,N,N-pentamethyldiethylenetriamine (PMDETA, ~2 mM) in nitrogen gas-purged methanol in a vial in the glovebox. The solution was thoroughly mixed to become light blue color. The deposited conjugated polymer film on a substrate was submerged in the precursor solution overnight to allow the in-film side chain functionalization to happen. After that, the films were taken out from the vial outside

### Author Manuscript

the glovebox and washed with water and methanol for several cycles. The film was blown to dry using nitrogen gas.

#### OECT fabrication and characterization.

First, a glass substrate was cleaned with acetone, isopropyl alcohol, and water, successively. After that, the source/drain gold electrodes (60-nm thick) were patterned via e-beam evaporation with a metal shadow mask. The channel length (L) and width (W) are 200 μm and 4 mm, respectively. The semiconducting polymer films were transferred onto the channel area using a PDMS stamp. The electrolyte of 0.1 M NaCl in water was dropped on top of the channel. The gate electrode was served by Ag/AgCl. The performance of the fabricated OECTs was measured using Keithley 4200 in an ambient environment. The

#### In vivo implantation experiment.

The semiconducting polymer films (thickness of ~150 nm or ~400 nm) were transferred onto both sides of O2-plasma-treated SEBS substrates (thickness of ~ 0.8 mm). Implants were prepared by punching the substrates into circular disks with a diameter of 5 mm. The implants were sterilized using 70 % ethanol and UV sterilization for 20 min each. During the implantation, the back skin of the mouse was depilated with a razor and disinfected with isopropyl alcohol tissue and povidone-iodine tissue. The backside skin of the mouse was minimally incised (~2 cm), and the materials were implanted in the subcutaneous region. Author Manuscript Then, the incise was closed by a skin clip. For the measurement of electrophysiological signals, the OECT device was autoclaved and implanted subcutaneously in either the left side of the upper back or the left hindlimb muscle. For the ECG measurement, the OECT device was covered with a hydrogel adhesive (prepared following previous literature59) on its back. The condition of the mice was checked daily, and the skin clip was removed 7 days after surgery.

#### In vitro ROS evaluation.

RAW 264.7 cells (30,000 cells per well) were seeded to a 96-well plate and incubated in a cell incubator for 24 h. The cell media was carefully exchanged with the cell media (5 % DMSO) containing polymers (final concentration was 20 μg/L) or monomers (final concentration was 1 mmol/L) for 6 h. Then, LPS (final concentration was 25 ng/mL) was

### Author Manuscript

added to the media, and the cells were incubated for 3 h for stimulation of the inflammation signal. After the stimulation, the cells were washed using PBS twice and incubated with PBS containing 2’,7’-dichlorodihydrofluorescein diacetate (DCFH2-DA, finial concentration was 5 μM) for 30 min. Then, the cells were washed using PBS twice and resuspended in a flow buffer (PBS with 2 % FBS). The cells were acquired using BD LSR flow cytometer and data was analyzed using FlowJo (TreeStar).

#### Test of the long-term performance stability during in vivo implantation.

Source/drain electrodes (Ti/Au, 5 nm/60 nm) were deposited on a PET substrate (Tekra, thickness of 120 μm). The substrate was treated with oxygen plasma for 3 min. The polymer semiconductor solution with a concentration of 20 mg/mL in chloroform was then spin- coated onto the substrate, which gives a film thickness of ~500 nm. To prevent mechanical

### Author Manuscript

abrasion and damage caused by surrounding tissues, a thin PET film with 700 μm spacers was added on the polymer film as a barrier layer, which still allows the full access of the biological and biochemical species to the polymer film. The sample implantation procedure was descried above. After retrieving the implants at predetermined intervals, their OECT performances were measured using a Keithley 4200 in an ambient environment, with Ag/ AgCl serving as the gate electrode.

#### Fabrication of implantable OECT devices.

The PET substrate (Tekra, thickness of 120 μm) was washed with water, acetone, and isopropanol successively using an ultrasonication bath. On the PET substrate, source/drain electrodes (Ti/Au, 5 nm/60 nm) were evaporated by an e-beam evaporator and patterned using a photomask. To enhance the interfacial adhesion between layers, the substrate was Author Manuscripttreated with oxygen plasma for 90 s before spin-coating the other layers. Then the polymer semiconductors with a concentration of 10 mg/mL in chloroform were spin-coated in the tip area, which gives a film thickness of ~200 nm for the semiconductor. Then PDMS stamps were cut and laminated on the gate and channel area. The film area was rinsed with chloroform for 5 s to remove the uncovered semiconductors and annealed for 1 min at 100 °C. For conducting the side chain functionalization, the semiconductor film was incubated in the precursor solution overnight following the general CLIP-based functionalization method. After that, SEBS (H1052) with a concentration of 100 mg/mL in toluene was spin-coated on the gold electrodes and soft baked at 110 °C for 2 min. To pattern the encapsulation

SEBS layer, a PDMS stamp was used to cover the required SEBS area on the exposed gold

### Author Manuscript

interconnections. The uncovered SEBS was developed in dodecane for 30 s. The full OECT device was finally baked at 110 °C for 5 min. To make connections with external circuits, perfluoroalkoxy alkane-coated silver wires (A-M systems) were bonded to the contacting pads with silver paste.

#### In vivo EMG measurement.

A male C57BL/6 mouse with an implanted OECT device for 17 days was deeply anaesthetized with isoflurane (3–4 %). The fur was removed from the hindquarters using surgical clippers and hair removal cream. An incision across the midline was made in the skin, and the fascial plane was opened between the gluteus maximus and the anterior head of the biceps femoris, exposing the sciatic nerve. The nerve was extruded using sutures

### Author Manuscript

and was stimulated by bipolar silver wire electrodes (0.003 μm, A-M Systems). Biphasic electrical impulses (current level: 500 μA; durations of cathodic phase, anodic phase and inter-phase interval: 0.2 ms) were generated by a potentiostat (SP-200, BioLogic) controlled with EC-Lab software. To record EMG, the OECT was connected to an external power source (Keithley 2450) and the output was connected to a C-ISO-256 preamplifier (iWorx). The EMG signal was amplified using an IA-400D amplifier (iWorx) and interfaced with a computer using a Digidata 1550 digitizer with Clampex software (Molecular Devices).

#### In vivo ECG measurement.

Mouse preparation was described as above. An incision across the upper midline was made in the skin, and a PEDOT:PSS-coated gold electrode was inserted into the right side of the upper back as a reference electrode. To record ECG signals, the OECT and the reference

### Author Manuscript

electrode were connected to an external power source (Keithley 2450) with a constant 0.4V drain bias. The current readout was collected by Keithley 2450. The signal was filtered with 4–150 Hz bandpass and 55–75 Hz band block using a software process.

### Statistical analysis

Statistical analyses were performed using GraphPad Prism9 software. Detailed statistical analysis methods and P values are specified in the figure legends. Sample sizes were predetermined from pilot experiments and/or experiments that have been done in the past14,19, to obtain statistically significant data. We conducted a pilot experiment to determine the effect size and sample size of the experiment. Then, we determined the sample size at which statistical significance was observed (P < 0.05) in the result value of each group through a one-way ANOVA test. Data distribution was assumed to be normal, but

### Author Manuscript

this was not formally tested. The numbers of experimental replicates (n) are indicated in the figure legends, and each sample used for statistical analysis is an independent biological replicate. The experimental groups were randomly assigned from the samples. No data points were excluded from the analyses. The investigators were blinded to allocation during experiments and analysis. For OECT test and applications, the authors are not blind to the conditions of the experiments.
