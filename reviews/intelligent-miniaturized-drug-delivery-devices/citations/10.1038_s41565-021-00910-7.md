**Abstract**

27 Bioorthogonal catalysis mediated by transition metals has inspired a new subfield of artificial chemistry 28 complementary to enzymatic reactions, enabling selectively labelling of biomolecules or in situ 29 synthesis of bioactive agents via non-natural processes. However, the effective deployment of 30 bioorthogonal catalysis in vivo remains challenging, mired by the safety concerns of metal toxicity or 31 complicated procedures to administer catalysts. Here, we describe a bioorthogonal catalytic device 32 comprising a microneedle array patch integrated with Pd nanoparticles deposited on TiO2 nanosheets. 33 This device is robust, removable and can mediate the local conversion of caged substrates into their 34 active states in high-level living systems. In particular, we show that such patch can promote activation 35 of a prodrug at subcutaneous tumour sites, restoring its therapeutic anti-cancer properties. This in situ 36 applied device potentiates local treatment efficacy and eliminates off-target prodrug activation and 37 dose-dependent side effects in healthy organs or distant tissues. 38 39

Bioorthogonal catalysis has enabled a wealth of non-natural chemical transformations in complex living environs, such as cross-coupling and protective group-cleavage reactions for biomolecular labeling and 1,2 42 prodrug activation, broadening our understanding and interrogation of biological processes. The 43 implementation of this class of chemistry mainly relies on abiotic transition metals (TMs), including 44 Pd, Au, Ru, Ir and Cu, which are typically categorized into two formulations, metal complexes and 3-25 45 metal nanoparticles. Because of their unpredicted toxicity, lack of targetability and limited stability, 3-17 46 the application of metal complexes has been largely restricted to bacterial and cellular studies. With 47 the aim to ameliorate the potential toxicity of TMs, researchers have shifted their attention to supported 48 TM nanoparticles, mostly on resin beads, to modulate heterogenous bioorthogonal catalysis both inside 18-25 49 and outside cells. By further transplanting resin-supported TMs into zebrafish embryos, orthogonal 20-22 50 conversion of exogeneous substrates has been demonstrated, but the further application of such 23 51 formulated TMs requires elaborate and complicated surgeries. Although progress has been made in 17, 24, 25 52 improving the possibility of bioorthogonal catalysis in vivo by using injectable TMs, concerns 53 still exist over how to withdraw TMs after treatment, potential metal toxicity, immunogenicity and 54 unexpected catalysis caused by nonspecific deposition, not to mention the requirements for disparate 55 dosage optimization of TMs and substrate molecules. In this line of reasoning, a bioorthogonal catalytic 56 device that can work in a minimally invasive and spatially controlled manner would provide vast 57 versatility for manipulating abiotic chemistry in higher-level living entities.

58 Toward this goal, herein we explored utilization of microneedle array patches to develop a simple 59 and robust device for catalyzing bioorthogonal chemistry both in vitro and in vivo (Fig. 1a). The 26-28 60 advances of microneedle array patches for biomedical applications, particularly in the fields of 29-31 32-34 61 intratumour/tissue therapeutic administration, transcutaneous insulin delivery and transdermal 35-37 62 vaccination, have been recently reported by our and other groups. In this study, we fabricated a 63 catalytically active microneedle patch device made of polyvinyl alcohol (PVA) matrix with TiO2 64 nanosheets-supported palladium nanoparticles (Pd-TNSs) as the nanofillers. The incorporation of the 65 Pd-TNSs enhanced the mechanical performance of the microneedles in the dry glass state, conferring 66 them sufficient strength to pierce into skin in a minimally invasive manner. Once placed in aqueous 67 environment, the microneedles turned into swollen hydrogel state, forming porous structures. As such, the three levels of structural hierarchies, namely the three-dimensional needle array, the micropores

within in each needle matrix and the highly exposed Pd-TNSs surface in the network, facilitated the diffusion of caged molecules to contact with the Pd nanoparticles and thereby promoted their activation. 71 Remarkably, by using a mouse melanoma model, local intratumour activation of a systemically 72 administered prodrug, N-(allyloxycarbonyl)doxorubicin, was demonstrated, which not only enabled 73 increasing of dosing but also limited the side effects towards healthy organs and tissues. Of note, the 74 interfacial interactions between Pd-TNSs and PVA as well as the hydrogen bonds formed by PVA chains 75 conferred the microneedle tips sufficient mechanical toughness in the hydrate state and made it easy to 76 remove the whole patch, without leaving hazardous transition metals or inducing inflammations.

77 **Fabrication of Catalytically Active Microneedle Patches**

78 Ultrathin TiO2 nanosheets (TNSs) were firstly prepared38 and utilized as the support for depositing Pd 79 catalysts (Supplementary Figs. 1, 2). Fig. 1b shows a representative transmission electron microscopy 80 (TEM) image of the obtained TNSs that were wrinkled due to the surface tension, which is often 81 observed for two-dimensional materials like graphene39. Infrared spectroscopy and thermalgravimetric 82 analysis indicated that the TNSs were covered with ethylene glycol (~20 w.t.%, Supplementary Figs. 3, 83 4). Pd nanoparticles with average size ~8 nm were then photodeposited over the TNSs surface (Pd- 84 TNSs, Fig. 1c). The distribution of Pd, Ti and O in the nanocomposites was revealed by elemental 85 mapping analysis (Fig. 1d, Supplementary Fig. 5) and the Pd content was about 2.1 w.t.%. Notably, no 86 obvious change was observed in either the morphology of the nanosheets or the amount of surface- 87 attached ethylene glycol (Supplementary Figs. 3, 4) after the deposition of Pd. Such two-dimensional 88 structures with high exposed Pd surface would be favorable for mass transfer during catalysis. 89 Furthermore, the chemical state and short-range structure of Pd in the Pd-TNSs hybrid was studied by 90 X-ray absorption near-edge structure (XAENS) and extended X-ray absorption fine structure spectra 91 (EXAFS) at Pd K-edge, respectively. Compared with PdO, the XANES of Pd-TNSs showed closer edge 92 position to Pd foil, indicating dominant form of metallic Pd (Supplementary Fig. 6). This is further 93 confirmed by the existence of strong Pd-Pd bonding in Pd-TNSs, as-revealed by Frontier transformed- 94 EXAFS (Fig. 1e). However, different from the Pd foil, Pd-O bonding in the region of 1.5 to 2 Å was 95 also observed in Pd-TNSs, suggesting that the photo-reduced Pd nanoparticles anchored onto the TNSs support mainly via Pd-O bonds.

The performance of Pd-TNSs for mediating bioorthogonal catalysis was examined using bis-N,N’- allyloxycarbonyl-caged rhodamine 110 (alloc-RhB 110, Supplementary Figs. 7-10) as the substrate in 99 an isotonic media, PBS buffer (pH 7.4). Upon release of rhodamine 110 (RhB 110) by catalytic cleavage 100 of the caging group, green fluorescence gradually increased for solutions containing both Pd-TNSs and 101 substrate (Fig. 1f, g). The conversion was determined to be 48.8% by using the standard curve 102 (Supplementary Fig. 11). By contrast, no generation of fluorescence was detected for control groups 103 with either catalysts or substrate omitted over the same time. The catalytic nature of the reaction was 104 confirmed by using sub-stoichiometric amounts of Pd, although a longer time, 60 h, was required to 105 reach the similar conversion efficiency (Supplementary Fig. 12a). Also, to demonstrate that the 106 conversion was directed by heterogenous catalysis, the Pd-TNSs were dispersed in PBS buffer for 24 h 107 first and then the suspension was centrifugated. Afterwards, the upper supernatant was mixed with alloc- 108 RhB 110 and incubated for 24 h, which failed to give detectable fluorescent RhB 110 (Supplementary 109 Fig. 12b).

110 To make a facile device for deploying bioorthogonal catalysis, microneedle array patches made of 111 PVA incorporated with Pd-TNSs (~6.25 w.t.%) as nanofillers were prepared (designed as PT-MNs). 112 PVA was chosen as the patch matrix because of its unique character of forming microcrystalline regions 40-42 113 as physical crosslinkers, non-toxic nature and good compatibility with metal oxide particles. The 114 microneedles were arranged in 15×15 array (Fig. 2a), and each one was conical in shape with a tip 115 radius of 10 μm, a height of 1500 μm, a base diameter of 500 μm and a center-to-center space of 800 116 μm (Fig. 2b). The overall view of incorporation of Pd-TNSs inside the needle was revealed by time-of- 117 flight secondary ion mass spectrometry (Supplementary Fig. 13). Moreover, SEM image of the 118 longitudinal section of one needle showed that the Pd-TNSs were homogeneously dispersed inside the 119 PVA matrix (Fig. 2c). The fracture force of the native microneedles was measured and gave a value of 120 2.97 N/needle, which was about 2-fold higher than that of pure PVA microneedles (Supplementary Fig. 121 14). This increase could be attributed to the good dispersion of Pd-TNSs inside the matrix (Fig. 2c) and 122 the interfacial interactions between TNSs and the hydroxyl groups on PVA backbone (Supplementary 123 Fig. 15), which reinforced the strength of PVA composites. Such enhanced strength facilitates skin 124 insertion.

## Patch-mediated bioorthogonal catalysis

126 To study the catalytic efficacy of this device, the needles were immersed in a homemade chamber 127 (Supplementary Fig. 16) filled with alloc-RhB 110-contained PBS buffer solution (Fig. 2d-i). Like the 128 case of free Pd-TNSs studied above, RhB 110 were gradually released with the assistance of PT-MNs, 129 whereas no obvious fluorescence was detected for control groups utilizing blank PVA MNs (Fig. 2d- 130 **iii). Meanwhile, the swelling behavior of the needles were studied by an optical microscope, which** 131 revealed that the length and width of the needles increased quickly after immersed within water and 132 reached plateaus within 30 min (Supplementary Fig. 17). After further lyophilizing the swelled MNs, 133 three-dimensional network with pores ranging from several to tens of microns were formed both on the 134 surface and inside the needle matrix with Pd-TNSs entrapped within the pore walls (Fig. 2e, f, 135 Supplementary Fig. 18). Such hierarchical porous structure is beneficial for facilitating the transport of 136 substrate or product molecules to contact with or leave the catalytic sites and thereby promoting their 137 conversion. At the same time, the Pd content in the aqueous part was measured by inductively coupled 138 plasma mass spectrometry, where only about 0.24 w.t.% of Pd was leaked (Supplementary Fig. 19). The 139 stability could result from the interaction between the Pd and TNSs support and the interaction between 140 TNSs and PVA, which together held the Pd catalyst inside the PVA matrix. This conclusion could be 141 further supported by two additional experiments. One is to remove the PT-MNs in the midterm, where 142 inconspicuous increase in fluorescence intensity was detected (Fig. 2d-ii). The other one showed that 143 about 18.1 w.t.% of Pd was released over the same soaking time when nonsupported free Pd 144 nanoparticles (Supplementary Fig. 20) were used as nanofillers (Supplementary Fig. 19).

145 Next, we studied the potency of PT-MNs in mediating bioorthogonal catalysis in the extracellular 146 environment. B16F10 cells were incubated in culture medium containing 25 µM alloc-RhB 110, within 147 which the needles of PT-MNs were immersed in the extracellular space. As indicated by flow cytometry 148 (Fig. 3a), the fluorescence of the whole cell population increased in a time-dependent fashion. While 149 for control groups in the absence of PT-MNs or alloc-RhB 110, no fluorescence evolved (Fig. 3b, 3c). 150 Consistent with the flow cytometry results, confocal laser microscopy allowed to visualize the gradually 151 increased green fluorescence inside the cells treated with PT-MNs and alloc-RhB 110 combination (Fig. **3d, 3e), but no distinct fluorescence was seen for the control groups (Fig. 3f-i). These results**

demonstrated that PT-MNs could bioorthogonally catalyze the uncage of alloc-RhB 110 in the extracellular medium and the released fluorophores then diffused into the entire cell population. Similar 155 catalytic trend were observed for HepG2 (Supplementary Fig. 21) and 4T1 cells (Supplementary Fig. 156 22).

## 157 Patch-mediated prodrug activation in vitro

158 The above results encouraged us to access the prodrug activation by PT-MNs for cancer therapy. 159 Different from bioliable prodrugs activated by the metabolic or physiological aberrations within 160 cancerous tissues43, prodrugs activated by bioorthogonal catalysis would provide an alternative 161 therapeutic paradigm. Here, we synthesized N-(allyloxycarbonyl)doxorubicin (alloc-DOX) as a 162 bioorthogonal prodrug (Supplementary Figs. 23-26). Doxorubicin (DOX) is clinically used for cancer 44 163 treatment which acts by binding DNA and eliciting enzyme-mediated strand breakage. By caging the 4-1 164 primary amine with allylcarbamate group, alloc-DOX (Kalloc-DOX = 7.96×10 M) showed a significantly 5-1 165 lower binding affinity toward calf thymus DNA than DOX (KDOX = 9.36×10 M), as revealed by 45 166 fluorescence titration method (Fig. 4a, b and Supplementary Fig. 27). This weaker DNA binding 167 ability of the alloc-DOX was due to that the reduction in positive charge of the daunosamine moiety 168 made the DNA-drug intercalating complex less stable. Meanwhile, the decaging of alloc-DOX 169 catalyzed by Pd-TNSs in solution was proved by high performance liquid chromatography (HPLC) 170 analysis (Supplementary Fig. 28). Such masking strategy could afford a less active prodrug while allow 171 restoration by bioorthogonal catalysis.

172 Next, a cell viability assay using B16F10 cells was employed to study the toxicity profiles of alloc- 173 DOX, DOX and alloc-DOX/PT-MNs combination at different concentrations from 5 nM to 0.2 mM. 174 Compared to the parent DOX which showed elevated toxicity as its concentration increased, alloc-DOX 175 displayed much lower antiproliferative activity (Fig. 4c). Alloc-DOX (IC50 = 9.29 µM) was nearly 120- 176 times less toxic than DOX (IC50 = 0.08 µM). In contrast, the combination of PT-MNs and alloc-DOX 177 led to a distinct decrease in cell viability at each concentration with an IC50 of 0.12 µM, comparable to 178 the trend manifested by DOX. Similar results were observed when the same experiments were performed with HepG2 (Supplementary Fig. 29) and 4T1 cells (Supplementary Fig. 30).

HPLC analysis of the culture medium immersed with PT-MNs verified the extracellular generation of DOX (Supplementary Fig. 31). Furthermore, flow cytometry (Fig. 4d) and terminal deoxynucleotidyl 182 transferase dUTP nick end labelling (TUNEL, Fig. 4e) assays showed a high apoptotic cell population 183 treated with alloc-DOX/PT-MNs combination, like the group treated with DOX. However, no 184 significant apoptotic cell death was observed in the control groups. These results corroborated that the 185 cytotoxicity induced by the alloc-DOX/PT-MNs combination was due to the bioorthogonal generation 186 of DOX in cell culture. Moreover, the PT-MNs at zero prodrug concentration showed no toxicity 187 towards the cells after different time exposure (Supplementary Fig. 32) and negligible leaked Pd was 188 detected at the end (Supplementary Fig. 33), which could be attributed to the stability of the catalytic 189 device as discussed above.

190 **Patch-mediated prodrug activation in vivo for cancer therapy**

191 Anticancer study was undertaken using B16F10 murine melanoma as a subcutaneous tumour model. 192 By pressing the patch with a gentle force against the skin over the melanoma tumour site, the 193 microneedles could fully penetrate the skin and insert thereinto (Supplementary Fig. 34). The needle 194 matrix swelled into three-dimensional porous networks after insertion (Supplementary Fig. 35), 195 structures of such could help to alleviate mass transport barriers during the bioorthogonal catalysis. 196 Meanwhile, the in vivo toxicity of alloc-DOX was determined by measuring the weight changes of mice 197 and compared with those of mice treated with DOX (Supplementary Fig. 36). Intraperitoneal (i.p.) 198 injection of alloc-DOX up to 150 mg/kg every 3 days led to weight loss of ~7.7% meanwhile serious 199 weight loss and death were caused by DOX when its dose was over 10 mg/kg, suggesting the low 200 toxicity of the prodrug in vivo. Based on the toxicity tolerance data, to underline the less toxic feature 201 of prodrugs while avoiding unexpected side effects at too high doses43, equitoxic doses of alloc-DOX 202 (100 mg/kg) and DOX (5 mg/kg), with a 20 times difference, were applied in the following antitumour 203 studies.

204 Next, B16F10 tumour-bearing mice were randomly divided into five groups and treated with alloc- 205 DOX/PT-MNs combination, DOX, alloc-DOX, PT-MNs and PBS, respectively, once every 3 days. DOX, alloc-DOX and PBS were administered by i.p. injection. Before starting injection of prodrugs,

PT-MNs were inserted into the tumour site from the surface skin and fixed there for one hour to allow the needles to swell thoroughly. Bioluminescence imaging and tumour volumes were recorded to 209 visualize tumour growth and evaluate the therapy efficiency (Fig. 5a). As shown in Fig. 5a-c, like the 210 control group treated with PBS, no delayed tumour growth was observed for groups treated with alloc- 211 DOX or PT-MNs alone, indicating that neither the prodrug nor the catalytic device alone had antitumour 212 effect. Moderate inhibitory outcome was observed in the DOX-treated group, but the tumours grew 213 quickly after DOX administration was stopped. By significant contrast, the alloc-DOX/PT-MNs 214 combination suppressed tumour growth much better, leading to the smallest tumours in mice after 4 215 times treatment. In addition, no obvious body weight fluctuation was observed for the mice in all groups 216 (Fig. 5d), indicating the low side effects of the prodrug and the PT-MNs device. Importantly, TUNEL 217 assay verified apoptotic signals only in the groups treated with alloc-DOX/PT-MNs and DOX, implying 218 similar tumour cell killing mechanism (Fig. 5e).

219 To gain further insights into these treatment results, the concentrations of drug/prodrug in the 220 plasma and major organs versus time were analyzed. As shown in Fig. 6a-g, almost no DOX was 221 detected in all tissues in the group treated with alloc-DOX alone, indicating the stability of the prodrug 222 against nonspecific activation by the high-level biological metabolic processes. In comparison with the 223 group treated with DOX, there was more alloc-DOX than DOX in all tissues, which was due to that the 224 dose of prodrug was allowed to be given higher. As for the alloc-DOX/PT-MNs combination-treated 225 group, the profiles of alloc-DOX concentration in plasma and major organs were similar to those in the 226 group treated with alloc-DOX alone. However, the generation of DOX within the tumour tissues from 227 alloc-DOX in the presence of PT-MNs was validated, and, notably, the concentration of DOX was 228 significantly higher at 6 and 12 h post i.p. injection than that of DOX-treated group. Also, importantly, 229 compared to the group treated with DOX, much lower concentrations of DOX was detected in the 230 plasma and major organs after treated with alloc-DOX/PT-MNs combination, indicating that the 231 activated drugs were well retained in the tumours. This might be attributed to the three-dimensional 232 configuration of PT-MNs that facilitated the uniform diffusion of the generated DOX within tumours 233 and thereby promoted their uptake by cancer cells.

In parallel, we studied the antitumour activity of DOX-loaded microneedle patches (DOX-MNs,

Supplementary Fig. 37) and intratumour injection of free DOX (Supplementary Fig. 38). Compared to PBS treated group, DOX-MNs and free DOX delayed tumour growth to some extent, but neither was 237 superior to alloc-DOX/PT-MNs combination (Supplementary Fig. 38a). Pharmacokinetics profiles 238 illustrated that there was relatively higher concentration of DOX in the tumours treated with alloc- 239 DOX/PT-MNs combination but lower nontargeted DOX exposure in normal tissues. Although it seems 240 direct and simple to use DOX-MNs or intratumour injection for local administration, the relative higher 241 nontargeted exposure of DOX would account for unfavorable side effects, especially when long-term 242 treatment or multiple drug administration cycles are required. However, this issue could be avoided by 243 the bioorthogonal prodrug activation strategy. Collectively, the accumulation of alloc-DOX inside 244 tumours and its locoregional activation by the bioorthogonal catalytic device restored the drug’s 245 pharmacological property to combat tumours from within while allowing minimization of side toxicity 246 towards distant organs and tissues.

247 Lastly, but importantly, the PT-MNs could be withdrawn in the hydrated state after treatment 248 without notable damage (Supplementary Fig. 35) or obvious Pd loss (Supplementary Fig. 39), which 249 could be attributed that the hydrogen bonds formed by PVA chains and the interfacial interaction 250 between TNSs and PVA conferred the swollen PT-MNs with enough mechanical strength. Analysis of 251 the Pd content in the PT-MNs-inserted tumour tissue part, plasma, or other major organs showed no 252 differences when compared with the blank control groups, suggesting that the stability of the device did 253 not allow catalyst leakage in living systems (Fig. 6h). Although trace Pd leakage from PT-MNs was 254 detected in catalytic (Supplementary Fig. 19) and cellular studies (Supplementary Fig. 33), these small 255 doses were far less than the reported medium lethal dose46. Such complete removal of bioorthogonal 256 catalysts in the form microneedle devices could relieve the concern of transition metal toxicity. 257 Moreover, the temporal microholes gradually resealed over 2 h after removing the MNs (Supplementary 258 Fig. 35b-c), which would reduce the chance of infection there. Also, no release of cancer cells into the 259 systematic circulation system by the microneedle array patch penetration was detected (Supplementary 260 Fig. 40), avoiding unexpected tumour metastasis. Hematoxylin and eosin (H&E) staining of the major 261 organs revealed no noticeable damage, inflammation, or metastasis for any group (Supplementary Fig. 262 41). And, the levels of factors, such as TNF-α, IFN-γ and IL-6, did not differ from those in the blank control group injected with PBS, indicating no inflammation was induced (Supplementary Fig. 42).

**Conclusions**

To conclude, we invented a microneedle-based catalytic device and demonstrated its application for mediating bioorthogonal transformation of biologically innocuous substances into active agents in vitro and in vivo. The device was built upon a polyvinyl alcohol matrix integrated with palladium nanoparticle deposited TiO2 nanosheets as the nanofillers, where the favorable interactions at PVA and nanosheet interfaces and the PVA intra- or intermolecular hydrogen bonds render the microneedles high mechanical strength in dry state for skin penetration and good stability in hydrated state against unexpected transition metal leakage. As such, this bioorthogonal device showed high stability, good biocompatibility, easy removability and could effectively convert inert substrates into their parental states in aqueous solution, extracellular space and a selected area of tissue. We substantialized that the device enabled activation of a caged anticancer drug at tumour site and recovery of its pharmacological properties in a spatiotemporally controlled manner, by which off-targeted activation or dose-dependent side effects towards organs or tissues in distance were mitigated. Such bioorthogonal process allows the generation of therapeutic agents only when prodrugs and microneedle device are combined, avoiding non-demanded drug leakage that may happen in conventional slow drug releasing systems47. This paves the way for locally interrogating the biological processes of a defined area in high-level livings with bioorthogonal chemistry.

544 **Preparation of microneedle patches. Preparation of Pd-TNSs-incorporated microneedles patches** 545 *(PT-MNs). The microneedles used in this study were prepared using six uniform silicone molds supplied* 546 by Blueacre Technology, Ltd. The conical cavities in the molds were machined by laser ablation. The 547 needle cavities were arranged in a 15 × 15 array with an 800 μm center-to-center space. Each needle 548 cavity was conical in shape with a base diameter of 500 μm, a height of 1500 μm and a tip radius of 10 549 μm. To fabricate Pd-TNSs-incorporated microneedles, PVA solution (20 w.t. %) was first prepared by 550 heating a mixture of PVA powder and water at 95 °C. Then, hot PVA solution was gradually added to a 551 suspension of Pd-TNSs at 95 °C until the final concentration of PVA reached 15 w.t. % and Pd-TNSs 552 concentration reached 1 mg/ml. During the whole process, the mixture was magnetically stirred and sonicated for 10 min before adding a new batch of 20 w.t. % PVA. To enable better filling the mold

channels with the Pd-TNSs/PVA mixture, the channels were wetted by immersing in water and pumped in vacuum until almost no bubbles were generated. After pipetting about 1 ml Pd-TNSs/PVA suspension 556 onto the molds, the covered molds were centrifugated at 600 ×g for 15 min to further promote the 557 filtration of the mixture into the mold channels. After that, the loaded molds were dried in vacuum 558 without the freezing process for 48 h at 25 °C. Then, the microneedle array patches were detached from 559 the molds carefully and stored in a desiccator for further use. To characterize the distribution of the Pd- 560 TNSs inside the PVA matrix, the microneedles were embedded in Optimal Cutting Temperature 561 Compound (cat. no. 23-730-571, Fisher Healthcare) and cut into thin sections in a cryostat. The obtained 562 slides were carefully washed with water and dried before characterization under SEM or time-of-flight 563 secondary ion mass spectrometry. For infrared spectra characterization of the nanocomposites, the same 564 mixture of Pd-TNSs-incorporated PVA was used to prepare thin films.

PdNPs-dispersed PVA solution was 568 prepared by adding a suspension of PdNPs to PVA solution and shaking until a homogeneous solution 569 was obtained.
