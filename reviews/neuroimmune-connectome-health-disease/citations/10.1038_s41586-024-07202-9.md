#### Abstract

There is increasing interest in how immune cells in the meninges—the membranes that surround

#### Author Manuscriptthe brain and spinal cord—contribute to homeostasis and disease in the central nervous system1,2.

The outer layer of the meninges, the dura mater, has recently been described to contain both innate and adaptive immune cells, and functions as a site for B cell development3–6. Here we identify organized lymphoid structures that protect fenestrated vasculature in the dura mater. The most elaborate of these dural-associated lymphoid tissues (DALT) surrounded the rostral-rhinal

#### Author Manuscriptconfluence of the sinuses and included lymphatic vessels. We termed this structure, which

interfaces with the skull bone marrow and a comparable venous plexus at the skull base, the rostral-rhinal venolymphatic hub. Immune aggregates were present in DALT during homeostasis and expanded with age or after challenge with systemic or nasal antigens. DALT contain germinal centre B cells and support the generation of somatically mutated, antibody-producing cells in

#### Author Manuscriptresponse to a nasal pathogen challenge. Inhibition of lymphocyte entry into the rostral-rhinal

hub at the time of nasal viral challenge abrogated the generation of germinal centre B cells and class-switched plasma cells, as did perturbation of B–T cell interactions. These data demonstrate a lymphoid structure around vasculature in the dura mater that can sample antigens and rapidly support humoral immune responses after local pathogen challenge.

Humoral immune responses generate high-affinity antibodies that are important for defence against many pathogens. This process is initiated when B cells are activated through B cell receptor (BCR) engagement by cognate antigens within the follicles of secondary lymphoid organs (lymph node and spleen), or mucosal-associated lymphoid tissue. BCR- bound antigen is internalized, processed and presented in the context of surface major histocompatibility complex (MHC) class II molecules. If the B cell MHC–peptide complex is recognized by a cognate CD4 T cell through its T cell receptor, a germinal centre (GC) response may develop7. GCs are highly organized structures containing not only B cells, but also follicular dendritic cells—specialized stromal cells that act as an antigen store8 and a source of B cell chemoattractants (such as CXCL13) and survival factors such as B-cell-activating factor of the TNF receptor family (B cell activating factor (BAFF))9,10. GC-localized PD1+ T follicular helper cells also provide contact-dependent help for B cells through co-stimulatory molecules such as CD40L11,12, as well as cytokines (particularly interleukin-21 (IL-21)) that are critical for the progression of a GC reaction13–15. Ultimately, the interaction of these three cell types leads to iterative rounds of GC B cell proliferation, during which there is somatic hypermutation and class switching from IgM to other isotypes, processes that function to generate high-affinity antibodies optimized to respond to the current local immunological challenge16,17.

The dura mater is rich in B cells, comprising 25–30% of dural immune cells3, and also contains plasma cells, including gut-derived IgA plasma cells that are situated along the walls of the dural venous sinuses, inhibiting pathogen entry into the central nervous system (CNS)18. #### GC B cells evident in DALT in unchallenged mice

We next sought to more fully characterize the immune composition of the rostral-rhinal hub. Compared with the dural tissue covering the hemispheres, the DALT within the hub contained a rich, heterogenous network of immune cells, with B cells constituting a prominent component (Fig. 2a–c and Extended Data Figs. 1d and 3a). Spatially, B cell clusters were evident within the rostral-rhinal hub, even in unchallenged wild-type (WT) mice, and increased in number with age (Extended Data Fig. 1e–g), but were absent in Rorc- deficient mice that lack lymphoid-tissue inducer cells25 (Extended Data Fig. 1h). Further characterization of the B cell compartment of the homeostatic rostral-rhinal hub revealed the presence of GL7+ proliferating GC B cells within B cell clusters that increased with age (Fig. 2a,d). Furthermore, CD138+BLIMP1+ plasma cells were evident at the periphery of these large B cell clusters, particularly in older mice, some of which were IgA-expressing

(Fig. 2b,c). BAFF-expressing cells were also localized at the centre of B cell aggregates

#### Author Manuscript I.v. antigens accumulate within DALT, activating B cells

Given that the rostral-rhinal and basal olfactory hubs contained a rich network of fenestrated blood and lymphatic vessels, we hypothesized that these regions would monitor and accrue antigens from circulation and surrounding structures, enabling the generation and

#### Author Manuscript elaboration of adaptive immune responses locally within the meninges. To test this

hypothesis, we challenged mice intravenously with 0.2 μm or 2.0 μm fluorescent beads. The smaller 0.2 μm beads accumulated much more substantially within DALT relative to the larger 2.0 μm beads (Fig. 3a–f). The 0.2 μm beads extravasated along the sagittal/ transverse sinuses, and especially in the rostral-rhinal hub, where beads were found within

#### Author Manuscriptextravascular immune cells (Fig. 3e), including B cells, macrophages, dendritic cells and

neutrophils (Fig. 3g and Extended Data Fig. 10b), as well as in LYVE1+ lymphatic vessels (Extended Data Fig. 3c). Similarly, intravenous Staphylococcus aureus particles (Fig. 3h) and dextran (Extended Data Fig. 5a,b) also accumulated within the rostral-rhinal hub, particularly within F4/80+ macrophages. Within 19 h of intravenous lipopolysaccharide (LPS) challenge, there was expansion of CD45+ cells and increased numbers of B cells within the rostral-rhinal hub in the same areas where beads accumulated (Fig. 3i–l). Together, these data are consistent with the conclusion that circulating antigens can both access and activate immune cells within the DALT.

#### Nasal infection promotes GC expansion and somatic hypermutation in the rostral-rhinal hub

The rostral-rhinal and basal olfactory venous hubs are in proximity to the cribriform plate, which contains perforations through which nerve fibres pass from the nasal epithelium. These fibres convey sensory information that is required for our sense of smell26. We therefore examined whether these hubs might also orchestrate local immune responses to nasal pathogens, in addition to responding to circulating antigens. To test this, we challenged mice intranasally with vesicular stomatitis virus (VSV) and found a marked expansion of immune cell clusters within the rostral-rhinal hub at day 7 after infection, including CXCR5+ B cells and CD45− CXCL13-expressing cells (Fig. 4a–d), suggesting that these structures may support the activation and expansion VSV-specific B cell responses. Consistent with this theory, VI10YEN VSV-specific B cells27 (but not KL25 LCMV-specific B cells27) that were transferred before intranasal challenge with VSV formed clusters in the rostral-rhinal hub (Fig. 4f), and around 71% acquired a GC phenotype, co-expressing GL7 and FAS. By contrast, B cells were more disorganized in lymphotoxin-α-deficient mice that were challenged intranasally with VSV, with fewer tight clusters present (Extended Data

Fig. 5d). At day 7 after nasal challenge in WT mice, an increased number of class-switched

IgA- and IgG-positive B cells was also evident in the rostral-rhinal hub, and these cells

By 3 weeks after VSV challenge, many more class-switched IgG-containing BCR clones were evident in the rostral-rhinal hub (Fig. 4j). #### Author Manuscript Discussion

Here we describe a network of organized lymphoid structures intertwined with fenestrated vasculature in the dura mater during steady state that we refer to as DALT. The most substantial area of DALT was found around large venous plexuses in the rostral-rhinal and basal olfactory hub regions, which are interconnected by the falcine veins. These venolymphatic hubs are inhabited by a diverse assortment of innate and adaptive immune cells, lymphatic vessels and supportive stromal cells such as fibroblastic reticular cells. We found that the rostral-rhinal hub is contiguous with the skull bone marrow and receives vascular input from many different sources, including the rostral-rhinal sinuses, olfactory sinus, superior sagittal sinus, bridging veins, cortical veins and diploic veins. It also contains immune aggregates that expand with age and is capable of rapidly acquiring and responding to blood-derived antigens. Moreover, the proximity of these structures to the cribriform plate enables the rapid generation of antigen-specific immune responses to nasal pathogens. Indeed, nasal infection resulted in immune cell activation and the expansion of antigen- specific B cells in the rostral-rhinal hub, with features of a GC reaction, including the presence of IL21-producing CD4+ T cells, GL7+ GC B cells and expanded B cell clones expressing class-switched BCRs. We propose that the complexity and openness of venous structures in the dura mater like the rostral-rhinal and basal olfactory hubs necessitate an organized immunological defence system to protect the underlying CNS parenchyma.

There has been recent intense interest in the presence of developing B cells in the dura mater and its potential as a site for negative selection of B cells with specificity for CNS antigens4–6. Notably, these studies did not evaluate the rostral-rhinal hub region and did not specifically identify GC B cells, but RAG1, one of the markers that is used to delineate developing B cells4, is also expressed by some GC B cells31,32. Our scRNA-seq data included some developing B cells, and micro-CT images revealed that the rostral-rhinal hub resides partly in a skull bone recess that is intimately associated with the bone marrow. Future studies are required to determine whether this hub functions as a site of negative

selection for bone-marrow-derived B cells. The rostral-rhinal and basal olfactory hubs

#### Author Manuscript

might also support the development of previously described B-cell-rich ectopic lymphoid structures in the meninges that sometimes appear during states of CNS inflammation19–22.

In summary, we have identified DALT hubs that are present in homeostasis and are anatomically poised to acquire local and systemic antigens. These venolymphatic hubs

#### Author Manuscriptfacilitate the rapid expansion of antigen-specific immune responses and probably help to

defend the underlying CNS parenchyma against foreign invaders.

##### Antibody administration

After homogenization, anti-CD40L-containing hydrogel, as well as control hydrogel made with isotype antibody (BioxCell), was collected into a syringe. A subcutaneous injection of 300 μl (200 μg of antibody total) of hydrogel under the scalp was administered to B6 mice at the time of VSV infection. The mice then received another subscalp administration of antibody regimens 3 and 6 days after VSV infection and were euthanized at the indicated timepoints.

For the experiment shown in Extended Data Fig. 9f,g, B6 mice were injected intravenously with 7 × 106 naive VSV-specific (Cd45.1+/−Tfp+/− VI10YEN) B cells at day 7 after VSV infection, which was one day after initiating treatment with anti-LFA1/VLA4 or isotypes control antibodies on day 6.

##### scRNA-seq analysis

Single-cell gene expression data from Cell Ranger (v.7) output was analysed using standard scanpy (v.1.8.2) workflow38,39. Doublet detection was performed using scrublet (v.0.2.3)40 with adaptations outlined previously41. In brief, after scrublet was performed, the data were

#### Author Manuscript

iteratively subclustered, and a median scrublet score for each subcluster was computed. Median absolute deviation scores were computed from the cluster scrublet scores and a one-tailed t-test was performed with Benjamini–Hochberg correction42 applied and cells with significantly outlying cluster scrublet scores (Benjamini–Hochberg-corrected P < 0.1) were flagged as potential doublets. The data were then processed using scanpy with standard quality-control steps; cells were filtered if the number of genes was >6,000 or <200. The mitochondrial content cut-off was set at 30%. Genes were retained if they were expressed by at least three cells. Gene counts for each cell were normalized to contain a total count equal to 10,000 counts per cell. This led to a working dataset of 10,839 cells. Highly variable genes were selected on the basis of the following parameters: minimum and maximum mean expression are ≥0.0125 and ≤3 respectively; minimum dispersion of genes = 0.5. Batch correction was performed using harmonypy (v.0.0.5) after principal component analysis. Clustering was performed using the Leiden algorithm (v.0.8.2)43 with the resolution set at 1.0. The size of local neighbourhoods was set at 10. Uniform manifold approximation and projection (UMAP; v.0.5.1)44 was used for dimensional reduction and visualization, the minimum distance was set at 0.3 and all other parameters as per default settings in scanpy. Cell type identification was guided by manual inspection of canonical marker gene expression and top marker genes identified with Wilcoxon rank-sum tests.

#### Author ManuscriptBCR network

BCR networks were constructed as described previously18 for each sample and condition (n = 5 biological replicates). The network generation algorithm was as previously described50. In brief, each vertex represents a unique sequence. Edges are generated between vertices that differ by non-indel differences and clusters are collections of related, connected vertices. Here, each network was downsampled to the same sequencing depth where the sum of the total UMI count contributed by randomly selected BCRs would amount to a fixed value across tissues/conditions (n = 2,500 for base + hub and sinus; n = 5,000 for bone marrow; samples not reaching this UMI value were excluded from downstream analyses). Edges were rescued in cases in which components from the original networks were disconnected due to the random downsampling process. Using the downsampled BCR networks, isotype usage by clones (connected components on BCR network) were computed and represented as the percentage of total isotype usage per sample. One of the samples was excluded from week 1 due to very low sequencing depth.

##### BCR overlap

To tabulate BCR overlap, each sample was downsampled to a fixed sequencing depth across conditions (per tissue type) and BCR sequences were randomly selected. On the basis of the BCR sequences, we then determined the number of clones that were unique to each tissue type (base + hub, sinus, bone marrow, lymph node, spleen), without any overlap, using the supervenn (v.0.3.2) Python package. The mean of 100 iterations was used. One of the samples was excluded from week 1 due to very low sequencing depth. Nonparametric Kruskal–Wallis tests with Dunn’s multiple-comparison correction were used for all statistical analyses in which adjusted P < 0.05 was considered to be statistically significant. For pie charts, the mean of all biological replicates in each tissue type and associated overlapping tissues and condition was used.

##### Lineage tree

#### Author Manuscript

Lineage trees (for each clone with a dominant isotype; ≥75% of a single isotype class) were generated using the buildPhylipLineage function within the Alakazam49 (v.1.1.0) package after merging sequences across all organs.

##### Statistical analysis

Results are represented as the mean ± s.d. or mean ± s.e.m. Statistical significance was determined using GraphPad Prism 7.0 using Student’s t-tests, Mann–Whitney rank-sum tests or one-way ANOVA. P < 0.05 was considered to be statistically significant.
