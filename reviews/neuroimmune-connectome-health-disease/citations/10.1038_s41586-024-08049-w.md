### Author Manuscript Main text

Th1 cells, characterized by the secretion of interleukin-2 (IL-2) and interferon-γ (IFNγ), play critical roles in the defense against viruses and intracellular pathogens 4,5, the pathogenesis of organ-specific autoimmune diseases, and in anti-tumor immunity 6,7. In addition to Th1 cells, other T helper (Th) cells, including Th2, Th17, and Treg cells, are crucial players in induction and regulation of adaptive immune responses 8. However, imbalances among T helper subsets can contribute to the immunopathology of severe diseases such as allergies, autoimmunity, and infectious diseases 1–3. In COVID-19 severe respiratory syndrome, mild disease correlated with elevated interferon responses, whereas severe COVID-19 infection correlated with reduced or dysfunctional Th cell responses in the peripheral blood and lungs 9,10. Skewed differentiation towards Th2 over Th1 cells correlated with increased mortality in COVID-19 patients 11,12.

### Author Manuscript

However, the mechanisms defining the differentiation, breakpoints, and cell-intrinsic and extrinsic regulators that promote optimal T cell differentiation into a specific lineage, while inhibiting other lineages, have not been fully understood. We 13,14 and others 15–18 have recently shown the importance of neuroimmune cross talk on innate immune cells during homeostasis, infection and allergic inflammation, by which neurotransmitters, like substance P, neuromedin U (NMU) and CGRP, regulate innate immune responses, and reciprocally, inflammatory mediators, such as cytokines, may modulate neuronal functions. However, we know little about contributions from cells in infected tissues in directing Th cell fate. Furthermore, whether neurotransmitters can direct T cell differentiation or determine functional outcomes during infection has not been investigated.

### Author Manuscript Here, we used single cell RNA-seq (scRNA-seq) to predict unique Th1 regulators during

in vitro differentiation of Th1 cells using a novel Th1/Th2 dichotomous differentiation protocol, as well as during in vivo viral infection. We performed CRISPR screens to identify novel causal regulators for both in vitro and in vivo Th1 differentiation. Among these, we discovered that RAMP3, a component of the receptor for the neuropeptide CGRP, is a novel regulator that promotes Th1 and inhibits Th2 differentiation, and that accelerates T cell mediated acute viral clearance. Upon LCMV infection, both neuronal CGRP and T cell intrinsic expression of RAMP3 played key roles in mediating an effective anti-viral T cell

### RESULTS

#### Dynamic Th1/ and Th2 differentiation

We examined the expression kinetics of key regulators of T helper cells at multiple time points during in vitro T cell differentiation under Th0, Th1 or Th2 conditions. The canonical Th1 regulators Stat1 and Tbx21 and Th2 regulators Il4ra and Gata3 were exclusively upregulated under Th1 or Th2 culture conditions, respectively 19,20(Extended Data Fig. 1a). We next profiled the transcriptome at 14 time points spanning 72 hours in Th0,

### Author Manuscript

Th1 or Th2 differentiation conditions (Extended Data Fig. 1b). Three major and distinct dynamic expression phases were observed for each differentiation condition: early (0–5 hours), intermediate (8–23 hours) and late (32–72 hours) (Extended Data Fig. 1c and 1d), in line with the expression dynamics of in vitro differentiated Th17 and type I regulatory T (Tr1) cells 21,22.

Early phase expression profiles included the upregulation of cytokine receptor genes, such as Ifngr1 and Ifngr2 in Th1 cells and Il4ra and Il13ra1 in Th2 cells. Key transcription factors (TFs) were induced at the intermediate phase, such as Stat4, Tbx21 in Th1 and Stat6 in Th2, which are critical in chromatin remodeling for Th1 or Th2 programs 20,23,24. Transition to the late phase was coupled with the induction of effector cytokine genes, including Ifng (Th1), Il4, Il5 and Il13 (Th2) ((Fig. 1a, Extended Data Fig. 1e, and Supplementary Table 1).

#### Regulation of Th1/Th2 networks

The balances and interactions among regulatory networks of Th1 and other T helper subtypes are important for effective Th1 responses, such as the cross-regulation between Th1 and Th2 programs 27,28. Differentiation conditions using either IL-12 or IL-4 in vitro yield efficient Th1 or Th2 differentiation (Extended Data Fig. 1b), but limit our ability to detect interactions among these different programs.

We developed a novel Th1/Th2 dichotomous culture condition by titrating both IL-12 and IL-4 (Extended Data Fig. 2a), to identify conditions that generated comparable percentages

### Author Manuscript

of IFNγ+ and IL-13+ cells in the same culture. Multiple Th1 and Th2 signature genes were upregulated in their resulting respective distinct cell populations (Fig. 2a). This dichotomous T cell differentiation was found to be independent of TCR clonality (Extended Data Fig. 2b). The mutually exclusive expression of IFNγ and IL-13 highlights the physiological relevance of this Th1/Th2 dichotomous culture system in simultaneously yielding cells with separate identities within a single culture (Fig. 2a).

To investigate the regulators balancing T cell differentiation, we used scRNA-seq to profile 27,406 cells collected every 4 hours along a 72-hour time course during the Th1/Th2 dichotomous condition (Fig. 2b). Consistent with conventional in vitro culture conditions, we observed three major temporal phases (Fig. 2b), but the transition from the early to intermediate phase occurred approximately 20 hours later than in conventional in vitro Author Manuscript conditions, potentially due to the lower concentration of IL-12 and IL-4 in the dichotomous condition. Ifng and Il13 transcripts showed mutually exclusive expression among the single cells, especially at late time points (Fig. 2c). The 2,060 cells from the 64-hour time point formed three major clusters enriched for naïve, Th1 and Th2 cells with negligible effects of cell cycle or other known technical covariates (Extended Data Fig. 2c). Th1 and Th2 expression programs were also mutually exclusive among the single cells, implying an inhibitory interaction between their underlying regulatory networks (Fig. 2d and Extended Data Fig. 2d).

Acquisition of Th1 or Th2 cell identity was further explored based on the relative expression of the signature gene sets over time (Fig. 2e). Key intrinsic regulators of the Th1/Th2 balance were identified among the genes that were differentially expressed between defined Th1 and Th2 cells (Extended Data Fig. 2e). Furthermore, we reconstructed a dichotomous

### Author Manuscript

trajectory, along which the cells collected from the early phase with an undefined cell identity progressed through an intermediate phase with a mixture of both cell identities, and then reached a branching point and developed into well-differentiated Th1 and Th2 cells at the late phase (Fig. 2f, 2g and 2h). Unique branching genes were defined, including signature genes for Th1 and Th2 cells, such as Gata3, Batf, Tbx21 and Stat4, as well as potential regulators such as Sat1 and Ramp3, whose functions have not been investigated in T cell differentiation (Fig. 2i).

#### CGRP-RAMP3 axis and Th1 development

Among the defined novel regulators, Ramp3 is one of the top-ranking IFNγ positive regulators in our CRISPR screens in vitro and ex vivo (Fig. 3c and Extended Data Fig.

### Author Manuscript

3k). RAMP3 is a member of the Receptor-Activity Modifying Proteins (RAMP) family, and similar to RAMP1 and RAMP2, interacts with GPCRs to modify the functions of the target cell 31. We and others, have shown previously that Ramp1 exhibits the highest expression in ILC2s14,32,33 (GSE136154). In contrast, Ramp3 has significantly higher expression than Ramp1 and Ramp2 in T cells (Extended Data Fig. 4a, 4b and 4c), and its expression was rapidly induced upon T cell activation with a higher expression level in Th1 cells compared with other T helper cell types at the early phase (Extended Data Fig. 4d), implying an important function for Ramp3 in early activated Th1 cells.

To identify the potential GPCR partner for RAMP3 in T cells, we focused on GPCR genes

### Author Manuscript

that co-vary with RAMP3 over time. Early Th1 cells had high expression levels of 18 of 109 expressed protein coding GPCR genes (Extended Data Fig. 4e). We found a positive correlation between the expression pattern of Ramp3 with 15/18 GPCRs, including the gene Calcrl (encoding CRLR) (Extended Data Fig. 4f and 4g). CRLR interacts with RAMP3 to form a heterodimeric receptor for the neuropeptides CGRP (α-CGRP and β-CGRP) and adrenomedullin (ADM) and ADM-2 37,38,39. In the Th1/Th2 dichotomous trajectory, Ramp3 clustered together with Ifng with unique expression in the Th1-arm (Fig. 2i), consistent with its role as a positive regulator for IFNγ expression, and as a negative regulator for IL-13 expression in the genetic screens (Extended Data Fig. 4h). Therefore, the RAMP3/CRLR together might function in balancing T helper subtypes in response to the neuropeptides at an early stage during T cell differentiation.

CGRP significantly enhanced IFNγ production during Th1 cell differentiation, whereas IL-13 expression in Th2 cells was inhibited (Fig. 4a). CGRP also induced IFNγ expression in CD8+ T cells (Extended Data Fig. 5a). Similarly, ADM, another ligand for RAMP3- CRLR, showed consistent effects with CGRP in differentially regulating T cell subtypes (Extended Data Fig. 5b). CGRP and ADM also inhibited the expression of IL-5 in Th2 differentiation conditions (Extended Data Fig. 5c). Skewing towards the Th1 lineage under Th1/Th2 dichotomous culture conditions confirmed the role of CGRP in balancing Th1 and Th2 differentiation (Extended Data Fig. 5d and 5e). Surprisingly, Ramp3 and Calca were upregulated upon CGRP treatment (Extended Data Fig. 5f). Calca encodes both the calcitonin and α-CGRP isoform transcripts. Using isoform-specific primers, we detected calcitonin, but not CGRP, in T cells (Extended Data Fig. 5g). Furthermore, the intracellular calcitonin level in T cells was increased upon CGRP treatment as detected by ELISA

The induction of IFNγ expression upon CGRP treatment was blocked by BIBN4096BS in a dose dependent manner (Extended Data Fig. 5i). #### CGRP-RAMP3 activates cAMP signaling

To dissect the underlying downstream signaling of the CGRP-RAMP3 axis, we tested the genes differentially expressed between the CGRP treated and control cells for functional enrichment (Methods). Genes upregulated by CGRP treatment were enriched for members of the cAMP signaling pathway (Extended Data Fig. 6a and 6b), including key cAMP

regulators such as Crem and Atf3. Consistently, intracellular cAMP concentrations were

### Author Manuscript

induced by CGRP or ADM in T cells (Extended Data Fig. 6c). Although the RAMP3-CRLR receptor has a higher affinity for ADM than α-CGRP in pharmacological comparisons34, CGRP was more effective in elevating cAMP levels and influencing Th1 programs during T cell differentiation (Extended Data Fig. 6a, 6c). Moreover, cell-permeable dibutyryl-cAMP (dbcAMP) treatment selectively induced the expression of Calca and Ramp3 in T cells, mirroring the effects observed with CGRP and ADM treatment (Extended Data Fig. 6d and 6e). Furthermore, we found that naïve T cells were more responsive to CGRP and ADM than well-differentiated T cells, in terms of Calca, Ramp3 and Crem gene expression (Extended Data Fig. 6f). Thus, CGRP induces cAMP signaling, and functions as an early- stage regulator during T cell differentiation.

We further investigated the function of cAMP by treating T cells with dbcAMP during

### Author Manuscript

in vitro differentiation. In a dose dependent manner, dbcAMP induced IFNγ expression under Th1 and Tc0 conditions, and inhibited IL-13 expression under Th2 conditions, largely recapitulating the effects of CGRP and ADM (Fig. 4e and Extended Data Fig. 6g). Conversely, blocking cAMP signaling with specific pharmaceutical inhibitors of adenylyl cyclase (SQ22536) or cAMP Response Element-Binding Protein (CREB) (666–15) decreased IFNγ expression, and abolished the effects of CGRP or ADM on the induction of Th1 differentiation (Fig. 4f and Extended Data Fig. 6h, 6i and 6j). Collectively, these results indicate that the CGRP-RAMP3 axis enhanced Th1 differentiation at an early stage by activating and amplifying cAMP signaling.

#### CGRP-RAMP3 axis during LCMV infection

We assessed the impact of physiologic production of CGRP on T cell responses to viral

infection. Following acute viral infection with LCMV Armstrong, the concentration of CGRP in the spleen increased gradually (Extended Data Fig. 8a). To further confirm the intrinsic function of RAMP3 in T cells, we performed an adoptive transfer experiment with antigen-specific (SMARTA) naïve CD4+ T cells from WT or −/− Ramp3 mice. Equal numbers of congenically-marked naïve CD4+ T cells from WT or Ramp3−/− mice were transferred to congenic recipient mice followed by LCMV Armstrong infection (Fig. 6i). ### DISCUSSION

By undertaking temporal transcriptional analysis and pooled CRISPR screens, we identified novel regulators of Th1 differentiation. We identified RAMP3 as a T cell intrinsic regulator

that alters the transcriptional program and epigenetic landscape during Th1 development.

### Author Manuscript

T cells respond to the neuropeptide transmitter CGRP through the RAMP3/CRLR receptor complex to enhance the Th1 response during viral infection. Furthermore, CGRP expression is increased in neurons during LCMV acute viral infection, and pharmacological deletion of CGRP+ neurons reduced the LCMV-specific Th1 response, suggesting a circuit whereby infected tissue cells can participate in the differentiation of T cell responses to promote viral clearance. As a result, genetic deletion of either the neuropeptide CGRP or the CGRP receptor RAMP3 on T cells diminished the antigen-specific Th1 response during LCMV Armstrong infection. Our work, therefore, has uncovered a mechanism by which a neuropeptide directly modulates T cell differentiation in an adaptive immune response, emphasizing a role for neuro-immune crosstalk in antiviral immunity.

CGRP is a key neurotransmitter in the neuro-immune axis and is widely expressed in

### Author Manuscript

the central and peripheral nervous system 40,41 by activation of peripheral nerves. CGRP receptors are widely expressed in multiple immune and non-immune cell types. Recent studies have illustrated a role for CGRP in myeloid cells, neutrophils, dendritic cells, and ILCs in the regulation of an immune response, leading to either pro-inflammatory or anti-inflammatory effects in different cellular contexts39,42–44. Our studies emphasize context and tissue-specific regulation of T cell differentiation, whereby CGRP produced by the infected target tissue further tunes differentiation of T cells towards a specific fate 45. In this study, we found that CGRP directly functions in T cells through its receptor RAMP3- CRLR to regulate the transcriptional and epigenetic program to promote Th1 differentiation. However, we could not exclude the possibility that CGRP is produced by other cell types besides TRPV1+ neurons to promote Th1 differentiation. Although CGRP can act on multiple immune cells including ILC2 and DCs, our adoptive transfer experiments (Fig.

### Author Manuscript6k) support a T cell intrinsic effect on Th1 differentiation. Monoclonal antibodies targeting

CGRP have been approved by the FDA for the prevention and treatment of migraine 46, however more information is needed to understand the potential for side effects of CGRP blockade, which include reports of upper respiratory tract infections 47,48.

We and others previously demonstrated that CGRP inhibits IL-13 in ILC2 and negatively regulates ILC2-mediated inflammation 14,32,33. Here, we found a similar effect in Th2 cells, supporting a more generalizable inhibitory role of CGRP on type 2 immune responses while promoting Th1 and Tc1 responses. We observed a strong induction of IFNγ secretion by CGRP treatment during Th1 in vitro differentiation, even though the cells proliferated more slowly (data not shown), which supports previous studies showing that CGRP suppresses lymphocyte proliferation through cAMP generation 49,50. However, we Author Manuscriptobserved no reduction in proliferation in the Ramp3−/− T cells during in vitro or in vivo differentiation.

Taken together, our temporal gene regulation analysis and functional CRISPR screens provide a highly robust resource to dissect key regulators for T helper cell differentiation. We defined a neuronal-T cell circuit mediated by a CGRP-RAMP3-cAMP signaling axis, where infected tissue may itself participate in directing T cell differentiation for optimal effector responses to clear infections and promote inflammation. Thus, the functional

screens allowed us to identify novel interactions and potential new therapeutic targets for
