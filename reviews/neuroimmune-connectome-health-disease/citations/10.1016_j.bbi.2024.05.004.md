### Abstract

Survivors of myocardial infarction are at increased risk for vascular dementia. Neuroinflammation has been implicated in the pathogenesis of vascular dementia, yet little is known about the cellular and molecular mediators of neuroinflammation after myocardial infarction. Using a mouse model of myocardial infarction coupled with flow cytometric analyses and immunohistochemistry, we discovered increased monocyte abundance in the brain after myocardial infarction, which was associated with increases in brain-resident perivascular macrophages and microglia. Myeloid cell recruitment and activation was also observed in post-mortem brains of humans that died after myocardial infarction. Spatial and single cell transcriptomic profiling of brain-resident myeloid cells after experimental myocardial infarction revealed increased expression of monocyte chemoattractant proteins. In parallel, myocardial infarction increased crosstalk between brain- resident myeloid cells and oligodendrocytes, leading to neuroinflammation, white matter injury, and cognitive dysfunction. Inhibition of monocyte recruitment preserved white matter integrity and cognitive function, linking monocytes to neurodegeneration after myocardial infarction. Together, these preclinical and clinical results demonstrate that monocyte infiltration into the brain after myocardial infarction initiate neuropathological events that lead to vascular dementia.

### INTRODUCTION

Myocardial infarction (MI) is a leading cause of disability and death worldwide. As advances in treatment have improved survival after acute MI, this has increased susceptibility to secondary adverse events including heart failure, cancer, and vascular dementia (VaD) (Hasin et al., 2016; Jenča et al., 2021; Sundbøll et al., 2018). Risk factors, including advanced age, obesity, arteriosclerosis, diabetes, metabolic syndrome, and hypertension, are shared between MI and VaD (Román, 2005), and as such these patients likely have a high burden of pre-existing cardiovascular disease. However, recent epidemiological studies have found that age-related cognitive decline is accelerated in the years following an MI, which occurs independent of known vascular risk factors (Johansen et al., 2023; Xie et al., 2019). This suggests that MI specifically triggers pathological changes that lead to VaD.

While the mechanisms for increased VaD risk after MI are unclear, inflammation has been proposed as a link between MI and VaD. Preclinical models of MI have found elevated levels of inflammatory cytokines in the brain after MI (Gouweleeuw et al., 2021; Zhang and Luo, 2020), as well as morphological changes in microglia consistent with reactivity (Gouweleeuw et al., 2021). Similar to humans, neuronal injury and cognitive dysfunction are not evident in mice after acute MI and only develop over weeks to months after the initial injury (Evonuk et al., 2017). #### MI leads to cognitive and behavioral dysfunction.

Cognitive and behavioral dysfunction have been observed in mice after experimental MI (Hong et al., 2013; Meissner et al., 2015), so we first established whether functional impairment developed after experimental MI in our hands. To determine whether cognitive and behavioral dysfunction was evident after MI, mice underwent sham or MI surgery and were then subjected to a battery of cognitive tests to assess anxiety-like behavior and memory 28 days later. While MI did not affect ambulation or anxiety-like behavior in the open field test, we observed increased anxiety-like behavior from MI mice compared to sham controls in the zero maze (Fig. S1, Table S1). Contextual or auditory cued memory after conditioned fear and recognition memory was also impaired in MI mice compared to sham controls (Fig. S1), demonstrating that experimental MI led to reproducible functional deficits in cognition to support further exploration into CNS myeloid cell responses after MI.

To obtain greater sequencing depth as well as capture the transcriptome of parenchymal and stromal cells of the CNS, we next performed single cell RNA sequencing on brains from mice 7 days after MI or sham controls. Unbiased clustering of cells combined from both groups revealed a diverse array of CNS cell populations, including astrocytes, neurons, oligodendrocytes, and vascular endothelial cells (Fig. 4A and B, Table S2). To validate these automated annotations, we identified cells using canonical marker genes such as Aif1 for microglia, Cd206 for perivascular macrophages, as well as Ly6c2 and Ccr2 for monocytes (Fig. 4C, Fig. S3). ### Author ManuscriptCcr2-deficiency attenuates neuroinflammation after MI

Given the increased expression of Ccl2 in perivascular macrophages as well as increased CNS recruitment of CCR2+ monocytes after MI, we sought to determine the requirements for CCL2-CCR2 signaling in neuroinflammation after MI. Mice lacking Ccr2 exhibit impaired monocyte mobilization from bone marrow under inflammatory conditions (Boring et al., 1997), so we compared myeloid cell responses after experimental MI in Ccr2- deficient mice and controls. As expected, CCR2+ monocyte abundance within the CNS was increased after MI in control mice, but this recruitment was abrogated in Ccr2-deficient mice (Fig. 5A). While Ccr2-deficiency had no effect on neutrophils within the CNS (Fig. 5B), perivascular macrophage expansion was blocked in Ccr2-deficient mice compared to controls after MI (Fig. 5C). Following activation, microglia increase expression of antigen presentation proteins, such as major histocompatibility complex II (MHCII) (Jurga et al.,

2020). This was evident in control mice after MI as measured by increased abundance of MHCII-expressing microglia (Fig. 5D). However, Ccr2-deficiency also prevented microglia activation after MI. To determine whether these changes persisted into the progression to heart failure, we measured CD206+ perivascular macrophages 28 days after MI and found a sustained reduction of perivascular macrophage abundance in Ccr2-deficient mice compared to controls (Fig. 5E). Importantly, Ccr2-deficiency did not affect acute infarct size or systolic function after progression to heart failure (Fig. S6), consistent with previous reports that found Ccl2- or Ccr2-deficiency had little to no effect on acute infarct size and systolic function (Dewald et al., 2005; Kaikita et al., 2004). Taken together, our data reveal that CCR2+ monocytes promoted perivascular macrophage expansion and microglia activation after MI independent of their role in the infarcted heart.

#### Ccr2-deficiency protects oligodendrocytes and preserves cognitive function after MI

In addition to promoting oligodendrocyte apoptosis, Sema4D also inhibits the differentiation of oligodendrocyte precursor cells (OPC) into mature oligodendrocytes (Yamaguchi et al., 2012), so we determined the functional consequence of Sema4D signaling in the brain after MI by measuring oligodendrocyte and OPC abundance. Using antibodies for aspartoacylase (ASPA) and platelet-derived growth factor receptor α (PDGFRα) to identify oligodendrocytes and OPCs, respectively, we found that mature oligodendrocyte abundance was reduced in the corpus callosum, a major white matter tract in the mouse brain, 28 days after MI (Fig. 7A). This was associated with increased white matter injury after MI as measured by reduced fluoromyelin and myelin basic protein intensity in the corpus callosum (Fig. S7). We also observed reductions, albeit not significant, in OPC abundance (Fig. 7B) and proliferation (Fig. S8) after MI. To determine whether CCR2+ monocytes were required for this loss of oligodendrocytes and the associated cognitive dysfunction after MI, we performed the same set of immunofluorescence analyses as well as measured cognitive function in Ccr2-deficient mice. In contrast to wild-type controls, oligodendrocytes and OPCs were protected by Ccr2-deficiency after MI (Fig. 7A and B). While no differences in ambulation or anxiety-like behavior was observed between Ccr2-deficient mice and controls after MI (Fig. S9, Table S1), contextual and auditory cued memory were preserved in Ccr2- deficient mice compared to controls after MI (Fig. 7C and D). We also measured recognition memory using the novel object recognition test and found that recognition memory was compromised 28 days after MI in wild-type controls (Fig. 7E). In contrast, Ccr2-deficiency rescued recognition memory after MI, linking CCR2+ monocytes to white matter injury and cognitive dysfunction after MI.

### DISCUSSION

Large scale epidemiological studies have established links both between inflammation and VaD and separately, MI and VaD (Schmidt et al., 2002; Sundbøll et al., 2018). Our data newly reveal CCR2+ monocytes as an inflammatory protagonist that links these associations together. According to our working model (Fig. S10), MI leads to mobilization of CCR2+ monocytes from bone marrow reservoirs in the periphery and skull. In parallel, perivascular macrophages within the CNS are activated to secrete monocyte chemoattractant proteins, including CCL2. Increased production of CCL2 leads to CCR2-dependent monocyte recruitment to the CNS and neuroinflammation through perivascular macrophages and microglia activation. This includes production of Sema4D, which causes loss of mature oligodendrocytes, leading to white matter injury, cognitive dysfunction, and progression to VaD. As discussed below, additional studies are needed to explore the role of aging in these processes before continuing to investigate CCR2+ monocytes as a target for cognitive protection in humans with cardiovascular disease.

In humans with cerebral small vessel disease, an increase in circulating CD14+ monocytes, which are equivalent to murine CCR2+ monocytes, is associated with progressive accumulation of white matter injury (Noz et al., 2018). Separately, higher plasma levels of soluble CD14 protein is associated with increased dementia risk and cognitive decline (Pase et al., 2020), implying a link between monocytes and VaD. Using an experimental model of MI, we discovered a direct role for CCR2+ monocytes in white matter injury and cognitive dysfunction, which was independent of their role in cardiac inflammation. Increased immunoreactivity for CCR2 was also observed in post-mortem brains of individuals with a history of MI, supporting increased CNS monocyte recruitment after MI in humans.

Monocytes sourced from peripheral bone marrow reservoirs were capable of trafficking to

the brain after MI, while MI also led to transient expansion of monocytes within skull bone marrow. Peripheral-derived monocytes are transcriptionally distinct from those derived from skull bone marrow (Cugurra et al., 2021), and as such may have distinct function in the brain after MI. Transcriptional profiling of CNS myeloid cells after MI revealed myeloid cell differentiation in both Mertk microglia and Cd206 perivascular macrophages. While Mertk

microglia were enriched for genes involved in homeostatic functions, Cd206 perivascular macrophages expressed inflammatory function, supporting differential contribution of local- and peripheral-derived monocytes to CNS myeloid cells. Microglia are long-lived cells, generally thought to expand through self-proliferation independent of monocyte contributions (Füger et al., 2017). However, Ccr2-deficiency impaired microglia expansion

after MI as well as in Alzheimer’s Disease (El Khoury et al., 2007), necessitating additional studies on crosstalk between monocytes and microglia after MI.

After MI, CCR2+ monocytes were recruited to the brain through perivascular macrophage expression of monocyte chemoattractant proteins, including CCL2. While we did not

### Author Manuscript resolve the stimulus for perivascular macrophage expression of Ccl2, hypoxia has been

implicated in Ccl2 expression (Mojsilovic-Petrovic et al., 2007). The adaptive response to tissue hypoxia is mediated by hypoxia inducible transcription factors, which promote a switch to anerobic glycolytic metabolism, and evidence for cerebral hypoxia after MI was evident as increased gene enrichment for HIF-1α stabilization in CD31+ endothelial cells and glycolysis in CX3CR1+ myeloid cells. A previous study using photoacoustic imaging to measure changes in vascular oxygen saturation found that cerebral oxygen levels were reduced in the first week after experimental MI before returning to baseline levels (David et al., 2021). These kinetics are consistent with the transient increase in CCR2+ monocyte influx into the CNS during the first week after MI. Separately, toll-like receptor (TLR) signaling (Akhter et al., 2018) has also been implicated in Ccl2 expression. Inflammation and cell death after MI leads to release of damage associated molecular patterns (DAMPs), which can activate TLRs on macrophages and microglia (Prabhu and Frangogiannis, 2016). Cardiac DAMPs, including mitochondrial DNA, are released into the circulation of patients after MI (Bliksøen et al., 2012), providing a putative signal between acute cardiac injury and neuroinflammation. Transcriptional profiling of CNS myeloid cells after MI revealed cellular responses to lipopolysaccharide, a TLR4 agonist, in Klf2 microglia. CX3CR1+ myeloid cells also had increased enrichment of genes involved in RAF activation, which is required for TNF production after TLR4 stimulation (Reimann et al., 1994). Local knockdown of TLR4 in the brains of rats after MI blunts downstream NF-κB activation and TNF production (Wang et al., 2019), supporting a role for TLR signaling in perivascular macrophage Ccl2 expression after MI.

CCR2+ monocytes have well-established roles in chronic inflammatory conditions, such as atherosclerosis (Tabas and Lichtman, 2017). Our discovery that CCR2+ monocytes are required for neuroinflammation and white matter injury after MI raises the possibility that CCR2+ monocytes link other inflammatory conditions to increased risk for dementia and other neurodegenerative diseases. In addition to MI, cardiovascular risk factors for VaD, such as obesity and hypertension, are associated with increased risk for dementia (Shang et al., 2021; Singh-Manoux et al., 2018). Higher numbers of circulating CD14+ monocytes are observed in ulcerative colitis patients with anxiety and depression (Gao et al., 2023), suggesting that monocytes may also link inflammatory bowel disease to cognitive dysfunction. Serial imaging of the brain after experimental MI revealed a biphasic pattern of neuroinflammation with a transient increase in the first week followed by a subsequent increase weeks later (Thackeray et al., 2018). Consistent with CNS monocyte influx as a trigger for progressive neuropathology after MI, we observed a CCR2-dependent expansion of perivascular macrophages and microglia that was sustained into the progression to heart failure. These cells acted as long-term effectors of monocyte-induced neuroinflammation through Sema4d expression. Sema4D is cytotoxic to mature oligodendrocytes (Wada et al.,

Furthermore, a Sema4D blocking antibody is currently in a phase 2 clinical trial for treatment of Huntington’s disease (Feigin et al., 2022), supporting further exploration of therapeutic targeting of Sema4D for cognitive protection after MI. Additional pathways for monocytes to promote progressive white matter injury after MI are long-lasting changes in monocyte progenitor cells or memory T cells. Recent work found that epigenetic reprogramming of CCR2+ monocytes and their progenitors within bone marrow after experimental MI promoted tumorigenesis during a secondary challenge with breast cancer (Koelwyn et al., 2020). While we did not measure changes in the epigenome, we observed a sustained increase in GMPs, which give rise to mature monocytes, in skull bone marrow after MI. The infarcted myocardium releases granulocyte/macrophage colony- stimulating factor (GM-CSF) to promote differentiation of bone marrow GMPs into mature monocytes (Anzai et al., 2017). Cerebrospinal fluid levels of GM-CSF are increased in VaD patients compared to healthy controls (Tarkowski et al., 2001), suggesting that long-lasting

changes in monocyte progenitors may fuel chronic neuroinflammation after MI. Separately,

A limitation to our findings in the brain is that our study was performed in younger animals and it is likely that age-related changes may affect any cognitive benefit associated with targeting monocytes or neuroinflammation after MI. Aging is a complex, multifactorial process (Smith et al., 2020; Sun et al., 2020), so we focused on how MI disrupted a young

### Author Manuscriptand healthy neuroimmune environment, which is still clinically important. Mice aged 4 to 6

months are the human equivalent of 20-35 year old (Flurkey et al., 2007), and cardiovascular disease in adult humans is linked to poorer white matter integrity and increased risk for dementia later in life (George et al., 2023; Shang et al., 2021; Singh-Manoux et al., 2018; Zeki Al Hazzouri et al., 2021). In humans, CD14+ monocytes from elderly donors

### Author Manuscript produce more inflammatory cytokines compared to younger donors (Puchta et al., 2016).

Similarly, CCR2+ monocytes are increased in abundance and inflammatory function from aged compared to younger mice. Since targeting these age-related inflammatory changes still conferred benefit in aged mice (Thevaranjan et al., 2017), our work sets the foundation for exploring the interactions between aging and CCR2-dependent neuroinflammation after MI. Another limitation is our study only used female mice, which was based on multiple observations in humans that females are at a higher risk for adverse outcomes after MI compared to males (Chang et al., 2021; Elgendy et al., 2022; Ende et al., 2020). Sex-specific differences are also observed in circulating cytokines and monocytes during coronary artery disease with monocytes from females exhibiting a stronger monocyte migration phenotype and elevated Ccl2 expression compared to males (Lu et al., 2023). While it is unclear whether sex affects dementia risk after MI in humans, sex-specific differences in depressive-like behavior have been reported after experimental MI in rats (Najjar et al., 2018), supporting additional studies of sex-specific differences in monocyte-mediated neuroinflammation after MI.

In conclusion, our findings demonstrate a new paradigm where CCR2+ monocytes trigger persistent neuroinflammation to accelerate VaD after MI. This provides a cellular link between systemic inflammation and neuropathology and establishes a putative link for other diseases, such as inflammatory bowel disease, where disruptions in systemic homeostasis are also associated with increased risk for dementia. By establishing CCR2+ monocytes as protagonists in neuroinflammation after MI, it also supports future exploration on how cardiovascular disease risk factors, aging, and sex interact with distinct neurodegenerative processes to worsen neuropathology, cognitive dysfunction, and clinical outcomes.

#### Single cell RNA sequencing

For the single cell RNA sequencing experiment, 4-month-old, female C57BL/6J mice were subjected to sham or MI surgery (n = 5 mice/group). On day 7 after surgery, brains were harvested and digested into single-cell suspensions using a Neuronal Tissue Dissociation Kit (P) for 30 minutes at 300 rpm and 37°C on a ThermoMixer (Eppendorf). Myelin debris was removed by density gradient centrifugation and biological replicates were pooled together by condition for processing in the 10X Genomics Chromium System. A targeted number of 10,000 cells were loaded for each experimental condition. Single cell mRNA sequencing libraries were prepared using the 10X Genomics Chromium Next GEM Single Cell 3’ Library and Gel Bead Kit (v3.1) pipeline following manufacturer protocols. Libraries were sequenced at a depth of 25,000 reads per cell using a Novaseq 6000 System (Illumina). The raw FASTQ files were first aligned to the murine transcriptome (GRCm38;

mm10) using CellRanger (10X Genomics, v4.0.0). Aligned reads were then filtered for

### Author Manuscript

valid barcodes and UMIs to generate gene-cell matrices used for downstream analysis. To filter low-quality cells from the dataset, gene-cell matrices were read into R using Seurat (v4.3.0) R toolkit (Butler et al., 2018). Cells that expressed less than 300 features, less than 1000 unique molecular identifiers, and greater than 30% mitochondrial gene content were removed. Additionally, features that were not detected in at least ten single cells

### Author Manuscript

were removed. With these criteria in place, our dataset consisted of 17,222 cells with 32,285 unique features. Next, we normalized for cell cycle variation and used sctransform (v0.3.5) based normalization to recover sharper biological distinctions compared to log- normalization (Hafemeister and Satija, 2019). To mitigate potential batch effects that are common for scRNAseq data, each dataset for each sample was integrated using

### Author Manuscript

Seurat’s FindIntegrationAnchors and IntegrateData function. Next, principal component analysis (PCA) was completed on the expression matrix. Using dimensional heatmaps, and an elbow plot, the number of significant principal components was determined for unsupervised clustering and analyses. UMAP was applied to visualize the clustering in a 2D space. To begin to identify each cluster, SingleR (v1.8.1) was implemented to

### Author Manuscript

annotate cells based on similarity to the ZeiselBrainData dataset, a publicly available murine brain scRNAseq dataset within the scRNAseq package (v2.12.0). To further validate these automatic annotations, canonical markers for immune cells were plotted by UMAP feature plots and violin plots. The following genes were used for cell type annotation: Cx3cr1, Aif1, Ccr2, Cd206 (Microglia/Perivascular Macrophages); Ly6c2, Plac8 (Monocytes); H2- Ab1, Itgax, Siglech (DCs); Cd3e (T Cells); Ncr1 (NK Cells); Cd19 (B cells); Pecam1 (ECs); Col3a1, Postn (Fibroblasts); Olig2 (Oligodendrocytes); Gfap, Slc1a2 (Astrocytes); Foxj1 (Ependymal Cells); Casq2 (Pericytes). To further refine cellular heterogeneity in myeloid cells microglia & perivascular macrophages were sectioned out and re-integrated as described above to further control for confounding batch effects. A new PCA analysis was completed and UMAP was applied to visualize clustering in a 2D space. Clusters were annotated based on SingleR annotations and expression of myeloid markers. To determine differentially expressed (DE) features, FindMarkers function in Seurat was used to apply the approach MAST (Finak et al., 2015) for DE testing. Features that had an adjusted P value less than 0.05 and an average log 2 fold change of 0.25 were determined to be differentially expressed. To infer changes in physiological function and biological processes, only increased DE features in each cluster and condition were submitted to gProfiler (Reimand et al., 2016). To quantitatively infer and analyze intercellular communication networks, CellChat (v1.5.0) was implemented on the dataset containing myeloid cells with oligodendrocytes or with vascular cells (pericytes, vascular endothelial cells, and ependymal cells) (Jin et al., 2021).

#### Behavioral Assays

Minimal effects are observed when a single mouse is subjected to a series of tests with

### Author Manuscript

rest in between (Lad et al., 2010), so mice were subjected to a battery of tests to minimize the number of animals needed for study completion. The following behavioral tests are described below and were performed in this order: (1) Zero Maze, (2) Open Field Test,

(3) Novel Object Recognition Test, and (4) Fear Conditioning Test. For the Zero Maze, the mouse is placed into a round maze that is elevated off the floor and is divided into 4 sections of equal area with 2 closed and 2 open arms. Activity is collected for 10 minutes using LimeLight video tracking software (Actimetrics) and anxiety-like behavior is measured as percent time spent in the closed arms. For the Open Field Test, the mouse is placed in the center of a square arena and its activity is collected for 5 minutes. Ambulation is assessed by the total distance traveled, while anxiety-like behavior is measured as percent time spent along the perimeter. For the Novel Object Recognition Test, the mouse is placed in the same square arena used in the Open Field Test and habituated to the arena for 10 minutes on 2 consecutive days. On the third day, the mouse is placed in the square arena along with 2 identical objects placed equidistant apart for 20 minutes for familiarization. On the fourth day, novel object testing occurs by replacing the least explored object with a novel object and recording the object interactions for 20 minutes. Novel object recognition is measured as the ratio of time exploring the novel object to total object exploration time. For fear conditioning, the mouse is placed in a conditioning shock chamber and subjected to 3 sequential trials that consist of a tone (cue) followed by a brief shock. After 24 hours, the mouse is first returned to the same chamber and its movement is recorded for 5 minutes using FreezeFrame software (Actimetrics) for the context test. Two to three hours later, the mouse is placed in a novel environment and its movement is recorded for 3 minutes after playing the tone from the first day for the cue test. For both tests, fear conditioning is measured as the percent of time the mouse exhibits little to no movement.
