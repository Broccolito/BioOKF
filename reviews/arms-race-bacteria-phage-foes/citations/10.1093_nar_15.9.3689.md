Bacillus subtdis phage SPR codes for a DNA methyltransferase with triple sequence specificity

Ursula Gunthert* and Luzia Reiners

Max Planck Institut fiir molekulare Genetik, D-1000 Berlin 33, FRG

Received February 9, 1987; Revised and Accepted April 10, 1987

ABSTRACT SPR, a temperate Bacillus subtilis phage, codes for a DNA methyltrans- ferase that can methylate the sequences GGCC (or GGCC) and CCGG at the cytosines indicated. We show here that it can also methylate the sequence C(A/T)GG and protect it from cleavage with EcoRII and ApyI. This methylation can be seen in vivo as well as in vitro with purified SPR methyltransferase. SPR19 and SPR83 are two mutant phages, defective in GGCC or CCGG methylation, respectively. These mutants have not lost their ability to methylate CC(A/T)GG sites. Mutation SPR26 has lost the ability to methylate all three sites. Thus the SPR methyltransferase codes for three genetically distinguishable methyla- tion abilities.

INTRODUCTION

The DNA methyltransferases (Mtases) encoded by the temperate B.subtilis phages SPR, O3T, p11B' p11S and SPp exhibit the structural and enzymatic properties of type II modification enzymes (1,2). The B.subtilis phage Mtases differ from all other known type II Mtases in their potential to methylate not one, but two or three recognition sequences. Methylation of the GGCC sequence is common to the five Mtases (3,4,5). The second recognition site for the O3T, p11B and SPp Mtases is GCNGC (4), for the p11s Mtase it is GAGCTC (5), and for the SPR Mtase it is the sequence CCGG (6). In this study we demon- strate that the SPR Mtase methylates yet a third sequence, namely CC(A/T)GG at both cytosines. Thus the SPR Mtase, a single gene product with a Mr of

48 000 to 50 000 (1,7), is capable of specifically methylating three distinct * ** ** ** sequences: GGCC (or GGCC), CCGG and CC(A/T)GG.

2. Specificity of methylation in E.coli and B.subtilis plasmids carrying

the SPR Mtase gene.

It is difficult to judge from a digestion pattern of such a heterogenous,

nonsequenced DNA as the SPR phage DNA (Mr about 100 kbp (4)), to which extent

the described mutant specificities (3, 6, 7) are expressed. SPR wt and mutant

Mtase genes have been cloned and expressed in E.coli and B.subtilis plasmids

(13). DNA from these recombinant plasmids carries the SPR specific methyla-

tion and thus can be used to monitor the degree and specificity of the Mtases

produced in homologous and heterologous hosts.

Dependent on the strength of exogenous promotors of the vector, the Mtases

can be overproduced to various degrees (13) and subsequently the recombinant

DNA can be digested with the relevant restriction enzymes (Fig. 2).

Concerning the GGCC sites, a tenfold overproduction of the Mtases by the

P1 promoter of pBR322 (25) leads to complete protection against HaeIII diges-

tion in the wt and SPR83 constructions, but almost no protection in the plas-

mids carrying SPR19 (Fig. 2). The SPR26 Mtase, incapable of methylating GGCC

sequences in pBR322 when expressed by the genuine promoter (data not shown)

will, however, partially methylate these sequences when 30-fold overproduced

by the CAT promoter in pBR328 (26) (Fig. 2).

The wt and SPR19 constructions are completely resistant to MspI digestion

(Fig. 2). 10-fold overproduction of the SPR83 Mtase will,however,methylate

several CCGG sequences and render them insensitive to MspI (Fig. 2),whereas

the SPR83 phage DNA is sensitive to MspI to the same degree as the SPR26

phage DNA (7). Overall,almost all CCGG sequences are methylated at the outer

cytosine if the CAT promoter is increasing expression of the SPR26 Mtase

(Fig. 2).

Protection against HpaII cleavage is, however, neither in the wt nor in the SPR19 constructions complete (Fig. 2), although the SPR wt and SPR19

phage DNA is fully resistent to HpaII cleavage (6). The SPR83 and SPR26

Mtases, deficient in CCGG methylation in their genuine environment (7, 3), do

4. Sequence preference in methylation by the SPR Mtase.

The SPR Mtase, interacting with three distinct recognition sequences, may

have a preference for methylating one of these sites. To test this possi-

bility, we have constructed a DNA substrate, a B.subtilis plasmid pC1946

(3.3 kb), which contains each of the three sequences once. The supercoiled

pC1946 DNA was methylated in vitro with purified SPR Mtase for various times

and subsequently linearized with PvuII. Methylation of pC1946 DNA was moni-

tored by digesting aliquots with MspI, HpaII, HaeIII or EcoRII. Resistance

to MspI and HpaII cleavage appeared almost simultaneously (Fig. 5). Both

methyl groups could be transferred to the CCGG sequence within a single

binding event of the SPR Mtase. Moreover, methylation of the CCGG site was

preferred over methylation of the GGCC and CC(A/T)GG sites (Fig. 5).

This is in contrast to results obtained with recombinant E.coli and B.

subtilis plasmids, methylated in vivo, which were never completely resistant

to HpaII digestion (Fig. 2 and 3), even when the SPR Mtase was highly over-

produced (data not shown). The SPR phage DNA, likewise methylated in vivo,

was, however, fully resistant to HpaII and MspI digestion (6). GC-rich re-

gions adjacent to CCGG sequences have an inhibitory influence on cleavage

with MspI (29, 30). As the majority of CCGG sites in pBR322 and pBR328 are

not located in GC-rich regions, it is unlikely that the differences in diges-

tion with HpaII and MspI are due to sequence abnormalities. Moreover, it was

reported that HpaII is not only inhibited by methylation of the inner cytosine,

but also by methylation of the outer cytosine (31). Assuming that the SPR

Mtase methylates only the outer cytosine in CCGG sites, resistance against

MspI and HpaII should always appear to the same extent. This is indeed only observed in the in vitro methylation studies (Fig. 4 and 5), but not in the

analyses of in vivo methylated recombinant plasmid DNAs (Fig. 2 and 3).

CONCLUS IONS

The SPR Mtase has the ability to specifically methylate three distinct * * ** ** sequences, namely GGCC (or GGCC), CCGG and CC(A/T)GG. This triple sequence

specificity and the capacity to transfer two methyl groups per recognition

site (possibly to all three of them) are unique features among DNA Mtases.

Although CC dinucleotides are common to the three recognition sequences, the

experiments presented here exclude the possibility of a Mtase activity

merely recognizing CC dinucleotides.Mutations in the SPR Mtase gene, reducing the ability to transfer methyl groups to either one or all of the

sequences are a useful tool for dissecting the properties of this multi-

specific Mtase.
