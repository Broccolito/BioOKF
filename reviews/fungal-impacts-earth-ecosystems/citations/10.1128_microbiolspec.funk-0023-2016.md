ABSTRACT Fungi are among the dominant causal agents of plant diseases. To colonize plants and cause disease, pathogenic fungi use diverse strategies. Some fungi kill their hosts and feed on dead material (necrotrophs), while others colonize the living tissue (biotrophs). For successful invasion of plant organs, pathogenic development is tightly regulated and specialized infection structures are formed. To further colonize hosts and establish disease, fungal pathogens deploy a plethora of virulence factors. Depending on the infection strategy, virulence factors perform different functions. While basically all pathogens interfere with primary plant defense, necrotrophs secrete toxins to kill plant tissue. In contrast, biotrophs utilize effector molecules to suppress plant cell death and manipulate plant metabolism in favor of the pathogen. This article provides an overview of plant pathogenic fungal species and the strategies they use to cause disease.

INTRODUCTION Fungi have developed a plethora of strategies to colonize plants, and these interactions result in a broad spectrum of outcomes ranging from beneficial interactions to death of the host. With respect to plant pathogens, fungi represent probably the most diverse group of ecologi- cally and economically relevant threats. Fungal plant pathogen species are primarily in the phyla Ascomycota and Basidiomycota. Among ascomycetes, plant patho- gens are in various classes such as the Dothideomycetes (e.g., Cladosporium spp.), Sordariomycetes (e.g., Mag- naporthe spp.), or the Leotiomycetes (e.g., Botrytis spp.). Basidiomycetes are represented by the two largest plant pathogen groups: the rusts (Pucciniomycetes) and the smuts (spread among the subphylum of Ustilagino- mycotina). Fungal infections cause an enormous spectrum of disease symptoms. Traditionally, fungal plant pathogens

are divided into two main groups: biotrophic patho- gens, which form intimate interactions with plants and can persist in and utilize living tissues (biotrophs), and necrotrophic pathogens, which kill the tissue to extract nutrients (necrotrophs). In addition to these two groups, members of another group, hemibiotrophic pathogens, start as biotrophs and then switch to become necro- trophs. Necrotrophs cause necrosis and eventually even death of infected plants. Symptoms caused by biotrophs in many cases may appear mild. Nevertheless, some of the economically most devastating pathogens belong to this group. Fascinating interactions can be found in highly specified biotrophic interactions, where organ development of the host is massively modulated by the microbe, e.g., leading to formation of pseudo-flowers (rust) or plant tumors (smuts), to promote fungal prop- agation. In this article, we give an overview of the biology of plant pathogenic fungi with a focus on differentiation of pathogenic structures and the function of virulence factors. The impact of plant pathogenic fungi on agri- culture is discussed, and important model organisms are profiled.

### IMPACT OF FUNGAL PATHOGENS

### ON AGRICULTURE

Phytopathogenic fungi have been devastating threats throughout the history of agriculture. In ancient times, plant diseases were blamed on gods, which was a major driving force for the development of religious beliefs. In Greece, Demeter was deified as the god of grain and fertility from about 1300 BC. In the ancient Roman empire from the 5th century BC, Ceres (crescere = growing) appeared as Demeter’s female pendant. An- cient Roman religion held a spring festival (Robigalia) for the god Robigus, who was the personal mani- festation of agricultural disease. Namely, this disease (Robigo) is a form of wheat rust causing red color, which was mythologically linked to the god Mars, a god of both agriculture and bloodshed. To please Robigus and prevent crops from being destroyed by rust disease, the Romans sacrificed red dogs, foxes, and cows—with probably uncertain success. The first relevant scientific contribution to the knowledge of fungal plant disease can be attributed to Theophrastus (370–288 BC), a student of Aristotle. In his botanical studies, he described the appearance of rust diseases on different host species and in the context of landscape. In retrospect, it appears staggering that for almost 2,000 years there was hardly any scientific progress on the understanding of plant infections, which were continually interpreted as the wrath of God. Be- sides smut fungi infecting monocot crops such as barley (e.g., Ustilago hordei), wheat (e.g., Ustilago tritici), or maize (e.g., Ustilago maydis), rusts are traditionally se- rious threats in agriculture. In the 17th century, wheat stem rust (Puccinia graminis f. sp. tritici) was found to correlate with the presence of barberry (Berberis vul- garis) plants growing near wheat fields. Consequently, these plants were eliminated to control rust infection of wheat. This rather simple cure was successful, since the P. graminis sexual life cycle requires barberry as an intermediate host that is infected by monokaryotic basidiospores to form spermatia (1). A fungal plant pathogen with enormous agricultural and cultural in- fluence throughout human history is Claviceps pur- purea, the causal agent of ergot of rye, barley, oats, and wheat. Ergot sclerotia, which contain a broad spec- trum of toxic alkaloids including LSD, replace kernels in the heads of crops and thereby cause contamination of harvested grains and, finally, flour. Numerous epi- demics of the resulting human diseases, such as “devil’s curse” or “holy fire,” have been reported, with many thousands of victims and social and political con- sequences (1).

Fungal epidemics also have had significant social and economical impact. One historical example is the coffee rust, Hemileia vastatrix. Until the 1870s, Ceylon (Sri Lanka) was one of the world’s greatest coffee producers. This dramatically changed after H. vastatrix reached Ceylon in 1875. From 1870 to 1885, its coffee produc- tion dropped about 95%, and the fungus completely destroyed coffee plantations in this region, which today is of course mainly known for its tea. Today, coffee rust is still a significant threat to coffee production, with re- cent outbreaks in Central and South American growth regions (2). While in modern agriculture, most fungal pathogens can principally be controlled by modern crop manage- ment, massive epidemics with devastating yield losses still occur. Recent examples of this are epidemics caused by the Asian soybean rust (Phakopsora pachyrhizi) and wheat blast outbreaks in several Asian countries caused by Brazilian Magnaporthe oryzae isolates (3). In the following section, we give an overview of a (non- comprehensive) selection of plant pathogenic fungi that play important roles in agriculture and plant pathology research.

### Botrytis and Sclerotinia spp.

Botrytis cinerea and Sclerotinia sclerotiorum are the best examples of broad-host-range, necrotrophic plant pathogens. They were originally treated as types of Sclerotinia, and largely on the authority of De Bary,

B. cinerea had been considered the conidial form of Sclerotinia fuckeliana (4). According to current phy- logeny, B. cinerea belongs to the genus Botrytis, and its sexual form is Botryotinia whetzel, family Sclero- tiniaceae (5). The genus Botrytis consists of 22 species and includes pathogens of monocotyledons and dico- tyledonous plant species (6). Except for B. cinerea, all other species are considered specialists with a narrow host range, with the exception of Botrytis fabae, which can infect species of the genera Vicia, Lens, Pisum, and Phaseolus (6). Whereas most Botrytis spp. have a nar- row host range and the majority of them infect mono- cotyledonous plants, B. cinerea belongs to a distinct phylogenetic clade of broad-host-range species which in- fect predominantly dicotyledonous plants. Other mem- bers of this clade are Botrytis pelargonii, Botrytis fabae, Botrytis pseudocinerea, and Botrytis calthae (5, 7). Based on a multiloci analysis, B. cinerea morphological species were further divided into two genetic groups,

group I (referred to as Botrytis pseudocinerea) and group II (B. cinerea sensu stricto)(8). Strains in these two groups exhibit differences in their ecology and their resistance pattern to fungicides and are unable to cross with each other, suggesting that they represent phylo- genetic species. Recently, a novel group, called Botrytis group S, was described (9). This group, which is closely related to but distinct from B. cinerea and the more host- specific species B. fabae, is widespread in strawberry fields in Germany but mostly absent in other regions. Among the various species of Botrytis, B. cinerea strains B05.10 and T-4 have been used in most studies. Both strains belong to group II and can be considered model strains in studies to elucidate B. cinerea pathogenicity. The most familiar disease caused by B. cinerea is gray mold. This disease is widespread and results in serious economic damage worldwide. It is most destructive on mature or senescent tissues of dicotyledonous hosts. Usually the fungus gains entry to such tissues at an early stage of crop development and remains quiescent until the environment is conducive and the host physiology changes. Therefore, serious damage is caused in storage following harvest of apparently healthy crops. B. cinerea also causes massive losses in field-and greenhouse- grown horticultural crops prior to harvest, primarily in fruits, vegetables, and ornamental flowers. The most common route of B. cinerea infection is by conidia, which penetrate the cuticle with the aid of nonmelanized appressoria. When plants are inoculated with mycelium, a different form of penetration takes place, whereby hyphae growing on the plant surface heavily ramify into short bulbous cell aggregates that resemble the “infection cushions” or “complex appres- soria” of the closely related fungus S. sclerotiorum. At the end of the infection cycle, the fungus develops sclerotia within dying host tissues, which represent an important survival mechanism. Mycelium can also survive within infected dead host tissues and inside some seeds to serve as primary inoculum. B. cinerea also forms microconidia from phialides abundantly in aging cultures, which function primarily as spermatia. The sexual cycle involves the spermatization of sclerotia, leading to the production of apothecia and asci with eight binucleate ascospores.

### Cladosporium fulvum (Passalora fulva)

C. fulvum (P. fulva) is a nonobligate biotrophic fungal pathogen that is a causal agent of tomato leaf mold (10, 11). Until now the teleomorph stage of C. fulvum has never been observed in nature or in laboratory conditions. The infectious cycle starts with germination
of conidia on the lower side of the tomato leaf. Sub- sequently, the runner hypha enters the host leaf only through the open stomata without differentiation of an appressorium and exclusively colonizes in the extra- cellular space of tomato leaves. At 12 days postinfec- tion C. fulvum produces many conidiophores that block the stomata and result in some chlorosis or necrosis on the leaf (11). C. fulvum was a serious disease of tomato that caused big losses until the 1960s; however, since the introduction of different Cf resistance genes into com- mercial tomato cultivars, this mold disease has been kept under control very efficiently. Although C. fulvum is no longer a threat for tomato production, it is a valuable model for the study of plant-fungus interactions.

C. fulvum belongs to the Dothidiomycete class, which
contains more than 6,000 species that can infect all types of crop plants. Therefore, the information obtained from the C. fulvum-tomato pathosystem can be used to en- lighten our understanding of other plant-microbe in- teractions. For example, homology-based BLAST search revealed the presence of two functional homologs of chitin-binding proteins of C. fulvum, Avr4 and Ecp6, in various phytopathogenic fungal species, including Mycosphaerella fijiensis (banana pathogen), Dothi- stroma septosporum (pine tree pathogen), Zymoseptoria tritici (wheat pathogen), and M. oryzae (rice patho- gen) (12–16). Like Avr4 from C. fulvum, Avr4 from

M. fijiensis can also bind chitin and protect the fun- gus from plant chitinases (14). Furthermore, Avr4 from both M. fijiensis and D. septosporum is recognized by tomato Cf-4 resistance protein (13, 14), indicating that this R-mediated resistance can be used against these two devastating pathogens of banana and pine, respec- tively. Functional characterization of LysM-containing proteins from Z. tritici (Mg3LysM) and M. oryzae (Slp1) showed that, like Ecp6 protein from C. fulvum, these proteins also have the ability to scavenge chitin frag- ments and prevent chitin-triggered immunity (12, 15,
16). The wide distribution of LysM proteins in the fungal kingdom indicates that scavenging of chitin fragments is a general strategy that is used by many pathogenic fungi to prevent induction of pathogen-associated molecular pattern (PAMP)-triggered immunity (PTI) or microbe- associated molecular pattern-triggered immunity.
### M. oryzae

The hemibiotrophic ascomycete M. oryzae is the causal agent of rice blast disease. This disease is one of the most devastating diseases of cultivated rice throughout the world, and the annual yield loss due to rice blast disease can be up to 30% of total rice production (17–19).

M. oryzae can also infect wheat and other small grains; this seriously threatens global wheat production and food security (20). Because of its economic significance and easy genetic and molecular manipulation, M. oryzae has become a model system and is widely used for an- alyzing plant-microbe interactions (21, 22).
M. oryzae is a typical hemibiotrophic pathogen
that derives nutrition from living cells at the early in- fection stage, transits into the necrotrophic stage to kill host cells for nutrition, and then produces asexual spores at later stages. To colonize and infect the rice leaves successfully, the M. oryzae conidia develop melanized appressorium, a special infection structure by which they penetrate through the rice cell wall via enormous turgor pressure (23, 24). Once it has successfully pene- trated to the plant cell via the penetration peg, the pri- mary hyphae spread through plant cells and develop into bulbous invasive hyphae that are sealed inside the plant membrane structure (25). Associated with the invasive hyphae, M. oryzae also develops a biotrophic interfacial complex structure, which is significant for the accumu- lation and secretion of effectors to avoid or suppress plant defense responses during the biotrophic phase (26). Subsequently, the primary hyphae of M. oryzae invade adjacent cells via plasmodesmata and switch to the necrotrophic stage, forming typical necrotic lesions on the surface of the rice leaves (22, 23). A number of studies have demonstrated that the cAMP signaling pathway is involved in surface recognition and appres- sorium initiation at the early infection stage and that the Pmk1 MAP kinase (MAPK) pathway regulates ap- pressorium formation, appressorium penetration, and invasive growth after penetration, which are two other critical infection processes in the infection cycle of

M. oryzae (27). For successful infection, M. oryzae suppresses the host immune response manifested as an oxidative burst or plant cell death during the biotrophic stage. This suppression is achieved by effectors, which are patho- gen molecules that have an impact on the outcome of the interaction with the host; they are discussed exten- sively in the section “Virulence Tools of Plant Patho- genic Fungi.” Other effectors are associated with the necrotrophic stage. A secreted effector protein, Slp1, of M. oryzae specifically accumulates at the interface of fungus-rice cells at the early stages of infection and competes with CEBiP (a chitin-binding protein of rice) for chitin oligosaccharides binding through LysM do- mains, thus interfering with chitin-triggered immunity in the host (16). M. oryzae can also secrete various effectors to suppress BAX (Bcl-2-associated X protein)-
and/or Nep-1 (Necrosis- and ethylene-inducing peptide-

1)-mediated programmed cell death (PCD) (28, 29). The cytoplasmic effector AvrPiz-t of M. oryzae can inhibit the activity of rice RING E3 ubiquitin ligase APIP6, thus suppressing the oxidative burst and PTI in rice and contributing to virulence (30). The secretory protein MSP1 can induce autophagy cell death, H2O2 produc- tion, and defense responses in rice leaves and contributes to pathogenicity of M. oryzae on barley leaves (31).
### U. maydis

U. maydis belongs to the group of smut fungi, which comprises more than 1,500 species parasitizing a wide range of mono- and dicotyledonous plants. Although of rather moderate economic relevance, U. maydis has been developed as the prime model organism for smut fungi and also is one of the best-characterized biotrophic plant pathogens in general (32). Accessibility to reverse genetics, simple axenic growth, and a compact haploid genome with little redundancy make U. maydis an ex- cellent tool for molecular fungal genetics (33). Its biphasic life cycle consists of a nonpathogenic, saprophytic phase and an infectious stage, which is initiated upon mating of compatible cells on the plant surface. U. maydis infects primordia of all aerial parts of the maize plant and establishes a biotrophic interaction, where the fungus spreads both inter- and intracellularly. Massive prolif- eration of fungal hyphae coincides with the formation of plant tumors, which represent the most prominent symptoms of the corn smut disease caused by U. maydis (34). In recent years, intensive efforts have been invested in the identification and functional characterization of U. maydis virulence factors, mainly secreted effector proteins that act in the host tissue to support fungal vir- ulence and reprogram host tissue, promoting tumor for- mation (35). Details on the functional characterization of U. maydis pathogenic development and its virulence factors are presented later in this article.
### Z. tritici (Mycosphaerella graminicola)

The ascomycete Z. tritici (syn. M. graminicola; the teleomorph of Septoria tritici) is a hemibiotrophic (or necrotrophic) fungal pathogen that is a causal agent of S. tritici blotch (STB) of wheat (Triticum aestivum) (36–38). Like C. fulvum, Z. tritici is a dothidiomycete. Both asexual and sexual stages of Z. tritici have been reported in all STB-occurring areas (39). Z. tritici causes one of the most economically important foliar diseases of wheat and is responsible for up to 30 to 50% of annual yield losses (40). STB disease control is mainly based on fungicide treatment; however, the fungus has

developed resistance to multiple fungicides (41, 42) and it is now difficult to control this disease worldwide. Currently, only a few wheat cultivars that have resis- tance trait(s) to STB are used worldwide to control this disease. Despite its agricultural importance, very few genes are known to be involved in the virulence and pathogenicity of Z. tritici. Moreover, most of these known virulence factors belong to global regulators and cell signaling gene families such as MAPK pathways and cyclic nucleotide signaling (43–45).

### KEY STEPS OF FUNGAL PATHOGENESIS

### Fungal Host Attachment and Penetration

To find their hosts, fungal spores are dispersed by wind, water, or insect vectors. These spores are able to attach to various surfaces including host or nonhost plants. Such attachment prevents spores from being washed away from the host surface before penetration. Secretion of adhesive extracellular matrix from spores is essen- tial for firm adhesion to the host surface. Hamer et al. showed that the attachment of M. oryzae spores to various surfaces requires spore-tip mucilage, which is stored in a periplasmic compartment at the conidial apex and is released upon hydration of conidia that results in rupturing of the spore wall (46). In favorable conditions, the infection cycle of plant pathogenic fungi starts with spore germination and fil- amentous germ tube formation, which require complete cell reprogramming and specific regulatory networks. Upon spore germination, runner hyphae show polarized cell growth and grow along the host plant surface, which depends on recognition of distinct physical (surface hardness, hydrophobicity) and chemical (cutin mono- mers, leaf waxes) stimuli (22). How fungi perceive these signals is not clear. A G-protein-coupled receptor, Pth11, and cognate G-α- and G-βγ-subunit proteins are essential for appressorium development on hydrophobic surfaces (19, 47).

### Mechanisms of Host Penetration

Many phytopathogenic fungi, such as M. oryzae, Colletotrichum spp., and Alternaria spp., differentiate a dome-shaped appressorium cell to penetrate their hosts by directly breaching the cuticle layer (48, 49). The dome-shaped appressorium cell has a specialized cell wall enriched with chitin and an inner layer lined with melanin. The formation of a melanin layer is essential to M. oryzae appressoria, since it allows the generation of high osmotic pressure by the accumulation of glyc-

erol (19, 23, 50). The appressorium tightly attaches to the host surface and applies a turgor-driven mechanical force onto a thin penetration peg that ruptures the cuticle at the base of the appressorium and grows into the underlying plant epidermal cell. Appressorial turgor pressure can be up to 8.0 MPa (50). This physical pres- sure and some degree of enzymatic degradation of the host cuticle and cell wall enable the peg to penetrate host tissues. Recent studies, mainly on M. oryzae, have provided im- pressive insights into the regulation of signaling cascades and cellular reorganization during the formation of ap- pressoria and their function to redirect fungal growth and initiate host infection (51). Saunders et al. (248) have reported that initiation of appressorium develop- ment in M. oryzae is regulated by a DNA replication- dependent checkpoint (Never-in-Mitosis 1). After a fungal spore attaches to the host leaf surface, the germ tube enters into a single round of mitosis, which is re- quired for the cellular differentiation of appressoria (51). In addition, it has been recently shown that actin re- modeling is required for appressorium development. M. oryzae appressoria invade plants via a septin-dependent mechanism (24). Septins form a hetero-oligomeric ring at the appressorium pore to organize a toroidal F-actin network and link this network to the appressorium membrane. Formation of the septin ring also serves as a diffusion barrier to prevent the diffusion of proteins that are involved in membrane curvature and F-actin polymerization. In this way, septins provide the cortical rigidity and membrane curvature for the extension of the penetration peg during host cuticle breach (24). Moreover, septins mediate assembly of the exocyst complex at the appressorium pore, which is necessary for appressorium repolarization (52). Other pathogens differentiate a hyphal tip swelling of the infection hypha, which reflects the point of host penetration, but this structure is not separated by a cell wall, and its cell wall is not fortified by melanization. Although these structures are often termed appressoria, it is likely that the activity of lytic enzymes rather than a mechanical force facilitates epidermal penetration from this type of infection structure. Pathogens that form nonmelanized, nonseparated appressorial swellings in- clude B. cinerea and U. maydis (53–55). A third group of species, including C. fulvum and most rust fungi, penetrate their hosts through a natural opening (sto- mata) of the host plants (11, 56). Fungi that use stomata for penetration of the host have evolved directional growth patterns on the host leaf surface to locate and recognize stomatal guard cells.

Physical contact of runner hyphae with topographical signals of the host leaf surface controls both oriented growth and appressorium formation. This movement of the fungal germ tubes in response to topography of the leaf surface toward the host stomata is called thigmotropism (57). Hoch and colleagues showed that the differentiation signal for Uromyces appendiculatus is simply the height of the ridge present on host leaf surface. Upon contact with the stomatal ridge (opti- mum ridge height is 0.5 μm) of the leaf, the germ tube of the rust fungus U. appendiculatus undergoes thigmo- differentiation and forms an appressorium directly over the stomata, and the penetration peg enters the host (57). Subsequently, the pegs differentiate into invasive hyphae that rapidly colonize to epidermal and mesophyll tissues.

### LIFESTYLES OF PLANT PATHOGENIC FUNGI

Plant pathogenic fungi are collectively those species that derive nutrients from plants and have a negative effect on plants’ health. Similar to the situation in the entire kingdom of Fungi, they vary in nutritional requirements; certain types of pathogens completely depend on their host (these are called obligate parasites); others form a close association with the host but may also complete the life cycle of the plant. Such facultative pathogens can prosper in a variety of environments other than the organism(s) in which they cause disease. The traditional categorization of plant pathogens (necrotrophs, biotrophs, hemibiotrophs) differentiates fungi according to their pathogenic lifestyle and the way they feed on the host. Recent findings suggest that the division is less strict than previously assumed; however, this categorization defines basic denominators that are common to all organisms in each class and simplifies the discussion of pathogenic lifestyles.

Biotrophs Pathogens with a biotrophic lifestyle are either obligate or nonobligate. Obligate biotrophs are the causative agents of powdery mildew (Ascomycota) and rust (Ba- sidiomycota) diseases. Downy mildews and white rusts are also obligate parasites; however, these species are Oomycota (protozoa) and will not be discussed here. We will also restrict the discussion to parasites and therefore will not include nonpathogenic biotrophic fungi such as mycorrhizae (58). The obligate biotrophic pathogens, rusts and pow- dery mildews, have evolved to match the life cycle of their host plants and depend on their host for comple- tion of their life cycle. Rusts in particular have developed

different spore types and developmental patterns, in- cluding infection of an alternative host upon senescence of the primary host (59). Rust diseases are caused by fil- amentous, basidiomycete fungi in the order Pucciniales (60). Eurediniospores (dikaryotic conidia) germinate on a leaf of the primary host plant and develop invading hyphae that grow into the mesophyll space through sto- mata. The invading hypha differentiates into substoma- tal vesicles, which produce primary hyphae. Powdery mildew diseases are caused by haploid filamentous as- comycetes in the order Erysiphales. Conidia of these fungi germinate on the leaf surface and penetrate the epidermal cells with the aid of appressoria (61, 62). Following transition from the surface to an internal space, these fungi cross the wall of mesophyll (rusts) or epidermis (powdery mildew) cells and differentiate haustoria, which are unique infection structures that serve as mediators of nutrient acquisition. The hausto- ria form behind the plant cell wall, but they do not disrupt the cell membrane. Instead, they push and in- vaginate the cell membrane, establishing a sustainable “cell within cell” complex. This fungus-plant hybrid structure consists of the plant cell cytoplasm and tono- plast, a polysaccharide-rich extra-haustorial matrix, and the haustorial wall and membrane (63). Following establishment within the host cells, the haustoria de- ploy a range of transporters to drive nutrients from the infected host cells into the haustorium (64–66). At the same time they also secrete effectors that suppress the host defense and keep the invaded cells alive (67–70) (see the section “Virulence Tools of Plant Pathogenic Fungi”). It is assumed that obligate biotrophic species are limited in their ability to utilize common substrates as energy sources, and therefore they fully depend on their host as an energy source, which makes them ob- ligatory parasites (58, 71). Genomic analyses indicate a loss of genes in key metabolic pathways, such as genes in the nitrogen and sulfur assimilation pathways (60, 61, 72, 73). It was also found that the genomes of the powdery mildew Blumeria graminis (61), the rusts

P. graminis and Melampsora larici-populina (60), and the nonobligate biotroph U. maydis (33) all have re- duced numbers of genes encoding hydrolytic carbohy- drate active enzymes (74). All these findings support the loss of some biosynthetic activities in obligate bio- trophs. It is important to note, however, that there is no clear evidence that the loss of biosynthetic and metabolic capacity is the limiting factor in the ability of obligate biotrophic fungal plant pathogens to grow out of their host (75, 76). Furthermore, although obligate biotrophic

species rely on their host plants for completion of their life cycle, all of them can be grown, although for a limited time, in axenic cultures (77, 78), suggesting that they have at least some capacity to extract nutrients and energy from a nonliving environment. An alternative hypothesis is that in obligate biotrophs the regulation of metabolic gene expression is dependent on signals and signal gradients that are only found in host plants. According to this hypothesis, obligatory biotrophs uti- lize cues from the host to control their metabolic ca- pacity. Evidence that supports this hypothesis is the coordinated regulation of expression of metabolic genes in barley powdery mildew, during both development and pathogenic attack (75), and the expression of im- portant metabolic and uptake functions in rust haustoria (66, 79, 80). Nonobligate biotrophic plant pathogens are taxo- nomically more variable than obligate biotrophs and can be found in a wider range of genera. Among them are the smuts (Basidiomycota, Ustilaginales) and certain species of Claviceps (Ascomycota, Claviceptacea). Un- like the obligate species, nonobligate species can survive and live without the host. For example, the ergot disease fungus C. purpurea behaves as a true biotroph in planta, but it can be easily grown in axenic culture (81). Infec- tion is initiated by wind-borne ascospores that germinate on the pistil surfaces of grass florets at anthesis. Fol- lowing penetration through stigmatic hairs, the fungus reaches and colonizes the ovarian tissue, grows down toward the base of the ovary, and establishes a spe- cific and persisting host-pathogen interface. A mycelial stroma develops in the ovary and produces masses of conidiospores that are exuded into a sugar-rich fluid derived from phloem sap. About 2 weeks postinfection, sclerotia are formed, which serve as overwintering struc- tures. C. purpurea shows no necrotrophic growth pat- tern in any phase of infection, and it does not kill host cells in advance of colonization. Although it does not produce classical haustoria, intracellular hyphae, which are completely covered by the host plasma membrane, could have haustorial functions (82). Among smut fungi, the maize pathogen U. maydis stands out because of its amenability to molecular genetic manipulation and its small genome size. U. maydis enters primordia of all aerial organs of the maize plant, where it locally induces formation of tumors, in which fungal spores develop (32).

Necrotrophs As opposed to biotrophic pathogens, which feed on living plant tissues, necrotrophic plant pathogens feed

on dead tissue. It is important to make a distinction between true necrotrophic pathogens, which attack and kill healthy plants, and secondary necrotrophic-like pathogens, which are saprophytic in nature but may occasionally infect plants that have been previously weakened, e.g., by other pathogens, injury, or abiotic effects. Here we only discuss true necrotrophs. The basic concept of necrotrophy is defined as the mode of infection in which the pathogen kills the tissue before colonization (83). This definition is somewhat oxymoronic since at least the initial contact of the pathogen is with a living tissue. Indeed, the early stages following the first encounter with the host are most critical; to survive, the pathogen must subvert the plant defense and generate a zone of dead (necrotic) tissue in which it will be protected from the host and on which it can feed. The following stages of necrotrophic diseases are characterized by spreading of necrosis around the initial infection zone, which precedes pathogen pro- gression. This two-phase strategy entails a “survive or die” scenario at the initial stage, in which the pathogens are in direct confrontation with the host defense, and a more quantitative scenario during disease spread. As described, biotrophic interactions also have initial and late stages; however, in this case the early stage does not involve direct conflict with the host defense, but rather a silent “sneaking in” strategy, while all later stages necessitate close contact with the living host and hence a continuous conflict with the host defense. Accordingly, biotrophic pathogens also have a “survive or die” stage at the first encounter with the plant defense, but only after penetration into the tissue, prior or during estab- lishment of the biotrophic interface. Because biotrophic pathogens thrive to keep the tissue alive, the plant de- fense strategy at this critical stage is a suicidal one in the form of a hypersensitive response (HR), while the pathogen strategy is prevention of this response. The realization that necrotrophic pathogens must cope with the plant defense during the first encounter and overcome it in order to initiate infection highlights the true nature of this type of pathogen. It is now clear that necrotrophic pathogens use an array of specific molecules (effectors) and mechanisms to cope with and manipulate the host defense during infection, in a man- ner that is not less sophisticated than biotrophic patho- gens. This is in contrast to an earlier paradigm that assumed a more simplistic strategy of massive killing of tissue using a battery of hydrolytic enzymes and toxins (8). Compared to true biotrophic pathogens, true necrotrophic pathogens include a much wider range of species that belong to diverse genera. Although the

two-phase paradigm holds true for all of the necro- trophic pathogens, not all necrotrophs are made equal. It is convenient to divide all necrotrophic pathogens to narrow-host-range and broad-host-range species (84). Host specificity of necrotrophic species is conferred through the production of host-specific toxins (HST), which are essential pathogenicity factors on a compati- ble host. Examples include the HSTs from Cochliobolus spp.: HC-toxin from Cochliobolus carbonum, T-toxin from Cochliobolus heterostrophus, and victorin from Cochliobolus victoriae (85). Other examples of HSTs are ToxA toxins, which determine the host range of the wheat tan spot, and blotch diseases caused by Pyreno- phora tritici-repentis and Parastagonospora nodorum, respectively (86). In all cases in which HSTs are in- volved, they interact with a specific host gene, much in the same way that Avr or effector proteins interact with resistance proteins (R proteins). In fact, HSTs share many characteristics with avirulence gene products: they have a primary virulence function, they are specif- ically recognized by host resistance counterparts, and they can be recognized by the plant immune system as virulence factors. It therefore seems appropriate to de- fine HSTs as effectors. This is discussed in more detail in reference 249. Broad-host-range necrotrophs lack HSTs and may attack a wide range of plant species. This group is best represented by B. cinerea and the taxonomically closely related S. sclerotiorum. Both fungi have considerably broader host ranges than most plant pathogens (200 to 400 plant species), and each causes multimillions of dollars in pre- and postharvest annual crop losses worldwide (87–89). Melanized sclerotia play a central role in the life cycle of both fungi by germinating either vegetatively for local colonization or carpogenically to initiate the sexual cycle, including production of apo- thecia from which ascospores are released. There are a plethora of additional broad-host-range necrotrophs, in- cluding many species in the genus Alternaria and others in different taxa. While simply inherited resistance traits are sufficient for conferring protection against host- specific necrotrophs, the genetic basis of resistance against broad-host-range necrotrophic pathogens is much more complex and typically quantitative (83, 84). This is in contrast to R-gene-mediated resistance or HST-blocking genes, which provide complete resistance. For this reason, it is much harder to control broad-host-range necro- trophic pathogens, which might partially explain their growing economical importance. Regardless of host range, the basic strategy of all necrotrophic plant pathogens is to convert living tissue

into dead material. To achieve this goal, necrotrophs target the host cell death pathways. Studies in recent years show that induced host cell death accompanies all stages of necrotrophic infection, and different types of cell death pathways are targeted. At early stages of infection, necrotrophs produce and secrete necrosis- inducing effectors such as Neps, cerato-platanin, and certain glycosyl hydrolases to induce local necrotic cell death (83, 84, 90–92). At later stages, they proba- bly require different types of effectors, which induce PCD. For example, S. sclerotiorum produces oxalic acid, which induces PCD in the host while suppressing autophagy (93, 94). It is important to note that the role of autophagy in pathogenicity is still vague: in some cases it is probably a salvation mechanism that alleviates damage and rescues host cells following PCD, but in other situations it serves as a prodeath mecha- nism (95). Production of reactive oxygen species (ROS) is another central element that mediates the necro- trophic lifestyle (96). Similar to autophagy, ROS prob- ably have multiple effects, and therefore, although they are considered a defense response, they might also serve as a virulence factor, at least in necrotrophic interactions. For example, active modulation of the host redox status appears to be crucial for B. cinerea and S. sclerotiorum diseases (8, 91, 94, 97). In fact,

B. cinerea seems to exploit the plant’s oxidative burst and might even contribute to ROS production. Similar results have been obtained for other necrotrophic fungi,
e.g., in Leptosphaeria maculans, a pathogen of Brassica napus (98). Originally, necrotrophy was interpreted as rather brutal toxin-assisted maceration of the host tissue. In- deed, studies showing the use of hydrolytic enzymes by necrotrophic pathogens go back as far as the beginning of the previous century (4, 99). While current studies show that these enzymes do not work alone, and they might have additional roles other than sheer hydrolysis of plant polymers, it is important to remember that successful necrotrophs rely on massive secretion of such hydrolytic enzymes for maceration and utilization of the plant tissues as a nutritional source. The relatively large number of genes coding for cell-wall-degrading and other hydrolytic enzymes that are present in genomes of nec- rotrophic fungi support this notion (88, 100). The high redundancy of many of these enzymes, which is unusual in fungi, also supports their essentiality in these species. Finally, transcriptome and secretome analyses show that a large number of these enzymes are produced and se- creted at various stages of the infection process (92, 101,
102).

Hemibiotrophs Intuitively, hemibiotrophic pathogens are those spe- cies that combine biotrophic and necrotrophic lifestyles. Most scientists define hemibiotrophs as species that have a variable length of a biotrophic phase before switch- ing to necrotrophy (103–105). This definition entails an initial true biotrophic phase that is mediated by special biotrophic organs. During this phase fungal pathogens also secrete effectors to suppress the plant defense. At the end of the transient biotrophic stage, the fungus undergoes a massive developmental change that medi- ates the transition from a biotrophic mode to a necro- trophic mode. Two cases best fit this scenario: the rice blast fungus M. oryzae and species within the genus Colletotrichum (103). Pathogens belonging to these groups answer the classical definition of hemibiotrophy: following penetration into the subepidermal or epider- mal cells, they develop specialized hyphae that form close contacts with the host and invaginate the host cell membrane, thereby forming a true (although transient) biotrophic interphase. After a certain period of time that may last from 1 to several days (depending on the species), the fungus switches to a classical necrotrophic mode. This transition includes differentiation of the new type of hyphae, secretion of enzymes and toxins, and delivery of specialized effectors. Magnaporthe and Colletotrichum species are im- portant plant pathogens; they can be cultured and are amenable to genetic manipulations, and they exhibit classical hemibiotrophic behavior. For these reasons,

M. oryzae and Colletotrichum spp. have been used as models for the hemibiotrophic lifestyle. Many other species that belong to a wide range of genera are also considered hemibiotrophic. Among them are important plant pathogens such as Fusarium (106, 107), Verti- cillium (108), Mycosphaerella (109, 110), and many others. According to a loose definition, all of the species that are not defined as necrotrophs or biotrophs are potentially hemibiotrophs. We believe that this group- ing might cause confusion, since most of these other species do not exhibit a true hemobiotrophic lifestyle. In fact, the common denominator of all of these species is a symptomless stage that varies in length, but in most cases they do not differentiate the typical biotrophic specialized organs and do not form close contact with the host cell, but rather remain in the apoplastic or in- tracellular sphere. As such, the pathogenic lifestyle of these species includes symptomless, quiescent, latent, or endophytic stages, but they do not meet the crite- ria of hemibiotrophy as defined above and therefore should not be treated in the same way. For this reason,
we limit the discussion of the hemibiotrophic lifestyle to M. oryzae and Colletotrichum spp. Further, although certain species, including M. oryzae and Colletotricum graminicola, can cause systemic infections, the main and prevalent mode of M. oryzae and Colletotricum spp. is foliar and stem infection, and only this mode will be reviewed here. Hemibiotrophic species seem to have evolved highly successful infection strategies, and they are among the most aggressive plant pathogens. Concerning host range, M. oryzae can cause disease in a limited number of grasses; however, it is most well known for the rice blast disease, the most destructive disease of rice (17, 19,

103). Colletotrichum spp. cause anthracnose diseases in more than 600 dicot and monocot plant species and include severe pathogens of important crops (48). Ex- amples are C. graminicola, which causes anthracnose in maize (111), Colletotrichum orbiculare, a pathogen of cucurbits (112), Colletotrichum acutatum, a pathogen of strawberries and tomatoes (113), Colletotrichum lindemuthianum, which attacks bean plants (105), and Colletotrichum gloeosporioides, which can attack a wide range of plant species (114). While some species have a relatively wide host range, others are highly spe- cific and specialize on a single host, and therefore they are defined as forma speciales (f. sp.). An example is
C. gloeosporioides f. sp. aeschynomene, which specifi- cally attacks the legume weed Aeschynomene virginica. Its high aggressiveness and restricted host range were exploited to develop a bioherbicide product (Collego) from conidia of this fungus (115). Infection of M. oryzae and Colletotrichum spp. is typically initiated by conidia. Following sensing of the leaf surface, conidia germinate and form a short germ tube. Interestingly, germination of conidia on a leaf or on surfaces with similar chemophysical properties differs significantly from germination in carbohydrate- rich media. When C. gloeosporioides is pregerminated in a rich medium, conidia are unable to infect plants. Hence, pathogenic and nonpathogenic germination styles were identified (116, 117). Studies with M. oryzae and C. gloeosporioides also have revealed that cellular processes during pathogenic germination are well or- chestrated, involving cell cycle and cell architecture as well as PCD (118). Pathogenic germination ends with the formation of a well-developed, dome-shaped ap- pressorium, which has become the hallmark of hemi- biotrophic species, in particular M. oryzae. A series of groundbreaking studies on appressorium formation in
M. oryzae has elucidated the structural, mechanistic, and molecular mode of action of appressoria (22, 49, 119,

120). Clearly, the main function of appressorium in these species is bringing the fungus underneath the cu- ticle without alarming the plant defense. This is achieved by poking an extremely thin hole in the cuticle by physical force, thereby avoiding release of plant-derived elicitors. Once the fungus has successfully moved from the surface of the leaf to the subcuticular space, a tran- sient biotrophic phase begins. Similar to the situation in the other lifestyles, these late infection stages are less uniform and vary in details from one species to another. In M. oryzae, the penetrating peg breaches the wall of the cell underneath the appressorium and immediately differentiates into a thin filamentous primary hypha that grows in the cell lumen and invaginates the host plasma membrane. In this respect, the first biotrophic stages of M. oryzae resemble the biotrophic development that is typical of powdery mildews. The primary invasive hyphae differentiate into bulbous invasive hyphae that become sealed in an extra-invasive hyphal membrane compartment and exhibit pseudohyphal growth as they fill the invaded cell (25, 26, 121). At a specific time, the invasive hyphae move to adjacent cells, probably though plasmodesmata, and repeat the same invagination and proliferation steps, while the hyphae in the original cell switch to a necrotrophic behavior and instantly kill the host cell. Clearly, this sequence of events must be closely coordinated with the host defense. Indeed, M. oryzae utilizes a range of stage-specific effectors to affect the host, starting at the stage of appressorium formation and continuing for the entire course of the interaction (103, 122, 123). Furthermore, M. oryzae uses distinct pathways for secreting cytoplasmic and apoplastic effectors (124). Secretion of apoplastic effectors occurs through the conventional Golgi-mediated secretion pathway. However, cytoplasmic effectors accumulate in the biotrophic interfacial complex. In Colletotrichum spp. there are two main types of biotrophic stages. The first resembles the biotrophic phases of rice blast and powdery mildews, in which the penetration peg breaches the wall of a subcuticular cell under the appressorium and differentiates a primary hypha, which invaginates the cell membrane, forming a transient biotrophic stage. This type of interaction is best studied in Colletotrichum higginsianum, a path- ogen of crucifers including Arabidopsis thaliana (104). In C. higginsianum, biotrophic infection is confined to the initially infected epidermal cell, and development then switches to necrotrophic infection hyphae, which ramify throughout the host cells. The second type of Colletotrichum biotrophy resembles the biotrophic phase of rusts, in which primary hyphae first invade the
mesophyll and then form intimate contact with meso- phyll cells. This mode of development is best exempli- fied by the maize pathogen C. graminicola. Biotrophic invasion of new cells by C. graminicola persists at the advancing colony margin. Necrotrophic hyphae develop later in the center of the colony and advance outward, resulting in the overlapping of biotrophic and necro- trophic phases (125, 125). Similar to M. oryzae, the genomes of Colletotrichum spp. encode a large number of putative effectors. Expression of many of these pu- tative effectors in C. higginsianum is highly orchestrated, and they fall into four distinct expression clusters (48). Gene expression studies also show orchestrated expres- sion of many genes of secondary metabolism, at least several of which are suspected to play a role in infec- tion (104, 127). As might be expected, the genomes of these species are also enriched for genes encoding plant cell wall-degrading enzymes (CWDEs), which are expected to be necessary at the necrotrophic phase (104).

### Plant Cell Death in Fungal Interactions

In a broad sense, all fungal pathogens might be divided into killers and nonkillers, regardless of the specific life- style (128). According to this point of view, the ultimate goal of killer pathogens is conversion of living tissue into dead organic matter, and therefore they are pro- grammed to efficiently kill host cells. Nonkiller patho- gens aim to keep their host alive, and therefore they are programmed to prevent host cells from dying. While the details differ from one pathogen to another, this categorization ultimately relies on the general infection strategy: immediate killing or prevention of death of host cells. As a consequence, cell-death-regulating path- ways are expected to play a central role in mediating all types of pathogenic fungal-host interactions. For example, it is intuitive to expect that nonkiller patho- gens are armed with effectors that suppress PCD, while killer pathogens have molecules that trigger and main- tain PCD. This hypothesis is supported by the oppo- site effects of antiapoptotic genes on infection by killer and nonkiller pathogens. For example, expression of heterologous (e.g., Bcl2, Bcl-xL) or endogenous (BI-1) antiapoptotic genes protects plants against killer path- ogens such as B. cinerea and S. sclerotiorum (129, 130) while increasing susceptibility to powdery mildew (131,

132).
### Host Specificity

Another aspect to consider is a possible connection be- tween lifestyle and host specificity. As described above,

host range varies considerably, from single-host-specific species to broad-host-range pathogens that are capable of causing different types of diseases either in the same host or on numerous hosts. Obligate biotrophic species seem to have more restricted host specificity than species with hemibiotrophic or necrotrophic lifestyles. Even nonobligate biotrophic species seem to have the capacity to infect a broad range of hosts, as demonstrated by

C. purpurea, which is capable of infecting more than 400 plant species (81). Moreover, at the genus level even obligate biotrophic pathogens may have a broad host range. For example, powdery mildews cause diseases in over 10,000 plant species, including monocots and dicots, shrubs and trees (133). Hence, species with a broad host range seem to exist in all types of lifestyles. Additionally, as mentioned earlier, some necrotrophic species are highly specialized, as demonstrated by spe- cies in the genus Cochliobolus (85). Thus, pathogens with a restricted or broad host range exist in the entire array of lifestyles. It is, however, possible that the host range in each class is limited by different types of elements of the plant defense. For example, the take-all fungus Gaeumannomyces graminis f. sp. tritici is un- able to infect oat because it is blocked by avenacine B, a saponin that accumulates in the oat roots (134, 135). However, a closely related species, G. graminis f. sp. avena can degrade avenacine B and therefore can infect both oat and wheat. In this case, host range is limited by a single defense molecule that confers resistance to the pathogen, while a single fungal enzyme is sufficient to extend the host range to additional plants. In the case of obligate parasites, it is enough for the plant to initiate the HR response for elimination of the pathogen. It is possible that for this reason host range is restricted by recognition factors (resistance/avirulence proteins) that instantly trigger HR, rather than by enzyme- or metabolite-mediated activities.
### VIRULENCE TOOLS OF PLANT

### PATHOGENIC FUNGI

Disease establishment of fungal pathogens on their host plants relies on diverse virulence factors, including effector proteins, secondary metabolites, and even small RNA molecules. These virulence factors could be in- volved in host penetration, prevention, or suppression of host defenses and nutrient acquisition. In addition to these factors, virulence of plant pathogenic fungi also requires morphological transitions to establish host colonization and complete their life cycles. On the other side, plants have evolved diverse innate immune systems

to defend against pathogen infection. The pattern rec- ognition receptors localized on the plant cell membrane are designed to recognize PAMPs. These microbial sig- natures (such as flagellin, chitin, and glucan fragments) trigger the basal resistance response called PTI, including oxidative burst, ion fluxes, activation of MAPK signal pathways, synthesis of plant hormone, and callose de- position (35, 136, 137). Many fungal virulence factors aim to inhibit or avoid PTI and dampen basal defenses, but they can also be perceived by plant leucine-rich re- peat proteins, which are known as R proteins, triggering an effector-triggered immunity (138–140). The effector- triggered immunity defense is often accompanied by localized plant cell death or HR (138, 141). To infect plant hosts effectively, these pathogens with different lifestyles adopt diverse strategies and/or virulence fac- tors to manipulate the physiology of host plants and thus to interfere with plant immunity systems and promote virulence or compatibility effectively.

Mycotoxins Under favorable environmental conditions, fungal path- ogens can secrete a variety of secondary metabolites, in- cluding mycotoxins and secretory proteinaceous toxins, which induce cell death and contribute to the pathoge- nicity on plant hosts (142). These toxins can be divided into general toxins and HSTs.

### General toxins

The best-known non-host-specific sesquiterpene phyto- toxin is botrydial, which is produced by the necrotroph

B. cinerea during plant infection (143). Botrydial in- duces chlorosis and collapse of French bean tissues (144) and causes necrosis in A. thaliana leaves (145). Further studies demonstrated that botrydial can also induce the accumulation of ROS and phenolic compounds, callose deposition, expression of the defense-associated genes PR1 and PDF1.2, which are involved in salicylic acid (SA) and jasmonic acid signal pathways, respectively, and the expression of HR marker HSR3 in host plants (145). Additionally, deletion of one of the botrydial biosynthetic pathway genes, bcbot1, led to reduced vir- ulence only in the T4 strain, indicating that botrydial is a strain-dependent virulence factor (146). Oxalic acid (OA), another well-known non-host- specific toxin, is considered an important pathogenicity factor in S. sclerotiorum. This pathogen secretes copious amounts of OA to rapidly kill host plants, chelate Ca
2+ , and reduce pH for activation of CWDEs during infec- tion to facilitate pathogenicity (147). Consequently, OA- deficient mutants were found to be nonpathogenic (148).

However, several studies indicated that OA has more significant roles than only functioning as a direct killer, because it can suppress host oxidative burst (149) and trigger ROS-mediated apoptotic-like PCD (94). Recent studies also indicate that OA can reduce the plant’s re- dox environment conditions ahead of advancing hyphae (93, 97), suppressing autophagy and callose deposition during an early infection stage, but once infection is established, promoting host PCD at a later infection stage (93). Sphinganine mycotoxin fumonisin B1 produced by several Fusarium spp. can induce plant cell death, ac- cumulation of camalexin and ROS, callose deposition, and expression of defense genes in A. thaliana (150,

151). Fumonisin B1 also contributes to fungal patho- genicity and is necessary for development of disease symptoms on susceptible maize seedlings (152, 153). Some Fusarium spp., such as Fusarium graminearum, can produce multiple trichothecene mycotoxins, in- cluding deoxynivalenol (DON), acetyldeoxynivalenol (3-ADON or 15-ADON), nivalenol (NIV) and acetyl- nivalenol (4-ANIV), and polyketide zearalenone, in infected plant tissues (154, 155). DON can inhibit pro- tein synthesis in eukaryotic organisms and also acts as a significant virulence factor during infection (156–
159). Other toxins, such as fusicoccin from Fusicoccum amygdali and AAL toxin from Alternaria alternata, can induce both expression of pathogenesis-related genes and cell death in host plants (160). HSTs HST proteins are produced by a small number of host- specific necrotrophs. These proteins can function as effectors by interacting with their host targets and uti- lizing plant resistance signaling pathways to trigger HR and/or PCD for facilitating infection (86, 161). To date, multiple interactions between HST effectors of necro- trophic fungal pathogens and their host target proteins have been reported. Victorin produced by C. victoriae can interact with its targets TRX-h5 as well as LOV1 of
A. thaliana, mimicking the roles of conventional effec- tors of biotrophic and hemibiotrophic pathogens to ac- tivate an HR-like response for promoting disease (162,
163). The Sorghum bicolor Pc locus determines the sensitivity to PC toxin protein of the pathogen Periconia circinata (164). This inverse gene-for-gene method also exists in the wheat pathosystem P. nodorum.Todate, nine pairs of secreted necrotrophic effector-host sensi- tivity gene interactions have been identified in this system: SnToxA-Tsn1 (165), SnTox1-Snn1 (166), SnTox2-Snn2 (167), SnTox3-Snn3-B1 (168, 169), SnTox3-Snn3-D1
(170), SnTox4-Snn4 (171), SnTox5-Snn5 (172), SnTox6- Snn6 (173), and SnTox7-Snn7 (174). These are discussed in some detail in reference 249.

CWDEs A diverse range of CWDEs, including xyloglucanases, polygalacturonases, glucanases, cellulases, and pectin- ases, are secreted by fungal pathogens. These enzymes are necessary for degradation of the main structural polysaccharide of plant cell walls, primarily cellulose, hemicellulose, and pectin (175–178). To study the direct contributions of CWDEs to the virulence of fungal path- ogens during infection, genes encoding CWDEs have been disrupted and the effect on virulence has been eval- uated in the mutants. Mutation in B. cinerea xylanase gene Xyn11A resulted in reduced ability of the mu- tants to infect grape berries and tomato leaves (179). In

C. gloeosporioides, deletion of the pectate lyase gene PelB resulted in weak virulence on avocado fruit (180), while deletion of another pectate lyase gene, CcpelA, in Colletotrichum coccodes led to a substantial loss of virulence on green tomato fruit (181). Two cellulases from M. oryzae, belonging to glycosyl hydrolase families 6 and 7, were proved by RNA interference to contrib- ute to penetration of the fungus into wheat leaf epider- mis and for further invasion (182). Deletion of either endopolygalacturonase gene Bcpg1 or Bcpg2 in B. cinerea resulted in obvious reduction in virulence on bean and tomato leaves (183, 184). Previously, it was generally accepted that the effect of CWDEs on pathogens’ virulence was dependent on its enzymatic activity, and the lack of a clear pathogenicity phenotype in many CWDE mutants was attributed to re- dundancy. However, several recent studies have shown that at least some CWDEs may also act as virulence factors, either dependent or independent of their enzyme degradation activity. Several studies demonstrated that multiple CWDEs are detected as PAMPs by plant innate immune systems to induce responses (185, 186). For ex- ample, several endopolygalacturonases from B. cinerea can be recognized as PAMPs by the A. thaliana receptor- like protein RLP42 to promote immune responses (187), and one of these endopolygalacturonases, BcPG1, can induce defense responses independently of its enzy- matic activity (188). The B. cinerea glucan 1,4-alpha- glucosidase BcGs1 can elicit systemic resistance against
B. cinerea, Pseudomonas syringae pv. tomato DC3000, and tobacco mosaic virus (189). After Xyn11A was found to be necessary for virulence of B. cinerea (179), further studies showed that the enzymatic activity did not contribute to virulence, which was only affected

by the necrosis-inducing activity of the peptide (190). The cutinase ScCut from S. sclerotiorum causes HR-like PCD in host plants and functions as an elicitor to trigger multiple defense responses in plants (191).

Effectors Biotrophic effectors Biotrophs depend on secreted effectors at several levels. Primarily, effectors are required during penetration to inhibit plant defense responses and/or to keep the host alive because of the lifestyle of biotrophic patho- gens. Functional studies of several effectors have been conducted in the maize smut U. maydis. The effector Pep1 was first identified to be required for epidermal penetration (192). Later it was found that Pep1 can suppress the activity of the maize peroxidase POX12, which is involved in ROS generation (193, 194). The secreted effector Pit2 from U. maydis can inhibit the activity of maize cysteine proteases which can induce plant defenses of SA signal pathways (195), and muta- tion of Pit2 in U. maydis causes severely attenuated tumors and virulence (196). The plant hormone SA is involved in a defense response against pathogens, espe- cially biotrophic pathogens (197, 198). The chorismate mutase Cmu1, which is secreted into plant cells’ cyto- plasm by U. maydis, can reduce the levels of SA pre- cursor chorismate, thus interfering with the synthesis of SA and promoting infection (199). The U. maydis effector See1 can interfere with the MAPK-triggered phosphorylation of maize SGT1, thus inhibiting its activity and resulting in modulation of reactivation of DNA synthesis in leaf cells, which is crucial for tumor progression (200). The U. maydis effector Tin2 interacts with and stabilizes maize kinase ZmTTK1, thus target- ing the pathway of the maize secondary metabolite an- thocyanin and promoting infection (201, 202). In the C. fulvum-tomato pathosystem, for almost all identified effectors there is a corresponding tomato Cf-R trait (203). The presence of many cloned and function- ally characterized avirulence genes and their corres- ponding Cf genes of tomato, and the emergence of many races overcoming newly introduced Cf genes, has made the C. fulvum-tomato interaction a good model system. Eleven effectors have been identified in C. fulvum, in- cluding Avr2, Avr4, Avr5, Avr9, Ecp1, Ecp2, Ecp4, Ecp5, and Ecp6 (203, 204). However, only three of them have been functionally characterized. Avr4 is a chitin- binding protein that shields the fungal cell wall and protects the fungus against the deleterious effect of plant chitinases (14). The LysM domain containing effector Ecp6 is another chitin binding protein that sequesters

chitin fragments released from fungal cell walls to pre- vent the elicitation of chitin-induced plant defenses (12). Avr2 is a cysteine protease inhibitor that binds to and inhibits the function of tomato Rcr3 cysteine protease activity (205). During coevolution, different pathogens that share the same host can independently develop different types of effectors that interact with the same host target important for plant immunity. For exam- ple, Avr2 of C. fulvum (fungi) (205, 206), EPIC1, and EPIC2B of Phytophthora infestans (an oomycete that causes late blight in potato and tomato) (207) and Gr- VAP1 of Globodera rostochiensis (yellow potato cyst nematode that can infect tomato) (208) can interact and inhibit tomato cysteine protease, Rcr3 pim . The effector candidate CSEP0055 from the biotrophic pathogen B. graminis f. sp. hordei can interact with plant pathogenesis-related proteins PR1 and PR17, lead- ing to inhibition of plant immunity (209). Silencing of CSEP0055 by host-induced gene silence technology resulted in decreased frequency of fungal penetration sites (209). Silencing of RNase-like effectors BEC1011 and BEC1054 in B. graminis f. sp. hordei indicated their involvement in virulence, and complementation experi- ments showed that both of them may function inside the plant cell and that BEC1011 may be involved in inter- fering with pathogen-induced host cell death (210). The putative effector BEC4 from B. graminis f. sp. hordei can interact with ADP ribosylation factor-GTPase-activating protein and may interfere with defense-associated host vesicle trafficking (211). The yeast two-hybrid assay indi- cated that candidate secreted effector proteins CSEP0105 and CSEP0162 from B. graminis f. sp. hordei can interact with small heat shock proteins Hsp16.9 and Hsp17.5 of barley. Silencing of either of them resulted in decreased rates of haustorial formation. CSEP0105 contributes to fungal virulence by compromising the chaperone activity of barley Hsp16.9, which is involved in defense and stress responses against pathogen infection (212).

### Hemibiotrophic effectors

Hemibiotrophic fungi require effectors to suppress the plant’s immune system during their biotrophic stage, while a different set of effectors is needed to kill plant cells at the necrotrophic stage. Prominent examples of effector interactions during the biotrophic phase in hemibiotrophs (but also in true biotrophs) include recognition of cell wall fragments. The LysM domain-containing pattern recognition re- ceptor protein CEBiP in rice can recognize and bind the chitin oligosaccharides released from the cell walls of fungal pathogens directly, thus promoting chitin-

triggered immune responses in rice cells (213, 214). The secretory effector protein Slp1 of M. oryzae also con- tains LysM domains, and it specifically accumulates at the interface of fungus and rice cells at the early stages of infection. Slp1 can compete with CEBiP for chitin oligosaccharides binding through the LysM domains, thus interfering with chitin-triggered immunity in the host. Consequently, virulence of M. oryzae slp1 gene deletion strain Δslp1 was decreased compared to wild type (16). The cytoplasmic effector AvrPiz-t of M. oryzae contributes to virulence in rice plants that carry the R protein Piz-t. AvrPiz-t interacts with the rice RING E3 ubiquitin ligase APIP6 and inhibits its activity, thus suppressing oxidative burst and PTI in rice, which promotes virulence of M. oryzae (30). The M. oryzae avirulence protein ACE1 contains a polyketide synthase and a nonribosomal peptide syn- thetase domain. ACE1 is exclusively upregulated in ap- pressoria during the penetration stage (215–217). The unknown secondary metabolite synthesized by Ace1 is recognized by the R protein Pi33 in rice, which causes resistance. Mutations in the polyketide synthase do- main of Ace1 led to the abolishment of Pi33-mediated avirulence activation (217). The predicted metallopro- tease protein Avr-Pita from M. oryzae could elicit R protein Pi-ta-dependent resistance response in rice cells, but it did not contribute to virulence (218). The viru- lence-associated effector MC69 is involved in develop- ment of invasive hyphae after appressorium formation in rice leaf sheath, indicating its important roles in in- teraction with host plants. In addition, deletion of the MC69 orthologous gene in C. orbiculare also reduced the pathogenicity on both cucumber and tobacco leaves (219).The Pwl effectors Pwl1 and Pwl4 in M. oryzae can also act as Avr proteins that confer species-specific avirulence on weeping lovegrass (220, 221). An effector from M. oryzae that contributes to the necrotrophic phase is the snodprot1 homolog, MSP1, a member of the cerato-platanin family. MSP1 is secreted and contributes to the pathogenicity of M. oryzae on barley leaves. Ap- plication of recombinant MSP1 produced in Escherichia coli induces autophagy cell death, H2O2 production, and defense responses in rice leaves (31). Recently, a number of novel effectors were uncovered by genomic and transcriptomic analysis in the most aggressive

M. oryzae strain, 98-06. Five of the tested proteins can inhibit BAX-mediated apoptosis-like PCD in Nicotiana benthamiana leaves. The isolate-specific effector can- didates Iug6 and Iug9 play significant roles in fungal propagation and pathogenicity, and overexpression of them suppresses the expression of defense-related genes
in rice, suggesting their roles in the inhibition of host SA and ethylene signal pathways (28). The tomato pathogen Fusarium oxysporum f. sp. lycopersici, which primarily grows in xylem vessels, secretes SIX (secreted-in-xylem proteins) effector pro- teins into the xylem to promote infection. To date, 14 SIX effector proteins (Six1-14) have been identi- fied using mass spectrometry in F. oxysporum f. sp. lycopersici-infected tomato (222, 223). Several studies demonstrated that SIX1 (Avr3), SIX3 (Avr2), and SIX6 are required for pathogenicity on susceptible tomato lines (224–226). All three proteins also function as Avr proteins that trigger activation of resistance in the presence of the resistance genes; SIX4 (Avr1), Avr2, and Avr3 can trigger HR in plants harboring the R proteins I-1, I-2, and I-3, respectively (226). Additionally, Avr1 inhibits I-2- and I-3-mediated resistance (227), and re- cently, SIX6 was suggested to be involved in suppression of Avr2/I-2-mediated cell death when coexpressed in

N. benthamiana (226, 228). Recently, a study showed that Avr2 interacts with SIX5, and the AVR2-SIX5 pair is required to activate I-2-mediated resistance in tomato (229). Hemibiotrophic pathogens from Colletotrichum spp. can devastate many crop plants worldwide. To date, a number of genomic and transcriptomic studies in Col- letotrichum spp. fungi have been conducted, and many candidate effectors were revealed during the interaction with host plants (48, 104, 127, 230, 231). The expres- sion of the CgDN3 gene encodes a secreted protein from
C. gloeosporioides that was induced at the early infec- tion stage, and the CgDN3 deletion transformant failed to infect and reproduce on intact host leaves. Further- more, CgDN3 was also involved in averting the hyper- sensitive-like response in host Stylosanthes guianensis (232). A necrosis-inducing secreted protein 1 (NIS1), which induces cell death in N. benthamiana leaves, was identified by functional screening of C. orbiculare cDNA, and the NIS1-induced cell death was suppressed by CgDN3 (233). The gene CtNUDIX, which encodes a nudix hydrolase domain containing effector from
C. truncatum, is exclusively expressed during the late biotrophic stage, and CtNUDIX can elicit HR-like cell death in tobacco leaves. However, overexpression of CtNUDIX in C. truncatum and M. oryzae resulted in an HR-like response in infected plants and caused incompatibility with the host plants, indicating that
C. truncatum utilizes CtNUDIX to elicit cell death to signal the transition from biotrophy to the necrotrophy phase (234). The host’s nuclear targeted effector CgEP1 from the maize anthracnose pathogen C. graminicola is

synthesized at the early stages of infection and contrib- utes to disease development in maize. CgEP1 can bind to the promoter regions of several genes, thus transcrip- tionally regulating their expression to affect host im- munity (235). Another effector of C. graminicola Cgfl (fungalysin metalloprotease), which specifically expressed at the biotrophic stage of infection, was identified with a role in virulence on maize leaves and roots. The maize leaves exhibited increased chitinase activity in the absence of Cgfl in the in vitro chitinase activity assays (236).

### Necrotrophic effectors

Since necrotrophs extract nutrition from dead plant tis- sue, their virulence factors mainly aim to induce host necrosis to facilitate infection. The cerato-platanin BcSpl1 secreted by B. cinerea elicits the hypersensitive response in several host plants, and deletion of this gene results in reduced virulence in a variety of host plants, indicating that BcSpl1 is required for virulence (237, 238). Fur- thermore, BcSpl1 also induces systemic acquired resis- tance in tobacco to P. syringae and B. cinerea (239).

B. cinerea could also manipulate the antagonistic effects between different immune pathways by utilizing exo- polysaccharide β-(1,3)(1,6)-D-glucan to activate the SA signal pathway in tomato, thus inhibiting the jasmonic acid signaling pathway through NPR-1 and resulting in enhanced host susceptibility to B. cinerea (230).
B. cinerea can also deliver virulent small RNA effectors into host plant cells to bind to Arabidopsis Argonaute 1 (AGO1), hijack the plant’s RNA interference machinery, and selectively silence host immunity genes, thus sup- pressing host immunity and facilitating infection (241).
S. sclerotiorum secretes an integrin-like protein,
SSITL, which functions as an effector to directly or indirectly suppress jasmonic/ethylene signal pathway- mediated immune responses in host plants at the early stages of infection. Silencing of SSITL caused a signifi- cant reduction in virulence and other phenotypic defects (242). Ss-Caf1 is another S. sclerotiorum small secretory protein with a putative Ca 2+ -binding motif. Ss-Caf1 functions as a pathogenicity factor to trigger host cell death during the early stages of S. sclerotiorum infection and plays a significant role in the formation of infec- tion cushion and sclerotial development (243). Another

S. scletoriorum protein, SsCm1, has high structural and functional similarity to the U. maydis effector Cmu1. One could speculate that this putative effector might serve a similar function as Cmu1 in U. maydis (199). A small secreted virulence-related protein, SsSSVP1 from
S. sclerotiorum, induces plant cell death, and targeted silencing of SsSSVP1 causes decreased virulence. In ad-
dition, SsSSVP1 can interact with and hijack the activ- ity of QCR8 (cytochrome b-c1 complex subunit 8) by changing its subcellular localization from mitochon- dria to cytoplasm, hence disabling its biological func- tions. This causes abnormal plant phenotype and cell death (244). A new proteinaceous elicitor, sclerotinia culture filtrate elicitor 1 (SCFE1) from S. sclerotiorum, has been partially purified and induces typical PAMP- triggered immune responses through the RLP30/SOBIR1/ BAK1-dependent signaling pathway in A. thaliana (245). A cysteine-rich secretory protein, SsCVNH from S. scle- rotiorum, is essential for the virulence and sclerotial de- velopment, suggesting its diverse roles in host-nonspecific necrotrophic fungi (246). Two genes encoding necrosis and ethylene-inducing peptides, SsNep1 and SsNep2, have been cloned from S. sclerotiorum. Transient expres- sion of them in tobacco leaves induces necrosis, and SsNep2 is involved in virulence (247).

CONCLUSIONS As outlined in this article, fungi are a highly diverse group of plant pathogens with an enormous impact on agriculture. Fungi have a broad spectrum of life- styles and high genetic flexibility, which allows patho- gens to quickly invade new hosts, as well as to develop resistance to fungicide treatments or to break R-gene- mediated plant resistance. Therefore, fighting fungal diseases will remain a major challenge in plant pathol- ogy for the future. To develop new strategies to control plant pathogenic fungi, we need to increase our knowl- edge of fungal virulence mechanisms, of how pathogenic traits evolve, of the molecular basis of host adaptation, and in particular, of how crop resistance can be en- gineered in a more sustainable way. Besides these applied aspects, plant pathogenic fungi are fascinating organisms that have developed a plethora of strategies to colonize a living plant. Studying the molecular mechanisms underlying these complex inter- actions will not only teach us about microbial virulence, but is also an excellent strategy to study cellular pro- cesses in plants. In particular, effector proteins are great molecular tools to study plant pathways, because they represent perfectly adapted probes to target specific cellular processes in the host. Beyond effector proteins, there is still little known about the functions of micro RNAs in fungal-plant interactions. Similarly, secondary metabolites are clearly an understudied field when it comes to biotrophic interactions. Finally, in past years, fungal-plant interactions were mostly studied in one-to- one situations, but this is very artificial because plants

are always colonized by a complex microbiome in which multiple microbes influence each other and affect the plants in multiple ways. It is getting more and more evident that multitrophic interactions shape the outcome of pathogen infections. Therefore, future research on fungal plant pathogens will need to consider the effect of microbial communities on pathogen development and plant health.
