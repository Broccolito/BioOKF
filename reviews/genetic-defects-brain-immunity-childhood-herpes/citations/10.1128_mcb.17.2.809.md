Pre-mRNAintronsareexcisedfromprecursorRNAinthe form of a lariat RNA structure during the process of RNA splicing.Inthisstructure,the59 endoftheintronisjoinedvia a29-59 phosphodiester linkage to an internal adenosine (A) residue (the branchpoint nucleotide). These introns are ex- cisedbyatwo-stepreaction(9,25,29).Inthefirststep,the59 splicesiteiscleavedtoyielda59 exonintermediate(exon1). Concurrently,the59 endoftheintronisjoinedtothebranch site(usuallyanadenosineresidue),forminga29-59 phosphodi- ester bond. Incontrasttothesplicingpathwayleadingfrompre-mRNA to mRNA, very little is known about the pathway of intron degradation. Considering the enormous quantity of intron RNA that is discarded during the process of normal gene expression in mammalian cells, for example, intron degrada- tion is likely to be an important pathway for normal cellular function.Onlyonecomponentoftheintronturnoverpathway has been identified biochemically and genetically, the RNA lariat-debranching enzyme (DBR). DBR specifically hydro- lyzes the 29-59 phosphodiester bond at the branchpoint of in- tron lariat RNAs, converting lariats into linear molecules (6,

15, 30). The linearized introns are subsequently degraded by undefinedexonucleases. In contrast to higher eukaryotes, which typically contain severallargeintronsinmostgenes,relativelyfew S.cerevisiae genes contain introns, and they nearly always contain only a single intron (12, 27). Atleasttwohypothesescanbeproposedfortheslowgrowth ofthemutant:(i)theloadofunprocessedintronsmayitselfbe physiologicallydeleteriousor(ii)debranchingactivitymaybe required for an essential activity, such as the efficient expres- sion of an intron-encoded product. In the first hypothesis, a large fraction of cellular nucleotides is tied up in the form of undegraded intron lariats. It may be that these nucleotides needtoberecycledforoptimalgrowth.Alternatively,thelarge quantities of excised introns could actively interfere with a process,suchasRNAsplicing,bytitratingoutessentialfactors. However, it does not appear that pre-mRNA splicing is gen- erally blocked in *S. pombe, because, at least for the two* mRNAsthatwehaveexamined,accumulationofunprocessed pre-mRNAs was not observed in the dbr1 mutant cells. This resembles the situation in S. cerevisiae, for which studies of severalintron-encodedgeneshaveshowntheaccumulationof intronsbutnotthecorrespondingpre-mRNAs(6,35). Recent results have shown that certain pre-mRNA introns can contain the coding sequences for small nucleolar RNAs (snoRNAs)thatmaybeimportantforrRNAassemblyormat- uration.Tycowskietal.(34)discoveredthatthehumanUHG transcript contains nine introns, eight of which encode snoR- NAs.Furthermore,thesplicedUHGtranscripthaslittlepro- tein-coding capacity or protein-coding sequence homology to themouseUHGtranscript.Thus,theintronsratherthanexons encode the critical functions of UHG. This unique RNA se- quence emphasizes the possibly important roles of so-called junk intron sequences and raises the possibility that lariat- debranching enzyme might be required for the efficient pro- cessing of these snoRNAs. Some of these snoRNAs are con- servedin S.pombe,inwhichtheyarealsointronencoded(2). SomestudiesoftheprocessingenzymesthatliberatetheseU

RNAs from their host introns indicate that they do so by exonucleolytic mechanisms (5, 19), so these processing en- zymeswouldbeblockedina dbr1 mutant.However,studiesof other snoRNA transcripts suggest that endonucleolytic path- waysmayalsooperate(4).Itispossiblethatintronlariatsare thenormalsourceofcertainsnoRNAsandmaynotproduceU RNAs efficiently, and this could lead to the observed growth defect. Finally, as-yet-undiscovered intron-encoded products may require lariat-debranching function for their efficient ex- pression. Further experiments will be required to distinguish betweentheseinterestingpossibilities.
